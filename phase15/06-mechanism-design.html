Mechanism Design

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 6

The problem

Game theory asks: given a game, what will rational players do? Mechanism design asks the reverse: given a desired outcome, can you design a game (a mechanism) that makes rational players produce it? This is "reverse game theory."

Vickrey (1961) showed how to design an auction where bidding truthfully is a dominant strategy. Arrow (1951) showed something darker: no voting system can satisfy a short list of reasonable properties simultaneously.

1. The mechanism design problem

A social planner wants to achieve some outcome — say, allocate a painting to whoever values it most — but does not know the players' private information. Each player $i$ has a type $\theta_i$ (e.g., their valuation for the painting), known only to themselves. The planner's goal is described by a social choice function $f(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$ that maps type profiles to outcomes.

The planner cannot simply ask players to report their types, because they may lie if lying gets them a better outcome. A mechanism $M = (S_1, \ldots, S_n, g)$ specifies a strategy set $S_i$ for each player and an outcome function $g : S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n \to \text{Outcomes}$. The planner designs $M$ so that rational play in $M$ produces the desired outcome $f(\theta)$ for every type profile $\theta$.

The question is: for which social choice functions does such a mechanism exist?

2. The Vickrey auction

One object is for sale. There are $n$ bidders. Bidder $i$ has a private valuation $v_i$ for the object. The planner wants to allocate the object to the bidder with the highest valuation — the efficient outcome.

Definition — second-price sealed-bid (Vickrey) auction

Each bidder submits a sealed bid $b_i$. The highest bidder wins the object. The winner pays the second-highest bid: $\max_{j \neq i} b_j$.

Theorem — truthful bidding in the Vickrey auction

In the second-price sealed-bid auction, bidding $b_i = v_i$ (your true valuation) is a weakly dominant strategy for every bidder.

Proof

Step 1. Fix bidder $i$ with valuation $v_i$. Let $p = \max_{j \neq i} b_j$ be the highest bid among the other bidders (which $i$ cannot control). We show that bidding $b_i = v_i$ is at least as good as any other bid $b_i'$, regardless of $p$.

Step 2. If $v_i > p$: bidding $v_i$ wins, paying $p$, giving surplus $v_i - p > 0$. Bidding $b_i' > p$ also wins with the same payment $p$ — same surplus. Bidding $b_i' < p$ loses, giving surplus $0$. Bidding $b_i' = p$ ties (surplus $\geq 0$). So $b_i = v_i$ is weakly best.

Step 3. If $v_i < p$: bidding $v_i$ loses, giving surplus $0$. Bidding $b_i' < p$ also loses — same surplus. Bidding $b_i' > p$ wins, but pays $p > v_i$, giving surplus $v_i - p < 0$. So $b_i = v_i$ is weakly best.

Step 4. If $v_i = p$: bidding $v_i$ ties, paying $p = v_i$, surplus $0$. Any other bid gives surplus $\leq 0$. So $b_i = v_i$ is weakly best.

Step 5. In every case, $b_i = v_i$ does at least as well as any alternative, and strictly better in cases where overbidding or underbidding changes the outcome. Truthful bidding is a weakly dominant strategy.

∎

The Vickrey auction achieves efficient allocation through a dominant strategy equilibrium (page 4). The key insight: by making the payment independent of the winner's own bid (you pay the second-highest bid), the auction removes any incentive to misreport your valuation.

3. The revelation principle

Must we search over all possible mechanisms to find one that implements a given social choice function? The revelation principle says no — we can restrict attention to a very simple class.

Definition — direct mechanism

A direct mechanism asks each player to report their type $\theta_i$. The outcome is then $g(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$. A direct mechanism is incentive-compatible if truthful reporting is an equilibrium.

Theorem — revelation principle

If a social choice function $f$ can be implemented by any mechanism $M$ in some equilibrium, then $f$ can also be implemented by a direct, incentive-compatible mechanism where truthful reporting is an equilibrium.

Proof (sketch)

Step 1. Suppose mechanism $M$ implements $f$. In equilibrium, each player $i$ of type $\theta_i$ plays strategy $s_i^*(\theta_i)$, and the outcome is $g(s_1^*(\theta_1), \ldots, s_n^*(\theta_n)) = f(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$.

Step 2. Construct a direct mechanism $M'$: players report types $\hat{\theta}_i$, and the mechanism computes the outcome as $g(s_1^*(\hat{\theta}_1), \ldots, s_n^*(\hat{\theta}_n))$.

Step 3. If player $i$ reports truthfully ($\hat{\theta}_i = \theta_i$), the outcome is the same as in $M$'s equilibrium. If $i$ misreports $\hat{\theta}_i \neq \theta_i$, this is equivalent to playing $s_i^*(\hat{\theta}_i)$ instead of $s_i^*(\theta_i)$ in $M$ — which is not profitable since $s_i^*(\theta_i)$ was an equilibrium strategy. Therefore truthful reporting is an equilibrium in $M'$.

∎

The revelation principle enormously simplifies mechanism design: instead of searching over all possible games, the planner need only check whether the desired outcome can be achieved with truthful reporting in a direct mechanism.

4. Arrow's impossibility theorem

Mechanism design also reveals fundamental limits. The most famous is Arrow's impossibility theorem, which shows that no voting system can satisfy a handful of natural desiderata.

Consider $n \geq 2$ voters and $m \geq 3$ candidates. Each voter submits a strict ranking of all candidates. A social welfare function $F$ aggregates individual rankings into a social ranking.

Theorem — Arrow's impossibility theorem (1951)

No social welfare function on $m \geq 3$ candidates can simultaneously satisfy:

(U) Unanimity: if every voter ranks $A$ above $B$, the social ranking places $A$ above $B$.

(IIA) Independence of irrelevant alternatives: the social ranking of $A$ vs $B$ depends only on voters' individual rankings of $A$ vs $B$, not on how they rank other candidates.

(ND) Non-dictatorship: there is no voter $d$ such that the social ranking always agrees with $d$'s ranking.

Proof (sketch for 2 voters and 3 candidates $A, B, C$)

Step 1. By unanimity, if both voters rank $A > B$, so does society. We investigate what happens when they disagree.

Step 2. Suppose voter 1 ranks $A > B > C$ and voter 2 ranks $B > C > A$. By unanimity, society ranks $B > C$ (both agree). The social ranking of $A$ vs $B$ must go one way — say $A > B$ (the argument is symmetric).

Step 3. By IIA, the social ranking of $A$ vs $B$ depends only on each voter's $A$-vs-$B$ preference. Voter 1 prefers $A > B$; voter 2 prefers $B > A$; society chose $A > B$. This means: whenever voter 1 ranks $A$ above $B$ and voter 2 ranks $B$ above $A$, society follows voter 1.

Step 4. Now consider voter 1: $C > A > B$, voter 2: $A > B > C$. By unanimity, $A > B$. By our finding in Step 3 (applied to $C$ vs $A$ by relabelling), voter 1 is decisive on any pair where they disagree. Iterating this argument across all pairs shows voter 1 determines the social ranking on every pair where voters disagree.

Step 5. But then voter 1 is a dictator, contradicting (ND). The three conditions are inconsistent.

∎

The full proof for $n$ voters uses the same logic: IIA forces the existence of a "decisive" voter on one pair, and unanimity propagates this decisiveness to all pairs, creating a dictator. The key is that with $m \geq 3$ candidates, IIA links the treatment of different pairs through "irrelevant" third candidates. With only 2 candidates, majority rule works perfectly.

5. Phase 15 recap

Six pages have built game theory from its foundations. On page 1, backward induction solved sequential games by reasoning from the end. On page 2, the minimax theorem (von Neumann 1928) showed that zero-sum games have a well-defined value, using the fixed-point machinery of Phase 9. On page 3, Nash's theorem extended this to all finite games: every game has an equilibrium in mixed strategies, proved via Brouwer's fixed-point theorem.

On page 4, dominance and iterated elimination connected strategic reasoning to common knowledge of rationality. On page 5, the folk theorem showed that repetition transforms the Prisoner's Dilemma: cooperation emerges from pure self-interest when the future matters enough. And here, mechanism design reversed the question — from predicting behaviour to engineering incentives — while Arrow's theorem revealed the hard limits of collective decision-making.

The unifying theme across all of Phase 15: strategic interaction has mathematical structure. The tools of topology (fixed-point theorems), convexity (minimax), probability (mixed strategies), and analysis (discounting, convergence) from Phases 0–14 combine to give precise answers to questions about conflict, cooperation, and collective choice.

Historical note

William Vickrey introduced the second-price auction in 1961; he shared the 1996 Nobel Prize in Economics, though he died just three days after the announcement. Kenneth Arrow proved his impossibility theorem in his PhD thesis (published 1951), winning the 1972 Nobel Prize at age 51 — the youngest economics laureate at the time.

Leonid Hurwicz, Eric Maskin, and Roger Myerson won the 2007 Nobel Prize for mechanism design theory. Hurwicz, at 90, became the oldest Nobel laureate in any field. The subject now sits at the intersection of mathematics, economics, and computer science — internet auctions, matching markets (for medical residencies and school choice), and spectrum allocation all use mechanism design.

What breaks

Without rationality: mechanism design assumes players maximise their payoffs. Behavioural agents may overbid (out of excitement), underbid (out of caution), or act spitefully (to harm others at personal cost). The Vickrey auction's truthfulness guarantee evaporates if bidders do not reason about dominant strategies.

Without private values: the Vickrey auction assumes each bidder's valuation depends only on their own information. In common-value auctions (e.g., bidding on oil drilling rights where the oil's value is the same for everyone but uncertain), the winner tends to be the bidder who most overestimated the value. This winner's curse means truthful bidding in a second-price auction is no longer optimal.

Without impossibility awareness: many voting reform proposals implicitly violate Arrow's theorem. Ranked-choice voting (instant runoff) violates IIA. Borda count violates IIA. Majority rule violates transitivity with three or more candidates (Condorcet cycles). Arrow's theorem does not say all systems are equally bad — it says none can be perfect on these three criteria simultaneously.

Mechanism design reverses game theory — engineer the game to get the outcome you want — but Arrow's theorem warns that no collective decision procedure can be everything at once.
