Dominant Strategies and Iterated Elimination

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 4

The problem

In the Prisoner's Dilemma (page 3), each player has a strategy that is best regardless of what the opponent does — defect dominates cooperate. Can we simplify other games by removing strategies that are never rational to play?

This is iterated elimination of dominated strategies. It sometimes solves the game completely without finding equilibria directly. But the order of elimination can matter, and the method often leaves multiple strategies surviving.

1. Strictly dominated strategies

Consider a finite game with players $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Player $i$ has a strategy set $S_i$ and payoff function $u_i$. Write $\sigma_{-i}$ for a strategy profile of all players except $i$.

Definition — strict dominance

A strategy $\sigma_i \in S_i$ is strictly dominated if there exists $\sigma_i' \in S_i$ such that:

$$u_i(\sigma_i', \sigma_{-i}) > u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}) \quad \text{for all } \sigma_{-i} \in S_{-i}$$

We say $\sigma_i'$ strictly dominates $\sigma_i$.

The inequality must hold for every possible combination of opponents' strategies, not just some. A rational player — one who maximises their payoff — never plays a strictly dominated strategy. Why would you, when another strategy does strictly better no matter what?

In the Prisoner's Dilemma, Defect strictly dominates Cooperate for both players. If your opponent cooperates, you get 0 (cooperate) vs 3 (defect). If your opponent defects, you get $-3$ (cooperate) vs $-1$ (defect). Defect wins in both cases.

2. Iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies

If a rational player never plays a strictly dominated strategy, we can remove it from the game. But removing it shrinks the opponents' relevant strategy space, which may cause new strategies to become dominated. So we repeat.

The IESDS procedure: at round 0, start with the full game. At round $k$, remove every strictly dominated strategy in the game that remains after round $k - 1$. Stop when no further strategies can be eliminated. The surviving set is the IESDS solution.

A natural worry: does the order in which we eliminate matter? If at round $k$ both strategy $A$ and strategy $B$ are dominated, does it matter whether we remove $A$ first or $B$ first?

Theorem — order independence of IESDS

In iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies, the final surviving set of strategy profiles is the same regardless of the order of elimination.

Proof

Step 1. Let $S^k$ and $T^k$ denote the surviving strategy sets after round $k$ under two different elimination orders. We show by induction that $S^k$ and $T^k$ produce the same final result.

Step 2. Suppose strategy $s_i$ is eliminated at round $k$ in the first order, meaning there exists $s_i'$ with $u_i(s_i', \sigma_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, \sigma_{-i})$ for all $\sigma_{-i} \in S_{-i}^{k-1}$. We claim $s_i$ is also eventually eliminated in the second order.

Step 3. In the second order, at some round $m$ we have $T_{-i}^m \subseteq S_{-i}^{k-1}$. (This must happen because any strategy eliminated in order 1 by round $k-1$ is strictly dominated in the full game, hence also strictly dominated in the game restricted to $T^m$ for $m$ large enough.) Once $T_{-i}^m \subseteq S_{-i}^{k-1}$, the domination $u_i(s_i', \sigma_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, \sigma_{-i})$ holds a fortiori for all $\sigma_{-i} \in T_{-i}^m$, since the condition was satisfied on the larger set $S_{-i}^{k-1}$.

Step 4. Therefore $s_i$ is dominated in the second order's game at round $m$ and will be eliminated at or before round $m$. By symmetry, the same holds in the other direction. Both orders eliminate exactly the same strategies.

∎

3. Weakly dominated strategies

Definition — weak dominance

A strategy $\sigma_i$ is weakly dominated by $\sigma_i'$ if:

$$u_i(\sigma_i', \sigma_{-i}) \geq u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}) \quad \text{for all } \sigma_{-i}$$

with strict inequality for at least one $\sigma_{-i}$.

The crucial difference: weak dominance only requires "at least as good everywhere, strictly better somewhere." And here order independence fails. Consider this $3 \times 3$ game (row player payoffs only):

$$\begin{array}{c|ccc} & L & C & R \\ \hline T & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ M & 1 & 0 & 1 \\ B & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}$$

Strategy $T$ weakly dominates $B$ (payoffs $1, 1, 0$ vs $0, 1, 1$ — $T$ is at least as good everywhere, strictly better against $L$). If we eliminate $B$ first, then in the reduced game $M$ is weakly dominated by $T$ (payoffs $1, 0$ vs $1, 1$ against $L, C$). We are left with $T$ only.

But $M$ also weakly dominates $T$ (payoffs $1, 0, 1$ vs $1, 1, 0$). If we eliminate $T$ first, the analysis changes and different strategies survive. The final answer depends on which we removed first.

4. Dominant strategy equilibrium

Definition — dominant strategy equilibrium

A strategy profile $(s_1^*, \ldots, s_n^*)$ is a dominant strategy equilibrium if each $s_i^*$ is a (weakly or strictly) dominant strategy for player $i$ — i.e., $s_i^*$ (weakly or strictly) dominates every other strategy in $S_i$.

When every player has a dominant strategy, the game has a unique prediction that requires no strategic reasoning at all. You do not need to think about what the other player will do — your best move is the same regardless.

Example 1: the Prisoner's Dilemma. Defect is strictly dominant for both players, so (Defect, Defect) is the dominant strategy equilibrium.

Example 2: the Vickrey (second-price sealed-bid) auction. Each bidder submits a sealed bid; the highest bidder wins but pays the second-highest bid. We will prove on page 6 that bidding your true valuation is a weakly dominant strategy. This gives a dominant strategy equilibrium where everyone bids truthfully.

Dominant strategy equilibria are rare. Most games do not have them, which is why we need the more general concept of Nash equilibrium (page 3).

5. Rationalisability

The strategies that survive IESDS have a deep justification. Bernheim (1984) and Pearce (1984), working independently, showed that the surviving strategies are exactly the rationalisable strategies — those consistent with common knowledge of rationality.

What does this mean? A strategy $s_i$ is rationalisable if:

(a) $s_i$ is a best response to some belief about the opponents' strategies; and
(b) that belief assigns positive probability only to strategies that are themselves best responses to some belief; and
(c) those beliefs assign positive probability only to strategies that are best responses… and so on, ad infinitum.

This infinite chain of reasoning — "I am rational, I know you are rational, I know you know I am rational, …" — is common knowledge of rationality. The remarkable result is that this chain of reasoning eliminates exactly the same strategies as IESDS. No more, no less.

In two-player games, rationalisability and IESDS coincide exactly. In games with three or more players, the equivalence requires allowing players to hold correlated beliefs about opponents (Bernheim 1984).

Historical note

Luce and Raiffa (1957) systematised dominance concepts in Games and Decisions, the first comprehensive textbook of game theory after von Neumann and Morgenstern. The order-independence result for strict dominance was formalised by Gilboa, Kalai, and Zemel (1990), though it was known informally much earlier — it was "obvious" to practitioners but the proof required care.

The concept of rationalisability was introduced independently by Bernheim (1984) and Pearce (1984) in back-to-back papers in Econometrica. Their work connected the procedural idea of iterated elimination to the epistemic idea of common knowledge, founding what is now called epistemic game theory.

What breaks

Without strict dominance: as shown in Section 3, weak dominance elimination depends on the order. Different removal sequences can leave different strategies surviving. There is no canonical answer.

Without common knowledge of rationality: IESDS assumes all players are rational, know all others are rational, know that all know, and so on to infinity. If player 1 thinks player 2 might be irrational (or might think player 1 is irrational), then dominated strategies for player 2 might reasonably be expected, and player 1's best response changes. The entire IESDS edifice rests on an infinite tower of mutual knowledge.

Without finite games: in games with continuous strategy spaces (e.g., choosing a price in $[0, \infty)$), a dominated strategy may not have a single dominating alternative. Instead, for every dominated strategy there is a different strategy that dominates it, and the elimination process may not terminate or may require transfinite induction.

A rational player never plays a strictly dominated strategy; removing such strategies iteratively — in any order — reveals the predictions consistent with common knowledge of rationality.
