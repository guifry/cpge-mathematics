Repeated Games and the Folk Theorem

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 5

The problem

The Prisoner's Dilemma (page 3) has a depressing conclusion: rational players always defect. But in real life, people cooperate all the time — because they interact repeatedly. If I defect today, you can punish me tomorrow.

Repeated interaction opens the door to cooperation, but only if the game continues long enough and players care enough about the future. The folk theorem — so named because it was known informally before anyone published it — makes this precise.

1. Repeated games

Take a stage game $G$ with players $1, \ldots, n$, strategy sets $S_i$, and payoff functions $u_i$. In the repeated game, $G$ is played over multiple rounds. Players observe all past actions before choosing at each round. We write $a^t = (a_1^t, \ldots, a_n^t)$ for the action profile at round $t$.

A strategy in the repeated game is a complete contingency plan: for each round $t$ and each possible history $h^t = (a^0, a^1, \ldots, a^{t-1})$, it specifies which action to play. This is vastly more complex than the stage game — strategies can condition on everything that has happened before.

The total payoff uses discounting. With discount factor $\delta \in (0, 1)$, player $i$'s payoff in the infinitely repeated game is:

$$U_i = (1 - \delta) \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \delta^t u_i(a^t)$$

The factor $(1 - \delta)$ normalises the sum so that receiving payoff $c$ every round gives total payoff $c$. The discount factor captures patience: $\delta$ close to 1 means future payoffs matter almost as much as today's. The geometric series converges because $\delta < 1$ (Phase 2, page 3).

2. Finitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

Suppose the Prisoner's Dilemma is repeated exactly $T$ times, and both players know $T$.

Round $T$ (the last round): this is a one-shot game. Both players face the standard Prisoner's Dilemma with no future consequences. By the dominance argument (page 4), both defect.

Round $T - 1$: both players know that round $T$ will produce mutual defection no matter what. So round $T - 1$ is effectively the last round where anything can be influenced. But it is again a one-shot game — the future is fixed. Both defect.

Round $T - 2$: by the same reasoning, both defect. The argument unravels backwards to round 1. This is backward induction (page 1) applied to the repeated game.

Theorem — finite unravelling

If the stage game has a unique Nash equilibrium, then the finitely repeated game (with known endpoint $T$) has a unique subgame-perfect equilibrium: play the stage Nash equilibrium in every round.

Cooperation cannot emerge in finitely repeated games when the stage game has a unique equilibrium. The known endpoint poisons everything. This is why infinite repetition (or uncertainty about when the game ends) is essential.

3. The folk theorem

For the infinitely repeated game, we need two sets of payoffs.

The feasible payoff set $V$ is the convex hull of all stage-game payoff vectors $\{(u_1(a), \ldots, u_n(a)) : a \in S\}$. Any point in $V$ can be achieved on average by mixing between different action profiles over time.

The minmax payoff for player $i$ is:

$$\underline{v}_i = \min_{\sigma_{-i}} \max_{s_i} u_i(s_i, \sigma_{-i})$$

This is the lowest payoff that the other players can force on $i$ when $i$ best-responds. No rational player accepts less than their minmax value in the long run — they can always guarantee at least $\underline{v}_i$ by best-responding to the worst case.

A payoff vector $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)$ is individually rational if $v_i \geq \underline{v}_i$ for every player $i$.

Theorem — folk theorem (Nash equilibrium version)

Let $v$ be a feasible and individually rational payoff vector. For $\delta$ sufficiently close to $1$, there exists a Nash equilibrium of the infinitely repeated game with payoff vector $v$.

The folk theorem says that anything reasonable can happen in a repeated game, provided players are patient enough. Cooperation, alternation, asymmetric splits — any payoff that is feasible and individually rational is an equilibrium outcome for some $\delta$.

4. Proof via grim trigger

We prove the folk theorem for the Prisoner's Dilemma with the standard payoffs: mutual cooperation gives $(2, 2)$, mutual defection gives $(1, 1)$, and unilateral defection gives $(3, 0)$.

Definition — grim trigger strategy

Cooperate in round 0. In round $t > 0$: cooperate if both players cooperated in every previous round; otherwise defect forever.

Proof — grim trigger sustains cooperation

Step 1. If both players follow grim trigger, they cooperate forever. Each player's payoff is $(1 - \delta) \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \delta^t \cdot 2 = 2$.

Step 2. Suppose player 1 considers deviating at round $t$ (defecting while player 2 cooperates). The immediate gain is $3$ instead of $2$ in round $t$. But from round $t + 1$ onwards, player 2 switches to permanent defection. The best player 1 can do thereafter is also defect, giving payoff $1$ per round.

Step 3. The payoff from deviating at round $t$ (normalised to round $t$) is:

$$(1 - \delta)\left[3 + \delta \cdot 1 + \delta^2 \cdot 1 + \cdots\right] = (1 - \delta) \cdot 3 + \delta \cdot 1 = 3 - 2\delta$$

Step 4. Cooperation is better than deviating when $2 > 3 - 2\delta$, i.e., $\delta > 1/2$. For any discount factor above $1/2$, neither player wants to deviate from grim trigger. It is a Nash equilibrium.

∎

The general proof works the same way. For an arbitrary feasible, individually rational payoff vector $v$, construct a strategy that plays actions achieving $v$ on average, and punishes any deviator by switching to the minmax strategy against them forever. For $\delta$ close enough to 1, the long-run punishment outweighs any short-run gain from deviating.

5. Tit-for-tat

Grim trigger is effective but harsh: a single defection triggers eternal punishment. In practice, tit-for-tat is more robust.

Definition — tit-for-tat

Cooperate in round 0. In round $t > 0$, play whatever your opponent played in round $t - 1$.

Robert Axelrod (1984) ran computer tournaments where submitted strategies played iterated Prisoner's Dilemma against each other. Tit-for-tat, submitted by Anatol Rapoport, won both tournaments despite being the simplest strategy entered.

Why does it work? Tit-for-tat has four properties: (1) nice — it starts by cooperating; (2) retaliatory — it punishes defection immediately; (3) forgiving — it returns to cooperation as soon as the opponent does; (4) clear — its behaviour is easy for the opponent to understand and predict.

Tit-for-tat is not a Nash equilibrium of the infinitely repeated game in general (two tit-for-tat players who accidentally defect enter a cycle of alternating defections). But its success in tournaments and evolutionary simulations suggests that robustness against a variety of opponents matters more than equilibrium optimality.

Historical note

The folk theorem was "folklore" among game theorists in the 1950s — everyone knew it was true, but no one bothered to publish it. Friedman (1971) gave the first formal version using Nash equilibrium threats. Aumann and Shapley (1976) and Rubinstein (1979) strengthened it to subgame-perfect equilibrium, where the punishment must itself be credible.

Axelrod's tournaments (1980, 1984), published in The Evolution of Cooperation, brought repeated games to public attention far beyond economics. Biologists, political scientists, and computer scientists all adopted the framework. The insight — that cooperation can emerge from pure self-interest, given repetition and patience — is one of the most celebrated results in all of social science.

What breaks

Without infinite repetition (or uncertainty about when the game ends): backward induction kills cooperation, as shown in Section 2. A known finite horizon unravels all incentives to cooperate. If the game instead ends at each round with probability $1 - \delta$, this is strategically equivalent to infinite repetition with discount factor $\delta$, and cooperation can be sustained.

Without discounting ($\delta = 1$): the sum $\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} u_i(a^t)$ diverges. Payoffs are not well-defined — receiving 2 forever and receiving 1 forever both give $+\infty$. Various alternative payoff definitions exist (limit of means, overtaking criterion), but the standard folk theorem requires $\delta < 1$.

Without observability: if players cannot see past actions, punishment is impossible — you cannot punish what you cannot detect. The folk theorem fails entirely. Games with private monitoring (each player sees only a noisy signal of the others' actions) require much more sophisticated analysis, and cooperation is far harder to sustain.

Repetition transforms the Prisoner's Dilemma: any feasible, individually rational outcome becomes an equilibrium when players are patient enough, because the future provides both the carrot and the stick.
