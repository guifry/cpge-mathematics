Combinatorial Games

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 1

The problem

Zermelo (1913) asked a question about chess: can one player always guarantee a win, or a draw? He proved that in any finite two-player game with perfect information (both players see everything) and no chance elements, either the first player has a winning strategy, or the second player has one, or both can force a draw. The proof uses backward induction on the game tree — working backwards from final positions to determine optimal play.

This is not a statement about which strategy wins chess. It says that one of three outcomes is forced, but we do not know which. The theorem is an existence result, not a computation. It applies equally to chess, draughts, Nim, and any game fitting the hypotheses.

1. Game trees

A combinatorial game is specified by:

  • A set of positions, one of which is the initial position.
  • For each position, a set of legal moves available to the player whose turn it is.
  • A set of terminal positions (positions with no legal moves), each labelled with an outcome: Player 1 wins, Player 2 wins, or draw.
  • A rule determining whose turn it is at each position.

The game is finite if every possible sequence of play terminates after finitely many moves. It has perfect information if both players can see the entire position at all times (no hidden cards, no simultaneous moves).

We represent the game as a game tree: the root is the initial position, each node's children are the positions reachable by one legal move, and the leaves are terminal positions. Consider a tiny example: Nim with 3 stones. Two players alternate turns. On each turn, a player removes 1 or 2 stones from the pile. The player who takes the last stone wins.

The game tree has root "3 stones, Player 1 to move." Player 1 can remove 1 (leaving 2) or remove 2 (leaving 1). From "2 stones, Player 2 to move," Player 2 can remove 1 (leaving 1) or remove 2 (leaving 0 — Player 2 wins). From "1 stone, Player 2 to move," Player 2 must remove 1 (leaving 0 — Player 2 wins). The tree is small enough to draw on paper and analyse completely.

2. Backward induction

Given a finite game tree, we determine the outcome under optimal play by working backwards from the leaves.

Step 1. Label each terminal node with its outcome (W1, W2, or D for draw).

Step 2. Consider any non-terminal node all of whose children are already labelled. If it is Player 1's turn, Player 1 will choose the child most favourable to Player 1 (preferring W1, then D, then W2). Label this node with the outcome Player 1 will achieve. Similarly if it is Player 2's turn.

Step 3. Repeat until the root is labelled.

This process terminates because the tree is finite and acyclic. At each node, the label records the outcome when both players play optimally from that position onwards. The label at the root is the value of the game.

Back to Nim with 3 stones. From "1 stone," the current player takes it and wins. From "2 stones," the current player can take 2 and win immediately, or take 1 and let the opponent win — so the current player wins. From "3 stones, Player 1 to move": removing 1 leaves 2 for Player 2, who wins; removing 2 leaves 1 for Player 2, who also wins. Player 1 loses under optimal play. The value of 3-stone Nim is W2.

3. Zermelo's theorem

Theorem — Zermelo (1913)

In any finite two-player game of perfect information with outcomes win/lose/draw, exactly one of the following holds:

  • Player 1 has a strategy that guarantees a win regardless of Player 2's play.
  • Player 2 has a strategy that guarantees a win regardless of Player 1's play.
  • Both players have strategies that guarantee at least a draw.
Proof

Step 1. The game tree is a finite rooted tree (finitely many positions, finitely many moves from each, and every play terminates). Perform backward induction as described in Section 2.

Step 2. Every node receives a label in $\{W1, W2, D\}$. At a terminal node, the label is given. At a non-terminal node where it is Player $i$'s turn, the label is the best outcome (for Player $i$) among the children's labels.

Step 3. The root receives some label $\ell \in \{W1, W2, D\}$. We claim that $\ell$ determines the outcome under optimal play. Define a strategy for each player: at any node labelled $\ell$ where it is their turn, they move to a child also labelled $\ell$ (such a child exists by the backward induction rule).

Step 4. If $\ell = W1$: Player 1 follows the strategy above. At every node along the resulting path, the label remains $W1$ (Player 1 ensures this at their nodes; at Player 2's nodes, the label is $W1$ because backward induction chose Player 2's best outcome and it was still $W1$). The terminal node reached is therefore labelled $W1$ — Player 1 wins. The argument for $\ell = W2$ is symmetric.

Step 5. If $\ell = D$: both players can independently follow the strategy of always moving to a child labelled $D$. This is possible because at a $D$-labelled node where it is Player 1's turn, the best outcome for Player 1 among children is $D$ (there is no $W1$ child, else the node would be labelled $W1$). So a $D$ child exists. Similarly for Player 2. The game ends at a draw.

∎

For chess specifically, Zermelo's theorem tells us that either White can force a win, Black can force a win, or both can force a draw. We do not know which. The game tree of chess has roughly $10^{120}$ nodes — far beyond computational reach. For draughts (8$\times$8 checkers), the answer is known since 2007: both players can force a draw.

4. Nim and the XOR strategy

Nim is the fundamental combinatorial game. There are $k$ piles of stones with sizes $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_k$. Two players alternate turns. On each turn, a player chooses one pile and removes any positive number of stones from it. The player who takes the last stone wins (this is normal play).

The Nim-value (or Nim-sum) of a position is

$$s = n_1 \oplus n_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus n_k$$

where $\oplus$ denotes bitwise XOR (exclusive or). Write each pile size in binary and XOR bit by bit.

Theorem — Nim strategy (Bouton, 1901)

A Nim position is a losing position for the player to move (a P-position) if and only if $s = n_1 \oplus n_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus n_k = 0$. Otherwise it is a winning position (an N-position).

Proof

Step 1. The only terminal position is $(0, 0, \ldots, 0)$, which has Nim-value $0$. The player to move at a terminal position has lost (no moves available), so the previous player took the last stone and won. Terminal positions are P-positions, and $s = 0$ there.

Step 2. We show: from any position with $s \neq 0$, there exists a move to a position with $s = 0$. Let $d$ be the index of the highest bit set in $s$. There must be some pile $n_j$ whose $d$-th bit is $1$ (otherwise that bit of $s$ would be $0$). Set $n_j' = n_j \oplus s$. Then $n_j' < n_j$ (the $d$-th bit of $n_j$ was $1$ and becomes $0$, and all higher bits are unchanged since $s$ has no bits above $d$). So reducing pile $j$ from $n_j$ to $n_j'$ is a legal move. The new Nim-value is

$$n_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus n_j' \oplus \cdots \oplus n_k = s \oplus n_j \oplus n_j' = s \oplus n_j \oplus (n_j \oplus s) = 0$$

using associativity, commutativity, and $a \oplus a = 0$.

Step 3. We show: from any position with $s = 0$, every move leads to a position with $s \neq 0$. A move changes exactly one pile, say from $n_j$ to $n_j' < n_j$ (so $n_j' \neq n_j$). The new Nim-value is $s \oplus n_j \oplus n_j' = 0 \oplus n_j \oplus n_j' = n_j \oplus n_j' \neq 0$ since $n_j \neq n_j'$.

Step 4. Steps 1–3 establish that the P-positions (losing for the mover) are exactly the positions with $s = 0$. The winning strategy from an N-position: move to a P-position (Step 2 shows how). Your opponent is then in a P-position and every move they make leads to an N-position (Step 3). You keep returning them to P-positions until the terminal P-position $(0, \ldots, 0)$.

∎

5. The Sprague–Grundy theorem

Nim is special, but the same theory applies to all impartial games: games where both players have the same moves available from every position (Nim, but not chess, where White and Black move different pieces).

Theorem — Sprague–Grundy

Every position in a finite impartial game under normal play is equivalent to a single Nim heap of some size $g \geq 0$. The number $g$ is the Grundy value (or nimber) of the position, defined recursively:

$$g(P) = \operatorname{mex}\{g(Q) : Q \text{ is reachable from } P \text{ in one move}\}$$

where $\operatorname{mex}(S)$ (minimum excludant) is the smallest non-negative integer not in $S$. A position is a P-position if and only if $g(P) = 0$.

The Grundy value generalises the Nim-value. For a sum of independent games (played in parallel, each move affecting one component), the Grundy value of the combined position is the XOR of the component Grundy values — exactly the Nim-sum rule.

This is a remarkable reduction: no matter how complicated an impartial game looks, its strategic content is captured by a single non-negative integer. The entire game is equivalent to a pile of $g$ stones in Nim.

Historical note

Zermelo (1913) proved his theorem about chess, though the modern formulation is cleaner than his original. His paper "On an Application of Set Theory to the Theory of Chess" was one of the earliest applications of mathematical logic to games.

Bouton (1901) solved Nim with the XOR strategy. Sprague (1935) and Grundy (1939) independently developed the theory of impartial games, proving that every such game reduces to Nim. Their work went largely unnoticed until it was synthesised in Berlekamp, Conway, and Guy's Winning Ways (1982).

Conway (1976) extended this to a full algebra of games in On Numbers and Games, constructing the surreal numbers — a proper class containing both the real numbers and the ordinals as special cases. Games became a number system.

What breaks

Without finiteness: infinite games can have no determined outcome. Assuming the axiom of choice, there exist non-determined infinite games on $\{0, 1\}^{\omega}$ (Gale and Stewart, 1953). Martin (1975) proved that all Borel games are determined — determinacy requires topological regularity.

Without perfect information: poker requires a fundamentally different theory. Players hold private cards, so the game tree branches into information sets (positions a player cannot distinguish). Optimal play involves mixed strategies — deliberately randomising to prevent opponents from exploiting predictable behaviour. This is the subject of the next page.

Without two players: multiplayer games introduce coalition dynamics that backward induction cannot handle. If three players remain and two of them can profitably gang up on the third, the analysis branches into cooperative game theory (Shapley values, the core) — a different field entirely.

In any finite game of perfect information, backward induction determines the outcome — and for impartial games, XOR is all you need.
