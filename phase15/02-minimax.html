Zero-Sum Games and the Minimax Theorem

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 2

The problem

In a zero-sum game, one player's gain is the other's loss. Consider matching pennies: you and I each choose heads or tails simultaneously. If we match, I win a pound; if not, you win a pound. There is no "pure" optimal strategy — if I always play heads, you play tails and win every time.

Von Neumann (1928) proved that if you allow randomised (mixed) strategies, an optimal strategy always exists, and both players can compute it. The value of the game is the "saddle point" of the payoff matrix. This was the founding theorem of game theory.

1. Zero-sum games in normal form

A finite two-player zero-sum game is specified by an $m \times n$ payoff matrix $A = (a_{ij})$. The row player (Player 1) chooses a row $i \in \{1, \ldots, m\}$; the column player (Player 2) simultaneously chooses a column $j \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$. Player 1 receives payoff $a_{ij}$; Player 2 receives $-a_{ij}$. The sum is always zero.

A pure strategy is a definite choice: row $i$ or column $j$. A mixed strategy is a probability distribution over pure strategies. Player 1's mixed strategy is a vector $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_m) \in \Delta_m$ where $\Delta_m = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^m : x_i \geq 0,\, \sum x_i = 1\}$ is the probability simplex. Player 2's mixed strategy is $y \in \Delta_n$.

Under mixed strategies $x$ and $y$, Player 1's expected payoff is

$$E(x, y) = x^T A y = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_i \, a_{ij} \, y_j$$

Player 1 wants to maximise this; Player 2 wants to minimise it.

2. Saddle points

A pair $(x^*, y^*)$ is a saddle point of $E$ if

$$E(x, y^*) \leq E(x^*, y^*) \leq E(x^*, y) \quad \text{for all } x \in \Delta_m,\, y \in \Delta_n$$

The left inequality says Player 1 cannot do better by switching from $x^*$; the right says Player 2 cannot do better by switching from $y^*$. Neither player has an incentive to deviate — this is an equilibrium.

Some games have saddle points in pure strategies. Consider the game with matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row player's worst case per row: row 1 guarantees at least $\min(3, 1) = 1$; row 2 guarantees at least $\min(2, 4) = 2$. So Player 1 prefers row 2 (guaranteeing 2). Column player's worst case per column: column 1 concedes at most $\max(3, 2) = 3$; column 2 concedes at most $\max(1, 4) = 4$. Player 2 prefers column 1 (conceding at most 3). The entry $a_{21} = 2$ is both the row maximum of column minima and the column minimum of row maxima — a pure saddle point. The value of the game is 2.

3. The minimax theorem

Not every game has a pure saddle point. Matching pennies has payoff matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row minima: both $-1$, so $\max_i \min_j a_{ij} = -1$. Column maxima: both $1$, so $\min_j \max_i a_{ij} = 1$. We have $\max\min = -1 < 1 = \min\max$. No pure saddle point exists.

In general, the weak inequality always holds:

Lemma — Max-min $\leq$ min-max

For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$: $\displaystyle\max_{x \in \Delta_m} \min_{y \in \Delta_n} x^T A y \leq \min_{y \in \Delta_n} \max_{x \in \Delta_m} x^T A y$.

Proof

Step 1. For any fixed $x \in \Delta_m$ and $y \in \Delta_n$:

$$\min_{y' \in \Delta_n} x^T A y' \leq x^T A y \leq \max_{x' \in \Delta_m} (x')^T A y$$

The left inequality holds because $y$ is one particular choice; the minimum over all $y'$ can only be smaller. The right holds symmetrically.

Step 2. Since the left side does not depend on $y$ and the right side does not depend on $x$, take $\max$ over $x$ on the left and $\min$ over $y$ on the right:

$$\max_{x} \min_{y'} x^T A y' \leq \min_{y} \max_{x'} (x')^T A y$$
∎

The hard part is proving equality. This is von Neumann's minimax theorem.

Theorem — Minimax (von Neumann, 1928)

For any $m \times n$ real matrix $A$:

$$\max_{x \in \Delta_m} \min_{y \in \Delta_n} x^T A y = \min_{y \in \Delta_n} \max_{x \in \Delta_m} x^T A y$$

This common value $v$ is the value of the game. Any $x^*$ achieving the left max and $y^*$ achieving the right min form a saddle point.

4. Proof sketch via Brouwer's fixed-point theorem

The full proof uses topology (IOU: Brouwer's fixed-point theorem, Phase 9). We outline the argument.

Step 1. The set $K = \Delta_m \times \Delta_n$ is a compact convex subset of $\mathbb{R}^{m+n}$. This follows from Phase 9: $\Delta_m$ and $\Delta_n$ are closed bounded subsets of Euclidean space, hence compact (Heine–Borel), and clearly convex. Their product inherits both properties.

Step 2. Define a map $f : K \to K$ as follows. Given $(x, y)$, let $x'$ be a best response to $y$: a mixed strategy maximising $x^T A y$ over $x \in \Delta_m$. Let $y'$ be a best response to $x$: a mixed strategy minimising $x^T A y$ over $y \in \Delta_n$. Set $f(x, y) = (x', y')$.

Step 3. Best responses may not be unique, so $f$ is a set-valued map (correspondence). To apply a fixed-point theorem, we use Kakutani's generalisation of Brouwer (1941): a set-valued map on a compact convex set with non-empty convex values and a closed graph has a fixed point.

Step 4. The best-response sets are non-empty (continuous linear function on a compact set achieves its optimum) and convex (if $x_1$ and $x_2$ both maximise $x^T A y$, so does any convex combination). The closed-graph property follows from continuity of $x^T A y$.

Step 5. By Kakutani, there exists $(x^*, y^*)$ with $x^*$ a best response to $y^*$ and $y^*$ a best response to $x^*$. This is precisely a saddle point: $E(x, y^*) \leq E(x^*, y^*) \leq E(x^*, y)$ for all $x, y$. Setting $v = E(x^*, y^*)$ gives $\max\min = v = \min\max$.

5. Solving $2 \times 2$ games

For a $2 \times 2$ game without a pure saddle point, we can compute the value and optimal strategies explicitly. Take matching pennies:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Let Player 1 play row 1 with probability $p$ and row 2 with probability $1 - p$. Player 1's expected payoff against column $j$:

$$\text{vs column 1: } p \cdot 1 + (1-p) \cdot (-1) = 2p - 1$$
$$\text{vs column 2: } p \cdot (-1) + (1-p) \cdot 1 = 1 - 2p$$

Player 1 wants to maximise the minimum of these two expressions (since Player 2 will pick the column that hurts Player 1 most). Set them equal: $2p - 1 = 1 - 2p$, giving $p = 1/2$. The value is $2(1/2) - 1 = 0$.

By symmetry, Player 2's optimal strategy is also $q = 1/2$. The game is fair: its value is $0$. Each player randomises uniformly. Any deviation from $50$-$50$ can be exploited by the opponent.

More generally, for a $2 \times 2$ game $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ with no pure saddle point, the optimal row-player probability is

$$p^* = \frac{d - c}{(a - b) - (c - d)}$$

and the value is $v = \dfrac{ad - bc}{(a - b) - (c - d)}$, provided the denominator is nonzero (which it is when no pure saddle point exists).

Check with matching pennies: $a = 1, b = -1, c = -1, d = 1$. The denominator is $(1 - (-1)) - ((-1) - 1) = 2 - (-2) = 4$. The value is $(1 \cdot 1 - (-1)(-1))/4 = (1 - 1)/4 = 0$. Player 1's optimal probability is $(1 - (-1))/4 = 2/4 = 1/2$. Everything checks out.

For larger games, the minimax strategies can be computed by linear programming. Rewrite the max-min problem as: maximise $v$ subject to $x^T A e_j \geq v$ for all $j$ and $x \in \Delta_m$. This is a linear programme in $(x, v)$, solvable by the simplex method. Dantzig observed this connection in 1951, linking game theory to optimisation.

Historical note

Von Neumann proved the minimax theorem in 1928 in "Zur Theorie der Gesellschaftsspiele" (On the Theory of Parlour Games). His original proof used Brouwer's fixed-point theorem (1911, topology — Phase 9). Later proofs by Ville (1938) used convex separation, and Loomis (1946) gave an elementary argument.

Von Neumann and Morgenstern's Theory of Games and Economic Behavior (1944) launched game theory as a discipline. It also introduced expected utility theory, axiomatising rational decision-making under uncertainty.

Dantzig (1947) showed that solving a zero-sum game is equivalent to solving a linear programme. The simplex method, originally developed for logistics, computes game values and optimal strategies in practice.

What breaks

Without mixed strategies: matching pennies has no saddle point in pure strategies. $\max\min = -1 < 1 = \min\max$. The gap between max-min and min-max is the "cost of determinism" — a predictable player can always be exploited.

Without finiteness: if the strategy sets are infinite (e.g. each player picks a real number in $[0, 1]$), the minimax theorem requires additional regularity. Sion (1958) proved a minimax theorem for quasiconcave-quasiconvex functions on compact convex sets, but without compactness or convexity the theorem can fail.

Without the zero-sum condition: if what I gain is not what you lose, there is no single "value" to the game. The minimax framework collapses, and we need the more general notion of Nash equilibrium — the subject of the next page.

In a zero-sum game, randomisation closes the gap between caution and aggression — the minimax value exists and both players can achieve it.
