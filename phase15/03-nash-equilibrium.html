Nash Equilibrium

Phase 15 (Bonus) — Game Theory, Page 3

The problem

Von Neumann's minimax theorem (previous page) handles zero-sum games — strictly competitive situations where one player's gain is the other's loss. But most real interactions are not zero-sum. Two firms can compete or collude. Two countries can arm or disarm. A gain for one side need not mean an equal loss for the other.

Nash (1950), in a 27-page PhD thesis written at age 21, asked: in any finite game — any number of players, any payoff structure — is there a strategy profile from which no player wants to unilaterally deviate? He proved the answer is yes, using Kakutani's fixed-point theorem. The concept now bears his name: Nash equilibrium.

1. General $n$-player games

A finite $n$-player game in normal form consists of:

  • Players $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$.
  • For each player $i$, a finite set of pure strategies $S_i = \{s_i^1, \ldots, s_i^{k_i}\}$.
  • For each player $i$, a payoff function $u_i : S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots \times S_n \to \mathbb{R}$.

A strategy profile is a tuple $s = (s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ with one strategy per player. The payoff to player $i$ under profile $s$ is $u_i(s)$.

A mixed strategy for player $i$ is a probability distribution $\sigma_i \in \Delta(S_i)$ over their pure strategies. Under mixed profile $\sigma = (\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n)$, player $i$'s expected payoff is

$$u_i(\sigma) = \sum_{s \in S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n} \sigma_1(s_1) \cdots \sigma_n(s_n) \, u_i(s)$$

We write $\sigma_{-i}$ for the strategies of all players except $i$, and $(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$ for the full profile. This notation lets us isolate one player's choice.

2. Nash equilibrium

Definition — Nash equilibrium

A mixed strategy profile $\sigma^* = (\sigma_1^*, \ldots, \sigma_n^*)$ is a Nash equilibrium if no player can increase their expected payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy:

$$u_i(\sigma_i^*, \sigma_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}^*) \quad \text{for all } i \text{ and all } \sigma_i \in \Delta(S_i)$$

The key word is unilaterally. Each player optimises given the others' strategies are fixed. No player has a profitable deviation — but this does not mean the outcome is globally optimal. It is a local stability concept: stable against individual defections, not coordinated ones.

Note that a Nash equilibrium in pure strategies is a special case: each $\sigma_i^*$ is concentrated on a single strategy $s_i^*$, and $u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*)$ for all $s_i \in S_i$.

3. The Prisoner's Dilemma

The most famous game in the social sciences. Two suspects are interrogated separately. Each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (betray the other). The payoff matrix (years of reduced sentence, higher is better):

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & C & D \\ \hline C & 3,\, 3 & 0,\, 5 \\ D & 5,\, 0 & 1,\, 1 \end{array}$$

If both cooperate, each gets 3. If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector gets 5 and the cooperator gets 0. If both defect, each gets 1.

Whatever Player 2 does, Player 1 is better off defecting: if Player 2 cooperates, defecting gives 5 vs 3; if Player 2 defects, defecting gives 1 vs 0. Defection is a dominant strategy for both players. The unique Nash equilibrium is $(D, D)$ with payoffs $(1, 1)$.

But $(C, C)$ gives $(3, 3)$ — strictly better for both. The Nash equilibrium is Pareto-dominated: there exists an outcome that every player prefers. This is the dilemma. Rational self-interest leads to a collectively suboptimal outcome. Nash equilibrium captures strategic stability, not social optimality.

4. Nash's existence theorem

Theorem — Nash (1950)

Every finite game (finitely many players, each with finitely many pure strategies) has at least one Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies.

Proof sketch

Step 1. The set of mixed strategy profiles is $\Delta = \Delta(S_1) \times \cdots \times \Delta(S_n)$. Each $\Delta(S_i)$ is a simplex in $\mathbb{R}^{k_i}$ — compact and convex. Their product $\Delta$ is compact and convex in $\mathbb{R}^{k_1 + \cdots + k_n}$.

Step 2. Define the best-response correspondence for each player $i$:

$$BR_i(\sigma_{-i}) = \arg\max_{\sigma_i \in \Delta(S_i)} u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$$

This is the set of mixed strategies that maximise player $i$'s payoff given the others' strategies. The combined best-response correspondence is

$$BR(\sigma) = BR_1(\sigma_{-1}) \times \cdots \times BR_n(\sigma_{-n})$$

A Nash equilibrium is precisely a fixed point of $BR$: a profile $\sigma^*$ with $\sigma^* \in BR(\sigma^*)$, meaning every player is already best responding.

Step 3. We verify the conditions of Kakutani's fixed-point theorem (1941), which generalises Brouwer (Phase 9) to set-valued maps: if $F : K \rightrightarrows K$ is a correspondence on a non-empty compact convex set $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ with non-empty convex values and a closed graph, then $F$ has a fixed point.

Step 4. Non-empty values: $u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$ is continuous in $\sigma_i$ and $\Delta(S_i)$ is compact, so the maximum is attained (we proved this for continuous functions on compact sets in Phase 9, page 5).

Step 5. Convex values: $u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$ is linear in $\sigma_i$ (it is a weighted sum over $\sigma_i$'s probabilities). If $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_i'$ both maximise this linear function, then any convex combination $t\sigma_i + (1-t)\sigma_i'$ also maximises it (a linear function's maximum set is convex). So $BR_i(\sigma_{-i})$ is convex.

Step 6. Closed graph: suppose $\sigma^{(k)} \to \sigma$ and $\tau^{(k)} \in BR(\sigma^{(k)})$ with $\tau^{(k)} \to \tau$. We need $\tau \in BR(\sigma)$. For each $i$: $u_i(\tau_i^{(k)}, \sigma_{-i}^{(k)}) \geq u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}^{(k)})$ for all $\sigma_i$. By continuity of $u_i$, taking limits gives $u_i(\tau_i, \sigma_{-i}) \geq u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})$ for all $\sigma_i$. So $\tau_i \in BR_i(\sigma_{-i})$.

Step 7. By Kakutani's theorem, $BR$ has a fixed point $\sigma^*$. This $\sigma^*$ is a Nash equilibrium.

∎

The proof is non-constructive — it guarantees existence but does not tell you how to find the equilibrium. Computing Nash equilibria is in general a hard problem (the complexity class PPAD, Daskalakis, Goldberg, and Papadimitriou, 2006).

5. Examples and computation

Coordination game. Two drivers approach each other. Each chooses Left or Right. If both choose the same side, they pass safely (payoff 1 each); otherwise they crash (payoff 0).

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & L & R \\ \hline L & 1,\, 1 & 0,\, 0 \\ R & 0,\, 0 & 1,\, 1 \end{array}$$

There are two pure Nash equilibria: $(L, L)$ and $(R, R)$. At either, no player wants to switch unilaterally. There is also a mixed equilibrium: each player randomises $50$-$50$. But this mixed equilibrium gives expected payoff $1/2$ — worse than either pure equilibrium. Mixed equilibria can be inefficient.

Battle of the Sexes. Two partners want to spend the evening together. Player 1 prefers the opera (O); Player 2 prefers football (F). Being together matters more than the activity:

$$\begin{array}{c|cc} & O & F \\ \hline O & 3,\, 2 & 0,\, 0 \\ F & 0,\, 0 & 2,\, 3 \end{array}$$

Two pure equilibria: $(O, O)$ with payoffs $(3, 2)$ and $(F, F)$ with payoffs $(2, 3)$. For the mixed equilibrium, each player randomises to make the other indifferent. Player 1 plays $O$ with probability $p$; Player 2 is indifferent when

$$2p + 0(1-p) = 0 \cdot p + 3(1-p) \implies 2p = 3 - 3p \implies p = 3/5$$

Similarly, Player 2 plays $O$ with probability $q = 2/5$. The mixed-equilibrium expected payoff for Player 1 is $3(3/5)(2/5) + 2(2/5)(3/5) = 6/5$. Both pure equilibria give higher payoffs — the mixed equilibrium is again the worst of the three.

The pattern generalises: in $2 \times 2$ games, to find the mixed Nash equilibrium, each player chooses probabilities that make the other player indifferent between their pure strategies. You solve for your opponent's indifference, not your own.

Historical note

Nash's 1950 paper "Equilibrium Points in N-person Games" is barely a page long in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. His PhD thesis at Princeton, "Non-Cooperative Games" (27 pages), developed the full theory. The key insight — reducing equilibrium existence to a fixed-point theorem — connects game theory to topology (Phase 9) and convex analysis.

Nash shared the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics with Harsanyi (who extended the theory to incomplete information) and Selten (who introduced refinements — ways to select among multiple equilibria). Nash's personal story, including his struggle with schizophrenia, was told in Sylvia Nasar's biography A Beautiful Mind (1998).

Cournot (1838) had essentially described Nash equilibrium for duopoly competition over a century earlier, but without the general existence proof. Nash's contribution was the theorem: every finite game has one.

What breaks

Without finiteness: if strategy spaces are infinite (say $S_i = [0, 1]$), existence requires additional structure. Glicksberg (1952) proved that Nash equilibria exist in mixed strategies when each $S_i$ is a compact metric space and payoffs are continuous. Without compactness, equilibria may fail to exist: two players each pick a positive integer, the higher number wins — no best response exists.

Without mixed strategies: the coordination game with a twist illustrates this. Consider a $3 \times 3$ game based on Rock-Paper-Scissors:

$$\begin{array}{c|ccc} & R & P & S \\ \hline R & 0,\, 0 & -1,\, 1 & 1,\, -1 \\ P & 1,\, -1 & 0,\, 0 & -1,\, 1 \\ S & -1,\, 1 & 1,\, -1 & 0,\, 0 \end{array}$$

No pure Nash equilibrium exists (every pure profile has a profitable deviation). The unique Nash equilibrium is the uniform mixture $(1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$ for each player. Without mixed strategies, this game has no equilibrium at all.

Without rationality: Nash equilibrium assumes each player is rational (maximises expected payoff) and knows the game structure and the other players' rationality. Behavioural game theory (Camerer, 2003) studies what happens when players are boundedly rational, use heuristics, or have biased beliefs. Experiments consistently show deviations from Nash predictions in games like the Traveller's Dilemma and the Centipede Game.

A Nash equilibrium is a profile where every player is already best responding — and one always exists in mixed strategies.
