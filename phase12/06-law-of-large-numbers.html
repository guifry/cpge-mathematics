The Law of Large Numbers

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 6

The problem

Flip a fair coin 10 times — you might get 7 heads (70%). Flip it 1000 times — getting 700 heads (70%) is astronomically unlikely. As the number of flips grows, the fraction of heads approaches $1/2$. Everyone believes this. But proving it took Jacob Bernoulli twenty years.

This is the most fundamental fact in probability: averages of repeated experiments stabilise. It is why polls work, why casinos profit, why insurance is possible. Bernoulli proved the first version in 1713. This page gives his proof — and the stronger version due to Kolmogorov.

1. Setup

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Define the sample mean:

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$$
Lemma

$E[\bar{X}_n] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$.

Proof

Step 1. By linearity of expectation (page 3): $E[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \cdot n\mu = \mu$.

Step 2. Since the $X_i$ are independent, $\text{Var}(\sum X_i) = \sum \text{Var}(X_i) = n\sigma^2$ (page 4, Section 5). By the scaling rule (page 4, Section 2): $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2} \cdot n\sigma^2 = \sigma^2/n$.

∎

The mean of the average is the true mean — no surprise. The crucial fact is that the variance of the average shrinks like $1/n$. This is why averaging works: the distribution of $\bar{X}_n$ concentrates around $\mu$ as $n$ grows.

2. The weak law of large numbers

Theorem (Weak law of large numbers)

For any $\varepsilon > 0$: $P\!\big(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\big) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

This is convergence in probability: $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$.

Proof (Chebyshev)

Apply Chebyshev's inequality (page 5) to $\bar{X}_n$:

$$P\!\big(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\big) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \to 0$$
∎

That is the entire proof. Three lines. Twenty years of Bernoulli's life, reduced to a direct application of Chebyshev's inequality. The variance of the average is $\sigma^2/n$; divide by $\varepsilon^2$; let $n \to \infty$.

3. What the weak law means

As you average more data, the result concentrates around the true mean. This justifies using sample averages to estimate population parameters. It is why Monte Carlo methods converge, why clinical trials yield useful estimates, and why gambling houses always win in the long run.

Note what the weak law does not say. It says that for each fixed $\varepsilon$, the probability of a large deviation vanishes. It does not say the entire sequence $\bar{X}_1, \bar{X}_2, \ldots$ converges in any pathwise sense. That is the strong law.

4. The strong law of large numbers

Theorem (Strong law of large numbers)

$P\!\Big(\bar{X}_n \to \mu \text{ as } n \to \infty\Big) = 1$.

This is almost sure convergence: the sample mean converges to $\mu$ with probability 1. Not just “probably close” for large $n$, but the entire infinite sequence converges.

Before proving this, we need a technical tool.

5. The Borel–Cantelli lemma

Let $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ be events. We write $\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\}$ (infinitely often) for the event that infinitely many of the $A_n$ occur. Formally, $\{A_n \text{ i.o.}\} = \limsup_{n} A_n = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$.

First Borel–Cantelli lemma

If $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, then $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = 0$.

Proof

Step 1. For each $n$, $P\!\big(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k\big) \leq \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k)$ by subadditivity.

Step 2. Since $\sum P(A_k)$ converges, the tail $\sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(A_k) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

Step 3. The events $B_n = \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k$ are nested downward: $B_1 \supseteq B_2 \supseteq \cdots$. By continuity of probability (Phase 12, page 1), $P(\bigcap B_n) = \lim P(B_n) = 0$.

∎
Second Borel–Cantelli lemma

If the events $A_n$ are independent and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) = \infty$, then $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = 1$.

Proof

Step 1. We show $P\!\big(\bigcap_{k=n}^{m} A_k^c\big) \to 0$ as $m \to \infty$. By independence: $P\!\big(\bigcap_{k=n}^{m} A_k^c\big) = \prod_{k=n}^{m}(1 - P(A_k))$.

Step 2. Using $1 - x \leq e^{-x}$ for $x \geq 0$: $\prod_{k=n}^{m}(1 - P(A_k)) \leq \exp\!\big(-\sum_{k=n}^{m} P(A_k)\big) \to 0$ since $\sum P(A_k) = \infty$.

Step 3. So $P\!\big(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k\big) = 1$ for every $n$. Hence $P(\bigcap_n \bigcup_{k \geq n} A_k) = 1$.

∎

6. Proof of the strong law

We prove the strong law under the additional assumption that $E[X_i^4] < \infty$. The full proof without this assumption uses truncation arguments; we omit it.

Proof (fourth moment method)

Step 1. Let $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu)$. We bound $E[S_n^4]$. Expanding $(X_1 - \mu + \cdots + X_n - \mu)^4$ produces terms $E[(X_i - \mu)(X_j - \mu)(X_k - \mu)(X_\ell - \mu)]$.

Step 2. By independence, any term where some index appears exactly once vanishes (since $E[X_i - \mu] = 0$). The surviving terms are:

  • $E[(X_i - \mu)^4]$: there are $n$ such terms, each bounded by some constant $M = E[(X_1 - \mu)^4]$.
  • $E[(X_i - \mu)^2(X_j - \mu)^2] = \sigma^4$ for $i \neq j$: there are $\binom{n}{2} \cdot \binom{4}{2} = 3n(n-1)$ such terms.

Step 3. So $E[S_n^4] \leq nM + 3n(n-1)\sigma^4 = O(n^2)$. Therefore $E[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4] = E[S_n^4]/n^4 = O(1/n^2)$.

Step 4. Apply Markov's inequality (page 5) to $(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4$:

$$P\!\big(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\big) = P\!\big((\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4 \geq \varepsilon^4\big) \leq \frac{E[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^4]}{\varepsilon^4} \leq \frac{C}{n^2\varepsilon^4}$$

for some constant $C$.

Step 5. Since $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1/n^2 < \infty$ (Phase 5, page 1), we have $\sum P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) < \infty$. By the first Borel–Cantelli lemma, $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon \text{ i.o.}) = 0$.

Step 6. This holds for every $\varepsilon > 0$. Take $\varepsilon = 1/m$ for $m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ and use countable subadditivity: $P(\bar{X}_n \not\to \mu) \leq \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq 1/m \text{ i.o.}) = 0$. So $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$ almost surely.

∎

7. Application: Monte Carlo estimation

To estimate $\pi$: generate random points $(U, V)$ uniformly in $[0, 1]^2$. Define $X_i = 1$ if $U_i^2 + V_i^2 \leq 1$ and $X_i = 0$ otherwise. Then $E[X_i] = \pi/4$ (the area of the quarter-circle). By the law of large numbers, $\bar{X}_n \to \pi/4$ almost surely. After $n$ samples, the typical error is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ — from the central limit theorem (next page).

This idea generalises vastly. Any quantity expressible as an expectation can be estimated by averaging random samples. The law of large numbers guarantees convergence; the rate $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ comes from the CLT. This is the foundation of all Monte Carlo methods.

Historical note

Jacob Bernoulli proved the weak law in his Ars Conjectandi (1713, published posthumously). He called it the “golden theorem” and worked on it for twenty years. The strong law was proved by Émile Borel (1909) for the special case of coin flips, and by Kolmogorov (1930) in full generality. The Borel–Cantelli lemma (Borel 1909, Cantelli 1917) is the technical workhorse behind almost-sure convergence results throughout probability theory.

What breaks

(a) Without finite variance. Chebyshev's proof fails, but the weak law can still hold under weaker conditions. Without any moment condition, the LLN fails entirely: the Cauchy distribution has no mean, and the sample mean of i.i.d. Cauchy variables has the same Cauchy distribution for every $n$ — no convergence at all.

(b) Without independence. Let $X_i = X_1$ for all $i$ (perfect dependence). Then $\bar{X}_n = X_1$ for every $n$ — the average never moves. It stays random, with no convergence to any constant. Independence (or at least weak dependence) is essential.

(c) Weak vs strong. The weak law says $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) \to 0$ for each fixed $\varepsilon$. The strong law says the entire sequence converges, with probability 1. The strong law is strictly stronger: there exist sequences satisfying the weak but not the strong law. The gap is the difference between “unlikely to be far away right now” and “the path eventually stays close forever.”

The law of large numbers: the sample mean converges to the true mean — in probability (weak) and almost surely (strong) — because the variance of the average vanishes.
