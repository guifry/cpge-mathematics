Conditional Expectation as Projection

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 8

The problem

You want to predict someone's income $Y$ given their years of education $X$. Your prediction will be some function $g(X)$, and you want to choose $g$ to minimise the average squared error $E[(Y - g(X))^2]$. This is a minimisation problem — but over what space? The space of all functions of $X$ is enormous.

The answer turns out to be the conditional expectation $E[Y|X]$. And the reason is stunning: random variables with finite second moments form a vector space, with inner product $\langle X, Y \rangle = E[XY]$, and $E[Y|X]$ is the orthogonal projection of $Y$ onto the subspace of functions of $X$. This is exactly the projection theorem from Phase 6, page 8, applied in a different space. Probability and linear algebra converge.

1. Conditional expectation given an event

Recall from page 2: for an event $B$ with $P(B) > 0$, the conditional expectation of $X$ given $B$ is

$$E[X|B] = \sum_x x \cdot P(X = x \mid B)$$

This is just the expected value of $X$, but computed using the conditional distribution $P(\cdot|B)$ instead of the original $P(\cdot)$. We restrict attention to outcomes where $B$ occurred, and average over those.

2. Conditional expectation given a random variable

Now we condition on a random variable rather than a single event. For discrete $X$ and $Y$, define the conditional expectation of $Y$ given $X = x$ as:

$$E[Y \mid X = x] = \sum_y y \cdot P(Y = y \mid X = x)$$

This is a number, one for each value $x$ that $X$ can take. The conditional expectation $E[Y|X]$ is then the random variable that takes the value $E[Y|X = x]$ when $X = x$. It is a function of $X$: if you know $X$, you know $E[Y|X]$. Think of it as the "best guess" of $Y$ once you observe $X$.

For example, if $Y$ is income and $X$ is education level, then $E[Y|X = 16]$ is the average income among people with 16 years of education. As $X$ varies, $E[Y|X]$ traces out this conditional average — it is itself random because $X$ is random.

3. The tower property

Theorem — Law of iterated expectations

For any random variables $X, Y$ with $E[|Y|] < \infty$:

$$E\big[E[Y|X]\big] = E[Y]$$

Averaging the conditional averages gives the overall average.

Proof

Step 1. Write out the outer expectation. Since $E[Y|X]$ is a function of $X$:

$$E\big[E[Y|X]\big] = \sum_x E[Y|X = x] \cdot P(X = x)$$

Step 2. Expand $E[Y|X = x]$:

$$= \sum_x \left(\sum_y y \cdot P(Y = y \mid X = x)\right) P(X = x)$$

Step 3. Swap the sums and use $P(Y = y \mid X = x) \cdot P(X = x) = P(Y = y, X = x)$:

$$= \sum_y y \sum_x P(Y = y, X = x) = \sum_y y \cdot P(Y = y) = E[Y]$$
∎

The name "tower property" comes from stacking expectations: $E[E[\,\cdots\,]] = E[\,\cdots\,]$. You can always "collapse" a tower of conditional expectations down to a single unconditional one.

4. Random variables as vectors

Here is the key bridge to linear algebra. Consider the set $L^2$ of all random variables $X$ with $E[X^2] < \infty$. This is a vector space: if $E[X^2] < \infty$ and $E[Y^2] < \infty$, then $E[(aX + bY)^2] < \infty$ for any constants $a, b$ (expand and use the triangle inequality).

Define the inner product:

$$\langle X, Y \rangle = E[XY]$$

We verify the inner product axioms (Phase 6, page 7):

  • Linearity: $\langle aX + bY, Z \rangle = E[(aX + bY)Z] = aE[XZ] + bE[YZ] = a\langle X, Z\rangle + b\langle Y, Z\rangle$, by linearity of expectation (page 3).
  • Symmetry: $\langle X, Y \rangle = E[XY] = E[YX] = \langle Y, X \rangle$.
  • Positive definiteness: $\langle X, X \rangle = E[X^2] \geq 0$, with equality iff $X = 0$ almost surely.

The induced norm is $\|X\| = \sqrt{E[X^2]}$. The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality from Phase 6, page 7 immediately gives:

$$|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]}\,\sqrt{E[Y^2]}$$

This is the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality for random variables — a result often proved separately in probability courses, but here we see it is just one instance of the general inner product inequality.

5. Conditional expectation as orthogonal projection

Let $\mathcal{H}_X \subset L^2$ be the subspace of all functions of $X$: random variables of the form $h(X)$ with $E[h(X)^2] < \infty$. This is a closed subspace, so the projection theorem (Phase 6, page 8) guarantees a unique closest point in $\mathcal{H}_X$ to any $Y \in L^2$.

Theorem — Conditional expectation is projection

$E[Y|X]$ is the orthogonal projection of $Y$ onto $\mathcal{H}_X$. Equivalently, $E[Y|X]$ minimises $E[(Y - g(X))^2]$ over all measurable functions $g$.

Proof

Let $\hat{Y} = E[Y|X]$. By the projection theorem, it suffices to show that $Y - \hat{Y}$ is orthogonal to every element $h(X) \in \mathcal{H}_X$, i.e., that $\langle Y - \hat{Y},\, h(X) \rangle = 0$.

Step 1. Expand the inner product:

$$E[(Y - \hat{Y})\,h(X)] = E[Y\,h(X)] - E[\hat{Y}\,h(X)]$$

Step 2. Since $\hat{Y} = E[Y|X]$ is a function of $X$, the product $\hat{Y}\,h(X)$ is also a function of $X$. Apply the tower property:

$$E[\hat{Y}\,h(X)] = E\big[E[Y|X]\,h(X)\big] = E\big[E[Y\,h(X) \mid X]\big]$$

where we pulled $h(X)$ inside the conditional expectation (it is $X$-measurable, so it acts as a constant when conditioning on $X$).

Step 3. By the tower property (Section 3):

$$E\big[E[Y\,h(X) \mid X]\big] = E[Y\,h(X)]$$

Step 4. Combining Steps 1–3:

$$E[(Y - \hat{Y})\,h(X)] = E[Y\,h(X)] - E[Y\,h(X)] = 0$$

Since $Y - \hat{Y}$ is orthogonal to every $h(X) \in \mathcal{H}_X$, the projection theorem tells us $\hat{Y}$ is the unique closest point in $\mathcal{H}_X$ to $Y$.

∎

6. The best predictor

Section 5 says: the best predictor of $Y$ from $X$ (in the mean-squared-error sense) is $\hat{Y} = E[Y|X]$. But what if you restrict yourself to linear predictors of the form $\hat{Y} = a + bX$? Then you are projecting onto $\text{span}\{1, X\}$, a two-dimensional subspace.

The projection conditions are $E[(Y - a - bX) \cdot 1] = 0$ and $E[(Y - a - bX) \cdot X] = 0$. The first gives $E[Y] - a - bE[X] = 0$, so $a = E[Y] - bE[X]$. The second gives $E[XY] - aE[X] - bE[X^2] = 0$. Substituting $a$:

$$E[XY] - (E[Y] - bE[X])E[X] - bE[X^2] = 0$$
$$E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = b(E[X^2] - (E[X])^2)$$
$$b = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

So the best linear predictor is:

$$\hat{Y} = E[Y] + \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}\,(X - E[X])$$

This is the regression line — the foundation of linear regression in statistics, derived here as an orthogonal projection. If the relationship between $X$ and $Y$ is genuinely nonlinear, the best linear predictor will be worse than the full conditional expectation $E[Y|X]$.

7. Phase 12 recap

Eight pages have built probability from the ground up:

  • Page 1 — Probability spaces: the axioms (Kolmogorov's framework).
  • Page 2 — Bayes' theorem: updating beliefs with evidence.
  • Page 3 — Expectation and linearity: the most important property in probability.
  • Page 4 — Variance and covariance: measuring spread and dependence.
  • Page 5 — Markov and Chebyshev: tail bounds from moments.
  • Page 6 — Law of large numbers: sample means converge to true means.
  • Page 7 — Central limit theorem: sums of independent variables become Gaussian.
  • Page 8 — Conditional expectation: prediction as projection (this page).

The unifying theme: random quantities have structure. Expectations are linear, variances add under independence, tail probabilities are bounded by moments, sums converge to a Gaussian, and the best predictor is an orthogonal projection. Probability is not a catalogue of tricks — it is linear algebra and analysis in disguise.

Historical note

The interpretation of conditional expectation as orthogonal projection was made precise by Kolmogorov (1933) in his Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung, which gave probability its axiomatic foundation. Doob developed this connection systematically in the 1940s, using $L^2$ projections to build martingale theory. The idea that "best prediction = projection" unifies three great traditions: linear regression (Gauss, 1809), the Wiener filter in signal processing (1949), and modern financial mathematics (pricing by conditional expectation). It is the same projection theorem from Phase 6 — just in a different inner product space.

What breaks

Without square-integrability. If $E[Y^2] = \infty$, the inner product $E[XY]$ may not converge, and the projection interpretation collapses. Conditional expectation still exists in full generality (defined via measure theory), but it can no longer be characterised as the minimiser of mean-squared error.

Confusing a random variable with a number. $E[Y|X]$ is not a single number — it is a random variable (a function of $X$). Two boundary cases clarify this: if $X$ is constant (carries no information), then $E[Y|X] = E[Y]$ (a constant). If $X = Y$ (carries perfect information), then $E[Y|X] = Y$ itself.

Restricting to linear predictors. The best linear predictor $a + bX$ is generally worse than the full conditional expectation: $E[(Y - a - bX)^2] \geq E[(Y - E[Y|X])^2]$, with equality only when the conditional expectation is itself linear in $X$ — for instance, when $(X, Y)$ are jointly Gaussian.

Conditional expectation is orthogonal projection in the space of random variables — the best predictor of $Y$ given $X$ minimises mean-squared error, exactly as in linear algebra.
