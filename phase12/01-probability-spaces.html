Probability Spaces

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 1

The problem

In 1654, Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat exchanged a series of letters about gambling. The question that sparked it all: two players are in the middle of a game of chance when it is interrupted. How should the stakes be divided fairly, given the current score?

To answer this, they needed to assign numbers to uncertain events — numbers that behave consistently when you combine events, split them apart, or negate them. Their correspondence founded probability theory. But what, mathematically, is a probability? What rules must these numbers obey to avoid contradictions?

1. Sample spaces and events

Every probabilistic situation starts with an experiment — rolling a die, drawing a card, measuring a voltage. The set of all possible outcomes is the sample space $\Omega$. For a single die, $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$. For a coin flipped twice, $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$.

An event is a subset of $\Omega$ — a collection of outcomes we might ask about. "The die shows an even number" is the event $\{2,4,6\} \subseteq \Omega$. When $\Omega$ is finite, every subset is a valid event: the set of all events is the power set $\mathcal{P}(\Omega)$.

For infinite $\Omega$ (say, $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$), not all subsets can be assigned probabilities consistently. We restrict to a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ — a collection of subsets closed under complements and countable unions. For CPGE work, we deal with countable or well-behaved sample spaces where this technicality does not bite.

2. The Kolmogorov axioms

Definition — Probability measure

A probability measure on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a function $P : \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ satisfying:

  1. Normalisation: $P(\Omega) = 1$.
  2. Non-negativity: $P(A) \geq 0$ for every event $A \in \mathcal{F}$.
  3. Countable additivity ($\sigma$-additivity): if $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ are pairwise disjoint events, then $P\!\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n).$

The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a probability space.

Three axioms. Everything else follows. Let us derive the immediate consequences.

Consequence 1 — Empty set

$P(\varnothing) = 0$.

Proof

Step 1. Write $\Omega = \Omega \cup \varnothing \cup \varnothing \cup \cdots$ (a countable union of pairwise disjoint sets).

Step 2. By countable additivity, $P(\Omega) = P(\Omega) + P(\varnothing) + P(\varnothing) + \cdots = P(\Omega) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(\varnothing)$.

Step 3. Subtract $P(\Omega)$ from both sides: $0 = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(\varnothing)$. Since $P(\varnothing) \geq 0$, the only way an infinite sum of non-negative identical terms equals zero is if $P(\varnothing) = 0$.

∎
Consequence 2 — Complement rule

$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$.

Proof

Step 1. $\Omega = A \cup A^c$ and $A \cap A^c = \varnothing$.

Step 2. By finite additivity (a special case of countable additivity), $P(\Omega) = P(A) + P(A^c)$.

Step 3. Since $P(\Omega) = 1$, we get $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$.

∎
Consequence 3 — Inclusion-exclusion for two sets

$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.

Proof

Step 1. Decompose: $A \cup B = (A \setminus B) \cup (A \cap B) \cup (B \setminus A)$. These three sets are pairwise disjoint.

Step 2. By additivity, $P(A \cup B) = P(A \setminus B) + P(A \cap B) + P(B \setminus A)$.

Step 3. Now $A = (A \setminus B) \cup (A \cap B)$, disjoint, so $P(A) = P(A \setminus B) + P(A \cap B)$, giving $P(A \setminus B) = P(A) - P(A \cap B)$. Similarly $P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.

Step 4. Substituting: $P(A \cup B) = [P(A) - P(A \cap B)] + P(A \cap B) + [P(B) - P(A \cap B)] = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.

∎

3. Equally likely outcomes

When $\Omega$ is finite and all outcomes are equally probable, the axioms force a unique choice: $P(\{\omega\}) = 1/|\Omega|$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$. Then for any event $A$:

$$P(A) = \frac{|A|}{|\Omega|}$$

Probability reduces to counting. A fair die: $P(\text{even}) = |\{2,4,6\}|/6 = 1/2$.

Two dice. The sample space is $\Omega = \{(i,j) : 1 \leq i,j \leq 6\}$, with $|\Omega| = 36$. What is $P(\text{sum} = 7)$? The favourable outcomes are $(1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)$ — six of them. So $P(\text{sum}=7) = 6/36 = 1/6$.

4. Counting tools

Equally-likely problems demand counting. The fundamental tools (reference Phase 1 for the arithmetic underpinnings):

Multiplication principle: if a first choice has $m$ options and a second has $n$ options (independent of the first), there are $m \cdot n$ combined options.

Permutations: the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects in a line is $n! = n \cdot (n-1) \cdots 2 \cdot 1$. The number of ways to choose and arrange $k$ of them is $n!/(n-k)!$.

Combinations: the number of ways to choose $k$ objects from $n$ without regard to order is the binomial coefficient:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$

Example: poker full house. A full house is three of one rank plus two of another. Choose the triple's rank: $\binom{13}{1}$. Choose 3 suits: $\binom{4}{3}$. Choose the pair's rank: $\binom{12}{1}$. Choose 2 suits: $\binom{4}{2}$. Total full houses: $13 \cdot 4 \cdot 12 \cdot 6 = 3744$. Total 5-card hands: $\binom{52}{5} = 2\,598\,960$. So $P(\text{full house}) = 3744/2598960 \approx 0.00144$.

5. The law of total probability

Theorem — Total probability

If $B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_n$ partition $\Omega$ (pairwise disjoint, $\bigcup B_i = \Omega$), then for any event $A$:

$$P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \cap B_i)$$
Proof

Step 1. Since the $B_i$ partition $\Omega$, we have $A = A \cap \Omega = A \cap (B_1 \cup \cdots \cup B_n) = (A \cap B_1) \cup \cdots \cup (A \cap B_n)$.

Step 2. These pieces are pairwise disjoint: if $i \neq j$, then $(A \cap B_i) \cap (A \cap B_j) = A \cap (B_i \cap B_j) = A \cap \varnothing = \varnothing$.

Step 3. By additivity, $P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \cap B_i)$.

∎

If we know conditional probabilities (defined on the next page), this becomes $P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A|B_i)\,P(B_i)$ — a formula we will use constantly.

6. Inclusion-exclusion

We proved the two-set formula above. For three sets:

$$P(A \cup B \cup C) = P(A) + P(B) + P(C) - P(A \cap B) - P(A \cap C) - P(B \cap C) + P(A \cap B \cap C)$$
Proof (three sets)

Step 1. Write $A \cup B \cup C = (A \cup B) \cup C$ and apply the two-set formula: $P((A \cup B) \cup C) = P(A \cup B) + P(C) - P((A \cup B) \cap C)$.

Step 2. Expand $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$.

Step 3. Distribute: $(A \cup B) \cap C = (A \cap C) \cup (B \cap C)$. Apply the two-set formula again: $P((A \cap C) \cup (B \cap C)) = P(A \cap C) + P(B \cap C) - P(A \cap B \cap C)$.

Step 4. Combine everything: $P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) + P(C) - [P(A \cap C) + P(B \cap C) - P(A \cap B \cap C)]$, which gives the stated formula.

∎

The general formula for $n$ sets alternates sums of intersections of increasing size:

$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (-1)^{k+1} \sum_{|S|=k} P\!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right)$$

Example: three events with $P(A_i) = 0.3$, all pairwise intersections having probability $0.1$, and $P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3) = 0.02$. Then $P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup A_3) = 3(0.3) - 3(0.1) + 0.02 = 0.62$.

Historical note

The Pascal–Fermat correspondence of 1654 is the founding document of probability theory. Pascal posed the "problem of points" — how to divide stakes in an interrupted game — and their solution used what we now call expected value and the law of total probability. For nearly three centuries, probability was developed through combinatorics and clever arguments, with no axiomatic foundation.

Andrei Kolmogorov changed this in 1933 with his monograph Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung (Foundations of the Theory of Probability). He showed that probability is simply a measure with total mass 1, unifying it with Lebesgue's measure theory. Three axioms — the ones above — replaced centuries of ad hoc reasoning. It took 280 years from Pascal to Kolmogorov, but the result is one of the cleanest axiom systems in all of mathematics.

What breaks

Without countable additivity (only finite additivity): you can define a "probability" on $\mathbb{N}$ that assigns $0$ to every singleton $\{n\}$ yet assigns $1$ to $\mathbb{N}$ itself — finitely additive but not countably additive. Countable additivity prevents this pathology by forcing $P(\mathbb{N}) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(\{n\}) = 0$, a contradiction. So with countable additivity, at least one singleton must have positive probability.

Without $\sigma$-algebras: on uncountable $\Omega$ (like $\mathbb{R}$), not all subsets can be assigned probabilities consistently. The Banach–Tarski paradox shows you can decompose a ball into pieces and reassemble them into two balls of the same size — impossible if every piece had a well-defined volume. Restricting to a $\sigma$-algebra is the price of consistency.

Without $P(\Omega) = 1$: you get a measure, not a probability. Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}$ satisfies non-negativity and countable additivity, but $\lambda(\mathbb{R}) = \infty$. The normalisation axiom is what makes probability about relative likelihood rather than absolute size.

Probability assigns a number between 0 and 1 to each event, with three axioms ensuring consistency — Kolmogorov's framework makes the gambling intuitions of Pascal and Fermat precise.
