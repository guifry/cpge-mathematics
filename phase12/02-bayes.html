Conditional Probability and Bayes' Theorem

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 2

The problem

A medical test for a rare disease is 99% accurate: it correctly identifies 99% of sick people and 99% of healthy people. You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

Intuition screams 99%. But the correct answer depends on how rare the disease is. If only 1 in 10,000 people have it, your probability of actually being sick is about 1% — not 99%. The rarity of the disease overwhelms the test's accuracy.

This counterintuitive result comes from Bayes' theorem (1763), which tells you how to update beliefs in light of evidence. Getting it wrong has real consequences: unnecessary treatments, false accusations, flawed AI systems.

1. Conditional probability

You know that event $B$ has occurred. How does this change the probability of $A$? The outcomes not in $B$ are now impossible, so the effective sample space shrinks from $\Omega$ to $B$. We need the fraction of $B$ that also lies in $A$.

Definition — Conditional probability

For events $A, B$ with $P(B) > 0$, the conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is:

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

This is not just notation — $P(\cdot|B)$ is itself a valid probability measure on $\Omega$. It satisfies all three Kolmogorov axioms (page 1):

  • Normalisation: $P(\Omega|B) = P(\Omega \cap B)/P(B) = P(B)/P(B) = 1$.
  • Non-negativity: inherited from $P$.
  • Countable additivity: for pairwise disjoint $A_n$, $P\bigl(\bigcup A_n \mid B\bigr) = P\bigl((\bigcup A_n) \cap B\bigr)/P(B) = \sum P(A_n \cap B)/P(B) = \sum P(A_n|B)$.

So conditioning on $B$ gives a new, legitimate probability space — the original one restricted to outcomes inside $B$.

Example. Roll a fair die. $P(\text{six} \mid \text{even}) = P(\{6\})/P(\{2,4,6\}) = (1/6)/(3/6) = 1/3$. Knowing the result is even restricts the sample space to three outcomes, one of which is six.

2. The multiplication rule

Rearranging the definition immediately gives:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B) = P(B|A) \cdot P(A)$$

The second equality follows by symmetry of intersection: $P(A \cap B) = P(B \cap A)$.

This extends to three events. We want $P(A \cap B \cap C)$. Think of it as $P\bigl((A \cap B) \cap C\bigr)$:

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(C \mid A \cap B) \cdot P(A \cap B) = P(C \mid A \cap B) \cdot P(B|A) \cdot P(A)$$

Each factor conditions on everything that came before — a chain of conditional probabilities, each shrinking the sample space further.

Example. An urn contains 3 red and 2 blue balls. Draw two without replacement. What is $P(\text{both red})$? Let $R_1$ = "first is red" and $R_2$ = "second is red." Then $P(R_1) = 3/5$ and $P(R_2|R_1) = 2/4 = 1/2$ (after removing one red ball, 2 red remain among 4). By the multiplication rule: $P(R_1 \cap R_2) = (3/5)(1/2) = 3/10$.

3. Independence

Definition — Independence

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

Equivalently (when $P(B) > 0$): $P(A|B) = P(A)$. Knowing $B$ occurred tells you nothing about $A$.

For $n$ events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$, mutual independence requires the product rule for every sub-collection: for every subset $S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,n\}$ with $|S| \geq 2$,

$$P\!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i)$$

Warning: pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence. Consider two fair coin flips. Let $A$ = "first is heads", $B$ = "second is heads", $C$ = "both coins show the same face." Each event has probability $1/2$. Check pairwise: $P(A \cap B) = P(HH) = 1/4 = P(A)P(B)$. Similarly $P(A \cap C) = P(HH) = 1/4$ and $P(B \cap C) = P(HH) = 1/4$. So any two are independent. But $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(HH) = 1/4 \neq 1/8 = P(A)P(B)P(C)$. The triple is not mutually independent.

4. Bayes' theorem

Theorem — Bayes' theorem

For events $A, B$ with $P(A) > 0$ and $P(B) > 0$:

$$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A)}$$

More generally, if $B_1, \ldots, B_n$ partition $\Omega$ with $P(B_i) > 0$ for all $i$:

$$P(B_i|A) = \frac{P(A|B_i) \cdot P(B_i)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} P(A|B_j) \cdot P(B_j)}$$
Proof

Step 1. By definition, $P(B|A) = P(A \cap B)/P(A)$.

Step 2. By the multiplication rule, $P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$.

Step 3. Substituting: $P(B|A) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B) / P(A)$.

Step 4. For the general form, expand $P(A)$ using total probability (page 1): $P(A) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} P(A|B_j) \cdot P(B_j)$. Substitute into the denominator.

∎

The terminology: $P(B_i)$ is the prior (what you believed before seeing evidence), $P(A|B_i)$ is the likelihood (how probable the evidence is under each hypothesis), and $P(B_i|A)$ is the posterior (your updated belief after seeing evidence).

5. The medical test solved

Let $S$ = "sick" and $H$ = "healthy". The data: $P(S) = 1/10000 = 0.0001$, so $P(H) = 0.9999$. Test sensitivity: $P(+|S) = 0.99$. Test specificity: $P(-|H) = 0.99$, so $P(+|H) = 0.01$.

We want $P(S|+)$. Apply Bayes with the partition $\{S, H\}$:

$$P(S|+) = \frac{P(+|S) \cdot P(S)}{P(+|S) \cdot P(S) + P(+|H) \cdot P(H)}$$

Step 1. Numerator: $0.99 \times 0.0001 = 0.000099$.

Step 2. Denominator: $0.000099 + 0.01 \times 0.9999 = 0.000099 + 0.009999 = 0.010098$.

Step 3. Result: $P(S|+) = 0.000099 / 0.010098 \approx 0.0098$.

About 1%. Out of every 10,000 people tested, roughly 1 is truly sick (and tests positive) while roughly 100 are healthy but test positive anyway. The true positive is drowned out by the false positives. The prior matters enormously.

6. The Monty Hall problem

A game show has three doors. Behind one is a car; behind the other two, goats. You pick door 1. The host, who knows where the car is, opens door 3 to reveal a goat. Should you switch to door 2?

Let $C_i$ = "car is behind door $i$". Prior: $P(C_1) = P(C_2) = P(C_3) = 1/3$. Let $H_3$ = "host opens door 3." We want $P(C_2|H_3)$.

Step 1. Compute the likelihoods. If the car is behind door 1 (your pick), the host chooses freely between doors 2 and 3: $P(H_3|C_1) = 1/2$. If the car is behind door 2, the host must open door 3: $P(H_3|C_2) = 1$. If the car is behind door 3, the host cannot open it: $P(H_3|C_3) = 0$.

Step 2. Total probability: $P(H_3) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 0 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$.

Step 3. Bayes: $P(C_2|H_3) = \frac{P(H_3|C_2) \cdot P(C_2)}{P(H_3)} = \frac{1 \cdot 1/3}{1/2} = \frac{2}{3}$.

Switching wins with probability $2/3$. Staying wins with probability $1/3$. You should always switch.

The intuition: when you first picked, you had a $1/3$ chance of being right. The host's reveal does not change that — it concentrates the remaining $2/3$ probability onto the single unopened door. Bayes' theorem makes this precise.

Historical note

Thomas Bayes (1701–1761) was an English Presbyterian minister and mathematician. His theorem appeared posthumously in 1763, published by his friend Richard Price, in a paper titled "An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances." Bayes was trying to solve the "inverse probability" problem: given that something has happened, what can you infer about the cause?

Pierre-Simon Laplace independently discovered the same result and used it extensively in his Théorie analytique des probabilités (1812). The "Bayesian" approach to probability — treating probability as a measure of belief, updated by evidence — is now foundational in statistics, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

What breaks

Without the prior $P(B)$: Bayes' theorem is meaningless. The posterior depends critically on the prior. Ignoring the base rate — like assuming $P(\text{sick}) = 1/2$ instead of $1/10000$ — leads to wildly wrong conclusions. This is the base rate fallacy, one of the most common errors in probabilistic reasoning.

Conditioning on a zero-probability event: $P(A|B)$ is undefined when $P(B) = 0$. For continuous distributions, the probability of any single value is zero, so "conditioning on $X = x$" requires conditional density, not conditional probability. This is a genuine subtlety, not just a technicality.

Independence is fragile under conditioning: $A$ and $B$ can be independent yet become dependent once you condition on a third event $C$. This is called explaining away (or Berkson's paradox). If $A$ and $B$ are two independent causes of $C$, then observing $C$ creates a dependence between $A$ and $B$: if $C$ happened but $A$ didn't cause it, then $B$ becomes more likely.

Bayes' theorem inverts conditional probabilities — the posterior is the likelihood times the prior, normalised, and the base rate matters more than you think.
