Markov and Chebyshev Inequalities

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 5

The problem

You know a random variable $X$ has $E[X] = 100$ and $\text{Var}(X) = 25$. Can $X$ reach 200? What is the probability that $X$ deviates from its mean by more than 50? You know nothing about the shape of the distribution — it could be Gaussian, uniform, discrete, anything. All you have is the mean and variance.

Chebyshev (1867) showed that these two numbers alone give universal bounds on the probability of large deviations. The bounds are often crude, but they work for any distribution. This is their power.

1. Markov's inequality

Theorem (Markov's inequality)

If $X \geq 0$ and $a > 0$, then $P(X \geq a) \leq \dfrac{E[X]}{a}$.

Proof

Step 1. Split the expectation according to whether $X \geq a$:

$$E[X] = \sum_{x} x\,P(X = x) \geq \sum_{x \geq a} x\,P(X = x)$$

since every omitted term has $x \geq 0$.

Step 2. In the remaining sum, every $x$ satisfies $x \geq a$, so

$$\sum_{x \geq a} x\,P(X = x) \geq a \sum_{x \geq a} P(X = x) = a\,P(X \geq a)$$

Step 3. Combining: $E[X] \geq a\,P(X \geq a)$, hence $P(X \geq a) \leq E[X]/a$.

∎

The intuition is clean: if the average of a non-negative quantity is small, that quantity cannot be large too often. A room of people with an average age of 30 cannot have more than half the room over 60.

2. Examples of Markov

(a) Let $X$ count the number of heads in 100 fair coin flips. Then $E[X] = 50$. Markov gives $P(X \geq 75) \leq 50/75 = 2/3$. The true probability is approximately $10^{-7}$ — Markov is wildly loose here because it uses only the mean and ignores the variance entirely.

(b) If a factory produces items with an average defect rate of 5%, Markov tells us that no more than $5\%/20\% = 1/4$ of production runs can have a defect rate of 20% or higher. Crude, but obtained with zero knowledge of the distribution of defect rates.

(c) Markov is tight. Define $X$ by: $X = 0$ with probability $1 - 1/a$ and $X = a$ with probability $1/a$. Then $E[X] = a \cdot (1/a) = 1$ and $P(X \geq a) = 1/a = E[X]/a$ exactly. So no improvement on Markov is possible without additional assumptions — the bound is achieved by this two-point distribution.

3. Chebyshev's inequality

Theorem (Chebyshev's inequality)

If $X$ has finite mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, then for any $k > 0$:

$$P\!\big(|X - \mu| \geq k\big) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}$$
Proof

Step 1. The event $|X - \mu| \geq k$ is the same as $(X - \mu)^2 \geq k^2$.

Step 2. The variable $(X - \mu)^2$ is non-negative, so apply Markov (Section 1) with threshold $k^2$:

$$P\!\big((X - \mu)^2 \geq k^2\big) \leq \frac{E[(X - \mu)^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}$$
∎

In terms of standard deviations, set $k = t\sigma$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{t^2}$$

This is the version to remember. The probability of being more than $t$ standard deviations from the mean is at most $1/t^2$. Notice how the proof is a one-line application of Markov to a transformed variable — this “transform then apply Markov” strategy is one of the most powerful tricks in probability.

4. Chebyshev in practice

Setting $t = 2$: at least $3/4$ of any distribution lies within 2 standard deviations of the mean. Setting $t = 3$: at least $8/9$ lies within 3 standard deviations. These are guaranteed for every distribution with finite variance.

For the normal distribution, the true probabilities are far better: about 95.4% within $2\sigma$ and 99.7% within $3\sigma$. Chebyshev gives worst-case guarantees, not typical-case estimates.

Back to our opening problem: $E[X] = 100$, $\text{Var}(X) = 25$, so $\sigma = 5$. Chebyshev gives $P(|X - 100| \geq 50) \leq 25/2500 = 1/100$. Without knowing the distribution, we can guarantee the probability of deviating by 50 is at most 1%.

5. One-sided Chebyshev (Cantelli's inequality)

Chebyshev bounds deviation in both directions. What if we only care about one side?

Theorem (Cantelli's inequality)

For $k > 0$: $P(X - \mu \geq k) \leq \dfrac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + k^2}$.

Proof

Step 1. For any $c > 0$: $P(X - \mu \geq k) = P(X - \mu + c \geq k + c) \leq P\!\big((X - \mu + c)^2 \geq (k+c)^2\big)$.

Step 2. Apply Markov to the non-negative variable $(X - \mu + c)^2$:

$$P(X - \mu \geq k) \leq \frac{E[(X - \mu + c)^2]}{(k + c)^2} = \frac{\sigma^2 + c^2}{(k + c)^2}$$

since $E[(X - \mu + c)^2] = \text{Var}(X) + c^2 = \sigma^2 + c^2$.

Step 3. Minimise over $c$. Set $f(c) = (\sigma^2 + c^2)/(k+c)^2$. Differentiating and setting to zero gives $2c(k+c)^2 - 2(k+c)(\sigma^2 + c^2) = 0$, so $c(k+c) = \sigma^2 + c^2$, hence $ck = \sigma^2$, i.e. $c = \sigma^2/k$.

Step 4. Substituting $c = \sigma^2/k$: numerator $= \sigma^2 + \sigma^4/k^2 = \sigma^2(k^2 + \sigma^2)/k^2$; denominator $= (k + \sigma^2/k)^2 = (k^2 + \sigma^2)^2/k^2$. The ratio is $\sigma^2/(k^2 + \sigma^2)$.

∎

Compare this with Chebyshev. Chebyshev gives the two-sided bound $P(|X - \mu| \geq k) \leq \sigma^2/k^2$, so the one-sided probability is at most $\sigma^2/k^2$ (and halving gives $\sigma^2/(2k^2)$, but that halving argument is not rigorous for asymmetric distributions). Cantelli gives $\sigma^2/(\sigma^2 + k^2)$, which is always at most $\sigma^2/k^2$ and often substantially tighter.

Example. Let $\sigma^2 = 1$ and $k = 1$. Chebyshev gives $P(|X - \mu| \geq 1) \leq 1$, which is trivial. Cantelli gives $P(X - \mu \geq 1) \leq 1/2$. For $k = 2$: Chebyshev gives $1/4$ for the two-sided bound; Cantelli gives $1/5$ for the one-sided bound. The improvement matters when you care about deviations in one direction only — for instance, that a bridge load exceeds its capacity.

6. Why these matter

Markov and Chebyshev are the weakest possible tail bounds — they assume almost nothing about the distribution. Their importance is not practical (for any specific distribution you can do better) but theoretical. Chebyshev's inequality is the engine behind the weak law of large numbers (next page). It is also the starting point for sharper concentration inequalities (Hoeffding, Chernoff) that assume more structure.

The pattern is worth noting: apply a monotone transformation (squaring, exponentiation) to convert a tail event into a moment bound via Markov. Chebyshev squares. Chernoff exponentiates (using $P(X \geq a) = P(e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}) \leq E[e^{tX}]/e^{ta}$ for the optimal $t > 0$). Each transformation exploits more structure and yields tighter bounds.

For practical computation, you will almost always use distribution-specific bounds (Gaussian tail bounds, Hoeffding for bounded variables, etc.). But every one of those sharper results is proved by the same Markov trick with a cleverer transformation. Markov is the root of the entire tree of concentration inequalities.

Historical note

Markov stated his inequality in 1884 as a tool in his probability-theoretic work. Chebyshev proved his inequality in 1867, as part of his effort to prove the law of large numbers in full generality. Chebyshev was Markov's doctoral supervisor in St. Petersburg — the inequality chain goes from teacher to student. Irénée-Jules Bienaymé had proved an equivalent result in 1853, so the result is sometimes called the Bienaymé–Chebyshev inequality.

What breaks

(a) Without non-negativity, Markov fails. Let $X$ take values $-100$ and $1$ with equal probability. Then $E[X] = -49.5$, but $P(X \geq 1) = 1/2$. A negative mean tells you nothing about $P(X \geq a)$ for positive $a$.

(b) Chebyshev is sharp. The distribution that puts mass $1/(2t^2)$ at each of $\mu + t\sigma$ and $\mu - t\sigma$, and mass $1 - 1/t^2$ at $\mu$, achieves $P(|X - \mu| \geq t\sigma) = 1/t^2$ exactly. No universal bound can do better.

(c) Ignoring shape. These bounds treat all distributions with the same mean and variance identically. For Gaussian, exponential, or bounded distributions, far tighter bounds exist. The price of universality is looseness. When you know the distribution, use its specific tail bounds instead.

Chebyshev's inequality: the probability of being more than $k$ standard deviations from the mean is at most $1/k^2$ — regardless of the distribution.
