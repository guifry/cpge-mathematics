Counting

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 0

The problem

The Chevalier de Méré (1654) asked Pascal: in a game of dice, how many times must you throw to have better than even odds of rolling at least one six? He had a conjecture from experience, but couldn't prove it. The answer requires counting the number of ways things can happen — and the total number of ways things can happen — then dividing.

Probability is fundamentally about counting. Before you can compute $P(A) = |A|/|\Omega|$, you need to know how to count $|A|$ and $|\Omega|$.

1. The multiplication principle

Multiplication principle

If a process has two stages, the first with $m$ outcomes and the second with $n$ outcomes, the total number of combined outcomes is $m \times n$.

This extends to any number of stages: $k$ stages with $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_k$ outcomes give $n_1 \times n_2 \times \cdots \times n_k$ total outcomes.

Example: a licence plate has 2 letters followed by 3 digits. Number of plates: $26^2 \times 10^3 = 676{,}000$.

2. Permutations

Definition

A permutation of $n$ objects is an arrangement of all $n$ in a specific order. The number of permutations of $n$ distinct objects is:

$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$$

By convention, $0! = 1$.

Why

The first position has $n$ choices. The second has $n-1$ (one object is used). The third has $n-2$. Continue: the total is $n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1 = n!$. This is the multiplication principle applied $n$ times.

∎

$k$-permutations: the number of ways to arrange $k$ objects chosen from $n$ (order matters):

$$P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} = n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1)$$

Example: how many ways can 3 runners finish in 1st, 2nd, 3rd from a race of 10? $P(10, 3) = 10 \times 9 \times 8 = 720$.

3. Combinations

Definition

A combination is a selection of $k$ objects from $n$ where order doesn't matter. The number of such selections is the binomial coefficient:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$

Read: "$n$ choose $k$."

Why

Start with the $k$-permutations $P(n,k)$. Each group of $k$ objects appears $k!$ times (once for each ordering). So the number of unordered groups is $P(n,k)/k! = n!/(k!(n-k)!)$.

∎

Example: a poker hand is 5 cards from 52. Number of hands: $\binom{52}{5} = \frac{52!}{5!\,47!} = 2{,}598{,}960$.

4. Properties of $\binom{n}{k}$

Key properties
  • Symmetry: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$. Choosing $k$ to include is the same as choosing $n-k$ to exclude.
  • Pascal's rule: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$. Either the new element is in the subset or it isn't.
  • Boundary: $\binom{n}{0} = \binom{n}{n} = 1$.
  • Sum: $\sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k} = 2^n$. Total number of subsets of an $n$-element set.
Proof of the sum

Each of the $n$ elements is either in or out of the subset: $2$ choices per element, $2^n$ total by the multiplication principle. Alternatively, set $x=1$ in the binomial theorem (see Refresher, page 2): $(1+1)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}$.

∎

5. Stars and bars

Stars and bars

The number of ways to distribute $n$ identical objects into $k$ distinct bins is:

$$\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$$

Example: distribute 10 identical sweets among 3 children. Answer: $\binom{12}{2} = 66$.

The name: represent objects as stars ($\star\star\star\star$) and bin separators as bars ($|$). A distribution of 10 sweets into 3 bins is a sequence of 10 stars and 2 bars, e.g. $\star\star\star|\star\star\star\star\star|\star\star$ means (3, 5, 2). The number of such sequences is $\binom{12}{2}$: choose where to place the 2 bars among 12 positions.

6. The de Méré problem

Back to the opening question. Throw a die $n$ times. The probability of no sixes is $(5/6)^n$ (multiplication principle: each throw is independent). So the probability of at least one six is $1 - (5/6)^n$.

For this to exceed $1/2$: $(5/6)^n < 1/2$, so $n > \ln(1/2)/\ln(5/6) \approx 3.80$. You need $n \geq 4$ throws.

De Méré knew this empirically. Pascal and Fermat proved it — and in doing so, founded probability theory.

Historical note

The binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$ appears in Pingala's work (India, ~200 BC), in Al-Karaji (Persia, ~1000 AD), and in Yang Hui's triangle (China, 1261) — all before Pascal's 1654 Traité du triangle arithmétique. The name "Pascal's triangle" is European-centric. Stars and bars was formalised by Feller (1950s) but the technique is implicit in Euler's work on partitions (1748).

What breaks

Confusing permutations and combinations: "How many 3-letter words from ABCDE?" is $P(5,3) = 60$ (order matters: ABC $\neq$ BCA). "How many 3-element subsets of $\{A,B,C,D,E\}$?" is $\binom{5}{3} = 10$ (order doesn't matter: $\{A,B,C\} = \{B,C,A\}$). Always ask: does order matter?

Stars and bars with restrictions: the formula $\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$ allows empty bins. If each bin must have at least 1 object, give 1 to each bin first, then distribute the remaining $n-k$: $\binom{n-1}{k-1}$.

Counting is the foundation of probability — $P(A) = |A|/|\Omega|$ requires knowing how to count both.
