The Central Limit Theorem

Phase 12 — Probability, Page 7

The problem

The law of large numbers (page 6) tells us that the sample mean $\bar{X}_n$ converges to the true mean $\mu$. Good. But how does it converge? What is the shape of the fluctuations around $\mu$? Are they symmetric? Concentrated? Spread out?

De Moivre (1733) discovered something extraordinary while studying coin flips. He found that the distribution of the number of heads, properly centred and scaled, approaches a specific bell-shaped curve — regardless of whether the coin is fair. Laplace later showed this holds for any distribution with finite variance. The properly scaled sum $\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$ always approaches the same bell curve: the Gaussian distribution. Tall or short, rich or poor, the sum of many independent random effects always looks Gaussian. This is why the bell curve is everywhere in nature.

1. The standard normal distribution

We need to define the target: the bell curve itself. The standard normal density is

$$\phi(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-x^2/2}$$

and the standard normal CDF is $\Phi(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} \phi(t)\,dt$. For this to be a valid density, we need $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\phi(x)\,dx = 1$, which amounts to showing $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2}\,dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$. This follows from the Gaussian integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-t^2}\,dt = \sqrt{\pi}$ (Phase 7, page 6) by the substitution $t = x/\sqrt{2}$, giving $dt = dx/\sqrt{2}$ and thus $\int e^{-x^2/2}\,dx = \sqrt{2}\int e^{-t^2}\,dt = \sqrt{2\pi}$.

A random variable $Z$ with density $\phi$ is called standard normal, written $Z \sim N(0,1)$. Its mean is zero by symmetry: $\phi(-x) = \phi(x)$, so $E[Z] = \int x\phi(x)\,dx = 0$ since the integrand is odd. For the variance, integrate by parts with $u = x$ and $dv = xe^{-x^2/2}\,dx$ (so $v = -e^{-x^2/2}$):

$$E[Z^2] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x^2 e^{-x^2/2}\,dx = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left[\underbrace{-xe^{-x^2/2}\Big|_{-\infty}^{\infty}}_{= 0} + \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2}\,dx\right] = \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} = 1$$

So $\text{Var}(Z) = E[Z^2] - (E[Z])^2 = 1 - 0 = 1$. The standard normal has mean 0 and variance 1 — hence the name $N(0,1)$.

2. Moment generating functions

The moment generating function (MGF) of a random variable $X$ is

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$$

defined for all $t$ where this expectation exists. Why "moment generating"? Expand $e^{tX} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(tX)^k}{k!}$ and take expectations term by term: $M_X(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^k}{k!}E[X^k]$. Differentiating $k$ times and setting $t = 0$ gives $M_X^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]$. In particular:

  • $M_X(0) = 1$ (always),
  • $M_X'(0) = E[X]$,
  • $M_X''(0) = E[X^2]$.

The crucial property we need: if $M_{X_n}(t) \to M_X(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighbourhood of 0, then $X_n \to X$ in distribution. That is, convergence of MGFs implies convergence of CDFs. We state this without proof — it is a deep result from analysis (the continuity theorem for Laplace transforms). This is our main tool for proving the CLT.

3. MGF of the standard normal

Let $Z \sim N(0,1)$. We compute $M_Z(t)$ directly.

$$M_Z(t) = E[e^{tZ}] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{tz}\,e^{-z^2/2}\,dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{tz - z^2/2}\,dz$$

Step 1. Complete the square in the exponent: $tz - z^2/2 = -\frac{1}{2}(z^2 - 2tz) = -\frac{1}{2}(z - t)^2 + \frac{t^2}{2}$.

Step 2. Substitute back: $M_Z(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(z-t)^2/2 + t^2/2}\,dz = \frac{e^{t^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(z-t)^2/2}\,dz$.

Step 3. The remaining integral is $\int e^{-(z-t)^2/2}\,dz = \sqrt{2\pi}$ (it is the Gaussian integral, shifted by $t$, which does not affect the value). Therefore:

$$M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}$$

This elegant formula is the fingerprint of the standard normal. If any sequence of random variables has MGFs converging to $e^{t^2/2}$, that sequence converges in distribution to $N(0,1)$.

4. The Central Limit Theorem

Theorem — Central Limit Theorem

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 \in (0, \infty)$. Define

$$Z_n = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$

Then $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$, meaning $P(Z_n \leq x) \to \Phi(x)$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

5. Proof via moment generating functions

Proof

Without loss of generality, assume $\mu = 0$ and $\sigma = 1$. (Otherwise replace each $X_i$ by $(X_i - \mu)/\sigma$, which has mean 0 and variance 1, and note that $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{X_i - \mu}{\sigma}$.) So we have i.i.d. $X_i$ with $E[X_i] = 0$, $E[X_i^2] = 1$, and $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum X_i$.

Step 1. Since the $X_i$ are independent, the MGF of a sum is the product of the MGFs (page 4). Since they are identically distributed:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = M_{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum X_i}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} M_{X_i}\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = \left[M_{X_1}\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

Step 2. Taylor-expand $M_{X_1}(s)$ around $s = 0$. Since $M_{X_1}(0) = 1$, $M_{X_1}'(0) = E[X_1] = 0$, and $M_{X_1}''(0) = E[X_1^2] = 1$:

$$M_{X_1}(s) = 1 + s \cdot 0 + \frac{s^2}{2}\cdot 1 + O(s^3) = 1 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

Step 3. Substitute $s = t/\sqrt{n}$:

$$M_{X_1}\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})$$

Step 4. Take the $n$th power:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)^n \longrightarrow e^{t^2/2} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

The limit uses $(1 + a/n + O(n^{-3/2}))^n \to e^a$, which is the classical exponential limit from Phase 4, page 3.

Step 5. We showed in Section 3 that $e^{t^2/2} = M_Z(t)$ for $Z \sim N(0,1)$. By the convergence theorem for MGFs (Section 2), $Z_n \to N(0,1)$ in distribution.

∎

6. The de Moivre–Laplace theorem

The historically first version of the CLT is the special case for coin flips. Let $X_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, so $E[X_i] = p$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = p(1-p)$. Then $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$, and the CLT says:

$$\frac{S_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

Since $S_n$ is discrete (integer-valued) while the normal is continuous, we use a continuity correction for better accuracy:

$$P(a \leq S_n \leq b) \approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{b + 0.5 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right) - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a - 0.5 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$

Example. Flip a fair coin 100 times. What is $P(45 \leq S_{100} \leq 55)$? Here $n = 100$, $p = 1/2$, so $np = 50$ and $\sqrt{np(1-p)} = 5$. With the continuity correction:

$$P(45 \leq S_{100} \leq 55) \approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{55.5 - 50}{5}\right) - \Phi\!\left(\frac{44.5 - 50}{5}\right) = \Phi(1.1) - \Phi(-1.1)$$

By symmetry, $\Phi(-1.1) = 1 - \Phi(1.1)$. From standard tables, $\Phi(1.1) \approx 0.8643$, so $P(45 \leq S_{100} \leq 55) \approx 2(0.8643) - 1 \approx 0.729$.

7. Why the Gaussian is everywhere

The CLT explains a pattern you see across every science: measurements cluster around a bell curve. This is not coincidence — it is a theorem. Any quantity that arises as the sum of many small, independent effects will be approximately Gaussian:

  • Human heights — the sum of many genetic and environmental factors.
  • Measurement errors — the sum of many small instrumental fluctuations.
  • Stock returns — the aggregate of many individual trades.
  • Exam scores — the sum of scores on many independent questions.

The bell curve does not need to be postulated or assumed. Whenever many independent sources of randomness add up, the Gaussian emerges by mathematical necessity.

Historical note

De Moivre (1733) proved the first CLT for coin flips — the de Moivre–Laplace theorem — as an approximation to the binomial. Laplace (1812) extended the result to sums of general random variables in his Théorie analytique des probabilités. The name "Central Limit Theorem" was coined by Pólya (1920), reflecting the theorem's central importance in probability theory. Lindeberg (1922) and Lévy (1935) gave the most general versions, weakening the i.i.d. requirement to allow non-identically distributed summands (the Lindeberg condition). The MGF proof we have given follows the approach of Laplace, made rigorous by Lévy.

What breaks

Without finite variance. The CLT fails completely. If $X_i$ are i.i.d. Cauchy (which has no finite mean, let alone variance), then $\bar{X}_n$ is again Cauchy — not Gaussian. Heavy-tailed distributions with infinite variance have their own limit theorems, converging to stable distributions rather than the Gaussian.

Without independence. Take $X_1 = X_2 = \cdots = X_n$ (perfectly correlated). Then $Z_n = \frac{nX_1 - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} = \sqrt{n}\,\frac{X_1 - \mu}{\sigma}$, which diverges — no convergence to anything. CLTs for dependent variables exist (mixing conditions, martingale CLT) but require additional structure.

Finite $n$ is not exact. For any fixed $n$, $Z_n$ is not Gaussian — the CLT is an asymptotic statement. The Berry–Esseen theorem quantifies the error: $\sup_x |P(Z_n \leq x) - \Phi(x)| \leq \frac{C\,\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt{n}}$, where $\rho = E[|X_1|^3]$ and $C \leq 0.4748$. The convergence rate is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$.

The central limit theorem: the sum of many independent random variables, properly scaled, always converges to a Gaussian — regardless of the original distribution.
