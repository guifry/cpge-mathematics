Groups

Phase 10 — Abstract Algebra, Page 1

The problem

In 1771, Joseph-Louis Lagrange asked a question that would reshape mathematics: why do formulas exist for solving polynomial equations of degree 2, 3, and 4, but not (apparently) degree 5? He studied what happens when you permute the roots of a polynomial. Some rearrangements leave certain expressions built from the roots unchanged; others don't.

The key wasn't the individual permutations — it was the structure of the collection. Which permutations can you compose? Can you undo any permutation? Does the order of composition matter? Lagrange discovered that these collections of permutations obey specific algebraic laws. The abstract distillation of those laws is the concept of a group.

1. The definition

We extract the essential properties that Lagrange's permutation collections share with integer addition, matrix multiplication, and symmetries of geometric figures.

Definition — Group

A group is a set $G$ together with a binary operation $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ satisfying:

  1. Closure: for all $a, b \in G$, the product $a \cdot b \in G$.
  2. Associativity: for all $a, b, c \in G$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$.
  3. Identity: there exists $e \in G$ such that $e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$ for all $a \in G$.
  4. Inverses: for every $a \in G$, there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$.

If additionally $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ for all $a, b \in G$, the group is called abelian (after Niels Henrik Abel).

We usually write $ab$ instead of $a \cdot b$, and $(G, \cdot)$ or just $G$ for the group. For additive groups, we write $a + b$, the identity as $0$, and the inverse of $a$ as $-a$.

2. First examples

(a) $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$. The integers under addition. The identity is $0$, the inverse of $n$ is $-n$, and addition is associative and commutative. This is an abelian group.

(b) $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$. The integers modulo $n$ under addition (we constructed this in Phase 1, page 3). The identity is $\bar{0}$, the inverse of $\bar{k}$ is $\overline{n - k}$. This is an abelian group of order $n$.

(c) $(\mathbb{R}^*, \times)$. The nonzero reals under multiplication. The identity is $1$, the inverse of $a$ is $1/a$. Abelian.

(d) $S_n$. The symmetric group on $n$ elements: all bijections $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\} \to \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ under composition. The identity is the identity permutation, the inverse is the inverse function. We have $|S_n| = n!$ (there are $n$ choices for where to send $1$, then $n-1$ for $2$, and so on). For $n \geq 3$, $S_n$ is not abelian. For example in $S_3$, let $\sigma = (1\;2)$ and $\tau = (1\;3)$. Then $\sigma\tau$ sends $1 \mapsto 3 \mapsto 3$... let us just compute: $\sigma\tau(1) = \sigma(3) = 3$, $\sigma\tau(2) = \sigma(2) = 1$, $\sigma\tau(3) = \sigma(1) = 2$, so $\sigma\tau = (1\;3\;2)$. But $\tau\sigma(1) = \tau(2) = 2$, $\tau\sigma(2) = \tau(1) = 3$, $\tau\sigma(3) = \tau(3) = 1$, so $\tau\sigma = (1\;2\;3)$. Different results.

(e) $\text{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$. The invertible $n \times n$ real matrices under matrix multiplication (we used these in Phase 6). The identity is $I_n$, inverses exist by definition. Non-abelian for $n \geq 2$.

3. Basic properties

Proposition — Uniqueness of identity and inverses

In any group $G$: (a) the identity element is unique, (b) the inverse of each element is unique, (c) $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$, and (d) $ab = ac \implies b = c$.

Proof

(a) Suppose $e$ and $e'$ are both identities. Then $e = ee' = e'$, using $e'$ as an identity for the first equality and $e$ for the second.

(b) Suppose $b$ and $c$ are both inverses of $a$. Then $b = be = b(ac) = (ba)c = ec = c$.

(c) Compute $(ab)(b^{-1}a^{-1}) = a(bb^{-1})a^{-1} = aea^{-1} = aa^{-1} = e$. By uniqueness of inverses, $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$.

(d) Left-multiply both sides of $ab = ac$ by $a^{-1}$: $a^{-1}(ab) = a^{-1}(ac)$, so $(a^{-1}a)b = (a^{-1}a)c$, so $eb = ec$, so $b = c$.

∎

Note the "socks and shoes" rule in (c): the inverse of a product reverses the order. If you put on socks then shoes, you take off shoes then socks.

4. Order of an element

Definition — Order

Let $g \in G$. The order of $g$, written $\text{ord}(g)$, is the smallest positive integer $n$ such that $g^n = e$. If no such $n$ exists, we say $g$ has infinite order.

In $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $\text{ord}(\bar{1}) = 6$ (you need to add $1$ six times to get back to $0$). $\text{ord}(\bar{2}) = 3$ because $\bar{2} + \bar{2} + \bar{2} = \bar{6} = \bar{0}$. $\text{ord}(\bar{3}) = 2$ because $\bar{3} + \bar{3} = \bar{0}$.

In $S_3$: a transposition $(1\;2)$ has order $2$ (swap twice and you're back). A $3$-cycle $(1\;2\;3)$ has order $3$. The identity has order $1$.

5. Cyclic groups

Definition — Cyclic group

A group $G$ is cyclic if there exists $g \in G$ such that every element of $G$ is a power of $g$: $G = \langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. We call $g$ a generator.

Theorem — Classification of cyclic groups

Every cyclic group is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}$ (if infinite) or $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (if finite of order $n$).

Proof

Step 1. Let $G = \langle g \rangle$ and define $\varphi: \mathbb{Z} \to G$ by $\varphi(k) = g^k$. This is surjective by definition. It is a homomorphism: $\varphi(k + l) = g^{k+l} = g^k g^l = \varphi(k)\varphi(l)$.

Step 2. If $\varphi$ is injective, then $G \cong \mathbb{Z}$. This happens precisely when no positive power of $g$ equals $e$ — i.e., $g$ has infinite order.

Step 3. If $g$ has finite order $n$, then $\ker(\varphi) = n\mathbb{Z}$ (the set of multiples of $n$). By the first isomorphism theorem (IOU: proved on page 3 of this phase), $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$.

∎

6. Dihedral groups

The dihedral group $D_n$ is the group of symmetries of a regular $n$-gon. It has $2n$ elements: $n$ rotations and $n$ reflections.

Let $r$ be rotation by $2\pi/n$ and $s$ a reflection. Then every element of $D_n$ is of the form $r^k$ or $sr^k$ for $0 \leq k < n$. The generators satisfy:

$$r^n = e, \quad s^2 = e, \quad srs = r^{-1}$$

The relation $srs = r^{-1}$ (equivalently $sr = r^{-1}s$) is what makes $D_n$ non-abelian for $n \geq 3$: rotating then reflecting differs from reflecting then rotating.

For $n = 3$: the triangle has $3$ rotations ($e, r, r^2$) and $3$ reflections ($s, sr, sr^2$), so $|D_3| = 6$. Each symmetry permutes the three vertices, and every permutation of three vertices is a symmetry of the triangle, so $D_3 \cong S_3$.

For $n = 4$: the square has $8$ symmetries ($4$ rotations, $4$ reflections), so $|D_4| = 8$. This is a proper subgroup of $S_4$ ($24$ elements) — not every permutation of the four vertices preserves adjacency. The group $D_4$ is our first concrete example of a non-abelian group that is not a symmetric group.

Historical note

The word "group" (groupe) was coined by Évariste Galois in 1832, in a letter written the night before his fatal duel. He used it for the collections of permutations that determine the solvability of polynomial equations. Arthur Cayley gave the first abstract definition in 1854, stating the axioms for a general set with an operation. But the full formalisation — including the explicit axiom of associativity — was completed by Walther von Dyck in 1882.

The concept was implicit in Lagrange's 1771 Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations, seventy years before anyone gave it a name. Lagrange studied which permutations of roots leave resolvent expressions invariant — precisely the group-theoretic structure that Galois would later exploit to prove the insolvability of the quintic.

What breaks

Without associativity: the octonions form a set with multiplication, identity, and inverses, but the operation is not associative. The product $(ab)c$ can differ from $a(bc)$. You lose the ability to write $abc$ unambiguously, and most of group theory collapses. Such structures are called loops, not groups.

Without inverses: $(\mathbb{N}, +)$ satisfies closure, associativity, and has an identity ($0$), but $3$ has no additive inverse in $\mathbb{N}$. This is a monoid. You can go forward but cannot undo — there is no cancellation law.

Without identity: the positive even integers under multiplication are closed and associative, but $1$ is not even. There is no identity element, so inverses are meaningless. This is merely a semigroup.

A group is a set with an operation that lets you combine, undo, and do nothing — the algebraic structure of symmetry.
