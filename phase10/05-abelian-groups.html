Classification of Finitely Generated Abelian Groups

Phase 10 — Abstract Algebra, Page 5

The problem

How many essentially different abelian groups of order $12$ are there? We know $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$, but is it the same as $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$? Yes — by the Chinese Remainder Theorem (Phase 1, page 6), since $\gcd(4,3) = 1$. Is it the same as $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$? Also yes, since $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, giving $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ — wait, that has two elements of order $2$, while $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ has only one. So these are genuinely different groups.

The classification theorem says every finitely generated abelian group decomposes uniquely into cyclic pieces, and tells us exactly what those pieces look like.

1. Direct products

Given groups $(G, \cdot_G)$ and $(H, \cdot_H)$, we build a new group from their Cartesian product.

Definition — Direct product

The direct product $G \times H$ is the set $\{(g, h) : g \in G, h \in H\}$ with component-wise operation: $(g_1, h_1)(g_2, h_2) = (g_1 g_2, h_1 h_2)$.

Proof that $G \times H$ is a group

Closure: if $g_1, g_2 \in G$ and $h_1, h_2 \in H$, then $g_1 g_2 \in G$ and $h_1 h_2 \in H$, so $(g_1 g_2, h_1 h_2) \in G \times H$.

Associativity: follows from associativity in each component.

Identity: $(e_G, e_H)$ satisfies $(e_G, e_H)(g, h) = (e_G g, e_H h) = (g, h)$.

Inverses: $(g, h)^{-1} = (g^{-1}, h^{-1})$.

∎

If both $G$ and $H$ are abelian, so is $G \times H$. The order is $|G \times H| = |G| \cdot |H|$.

Key example. $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$. The element $(\bar{1}, \bar{1})$ has order $\text{lcm}(2, 3) = 6$, so it generates the entire product. This is the Chinese Remainder Theorem for groups (Phase 1, page 6).

2. When does $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$?

Theorem — CRT for groups

$\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$ if and only if $\gcd(m, n) = 1$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $d = \gcd(m, n) > 1$. Then every element $(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ satisfies $\text{lcm}(m,n) \cdot (a, b) = (0, 0)$. Since $\text{lcm}(m,n) = mn/d < mn$, no element has order $mn$. But $\mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$ has an element of order $mn$. So they cannot be isomorphic.

($\Leftarrow$) If $\gcd(m,n) = 1$, the element $(\bar{1}, \bar{1})$ has order $\text{lcm}(m, n) = mn$. So $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic of order $mn$, hence isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z}$ by the classification of cyclic groups (page 1).

∎

Contrast: $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ is not cyclic. Every nonzero element has order $2$, so no single element generates the group. This is the Klein four-group $V_4$, genuinely different from $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.

3. The classification theorem (finite case)

Theorem — Fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups

Every finite abelian group $G$ is isomorphic to a direct product of cyclic groups of prime-power order:

$$G \cong \mathbb{Z}/p_1^{a_1}\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/p_2^{a_2}\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/p_k^{a_k}\mathbb{Z}$$

where each $p_i$ is prime and each $a_i \geq 1$. This decomposition is unique up to reordering of the factors.

4. Proof sketch

The proof has two main steps: decompose by primes, then classify each prime component.

Step 1: Primary decomposition. Write $|G| = p_1^{n_1} \cdots p_r^{n_r}$. For each prime $p$, define the $p$-primary component:

$$G_p = \{g \in G : \text{ord}(g) \text{ is a power of } p\}$$

Since $G$ is abelian, $G_p$ is a subgroup (if $g^{p^a} = e$ and $h^{p^b} = e$, then $(gh)^{p^{\max(a,b)}} = g^{p^{\max(a,b)}} h^{p^{\max(a,b)}} = e$ — here we use commutativity). The map $G_{p_1} \times \cdots \times G_{p_r} \to G$ sending $(g_1, \ldots, g_r) \mapsto g_1 \cdots g_r$ is an isomorphism. This follows from the fact that every element can be uniquely written as a product of elements of coprime order, using Bézout's identity (Phase 1, page 2).

Step 2: Classify abelian $p$-groups. We now have an abelian group $G$ of order $p^n$. Proceed by induction on $n$. Take $g \in G$ of maximal order $p^m$. The cyclic subgroup $\langle g \rangle$ has a complement $H$ in $G$: a subgroup with $G = \langle g \rangle \times H$. This complement exists because $G$ is abelian and $g$ has maximal order (the proof uses the fact that the quotient $G / \langle g \rangle$ is a smaller abelian $p$-group, and one lifts a complement from the quotient). By induction, $H$ is a product of cyclic $p$-groups, completing the decomposition.

Uniqueness: the number of elements of order $p$ in $G$ determines the number of factors. More precisely, the sequence of exponents $(a_1, \ldots, a_k)$ (sorted) is determined by the orders of the iterated quotients $G / pG$, $G / p^2 G$, etc.

5. Counting abelian groups

The classification reduces counting to combinatorics. An abelian group of order $n = p_1^{n_1} \cdots p_r^{n_r}$ corresponds to choosing, for each $p_i$, a partition of $n_i$: a way to write $n_i$ as a sum of positive integers (order doesn't matter).

Example: order $36 = 2^2 \cdot 3^2$. Partitions of $2$: $(2)$ or $(1,1)$. For the factor of $2^2$: either $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. For the factor of $3^2$: either $\mathbb{Z}/9\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$. Total: $2 \times 2 = 4$ abelian groups of order $36$.

The four groups are: $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/9\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/36\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/9\mathbb{Z}$, and $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.

Back to our motivating question: for order $12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$, partitions of $2$ give $2$ choices, partitions of $1$ give $1$ choice, so there are exactly $2$ abelian groups of order $12$: $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$.

6. The general case: finitely generated abelian groups

Not every abelian group is finite. $\mathbb{Z}$ is an infinite abelian group generated by a single element. The classification extends to all finitely generated abelian groups by allowing a free part.

Theorem — Structure of finitely generated abelian groups

Every finitely generated abelian group $G$ is isomorphic to

$$G \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \times \mathbb{Z}/d_1\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/d_2\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/d_k\mathbb{Z}$$

where $r \geq 0$ and $d_1 \mid d_2 \mid \cdots \mid d_k$ with each $d_i \geq 2$. This is the invariant factor form. The integer $r$ is the rank (the free part), and the $d_i$ are the invariant factors.

Equivalently, in elementary divisor form: $G \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \times \mathbb{Z}/p_1^{a_1}\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/p_m^{a_m}\mathbb{Z}$ where each $p_i$ is prime. Both decompositions are unique.

The torsion subgroup $T(G) = \{g \in G : g \text{ has finite order}\}$ captures all the finite-order elements, giving $G \cong \mathbb{Z}^r \times T(G)$. The rank $r$ and the torsion part are both invariants of the group.

This theorem is really a special case of the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a principal ideal domain (PID), applied to $\mathbb{Z}$-modules — which are exactly abelian groups. We will see PIDs on page 6.

Historical note

The classification of finite abelian groups was proved independently by Leopold Kronecker (1870) and by Ferdinand Georg Frobenius and Ludwig Stickelberger (1879). Kronecker's approach used Smith normal form for integer matrices; Frobenius and Stickelberger gave a more group-theoretic argument.

The extension to finitely generated abelian groups rests on the structure theorem for modules over a PID, developed systematically by Emmy Noether and her school in the 1920s. Noether recognised that the parallel between integer arithmetic and polynomial arithmetic — both being PIDs — could be unified in a single abstract framework.

What breaks

Non-abelian groups: the classification fails spectacularly. There are two groups of order $4$ (both abelian), but already two non-isomorphic groups of order $6$ ($\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ and $S_3$). Groups of order $p^3$ include non-abelian examples, and the classification of finite simple groups took over a century and tens of thousands of pages.

Infinitely generated groups: $\mathbb{Q}$ under addition is abelian but not a direct sum of cyclic groups. It is divisible (for every $q \in \mathbb{Q}$ and $n \geq 1$, there exists $r$ with $nr = q$), while cyclic groups are never divisible. The theory of infinitely generated abelian groups is vastly more complicated.

Uniqueness subtlety: $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ looks like two different decompositions, but the theorem says the prime-power decomposition is unique. Both expressions give the same list: $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.

Every finitely generated abelian group is a direct product of cyclic groups — $\mathbb{Z}$'s for the free part, $\mathbb{Z}/p^a\mathbb{Z}$'s for the torsion.
