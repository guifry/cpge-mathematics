Galois Theory and Abel-Ruffini

Phase 10 — Abstract Algebra, Page 8

The problem

The quadratic formula solves $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ using $+, -, \times, \div$, and square roots. Cardano (1545) found a formula for degree $3$; Ferrari found one for degree $4$. For three centuries, mathematicians searched for a degree-$5$ formula. Abel (1824) proved that no such formula exists. Galois (1832, written the night before his fatal duel at age $20$) explained why: the solvability of a polynomial by radicals depends on the symmetry group of its roots.

1. Field extensions

Definition — Field extension

A field extension $L/K$ is a pair of fields with $K \subseteq L$. The degree of the extension is $[L : K] = \dim_K(L)$, the dimension of $L$ as a $K$-vector space (Phase 6).

Examples. $[\mathbb{C} : \mathbb{R}] = 2$, with basis $\{1, i\}$. $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2$, with basis $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$. Here $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{a + b\sqrt{2} : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}$, the smallest field containing $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\sqrt{2}$.

Theorem — Tower law

If $K \subseteq L \subseteq M$ are fields, then $[M : K] = [M : L] \cdot [L : K]$.

Proof

Let $\{e_1, \ldots, e_m\}$ be a basis of $L$ over $K$ and $\{f_1, \ldots, f_n\}$ a basis of $M$ over $L$. We claim $\{e_i f_j\}$ is a basis of $M$ over $K$.

Spanning: any $x \in M$ can be written $x = \sum_j l_j f_j$ with $l_j \in L$. Each $l_j = \sum_i a_{ij} e_i$ with $a_{ij} \in K$. So $x = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} e_i f_j$.

Independence: suppose $\sum_{i,j} a_{ij} e_i f_j = 0$. Rearranging: $\sum_j \left(\sum_i a_{ij} e_i\right) f_j = 0$. Since the $f_j$ are independent over $L$, each $\sum_i a_{ij} e_i = 0$. Since the $e_i$ are independent over $K$, each $a_{ij} = 0$.

∎

2. Splitting fields

Definition — Splitting field

The splitting field of $f \in K[x]$ is the smallest extension $L/K$ in which $f$ factors completely into linear factors.

Example. The splitting field of $x^2 + 1$ over $\mathbb{R}$ is $\mathbb{C}$: $x^2 + 1 = (x - i)(x + i)$, and no proper subfield of $\mathbb{C}$ containing $\mathbb{R}$ contains $i$.

Example. The splitting field of $x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. The roots are $\sqrt[3]{2}$, $\sqrt[3]{2}\omega$, $\sqrt[3]{2}\omega^2$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. The splitting field is $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$. By the tower law: $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 3$ (minimal polynomial $x^3 - 2$) and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})] = 2$ (minimal polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$). So the total degree is $6$.

3. The Galois group

Definition — Galois group

For a field extension $L/K$, the Galois group is $\text{Gal}(L/K) = \{\sigma : L \to L \mid \sigma \text{ is an automorphism with } \sigma(a) = a \text{ for all } a \in K\}$, with group operation being composition.

Example. $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}) = \{\text{id}, \overline{\phantom{z}}\}$ where $\overline{\phantom{z}}$ is complex conjugation. This is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

Example. $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q})$. Any automorphism $\sigma$ fixing $\mathbb{Q}$ must send $\sqrt{2}$ to a root of $x^2 - 2$, so $\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \pm\sqrt{2}$. Both choices extend to automorphisms, giving $\text{Gal} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

Example. For $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, the Galois group has order $6 = [L : \mathbb{Q}]$. An automorphism $\sigma$ is determined by where it sends $\sqrt[3]{2}$ (three choices) and $\omega$ (two choices). This gives $\text{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3$: the six automorphisms permute the three roots of $x^3 - 2$.

4. The Galois correspondence

Theorem — Fundamental theorem of Galois theory

Let $L/K$ be a Galois extension with Galois group $G = \text{Gal}(L/K)$. There is an order-reversing bijection:

$$\{\text{subgroups } H \leq G\} \longleftrightarrow \{\text{intermediate fields } K \subseteq M \subseteq L\}$$

given by $H \mapsto L^H = \{x \in L : \sigma(x) = x \text{ for all } \sigma \in H\}$ and $M \mapsto \text{Gal}(L/M)$. Moreover, $[L : L^H] = |H|$ and $[L^H : K] = [G : H]$. Normal subgroups correspond to Galois sub-extensions.

We state this without full proof — the proof requires the theory of separable extensions and Dedekind's lemma on linear independence of characters. But the statement reveals the deep connection: subgroup structure of $G$ mirrors the field structure of the extension.

5. Solvable groups

Definition — Solvable group

A group $G$ is solvable if there exists a chain of subgroups

$$G = G_0 \trianglerighteq G_1 \trianglerighteq \cdots \trianglerighteq G_k = \{e\}$$

where each $G_i \trianglelefteq G_{i-1}$ (normal subgroup) and each quotient $G_{i-1}/G_i$ is abelian.

Examples. Every abelian group $G$ is solvable: take $G \trianglerighteq \{e\}$. $S_3$ is solvable: $S_3 \trianglerighteq A_3 \trianglerighteq \{e\}$, with $S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ and $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, both abelian. $S_4$ is solvable: $S_4 \trianglerighteq A_4 \trianglerighteq V_4 \trianglerighteq \langle(12)(34)\rangle \trianglerighteq \{e\}$, where $V_4 = \{e, (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\}$ is the Klein four-group.

6. $S_5$ is not solvable

The key fact is that $A_5$ is simple: its only normal subgroups are $\{e\}$ and $A_5$ itself.

Proof that $A_5$ is simple

$|A_5| = 60$. The conjugacy classes in $A_5$ have sizes $1, 12, 12, 15, 20$. (These correspond to: the identity; the two classes of $5$-cycles; the $3$-cycles; and the double transpositions.)

Any normal subgroup $N$ of $A_5$ is a union of conjugacy classes and must contain the identity (size $1$). So $|N|$ is a sum of a subset of $\{1, 12, 12, 15, 20\}$ that includes $1$, and $|N|$ must divide $60$. The divisors of $60$ are $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60$. No sum $1 + (\text{subset of } \{12, 12, 15, 20\})$ equals any of $2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30$. Check: $1 + 12 = 13$, $1 + 15 = 16$, $1 + 20 = 21$, $1 + 12 + 12 = 25$, $1 + 12 + 15 = 28$, $1 + 12 + 20 = 33$, $1 + 15 + 20 = 36$, $1 + 12 + 12 + 15 = 40$, $1 + 12 + 12 + 20 = 45$, $1 + 12 + 15 + 20 = 48$. None divides $60$ (other than $1$ and $60$ itself). So $N = \{e\}$ or $N = A_5$.

∎

Now, $S_5$ is not solvable. In any composition series for $S_5$, the factor $A_5$ must appear (since $[S_5 : A_5] = 2$ and $A_5$ is the unique subgroup of index $2$). But $A_5$ is simple and non-abelian, so it cannot be broken down further into abelian quotients. Therefore no solvable series exists.

7. The Abel-Ruffini theorem

Theorem — Abel-Ruffini

The general polynomial of degree $n \geq 5$ is not solvable by radicals.

The proof rests on three pillars:

(1) Galois's criterion. A polynomial $f \in K[x]$ is solvable by radicals (its roots can be expressed using $+, -, \times, \div$, and $n$th roots starting from the coefficients) if and only if its Galois group $\text{Gal}(L/K)$ is a solvable group. This is the deep theorem — the Galois correspondence translates the algebraic operation of "adjoin an $n$th root" into the group-theoretic operation of "pass to a normal subgroup with cyclic quotient."

(2) The generic Galois group. The Galois group of the "general" degree-$n$ polynomial $x^n + t_1 x^{n-1} + \cdots + t_n$ (where $t_1, \ldots, t_n$ are algebraically independent transcendentals) is $S_n$. The coefficients are the elementary symmetric polynomials in the roots, and every permutation of the roots preserves these symmetric functions.

(3) $S_n$ is not solvable for $n \geq 5$. We proved $A_5$ is simple and non-abelian. The same argument (conjugacy class counting) shows $A_n$ is simple for all $n \geq 5$. So $S_n$ has no solvable series for $n \geq 5$.

A concrete example. The polynomial $f = x^5 - 4x + 2 \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$ is irreducible by Eisenstein's criterion with $p = 2$ (page 7). Its Galois group over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $S_5$ (this can be shown by analysing the cycle types of the Frobenius elements at small primes). Since $S_5$ is not solvable, the roots of $x^5 - 4x + 2$ cannot be expressed using $+, -, \times, \div$, and radicals.

8. Phase 10 recap

We have traced the arc of abstract algebra from Lagrange's 1771 observation to Galois's 1832 resolution. The journey: groups (page 1), subgroups and Lagrange's theorem (page 2), homomorphisms and the isomorphism theorem (page 3), Cayley's theorem and Sylow theorems (page 4), the classification of abelian groups (page 5), rings and polynomial division (page 6), irreducibility and the FTA (page 7), and finally Galois theory and Abel-Ruffini (this page).

The unifying insight: algebraic structure controls algebraic solvability. The integers and polynomials share the same arithmetic (Euclidean domains). The symmetries of polynomial roots form a group whose structure determines what formulas can exist. Mathematics moved from solving specific equations to understanding why some equations resist solution — and the answer was always about structure.

Historical note

Évariste Galois wrote his main results the night of 29 May 1832, in a letter to Auguste Chevalier, before his fatal duel the following morning. He was twenty years old. His manuscripts were published by Joseph Liouville in 1846, fourteen years later.

Niels Henrik Abel proved the impossibility of a quintic formula in 1824, at age 21. Paolo Ruffini had attempted the same result in 1799, but his proof had gaps (he assumed without proof that the Galois group of the general quintic is $S_5$). Galois went further than both: he characterised exactly which polynomials are solvable by radicals, in terms of their symmetry group.

This result transformed algebra from the art of equation-solving into the science of structure. Every branch of modern algebra — group theory, ring theory, field theory, representation theory — traces back to Galois's twenty pages of hastily written mathematics.

What breaks

Degree $\leq 4$: the Galois groups $S_2, S_3, S_4$ are all solvable, so formulas exist. The cubic formula (Cardano, 1545) and quartic formula (Ferrari, 1545) are complicated — involving nested cube roots and square roots — but they exist. Solvability of $S_4$ is what makes this possible.

"Not solvable by radicals" $\neq$ "no roots". The roots of $x^5 - 4x + 2$ are real numbers (three real, two complex). They exist; they just cannot be written using $+, -, \times, \div$, and $n$th roots. There is no finite algebraic formula, but the roots can be approximated to arbitrary precision.

Some quintics are solvable. $x^5 - 2$ has Galois group of order $20$ (the Frobenius group $F_{20}$), which is solvable. Its roots are $\sqrt[5]{2} \cdot \zeta$ where $\zeta$ ranges over the fifth roots of unity — expressible by radicals. The Abel-Ruffini theorem says the general quintic is not solvable, not that every specific one fails.

The quintic has no formula because $S_5$ is not solvable — Galois showed that the symmetry group of the roots controls whether a polynomial can be solved by radicals.
