Subgroups and Lagrange's Theorem

Phase 10 — Abstract Algebra, Page 2

The problem

Lagrange (1771) studied the permutations of roots of a polynomial of degree $n$. He observed that certain sub-collections of permutations are closed under composition: composing two permutations from the sub-collection always yields another one in it. These sub-collections have a striking numerical property — their size always divides $n!$, the total number of permutations.

Is this a coincidence? It is not. The pattern — the size of a substructure divides the size of the whole — holds for every finite group and every subgroup. The proof is surprisingly clean: the subgroup tiles the group into equal-sized pieces.

1. Subgroups

Definition — Subgroup

Let $(G, \cdot)$ be a group. A subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup, written $H \leq G$, if $H$ is itself a group under the same operation.

Checking all four axioms from scratch is tedious. The following test cuts the work down.

Theorem — Subgroup test

$H \leq G$ if and only if $H \neq \varnothing$ and for all $a, b \in H$, $ab^{-1} \in H$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) If $H$ is a subgroup, it contains $e$ (so $H \neq \varnothing$) and is closed under products and inverses, so $ab^{-1} \in H$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $H \neq \varnothing$ and $ab^{-1} \in H$ for all $a, b \in H$.

Identity: pick any $a \in H$ (possible since $H \neq \varnothing$). Then $e = aa^{-1} \in H$.

Inverses: for any $a \in H$, $a^{-1} = ea^{-1} \in H$ (using $e, a \in H$).

Closure: for $a, b \in H$, we have $b^{-1} \in H$, so $ab = a(b^{-1})^{-1} \in H$.

Associativity is inherited from $G$.

∎

2. Examples

(a) $n\mathbb{Z} \leq \mathbb{Z}$. The multiples of $n$ form a subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$. Check: $0 \in n\mathbb{Z}$, and if $a, b \in n\mathbb{Z}$ then $a - b \in n\mathbb{Z}$.

(b) $A_n \leq S_n$. The alternating group $A_n$ consists of even permutations — those expressible as a product of an even number of transpositions. The composition of two even permutations is even, and the inverse of an even permutation is even. We have $|A_n| = n!/2$.

(c) $\langle g \rangle \leq G$. For any $g \in G$, the cyclic subgroup generated by $g$ is a subgroup (page 1, section 5).

(d) $\text{SL}_n(\mathbb{R}) \leq \text{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$. Matrices with determinant $1$. If $\det(A) = \det(B) = 1$, then $\det(AB^{-1}) = \det(A)/\det(B) = 1$.

3. Cosets

Definition — Left coset

For $H \leq G$ and $g \in G$, the left coset of $H$ by $g$ is $gH = \{gh : h \in H\}$.

Theorem — Cosets partition $G$

The distinct left cosets of $H$ in $G$ form a partition of $G$: every element of $G$ lies in exactly one coset, and all cosets have the same size $|H|$.

Proof

Step 1. Every element lies in a coset. For any $g \in G$, we have $g = ge \in gH$.

Step 2. Cosets are equal or disjoint. Suppose $aH \cap bH \neq \varnothing$. Pick $c \in aH \cap bH$, so $c = ah_1 = bh_2$ for some $h_1, h_2 \in H$. Then $a = bh_2 h_1^{-1}$, so for any $h \in H$, $ah = b(h_2 h_1^{-1} h) \in bH$. Hence $aH \subseteq bH$. By symmetry, $bH \subseteq aH$, so $aH = bH$.

Step 3. All cosets have size $|H|$. The map $\varphi: H \to gH$ defined by $\varphi(h) = gh$ is a bijection: it is surjective by definition, and injective because $gh_1 = gh_2$ implies $h_1 = h_2$ by left cancellation.

∎

4. Lagrange's theorem

Theorem — Lagrange's theorem

If $G$ is a finite group and $H \leq G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$. The number of distinct cosets $[G:H] = |G|/|H|$ is called the index of $H$ in $G$.

Proof

The distinct left cosets of $H$ partition $G$ (proved above). There are $[G:H]$ cosets, each of size $|H|$. Counting elements:

$$|G| = [G:H] \cdot |H|$$

Since $[G:H]$ is a positive integer, $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

∎

The proof is a counting argument. Nothing deeper is needed — the cosets tile $G$ like identical tiles covering a floor.

5. Consequences

Corollary (a) — Order of elements divides $|G|$

If $G$ is finite and $g \in G$, then $\text{ord}(g)$ divides $|G|$.

Proof

$\langle g \rangle \leq G$ and $|\langle g \rangle| = \text{ord}(g)$. Apply Lagrange.

∎
Corollary (b) — Groups of prime order are cyclic

If $|G| = p$ (prime), then $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$.

Proof

Pick any $g \neq e$. Then $\text{ord}(g) > 1$ and $\text{ord}(g)$ divides $p$. Since $p$ is prime, $\text{ord}(g) = p$, so $\langle g \rangle = G$.

∎
Corollary (c) — Fermat's little theorem recovered

If $p$ is prime and $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$.

Proof

The multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ has order $p - 1$ (Phase 1, page 4). The element $\bar{a}$ has some order $d$ dividing $p - 1$. Then $a^{p-1} = (a^d)^{(p-1)/d} \equiv 1^{(p-1)/d} = 1 \pmod{p}$.

∎

6. The converse fails

Lagrange says $|H|$ divides $|G|$. Does every divisor of $|G|$ appear as the order of some subgroup? No. The alternating group $A_4$ has order $12$, and $6$ divides $12$, but $A_4$ has no subgroup of order $6$.

Proof that $A_4$ has no subgroup of order 6

Suppose $H \leq A_4$ with $|H| = 6$. Then $[A_4 : H] = 2$. Any subgroup of index $2$ is normal (for any $g \notin H$, the two left cosets are $H$ and $gH$, and the two right cosets are $H$ and $Hg$; since there are only two cosets and $g \notin H$, we must have $gH = Hg$).

So $H \trianglelefteq A_4$. Now $A_4$ contains exactly eight $3$-cycles (elements of order $3$): $(1\;2\;3)$, $(1\;3\;2)$, $(1\;2\;4)$, $(1\;4\;2)$, $(1\;3\;4)$, $(1\;4\;3)$, $(2\;3\;4)$, $(2\;4\;3)$. If $\sigma$ is a $3$-cycle in $H$, then $\sigma^2$ (also a $3$-cycle) is in $H$. Since $H$ is normal, the entire conjugacy class of $\sigma$ lies in $H$. The $3$-cycles split into conjugacy classes of size $4$ in $A_4$. So $H$ would contain at least $4$ elements of order $3$, plus the identity, plus their squares (another $4$ elements of order $3$), giving at least $9$ elements — contradicting $|H| = 6$.

∎
Historical note

Lagrange never stated this theorem in group-theoretic language. His 1771 Réflexions observed the divisibility pattern for permutation groups of polynomial roots. The theorem was first proved in its modern form — for abstract groups, using cosets — by later algebraists building on Galois's work. The coset-based proof is so natural that it is now given in every first course in algebra, but it took sixty years from Lagrange's observation to the clean argument we presented above.

What breaks

For infinite groups: Lagrange's theorem does not apply. We have $2\mathbb{Z} \leq \mathbb{Z}$, both infinite — the "ratio" $|\mathbb{Z}|/|2\mathbb{Z}|$ is not a finite number. The index $[\mathbb{Z} : 2\mathbb{Z}] = 2$ is still well-defined (two cosets: even and odd integers), but divisibility of cardinalities is meaningless.

Converse fails: as proved above, $A_4$ has no subgroup of order $6$. The partial converse is Sylow's theorem (page 4): subgroups of prime-power order always exist.

Without the group structure: cosets of a mere subset of a set have no reason to partition anything. You need associativity and inverses to guarantee that coset membership is an equivalence relation.

Lagrange's theorem: in a finite group, the size of any subgroup divides the size of the group — the cosets tile it perfectly.
