Phase 0 — Geometry and Measurement

Where humanity started. You want to measure the world — lengths, areas, angles, the height of a building from its shadow. You need tools.

  • 1. Similar Triangles and Measurement
    Thales measures the Great Pyramid. Same angles → same ratios. The foundation of trigonometry.
  • 2. The Pythagorean Theorem
    $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Two proofs. Why it's about areas, not algebra. The diagonal of a unit square.
  • 3. $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational — The First Crisis
    A length you can draw but can't write as a fraction. The rationals have holes. Why $\mathbb{R}$ was invented.
  • 4. Trigonometry from Scratch
    Hipparchus needs the distance to the Moon. Sine and cosine as angle-to-ratio converters. Built on similar triangles.
  • 5. The Unit Circle
    Extending trig beyond right triangles. $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ is the Pythagorean theorem on a circle. Proves $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$.
  • 6. Coordinate Geometry — Algebra Meets Geometry
    Descartes turns points into numbers and curves into equations. Makes calculus possible.

Phase 1 — The Infinite: Limits and Basic Convergence

Archimedes wanted to compute the area of a circle. He trapped it between polygons with more and more sides. The area is what the polygon areas "approach." But what does "approach" mean, precisely?

  • 1. Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$
    The rationals have holes. The supremum axiom fills them. Why $\mathbb{R}$ exists and $\mathbb{Q}$ doesn't suffice.
  • 2. The Squeeze Theorem
    Archimedes traps $\pi$ between polygons. If two sequences squeeze a third, it converges to their shared limit.
  • 3. Monotone Convergence Theorem
    The Babylonian method for $\sqrt{2}$. A bounded, monotone sequence must converge — completeness in action.
  • 4. Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem
    Every bounded sequence hides a convergent subsequence. The bisection argument.
  • 5. The Cauchy Criterion
    Convergence without knowing the limit. Terms getting closer to each other — the internal test.
  • 6. Intermediate Value Theorem
    A continuous function that's negative then positive must cross zero. Continuity defined, bisection proof.
  • 7. Dense Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$
    $\mathbb{Q}$ is everywhere dense yet full of holes. Phase 1 recap: the architecture of the real line.
