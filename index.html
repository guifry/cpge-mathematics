CPGE Mathematics

Hybrid Curriculum — Bourbaki rigour, historical order

Every concept motivated by the problem that created it. Every tool proved before being used. No black boxes.

Phase 0 — Geometry and Measurement

Where humanity started. You want to measure the world — lengths, areas, angles, the height of a building from its shadow. You need tools.

  • 1. Similar Triangles and Measurement
    Thales measures the Great Pyramid. Same angles → same ratios. The foundation of trigonometry.
  • 2. The Pythagorean Theorem
    $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Two proofs. Why it's about areas, not algebra. The diagonal of a unit square.
  • 3. $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational — The First Crisis
    A length you can draw but can't write as a fraction. The rationals have holes. Why $\mathbb{R}$ was invented.
  • 4. Trigonometry from Scratch
    Hipparchus needs the distance to the Moon. Sine and cosine as angle-to-ratio converters. Built on similar triangles.
  • 5. The Unit Circle
    Extending trig beyond right triangles. $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ is the Pythagorean theorem on a circle. Proves $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$.
  • 6. Coordinate Geometry — Algebra Meets Geometry
    Descartes turns points into numbers and curves into equations. Makes calculus possible.

Phase 1 — Number Theory and Arithmetic

The Pythagoreans worshipped whole numbers. They discovered primes, divisibility, and the structure hidden in the integers. The oldest mathematics — counting, dividing, and the atoms of arithmetic.

  • 1. Divisibility and the Euclidean Algorithm
    Euclid needs the largest common measure of two lengths. The division algorithm, GCD, Bézout's identity, and the LCM.
  • 2. Prime Numbers and the Fundamental Theorem
    The atoms of arithmetic. Sieve of Eratosthenes, Euclid's lemma, infinitely many primes, unique factorisation.
  • 3. Modular Arithmetic
    Clock arithmetic made rigorous. Congruence, residue classes, inverses via Bézout, $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ as a field.
  • 4. Fermat's Little Theorem
    A pattern in powers mod $p$. The permutation proof, fast exponentiation, and the trap of Carmichael numbers.
  • 5. Euler's Totient Function
    Counting invertible residues. $\varphi(n)$, the prime power formula, Euler's theorem generalising Fermat, and a glimpse of RSA.
  • 6. The Chinese Remainder Theorem
    Sun Tzu's counting puzzle. Simultaneous congruences, constructive proof, and the payback — $\varphi$ is multiplicative.
  • 7. Diophantine Equations
    Integer solutions only. Linear Diophantine equations, existence via GCD, all solutions parametrised. Pythagorean triples. Phase 1 recap.

Phase 2 — The Infinite: Limits and Basic Convergence

Archimedes wanted to compute the area of a circle. He trapped it between polygons with more and more sides. The area is what the polygon areas "approach." But what does "approach" mean, precisely?

  • 1. Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$
    The rationals have holes. The supremum axiom fills them. Why $\mathbb{R}$ exists and $\mathbb{Q}$ doesn't suffice.
  • 2. The Squeeze Theorem
    Archimedes traps $\pi$ between polygons. If two sequences squeeze a third, it converges to their shared limit.
  • 3. Monotone Convergence Theorem
    The Babylonian method for $\sqrt{2}$. A bounded, monotone sequence must converge — completeness in action.
  • 4. Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem
    Every bounded sequence hides a convergent subsequence. The bisection argument.
  • 5. The Cauchy Criterion
    Convergence without knowing the limit. Terms getting closer to each other — the internal test.
  • 6. Intermediate Value Theorem
    A continuous function that's negative then positive must cross zero. Continuity defined, bisection proof.
  • 7. Dense Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$
    $\mathbb{Q}$ is everywhere dense yet full of holes. Phase 2 recap: the architecture of the real line.

Phase 3 — Calculus: Derivatives and Integration

Fermat wanted to find where a curve peaks. Newton and Leibniz wanted to compute areas under curves. The derivative and the integral — two seemingly unrelated ideas — turn out to be inverse operations. Built from scratch, every rule proved.

  • 1. The Derivative
    Fermat's tangent problem. The slope of a curve at a point, defined as the limit of secant slopes. Power rule proved.
  • 2. Differentiation Rules
    Sum, product, quotient, chain rule — each proved from the limit definition. No memorisation without proof.
  • 3. Derivatives of Sine and Cosine
    The geometric squeeze: $\sin(h)/h \to 1$. Then $d/dx \sin x = \cos x$ from the definition. Phase 0 meets Phase 2.
  • 4. The Mean Value Theorem
    If you drove 200 km in 2 hours, your speed was exactly 100 km/h at some instant. Local → global.
  • 5. L'Hôpital's Rule
    When $0/0$ blocks you, compare rates of vanishing. Proved from Cauchy's generalised MVT.
  • 6. The Riemann Integral
    Archimedes' area problem formalised. Partitions, upper and lower sums, the definition from scratch.
  • 7. Integrability and Properties
    Continuous on a closed interval means integrable. Uniform continuity, linearity, monotonicity of the integral.
  • 8. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
    The central result. Differentiation and integration are inverse operations. Both parts proved.
  • 9. Integration Techniques
    Substitution = chain rule backwards. Integration by parts = product rule backwards. Phase 3 recap.

Phase 4 — Infinite Polynomials: Power Series and Taylor

Newton had a radical insight: write complicated functions as infinite polynomials, then just do polynomial arithmetic. Taylor systematised this — if you know all the derivatives at one point, you can reconstruct the entire function. Here we define $e^x$, $\sin$, $\cos$, and $\ln$ from scratch.

  • 1. Taylor's Theorem
    Newton's infinite-polynomial idea. Matching derivatives to find coefficients. The Lagrange remainder bounds the error.
  • 2. Power Series and Radius of Convergence
    A disc of convergence for every power series. Ratio test, Cauchy-Hadamard. Why $1/(1+x^2)$ has radius $1$ despite being smooth everywhere.
  • 3. The Exponential Function
    $e^x = \sum x^n/n!$ — defined by the series, all properties proved. The number $e$, the natural logarithm, and the IOU from Phase 3 closed.
  • 4. Sine and Cosine from Scratch
    Trig functions from pure arithmetic. $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ proved algebraically. $|\sin x| \leq 1$ proved. IOUs from Phases 0 and 2 closed.
  • 5. Term-by-Term Operations
    Differentiating and integrating power series term by term — when and why it works. Series for $\ln(1+x)$ and $\arctan(x)$ derived.
  • 6. Abel's Theorem
    Boundary behaviour of power series. $\ln(2) = 1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - \cdots$ and $\pi/4 = 1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - \cdots$. Phase 4 recap.

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit

Euler computed with infinite series freely and brilliantly — and sometimes wrongly. Cauchy decided to clean up the mess: which manipulations are valid? When does an infinite sum produce a meaningful answer? The systematic tools for taming series.

  • 1. The Comparison Test
    Euler wants to know if $\sum 1/n^2$ converges without computing the sum. Compare with a series you already know.
  • 2. The Ratio and Root Tests
    d'Alembert automates comparison: look at the ratio of consecutive terms. Cauchy's root test completes the picture.
  • 3. The Alternating Series Test
    The harmonic series diverges, but alternating signs rescue it. Leibniz's criterion and the free error bound.
  • 4. Absolute and Conditional Convergence
    Riemann's disturbing theorem: rearranging a conditionally convergent series can produce any sum. Absolute convergence is the safe notion.
  • 5. Cesàro Means
    When averages converge even if the sequence doesn't. The Cesàro mean theorem and Cesàro summability.
  • 6. Uniform Convergence
    Cauchy's error: the limit of continuous functions isn't always continuous. Weierstrass's fix — uniform convergence.
  • 7. The Weierstrass M-Test
    The practical shortcut for proving uniform convergence. Dominate each term by a constant, converge the constants.
  • 8. The Weierstrass Approximation Theorem
    Every continuous function on $[a,b]$ is uniformly approximable by polynomials. Bernstein's constructive proof. Phase 5 recap.

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra

Leibniz and Cramer were solving systems of equations. Cayley realised the patterns were instances of a deeper structure: linear transformations of space. The question shifted from "solve this system" to "what does this transformation do to space?"

  • 1. Vector Spaces
    Solutions to $Ax = 0$ have structure: add two solutions, get a solution. Grassmann's abstraction — the 8 axioms.
  • 2. Basis and Dimension
    How many vectors span a space? The Steinitz exchange lemma proves all bases have the same size. Dimension is well-defined.
  • 3. Linear Maps and Rank-Nullity
    What you lose (kernel) plus what you hit (image) equals what you started with. The conservation law of linear algebra.
  • 4. Matrices and Change of Basis
    Cayley turns abstract maps into arrays of numbers. Different bases give different matrices — $P^{-1}AP$ translates between them.
  • 5. Determinants
    One number detects invertibility and measures volume scaling. The axiomatic approach: multilinear, alternating, normalised.
  • 6. Eigenvalues and the Characteristic Polynomial
    Special directions that a map merely stretches. The characteristic polynomial, diagonalisability, and when it fails.
  • 7. Inner Products and Gram-Schmidt
    Measuring length and angle in abstract spaces. Cauchy-Schwarz, orthogonality, and the orthonormalisation process.
  • 8. Orthogonal Projection
    The closest point in a subspace. Drop a perpendicular — the projection theorem and best approximation.
  • 9. The Spectral Theorem
    Symmetric matrices always diagonalise — with an orthonormal eigenbasis. Real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors, $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$.
  • 10. The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem
    Every matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial. The "obvious" proof is wrong — the real proof uses the adjugate.
  • 11. Jordan Normal Form
    When diagonalisation fails, Jordan provides the next best thing. Generalised eigenvectors and the complete classification. Phase 6 recap.

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus

Temperature, pressure, velocity — quantities that depend on position in space. Newton's calculus handled one variable. The real world has three spatial dimensions plus time. Euler, Lagrange, and Gauss extended calculus to multiple variables.

  • 1. Partial Derivatives and the Gradient
    Temperature varies across a room. Differentiate in each direction separately. The gradient points uphill.
  • 2. The Multivariable Chain Rule
    A particle moves through a temperature field. The chain rule is matrix multiplication — the Jacobian.
  • 3. Schwarz's Theorem
    Does the order of differentiation matter? $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ when the mixed partials are continuous. Peano's counterexample.
  • 4. The Implicit and Inverse Function Theorems
    When can you solve $F(x,y) = 0$ for $y$? When the Jacobian is invertible. Two theorems, one engine.
  • 5. Lagrange Multipliers and the Second Derivative Test
    Optimise under constraints. At a constrained extremum, $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$. The Hessian classifies critical points.
  • 6. Multiple Integrals and Fubini's Theorem
    Volume under a surface. Double integrals as iterated single integrals. Change of variables and the Gaussian integral.
  • 7. Green's Theorem
    Circulation around a boundary equals total curl inside. Line integrals, conservative fields, the area formula.
  • 8. The Divergence Theorem and Stokes' Theorem
    Flux through a surface = divergence inside. The grand unification: FTC, Green, Gauss, Stokes — all one theorem. Phase 7 recap.

Phase 8 — Differential Equations

A population grows proportionally to its size. A spring oscillates. A pendulum swings. Newton invented calculus largely to solve these equations — find a function from a relationship between the function and its derivatives.

  • 1. First-Order ODEs
    Separable and linear equations. The integrating factor. When solutions exist, when they blow up.
  • 2. The Cauchy-Lipschitz Theorem
    Existence and uniqueness. The Lipschitz condition, Picard iteration, and the constructive proof.
  • 3. Gronwall's Inequality
    The universal growth bound. If $u \leq \alpha + \beta\int u$, then $u \leq \alpha e^{\beta t}$. Continuous dependence on initial data.
  • 4. Linear ODE Solution Structure
    Solutions form a vector space. The Wronskian, Abel's identity, constant-coefficient equations via the characteristic polynomial.
  • 5. Variation of Parameters
    Lagrange's method for non-homogeneous equations. Let the constants vary — the Wronskian does the rest.
  • 6. Systems and the Matrix Exponential
    $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}(0)$. Exponentiating a matrix via its power series. Diagonalisation and Jordan form compute it.
  • 7. Stability and Lyapunov's Method
    Prove stability without solving the ODE. Energy-like functions, linearisation, eigenvalue criteria. The stable manifold.
  • 8. The Poincaré-Bendixson Theorem
    In 2D, bounded trajectories either settle or orbit. No chaos below three dimensions. Phase 8 recap.

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces

By the late 19th century, mathematicians were working with function spaces, probability distributions, and stranger "spaces" than ℝ&sup n;. The properties of ℝ that make analysis work — completeness, compactness — needed to be abstracted. What is the minimal structure needed?

  • 1. Metric Spaces
    Fréchet asks: what is distance? Three axioms abstract $|x-y|$ to any set. Convergence in function spaces.
  • 2. Open and Closed Sets
    Open sets give breathing room, closed sets hold their limit points. Interior, closure, boundary. The topology of a metric space.
  • 3. Completeness in Metric Spaces
    Cauchy sequences with nowhere to escape. $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{R}^n$, $\mathcal{C}([a,b])$ are complete; $\mathbb{Q}$ is not. Completion.
  • 4. Compactness
    Heine-Borel: compact $\Leftrightarrow$ closed + bounded in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Sequential compactness. The next best thing to finiteness.
  • 5. Continuity on Compact Sets
    EVT generalised, Heine's uniform continuity theorem. On compact sets, continuous functions cannot misbehave.
  • 6. The Banach Fixed Point Theorem
    A contraction on a complete space has exactly one fixed point. The engine behind Picard iteration and Newton's method.
  • 7. Baire Category and Arzelà-Ascoli
    Complete spaces are topologically large (Baire). Equicontinuity is the missing ingredient for compactness in function spaces. Phase 9 recap.

Phase 10 — Abstract Algebra: Groups, Rings, Polynomials

Galois (1832, written the night before his duel at age 20) discovered that whether a polynomial equation can be solved by radicals depends entirely on the symmetries of its roots. This transformed algebra from "find the answer" to "study the structure."

  • 1. Groups
    Lagrange permutes roots. Closure, associativity, identity, inverses — the algebraic structure of symmetry.
  • 2. Subgroups and Lagrange's Theorem
    Cosets tile the group perfectly. Subgroup order divides group order. Fermat's little theorem recovered.
  • 3. Homomorphisms and the First Isomorphism Theorem
    Structure-preserving maps. Kernel, image, normal subgroups, quotient groups. $G/\ker(\varphi) \cong \operatorname{im}(\varphi)$.
  • 4. Cayley's Theorem and the Sylow Theorems
    Every group is a permutation group. Prime-power subgroups always exist. The twin pillars of finite group structure.
  • 5. Classification of Finitely Generated Abelian Groups
    Every finitely generated abelian group is a direct product of cyclic groups. The structure theorem.
  • 6. Rings and Polynomial Division
    Two operations, one structure. Euclidean division for polynomials mirrors integer division. $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \mathbb{C}$.
  • 7. Irreducibility and the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
    Eisenstein detects irreducibility over $\mathbb{Q}$. Over $\mathbb{C}$, every polynomial splits — Gauss's FTA.
  • 8. Galois Theory and Abel-Ruffini
    The quintic has no formula because $S_5$ is not solvable. Symmetry of roots controls solvability by radicals. Phase 10 recap.
