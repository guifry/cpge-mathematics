Hybrid Curriculum — Bourbaki rigour, historical order

Every concept motivated by the problem that created it. Every tool proved before being used. No black boxes.

Phase 0 — Geometry and Measurement

Where humanity started. You want to measure the world — lengths, areas, angles, the height of a building from its shadow. You need tools.

  • 1. Similar Triangles and Measurement
    Thales measures the Great Pyramid. Same angles → same ratios. The foundation of trigonometry.
  • 2. The Pythagorean Theorem
    $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Two proofs. Why it's about areas, not algebra. The diagonal of a unit square.
  • 3. $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational — The First Crisis
    A length you can draw but can't write as a fraction. The rationals have holes. Why $\mathbb{R}$ was invented.
  • 4. Trigonometry from Scratch
    Hipparchus needs the distance to the Moon. Sine and cosine as angle-to-ratio converters. Built on similar triangles.
  • 5. The Unit Circle
    Extending trig beyond right triangles. $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ is the Pythagorean theorem on a circle. Proves $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$.
  • 6. Coordinate Geometry — Algebra Meets Geometry
    Descartes turns points into numbers and curves into equations. Makes calculus possible.

Phase 1 — Number Theory and Arithmetic

The Pythagoreans worshipped whole numbers. They discovered primes, divisibility, and the structure hidden in the integers. The oldest mathematics — counting, dividing, and the atoms of arithmetic.

  • 1. Divisibility and the Euclidean Algorithm
    Euclid needs the largest common measure of two lengths. The division algorithm, GCD, Bézout's identity, and the LCM.
  • 2. Prime Numbers and the Fundamental Theorem
    The atoms of arithmetic. Sieve of Eratosthenes, Euclid's lemma, infinitely many primes, unique factorisation.
  • 3. Modular Arithmetic
    Clock arithmetic made rigorous. Congruence, residue classes, inverses via Bézout, $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ as a field.
  • 4. Fermat's Little Theorem
    A pattern in powers mod $p$. The permutation proof, fast exponentiation, and the trap of Carmichael numbers.
  • 5. Euler's Totient Function
    Counting invertible residues. $\varphi(n)$, the prime power formula, Euler's theorem generalising Fermat, and a glimpse of RSA.
  • 6. The Chinese Remainder Theorem
    Sun Tzu's counting puzzle. Simultaneous congruences, constructive proof, and the payback — $\varphi$ is multiplicative.
  • 7. Diophantine Equations
    Integer solutions only. Linear Diophantine equations, existence via GCD, all solutions parametrised. Pythagorean triples. Phase 1 recap.

Phase 2 — The Infinite: Limits and Basic Convergence

Archimedes wanted to compute the area of a circle. He trapped it between polygons with more and more sides. The area is what the polygon areas "approach." But what does "approach" mean, precisely?

  • 1. Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$
    The rationals have holes. The supremum axiom fills them. Why $\mathbb{R}$ exists and $\mathbb{Q}$ doesn't suffice.
  • 2. The Squeeze Theorem
    Archimedes traps $\pi$ between polygons. If two sequences squeeze a third, it converges to their shared limit.
  • 3. Monotone Convergence Theorem
    The Babylonian method for $\sqrt{2}$. A bounded, monotone sequence must converge — completeness in action.
  • 4. Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem
    Every bounded sequence hides a convergent subsequence. The bisection argument.
  • 5. The Cauchy Criterion
    Convergence without knowing the limit. Terms getting closer to each other — the internal test.
  • 6. Intermediate Value Theorem
    A continuous function that's negative then positive must cross zero. Continuity defined, bisection proof.
  • 7. Dense Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$
    $\mathbb{Q}$ is everywhere dense yet full of holes. Phase 2 recap: the architecture of the real line.

Phase 3 — Calculus: Derivatives and Integration

Fermat wanted to find where a curve peaks. Newton and Leibniz wanted to compute areas under curves. The derivative and the integral — two seemingly unrelated ideas — turn out to be inverse operations. Built from scratch, every rule proved.

  • 1. The Derivative
    Fermat's tangent problem. The slope of a curve at a point, defined as the limit of secant slopes. Power rule proved.
  • 2. Differentiation Rules
    Sum, product, quotient, chain rule — each proved from the limit definition. No memorisation without proof.
  • 3. Derivatives of Sine and Cosine
    The geometric squeeze: $\sin(h)/h \to 1$. Then $d/dx \sin x = \cos x$ from the definition. Phase 0 meets Phase 2.
  • 4. The Mean Value Theorem
    If you drove 200 km in 2 hours, your speed was exactly 100 km/h at some instant. Local → global.
  • 5. L'Hôpital's Rule
    When $0/0$ blocks you, compare rates of vanishing. Proved from Cauchy's generalised MVT.
  • 6. The Riemann Integral
    Archimedes' area problem formalised. Partitions, upper and lower sums, the definition from scratch.
  • 7. Integrability and Properties
    Continuous on a closed interval means integrable. Uniform continuity, linearity, monotonicity of the integral.
  • 8. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
    The central result. Differentiation and integration are inverse operations. Both parts proved.
  • 9. Integration Techniques
    Substitution = chain rule backwards. Integration by parts = product rule backwards. Phase 3 recap.
