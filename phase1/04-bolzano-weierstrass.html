The Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem

Clustering in Bounded Sets — Phase 1, Page 4

The problem

The problem

Consider the sequence $a_n = \sin(n)$. Compute a few terms: $\sin(1) \approx 0.841$, $\sin(2) \approx 0.909$, $\sin(3) \approx 0.141$, $\sin(4) \approx -0.757$, $\sin(5) \approx -0.959$, $\sin(6) \approx -0.279$, $\sin(7) \approx 0.657\ldots$ The sequence bounces around inside $[-1, 1]$ without ever settling down. It doesn't converge.

But it's bounded. Every term lives in $[-1, 1]$. And there are infinitely many terms crammed into this finite interval. Surely some of those terms must cluster near a particular value?

The question: even though the whole sequence refuses to converge, can we cherry-pick terms from it — keeping their original order — to form a new sequence that does converge?

Subsequences

Before stating the theorem, we need a precise definition. A subsequence of $(a_n)$ is formed by choosing an infinite collection of indices $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$ and taking the terms $a_{n_1}, a_{n_2}, a_{n_3}, \ldots$ You keep the original order. You skip terms. But you never go backwards, and you never stop — a subsequence must be infinite.

We write this subsequence as $(a_{n_k})_{k \geq 1}$, where the map $k \mapsto n_k$ is strictly increasing.

Example. From $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, \ldots$ take only the even-indexed terms: $a_2, a_4, a_6, \ldots$ Here $n_k = 2k$. Or take every tenth term: $n_k = 10k$. Or take primes: $n_1 = 2, n_2 = 3, n_3 = 5, n_4 = 7, \ldots$ All valid subsequences.

What is not a subsequence: picking $a_5, a_3, a_7$ (indices aren't increasing), or picking finitely many terms (a subsequence must be infinite).

Back to $\sin(n)$: we want to find indices $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$ such that $\sin(n_k)$ converges to some limit $L$ as $k \to \infty$. Can we always do this for any bounded sequence?

Theorem (Bolzano-Weierstrass)

Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ has a convergent subsequence.

Historical note

Bolzano proved this in 1817, but not because he cared about subsequences. He was trying to give the first rigorous proof of the Intermediate Value Theorem (if a continuous function goes from negative to positive, it must cross zero). His proof required a lemma: if infinitely many points are packed into a bounded interval, at least some must cluster together. He proved the lemma — and it turned out to be far more important than the IVT itself.

Weierstrass, decades later, generalised it, taught it in his Berlin lectures, and gave it the central place it holds today. The theorem they were after (IVT) became a corollary. The tool (Bolzano-Weierstrass) became a pillar of analysis.

The proof: bisection

This is one of the most elegant constructions in analysis. It's also fully constructive — it tells you exactly how to build the convergent subsequence, step by step.

Proof

Let $(a_n)$ be a bounded sequence. Since it's bounded, all terms sit inside some interval $[a, b]$.

Step 1. First bisection. Cut $[a, b]$ into two halves: $\bigl[a, \frac{a+b}{2}\bigr]$ and $\bigl[\frac{a+b}{2}, b\bigr]$. The sequence has infinitely many terms in $[a, b]$. At least one of the two halves must contain infinitely many terms. (If both halves had only finitely many, the total would be finite — contradiction.) Pick a half that contains infinitely many terms. Call it $[a_1, b_1]$. Choose any term of the sequence that lies in $[a_1, b_1]$; call it $a_{n_1}$.

Step 2. Second bisection. Cut $[a_1, b_1]$ in half. Again, at least one half contains infinitely many terms of the sequence. Pick that half; call it $[a_2, b_2]$. Now choose a term $a_{n_2}$ that lies in $[a_2, b_2]$ with $n_2 > n_1$. We can always find such a term because $[a_2, b_2]$ contains infinitely many terms — so in particular, it contains terms with arbitrarily large indices.

Step $k$. Repeat. At stage $k$, we have a nested interval $[a_k, b_k]$ of length $\frac{b - a}{2^k}$, and we've selected a term $a_{n_k}$ inside it with $n_k > n_{k-1}$.

What have we built? Two things:

  1. A sequence of nested intervals $[a_1, b_1] \supset [a_2, b_2] \supset [a_3, b_3] \supset \cdots$ with lengths $b_k - a_k = \frac{b-a}{2^k} \to 0$.
  2. A subsequence $(a_{n_k})$ with $a_{n_k} \in [a_k, b_k]$ for every $k$, and $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$

Convergence. The left endpoints $a_1 \leq a_2 \leq a_3 \leq \cdots$ form an increasing sequence (each bisection can only move the left endpoint rightward or keep it). They're bounded above by $b$. So by the Monotone Convergence Theorem (page 03), the sequence $(a_k)$ converges to some limit $L$.

Similarly, $b_k = a_k + \frac{b-a}{2^k}$, so $b_k \to L + 0 = L$.

Now: for every $k$, the subsequence term $a_{n_k}$ satisfies $a_k \leq a_{n_k} \leq b_k$. Both $a_k \to L$ and $b_k \to L$. By the Squeeze Theorem (page 02):

$$a_{n_k} \to L$$

The subsequence converges.

∎

Unpacking the proof

Notice what happened. The proof used two results from previous pages: the Monotone Convergence Theorem (to show the left endpoints converge) and the Squeeze Theorem (to trap the subsequence between converging bounds). Bolzano-Weierstrass isn't conjured from nothing — it's built on the machinery we've already established.

The engine of the proof is the pigeonhole step: when you cut an interval containing infinitely many terms into two halves, at least one half must still contain infinitely many terms. You can't split infinity into two finite pieces. This simple observation is what lets the construction continue forever — at every stage, there are enough terms left to pick from.

The bisection halves the interval each time. After $k$ steps, the interval has length $(b-a)/2^k$, which marches toward zero. The subsequence terms are trapped in ever-shrinking intervals, forced to converge whether they like it or not.

Back to $\sin(n)$

The theorem guarantees that $(\sin(n))$ has a convergent subsequence. It doesn't tell us which indices to pick — finding them explicitly is surprisingly hard. It involves the distribution of integers modulo $2\pi$, which is governed by the irrationality of $\pi$ and Weyl's equidistribution theorem. The terms $\sin(n)$ are in fact dense in $[-1, 1]$: for any target value $L \in [-1, 1]$, you can find a subsequence converging to it. Bolzano-Weierstrass says at least one such subsequence exists; the equidistribution theory says every $L \in [-1, 1]$ works.

But we didn't need any of that machinery. The proof only needed boundedness.

A simpler example

Take $a_n = (-1)^n$, i.e. $-1, 1, -1, 1, -1, 1, \ldots$ This is bounded (by $[-1, 1]$) and doesn't converge (it oscillates forever).

Subsequences:

  • Odd terms: $a_1, a_3, a_5, \ldots = -1, -1, -1, \ldots \to -1$
  • Even terms: $a_2, a_4, a_6, \ldots = 1, 1, 1, \ldots \to 1$

Two different convergent subsequences with two different limits. Bolzano-Weierstrass guarantees at least one; there could be many. In fact, the set of all subsequential limits of a bounded sequence is always closed and non-empty — a deeper result you'll meet later.

What breaks

Remove "bounded"

Take $a_n = n$: $1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots$ Every subsequence still diverges to $+\infty$. More precisely, if $n_{k+1} > n_k$ then $a_{n_k} = n_k \geq k$, so $a_{n_k} \to \infty$. No convergent subsequence exists. Boundedness is essential.

Work in $\mathbb{Q}$ instead of $\mathbb{R}$

Consider the decimal approximations of $\sqrt{2}$: $1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, 1.4142, \ldots$ Every term is rational. The sequence is bounded (all terms in $[1, 2]$). It converges — but its limit is $\sqrt{2}$, which is not in $\mathbb{Q}$. If we demand the subsequence converge to a rational limit, the theorem fails. A bounded sequence of rationals can have only irrational cluster points.

Bolzano-Weierstrass relies on the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ (page 01). The bisection proof invokes the Monotone Convergence Theorem, which itself depends on completeness. Pull out completeness, and the whole construction collapses.

Why this matters

Bolzano-Weierstrass is the concrete version of compactness. In $\mathbb{R}$, it says: bounded closed sets have the "clustering property" — any sequence inside them has a convergent subsequence whose limit is also in the set. In Phase 8 (topology), this becomes the abstract definition of sequential compactness. But the idea is born right here, in this bisection argument.

Practically, BW is the workhorse behind dozens of existence theorems. The Extreme Value Theorem (a continuous function on $[a, b]$ attains its maximum) uses it. The Arzelà-Ascoli theorem (compactness in function spaces) generalises it. Every time you need to show that some convergent subsequence exists — without exhibiting it explicitly — Bolzano-Weierstrass is the tool.

Cram infinitely many points into a bounded interval and some must cluster together — you can always extract a convergent subsequence.
