Diophantine Equations

Phase 1 — Number Theory, Page 7

The problem

Diophantus of Alexandria (3rd century AD) studied equations where only integer solutions count. The equation $3x + 5y = 1$ has the real solution $x = 0$, $y = 0.2$ — but that's no good if $x$ and $y$ must be whole numbers. It also has $x = 2$, $y = -1$ — that works.

The question: when does an equation have integer solutions, how do you find them, and how many are there? This is a fundamentally different challenge from real-valued algebra. Over $\mathbb{R}$, a single linear equation in two unknowns always has infinitely many solutions (a line). Over $\mathbb{Z}$, it might have none at all.

1. The linear Diophantine equation

A linear Diophantine equation is an equation of the form

$$ax + by = c$$

where $a$, $b$, $c$ are given integers and we seek integer solutions $(x, y) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$. The word "linear" means the unknowns appear to the first power only. The word "Diophantine" means we demand integer solutions.

Over $\mathbb{R}$, this equation describes a line, and any point on that line is a solution. Over $\mathbb{Z}$, we only want the lattice points — the points with integer coordinates that happen to lie on that line. There might be none, or infinitely many, but never just a finite number (we'll see why shortly).

2. When do solutions exist?

The answer turns out to be a clean divisibility condition — exactly the kind of criterion that Bezout's identity (page 1) was built for.

Theorem (Existence criterion)

The equation $ax + by = c$ has integer solutions if and only if $\gcd(a, b) \mid c$.

Proof

Necessity ($\Rightarrow$). Suppose $(x_0, y_0)$ is an integer solution, so $ax_0 + by_0 = c$. Let $d = \gcd(a, b)$. Since $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$, we have $d \mid (ax_0 + by_0)$, so $d \mid c$.

Sufficiency ($\Leftarrow$). Suppose $d \mid c$. By Bezout's identity (page 1), there exist integers $u, v$ with $au + bv = d$. Multiply both sides by $c/d$ (which is an integer since $d \mid c$):

$$a\!\left(\frac{c}{d} u\right) + b\!\left(\frac{c}{d} v\right) = c$$

So $(x_0, y_0) = (cu/d, \, cv/d)$ is an integer solution.

∎

Example. Consider $6x + 10y = 8$. We have $\gcd(6, 10) = 2$ and $2 \mid 8$, so solutions exist. But $6x + 10y = 7$ has no solutions, because $2 \nmid 7$. No amount of searching will find one — the divisibility structure of the integers prohibits it.

3. Finding all solutions

The existence theorem tells us whether solutions exist. The next theorem tells us what they all look like. The answer is a one-parameter family — once you have one solution, you get all of them by sliding along the line in steps dictated by $a$ and $b$.

Theorem (General solution)

If $(x_0, y_0)$ is one particular solution of $ax + by = c$, and $d = \gcd(a, b)$, then all integer solutions are given by

$$x = x_0 + \frac{b}{d}\,t, \qquad y = y_0 - \frac{a}{d}\,t \qquad (t \in \mathbb{Z})$$
Proof

Step 1. These are indeed solutions. Substitute into $ax + by$:

$$a\!\left(x_0 + \frac{b}{d}t\right) + b\!\left(y_0 - \frac{a}{d}t\right) = ax_0 + by_0 + \frac{ab}{d}t - \frac{ab}{d}t = c$$

So every $(x_0 + (b/d)t, \; y_0 - (a/d)t)$ is a solution, for any $t \in \mathbb{Z}$.

Step 2. These are all solutions. Suppose $(x_1, y_1)$ is any other integer solution. Then $ax_1 + by_1 = c$ and $ax_0 + by_0 = c$. Subtract:

$$a(x_1 - x_0) + b(y_1 - y_0) = 0$$

Divide through by $d$:

$$\frac{a}{d}(x_1 - x_0) = -\frac{b}{d}(y_1 - y_0)$$

Now $\gcd(a/d, \, b/d) = 1$ (we divided out the full GCD). So $(b/d) \mid (x_1 - x_0)$ by Gauss's lemma (page 2): since $b/d$ divides the product $(a/d)(x_1 - x_0)$ and is coprime to $a/d$, it must divide $x_1 - x_0$.

Write $x_1 - x_0 = (b/d)t$ for some integer $t$. Then from the equation above, $(a/d)(b/d)t = -(b/d)(y_1 - y_0)$, so $y_1 - y_0 = -(a/d)t$. This gives $x_1 = x_0 + (b/d)t$ and $y_1 = y_0 - (a/d)t$, which is exactly the parametric family.

∎

The key insight: solutions form an infinite lattice along the line, spaced at intervals of $(b/d, -a/d)$. There are either zero solutions (when $d \nmid c$) or infinitely many (when $d \mid c$). Never a finite nonzero number.

4. Worked example

Solve $252x + 105y = 21$.

Step 1: check existence. Run the Euclidean algorithm (page 1):

$$252 = 2 \times 105 + 42$$
$$105 = 2 \times 42 + 21$$
$$42 = 2 \times 21 + 0$$

So $\gcd(252, 105) = 21$. Since $21 \mid 21$, solutions exist.

Step 2: find one solution via back-substitution. From the algorithm:

$$21 = 105 - 2 \times 42 = 105 - 2(252 - 2 \times 105) = 5 \times 105 - 2 \times 252$$

So $252 \times (-2) + 105 \times 5 = 21$. One solution is $(x_0, y_0) = (-2, 5)$.

Step 3: write the general solution. With $d = 21$, $b/d = 105/21 = 5$, $a/d = 252/21 = 12$:

$$x = -2 + 5t, \qquad y = 5 - 12t \qquad (t \in \mathbb{Z})$$

Step 4: find specific solutions. A few values:

$t$$x$$y$
$-1$$-7$$17$
$0$$-2$$5$
$1$$3$$-7$
$2$$8$$-19$

The smallest positive $x$ is $x = 3$ (at $t = 1$), giving $(3, -7)$. Check: $252 \times 3 + 105 \times (-7) = 756 - 735 = 21$. Correct.

5. Pythagorean triples

Linear Diophantine equations are the simplest case. What about nonlinear ones? The most famous is

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

where we seek positive integer solutions $(a, b, c)$. These are called Pythagorean triples — they correspond to right triangles with integer side lengths. You know $(3, 4, 5)$ and $(5, 12, 13)$. Are there infinitely many? Can we find them all?

A triple is primitive if $\gcd(a, b, c) = 1$. Every Pythagorean triple is a multiple of a primitive one, so it suffices to classify the primitive triples.

Theorem (Parametrisation of primitive Pythagorean triples)

Every primitive Pythagorean triple (with $b$ even) is of the form

$$a = m^2 - n^2, \qquad b = 2mn, \qquad c = m^2 + n^2$$

where $m > n > 0$, $\gcd(m, n) = 1$, and $m - n$ is odd.

Proof (derivation)

Step 1: parity. In a primitive triple, $a$ and $b$ cannot both be even (then $2 \mid \gcd(a, b, c)$). They cannot both be odd either: if $a$ and $b$ are both odd, then $a^2 + b^2 \equiv 1 + 1 = 2 \pmod{4}$, but every perfect square is $\equiv 0$ or $1 \pmod{4}$, so $c^2 \not\equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ — contradiction. So exactly one of $a, b$ is even. Assume $b$ is even (relabelling if needed).

Step 2: factorise. Since $b$ is even, write $b = 2k$. Then $c^2 - a^2 = b^2 = 4k^2$, so $(c - a)(c + a) = 4k^2$. Since $a$ and $c$ are both odd, $c - a$ and $c + a$ are both even. Write $c - a = 2s$ and $c + a = 2r$ where $r > s > 0$. Then $4sr = 4k^2$, so $sr = k^2$.

Step 3: $\gcd(r, s) = 1$. Any common divisor of $r$ and $s$ divides $r + s = c$ and $r - s = a$, hence divides $\gcd(a, c)$. But any prime $p \mid \gcd(a, c)$ also divides $c^2 - a^2 = b^2$, so $p \mid b$, contradicting $\gcd(a, b, c) = 1$. Therefore $\gcd(r, s) = 1$.

Step 4: both factors are perfect squares. We have $sr = k^2$ with $\gcd(s, r) = 1$. By unique factorisation (page 2), since the product is a perfect square and the factors are coprime, each factor must individually be a perfect square. Write $s = n^2$ and $r = m^2$ for positive integers $m > n$ with $\gcd(m, n) = 1$.

Step 5: extract $a, b, c$. From $c - a = 2n^2$ and $c + a = 2m^2$:

$$a = m^2 - n^2, \qquad c = m^2 + n^2, \qquad b = 2k = 2mn$$

(since $k^2 = sr = m^2 n^2$, so $k = mn$). Finally, $m - n$ must be odd: if $m$ and $n$ had the same parity, then $a = m^2 - n^2 = (m-n)(m+n)$ would be even (both factors even), contradicting $a$ odd.

Conversely, any $m > n > 0$ with $\gcd(m, n) = 1$ and $m - n$ odd produces a primitive triple. (Checking: $a^2 + b^2 = (m^2 - n^2)^2 + 4m^2n^2 = m^4 - 2m^2n^2 + n^4 + 4m^2n^2 = m^4 + 2m^2n^2 + n^4 = (m^2 + n^2)^2 = c^2$.)

∎

A few primitive triples from this formula:

$m$$n$$a$$b$$c$
$2$$1$$3$$4$$5$
$3$$2$$5$$12$$13$
$4$$1$$15$$8$$17$
$4$$3$$7$$24$$25$

This connects back to Phase 0: the Pythagorean theorem tells us these triples exist geometrically, and number theory classifies them completely. Every right triangle with integer sides comes from a pair $(m, n)$.

6. Phase 1 recap

This completes Phase 1. Here is what you now have from number theory:

  • Division algorithm and Euclidean algorithm (page 1) — computing GCDs efficiently
  • Bezout's identity (page 1) — $\gcd(a, b)$ is a linear combination of $a$ and $b$
  • Primes and unique factorisation (page 2) — the fundamental theorem of arithmetic
  • Modular arithmetic and inverses (page 3) — arithmetic modulo $n$, when inverses exist
  • Fermat's little theorem (page 4) — $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$
  • Euler's totient and theorem (page 5) — $a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$
  • Chinese Remainder Theorem (page 6) — solving systems of congruences
  • Diophantine equations (this page) — integer solutions to $ax + by = c$, Pythagorean triples

These tools reappear throughout mathematics: Bezout and CRT in algebra (Phase 10), modular arithmetic in cryptography, Fermat and Euler in computational number theory. The Pythagorean triple parametrisation is a first taste of how number theory classifies integer solutions to polynomial equations — a thread that leads to elliptic curves and ultimately to Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem.

Historical note

Diophantus of Alexandria (circa 250 AD) authored the Arithmetica, a collection of 130 problems asking for rational (not just integer) solutions to polynomial equations. Despite this, the tradition named after him focuses on integer solutions. Only six of the original thirteen books survive.

The most consequential margin note in mathematical history was written in a copy of Arithmetica. Around 1637, Pierre de Fermat scribbled next to the equation $x^n + y^n = z^n$: "I have discovered a truly marvellous proof of this proposition, which this margin is too narrow to contain." The claim — that there are no positive integer solutions for $n \geq 3$ — became Fermat's Last Theorem, and resisted proof for 358 years until Andrew Wiles settled it in 1995 using the modularity theorem for elliptic curves. Diophantus could not have imagined the depth of the subject his textbook problems would spawn.

What breaks

If $\gcd(a, b) \nmid c$: the equation $ax + by = c$ is literally impossible. This isn't "we haven't looked hard enough" — the divisibility structure of the integers prohibits any solution. Every integer combination of $a$ and $b$ is a multiple of $\gcd(a, b)$, and $c$ isn't one. The sharpest form of "no solution exists."

If we allow rationals instead of integers: the problem becomes trivial. $ax + by = c$ always has rational solutions (just set $x = c/a$, $y = 0$, assuming $a \neq 0$). Similarly, Pythagorean triples over $\mathbb{Q}$ correspond to rational points on the unit circle $X^2 + Y^2 = 1$, which are easy to parametrise (draw a line from $(-1, 0)$ with rational slope). The integer constraint is what creates the deep structure. Integers are harder than rationals.

$ax + by = c$ has integer solutions exactly when $\gcd(a, b)$ divides $c$ — Bezout turns GCD theory into a complete solvability criterion for linear equations in whole numbers.
