Divisibility and the Euclidean Algorithm

Phase 1 — Number Theory, Page 1

The problem

You have 1230 soldiers and want to arrange them in equal rows. How many per row? Which arrangements work? 10 rows of 123? 6 rows of 205? 7 rows of... something? Try dividing 1230 by 7 and you get 175 with 5 left over. Seven doesn't work.

The Egyptians and Babylonians faced this constantly: dividing land into equal plots, distributing grain among households, fitting days into calendar cycles. The question is ancient and concrete: when does one number divide another evenly, and what happens when it doesn't?

1. The division algorithm

Before we can talk about divisibility, we need a precise statement of what happens when you divide one integer by another. You get a quotient and a remainder, and both are unique. This is so fundamental it gets its own name.

Theorem (Division algorithm)

For any integers $a$ and $b$ with $b > 0$, there exist unique integers $q$ (the quotient) and $r$ (the remainder) such that

$$a = bq + r \quad \text{and} \quad 0 \leq r < b.$$

Unpack this. The theorem says two things: these integers exist, and they are the only pair that works. The condition $0 \leq r < b$ is what pins them down. Without it, you could write $17 = 5 \cdot 2 + 7$ or $17 = 5 \cdot 4 + (-3)$ — multiple representations. Forcing $r$ into $\{0, 1, \ldots, b-1\}$ makes the decomposition unique.

Proof

Existence. Consider the set $S = \{a - bk : k \in \mathbb{Z},\; a - bk \geq 0\}$. This is the set of all non-negative values you can get by subtracting multiples of $b$ from $a$. The set $S$ is non-empty: if $a \geq 0$, take $k = 0$ to get $a \in S$; if $a < 0$, take $k = a$ (so $a - ba = a(1 - b) \geq 0$ since $b \geq 1$ and $a < 0$).

Since $S$ is a non-empty set of non-negative integers, the well-ordering principle guarantees it has a smallest element. Call it $r = a - bq$ for the corresponding $k = q$. By construction, $r \geq 0$.

We need $r < b$. Suppose for contradiction that $r \geq b$. Then $r - b = a - bq - b = a - b(q+1) \geq 0$, so $r - b \in S$. But $r - b < r$, contradicting the fact that $r$ is the smallest element of $S$. Therefore $r < b$.

Uniqueness. Suppose $a = bq_1 + r_1$ and $a = bq_2 + r_2$ with $0 \leq r_1, r_2 < b$. Then $bq_1 + r_1 = bq_2 + r_2$, so $b(q_1 - q_2) = r_2 - r_1$. The left side is a multiple of $b$. The right side satisfies $|r_2 - r_1| < b$ (since both remainders lie in $\{0, \ldots, b-1\}$). The only multiple of $b$ with absolute value less than $b$ is $0$. So $r_1 = r_2$ and $q_1 = q_2$.

∎

The key observation: $b$ divides $a$ evenly precisely when $r = 0$, i.e. when $a = bq$ with nothing left over.

2. Divisibility

We say $b$ divides $a$, written $b \mid a$, if there exists an integer $k$ such that $a = bk$. Equivalently, dividing $a$ by $b$ leaves remainder $0$. We also say $b$ is a divisor or factor of $a$, and that $a$ is a multiple of $b$.

Examples: $3 \mid 12$ (since $12 = 3 \cdot 4$), $7 \mid 0$ (since $0 = 7 \cdot 0$), $1 \mid n$ for every integer $n$, and $5 \nmid 17$ (since $17 = 5 \cdot 3 + 2$, remainder $\neq 0$).

Divisibility behaves well under addition and linear combinations:

Proposition (Linearity of divisibility)

If $b \mid a$ and $b \mid c$, then for any integers $m, n$:

$$b \mid (ma + nc).$$

In particular, $b \mid (a + c)$ and $b \mid (a - c)$.

Proof

Since $b \mid a$, write $a = bj$ for some integer $j$. Since $b \mid c$, write $c = bk$ for some integer $k$. Then

$$ma + nc = m(bj) + n(bk) = b(mj + nk).$$

Since $mj + nk$ is an integer, $b \mid (ma + nc)$.

∎

This "linearity" property is the engine behind almost everything that follows. It says the set of multiples of $b$ is closed under integer linear combinations.

3. Greatest common divisor

Given two integers $a$ and $b$ (not both zero), their greatest common divisor $\gcd(a, b)$ is the largest positive integer that divides both $a$ and $b$.

Why does this exist? The set of common divisors of $a$ and $b$ is non-empty ($1$ divides everything) and bounded above by $\min(|a|, |b|)$ when both are nonzero. A non-empty bounded set of positive integers has a maximum. So $\gcd(a, b)$ is well-defined.

Examples: $\gcd(12, 18) = 6$ (common divisors: $1, 2, 3, 6$), and $\gcd(7, 15) = 1$ (no common factor besides $1$). When $\gcd(a, b) = 1$, we say $a$ and $b$ are coprime.

Computing the GCD by listing all divisors works for small numbers. For large ones, you need something better. Euclid found something much better, over 2300 years ago.

4. The Euclidean algorithm

The crucial insight: if $a = bq + r$ (the division algorithm), then $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, r)$. You can replace the problem with a smaller one, and repeat.

Lemma

If $a = bq + r$, then $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, r)$.

Proof

Let $d$ be any common divisor of $a$ and $b$. Then $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$, so by linearity of divisibility, $d \mid (a - bq) = r$. So $d$ also divides $r$. Every common divisor of $a$ and $b$ is a common divisor of $b$ and $r$.

Conversely, let $d$ be any common divisor of $b$ and $r$. Then $d \mid b$ and $d \mid r$, so $d \mid (bq + r) = a$. Every common divisor of $b$ and $r$ is a common divisor of $a$ and $b$.

The two sets of common divisors are identical. Their maxima are therefore equal: $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, r)$.

∎

Now iterate. Apply the division algorithm repeatedly, replacing $a$ with $b$ and $b$ with $r$ each time, until the remainder is $0$. The last nonzero remainder is the GCD.

Full worked example: $\gcd(252, 105)$.

$$252 = 105 \cdot 2 + 42$$
$$105 = 42 \cdot 2 + 21$$
$$42 = 21 \cdot 2 + 0$$

The last nonzero remainder is $21$, so $\gcd(252, 105) = 21$.

Why does it terminate? The remainders form a strictly decreasing sequence of non-negative integers: $42 > 21 > 0$. A strictly decreasing sequence of non-negative integers must reach $0$ in finitely many steps (again, the well-ordering principle).

Why does it give the GCD? By the lemma, each step preserves the GCD: $\gcd(252, 105) = \gcd(105, 42) = \gcd(42, 21) = \gcd(21, 0) = 21$. The last equality holds because every integer divides $0$, so $\gcd(d, 0) = d$.

5. Bezout's identity

Here is the deepest result on this page. The GCD of $a$ and $b$ can always be written as an integer linear combination of $a$ and $b$. Always.

Theorem (Bezout's identity)

For any integers $a$ and $b$ (not both zero), there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that

$$ax + by = \gcd(a, b).$$
Proof

Run the Euclidean algorithm, then substitute backwards. We demonstrate with our example $\gcd(252, 105) = 21$.

Forward pass (Euclidean algorithm):

$$252 = 105 \cdot 2 + 42 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 42 = 252 - 105 \cdot 2$$
$$105 = 42 \cdot 2 + 21 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 21 = 105 - 42 \cdot 2$$

Back-substitution: Start from the last equation giving the GCD.

$$21 = 105 - 42 \cdot 2$$

Substitute $42 = 252 - 105 \cdot 2$ from the first equation:

$$21 = 105 - (252 - 105 \cdot 2) \cdot 2$$
$$21 = 105 - 252 \cdot 2 + 105 \cdot 4$$
$$21 = 105 \cdot 5 + 252 \cdot (-2)$$

So $21 = 252 \cdot (-2) + 105 \cdot 5$, giving $x = -2$ and $y = 5$.

General argument. The back-substitution always works because each remainder in the Euclidean algorithm is an integer linear combination of the two remainders from the previous step ($r_i = r_{i-2} - r_{i-1} q_i$). Working backwards, each remainder is a linear combination of the original $a$ and $b$. The last nonzero remainder is $\gcd(a, b)$, so it too is a linear combination of $a$ and $b$.

∎

Let's verify: $252 \cdot (-2) + 105 \cdot 5 = -504 + 525 = 21$. It works.

Why this matters so much. Bezout's identity says the GCD isn't just a number you can compute — it's a number you can build from $a$ and $b$ using only addition, subtraction, and multiplication by integers. This constructive property is what makes it so powerful. Nearly every result in the rest of this phase (Euclid's lemma, the fundamental theorem of arithmetic, Fermat's little theorem, the Chinese remainder theorem) ultimately rests on Bezout.

6. Least common multiple

The least common multiple of $a$ and $b$, written $\operatorname{lcm}(a, b)$, is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both $a$ and $b$.

Proposition

For positive integers $a$ and $b$,

$$\operatorname{lcm}(a, b) = \frac{ab}{\gcd(a, b)}.$$
Proof

Let $d = \gcd(a, b)$ and write $a = da'$, $b = db'$ where $\gcd(a', b') = 1$ (dividing out the GCD leaves coprime factors). Set $L = ab/d = da'b'$.

$L$ is a common multiple. $L = da'b' = a \cdot b'$ (a multiple of $a$) and $L = da'b' = b \cdot a'$ (a multiple of $b$).

$L$ is the least such. Let $m$ be any positive common multiple of $a$ and $b$. Write $m = a \cdot k = da'k$ for some positive integer $k$. Since $b \mid m$, we have $db' \mid da'k$, so $b' \mid a'k$. But $\gcd(a', b') = 1$, so $b' \mid k$ (IOU: this uses Euclid's lemma, which we prove on the next page from Bezout's identity). Write $k = b'j$, giving $m = da'b'j = Lj$. So $L \mid m$, meaning $L \leq m$.

∎

Example: $\operatorname{lcm}(12, 18) = (12 \cdot 18)/6 = 36$.

Historical note

The Euclidean algorithm appears in Book VII of Euclid's Elements (~300 BC), making it one of the oldest algorithms in mathematics — older than the word "algorithm" itself, which derives from al-Khwarizmi (9th century AD). Euclid stated it for line segments, not numbers: repeatedly subtract the shorter from the longer. The numerical version we use is equivalent.

Bezout's identity is named after Etienne Bezout, who published it in 1770. But the result was already implicit in Euclid's work and explicitly stated by Bachet de Meziriac in 1624. The back-substitution method is essentially what Euclid was doing, just without algebraic notation.

What breaks

Without the division algorithm? You can't define divisibility rigorously. The uniqueness of quotient and remainder is what makes "$b$ divides $a$" an unambiguous statement. Without it, the Euclidean algorithm doesn't even make sense — you need the remainder to be well-defined at each step.

Without Bezout's identity? Many critical results collapse. Here is the most important: the characterisation of coprimality. $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ if and only if there exist integers $x, y$ with $ax + by = 1$. The "only if" direction is Bezout. Without it, you cannot prove Euclid's lemma (if $p \mid ab$ and $p$ is prime, then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$). Without Euclid's lemma, you cannot prove unique prime factorisation. Without unique factorisation, vast areas of number theory and algebra are unmoored. Bezout is the load-bearing wall.

The Euclidean algorithm finds the GCD without factoring, and Bezout's identity proves the GCD is always a linear combination of the two numbers.
