Bilinear Forms

Phase 13 — Bilinear Algebra and Spectral Theory, Page 1

The problem

In his Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801), Gauss attacked a deceptively simple question: which integers can be written in the form $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ for integer values of $x$ and $y$? For instance, $x^2 + y^2$ represents 5 (take $x = 1, y = 2$) but not 3. The form $x^2 + 2y^2$ represents 3 (take $x = 1, y = 1$) but not 5.

Gauss realised that the key to classifying these forms was understanding how they transform under changes of variable. If you substitute $x = \alpha X + \beta Y$, $y = \gamma X + \delta Y$ (with $\alpha\delta - \beta\gamma = \pm 1$ to keep integer solutions), the expression $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ becomes $a'X^2 + 2b'XY + c'Y^2$ — a new form with different coefficients but the same representational power.

This transformation is not a property of quadratic expressions alone. It is a question about bilinear maps — functions that are linear in each variable separately. The expression $ax_1y_1 + b(x_1y_2 + x_2y_1) + cx_2y_2$ is linear in $(x_1, x_2)$ for fixed $(y_1, y_2)$ and vice versa. To study quadratic forms properly, we must first understand bilinear forms.

1. Definition of a bilinear form

Let $V$ be a real vector space (we work over $\mathbb{R}$ throughout; the complex case requires sesquilinearity, which we shall not need here). A bilinear form on $V$ is a function $\varphi : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying two families of conditions:

Definition — Bilinear form

A map $\varphi : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ is bilinear if:

Linearity in the first variable: for all $u, u' \in V$, $v \in V$, $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$\varphi(u + u', v) = \varphi(u, v) + \varphi(u', v)$$
$$\varphi(\lambda u, v) = \lambda\,\varphi(u, v)$$

Linearity in the second variable: for all $u \in V$, $v, v' \in V$, $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$\varphi(u, v + v') = \varphi(u, v) + \varphi(u, v')$$
$$\varphi(u, \lambda v) = \lambda\,\varphi(u, v)$$

In short: fix either argument, and $\varphi$ becomes a linear map in the other. The dot product $\langle u, v \rangle = \sum u_i v_i$ from Phase 6 is the prototypical example — it is bilinear, symmetric, and positive definite. But bilinear forms need not be any of these last two things.

2. The matrix of a bilinear form

Let $\mathcal{B} = (e_1, \dots, e_n)$ be a basis of $V$. Any vectors $u, v \in V$ can be written as $u = \sum_i x_i e_i$ and $v = \sum_j y_j e_j$. By bilinearity:

$$\varphi(u, v) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n x_i\, y_j\, \varphi(e_i, e_j)$$

Define the matrix of $\varphi$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$ as the $n \times n$ matrix $M$ with entries $M_{ij} = \varphi(e_i, e_j)$. Then:

Matrix representation
$$\varphi(u, v) = [u]_\mathcal{B}^T \, M \, [v]_\mathcal{B}$$

where $[u]_\mathcal{B}$ is the column vector of coordinates of $u$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$.

Example in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Take the standard basis and $\varphi(u,v) = 2x_1y_1 + 3x_1y_2 - x_2y_1 + x_2y_2$. Then:

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

You can verify: $\begin{pmatrix}x_1 & x_2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2&3\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}y_1\\y_2\end{pmatrix} = 2x_1y_1 + 3x_1y_2 - x_2y_1 + x_2y_2$.

Example in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The standard dot product $\langle u, v \rangle = x_1y_1 + x_2y_2 + x_3y_3$ has matrix $M = I_3$, the $3 \times 3$ identity. A less trivial example: the Lorentz form of special relativity on $\mathbb{R}^3$ (really $\mathbb{R}^{1+2}$), $\varphi(u,v) = u_0 v_0 - u_1 v_1 - u_2 v_2$, has matrix:

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is symmetric and non-degenerate ($\det M = 1 \neq 0$) but not positive definite: $\varphi(v, v) = v_0^2 - v_1^2 - v_2^2$ can be negative. The distinction between positive definite and indefinite bilinear forms is the subject of the next page.

3. Change of basis

Suppose we switch from basis $\mathcal{B}$ to basis $\mathcal{B}'$, with change-of-basis matrix $P$ (so the $j$-th column of $P$ gives the coordinates of the $j$-th new basis vector in the old basis). If $[u]_\mathcal{B} = P[u]_{\mathcal{B}'}$, then:

$$\varphi(u,v) = [u]_\mathcal{B}^T M [v]_\mathcal{B} = (P[u]_{\mathcal{B}'})^T M (P[v]_{\mathcal{B}'}) = [u]_{\mathcal{B}'}^T (P^T M P) [v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$$
Theorem — Change of basis for bilinear forms

If $M$ is the matrix of $\varphi$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$ and $P$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{B}'$ to $\mathcal{B}$, then the matrix of $\varphi$ in $\mathcal{B}'$ is:

$$M' = P^T M P$$
Proof

Step 1. Write $u = \sum_i x_i e_i$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$, and $u = \sum_k x'_k e'_k$ in basis $\mathcal{B}'$. By definition of $P$, we have $[u]_\mathcal{B} = P [u]_{\mathcal{B}'}$, i.e. $x_i = \sum_k P_{ik} x'_k$.

Step 2. Substitute into the matrix formula:

$$\varphi(u,v) = [u]_\mathcal{B}^T M [v]_\mathcal{B} = (P[u]_{\mathcal{B}'})^T M (P[v]_{\mathcal{B}'})$$

Step 3. Using $(PX)^T = X^T P^T$:

$$= [u]_{\mathcal{B}'}^T P^T M P [v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$$

Since this holds for all $u, v$, the matrix of $\varphi$ in basis $\mathcal{B}'$ is $M' = P^T M P$.

∎

This transformation $M \mapsto P^T M P$ is called congruence. It is not the same as similarity ($M \mapsto P^{-1}MP$) which governs change of basis for linear maps (Phase 6). Bilinear forms transform by congruence; endomorphisms transform by similarity.

Concrete example. Take $M = \begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$ in the standard basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$, and the change-of-basis matrix $P = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ (so the new basis is $(e_1, e_1 + e_2)$). Then:

$$M' = P^T M P = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2&3\\3&7\end{pmatrix}$$

Different matrices, same bilinear form. You can check: $\det(M') = 14 - 9 = 5 = \det(M) \cdot (\det P)^2$, consistent with $\det(P^TMP) = (\det P)^2 \det M$.

4. Symmetry and skew-symmetry

Definition — Symmetric and skew-symmetric

A bilinear form $\varphi$ is symmetric if $\varphi(u,v) = \varphi(v,u)$ for all $u, v \in V$.

It is skew-symmetric (or alternating) if $\varphi(u,v) = -\varphi(v,u)$ for all $u, v$.

In terms of the matrix: $\varphi$ is symmetric if and only if $M = M^T$, and skew-symmetric if and only if $M = -M^T$. This follows directly from $\varphi(e_i, e_j) = M_{ij}$ and $\varphi(e_j, e_i) = M_{ji}$.

Any bilinear form can be decomposed into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts:

$$\varphi = \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}(\varphi + \varphi^*)}_{\text{symmetric}} + \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}(\varphi - \varphi^*)}_{\text{skew-symmetric}}$$

where $\varphi^*(u,v) = \varphi(v,u)$. This mirrors the decomposition of any matrix as $\tfrac{1}{2}(M + M^T) + \tfrac{1}{2}(M - M^T)$.

The dot product is symmetric. The cross product's "scalar triple product" $\varphi(u,v) = \det(u \mid v)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$ is skew-symmetric: swapping $u$ and $v$ negates the determinant.

5. Non-degeneracy

A bilinear form $\varphi$ defines, for each $u \in V$, a linear functional $\varphi(u, \cdot) : V \to \mathbb{R}$ sending $v \mapsto \varphi(u, v)$. This gives a linear map $\Phi : V \to V^*$ (the dual space, Phase 7) defined by $\Phi(u) = \varphi(u, \cdot)$.

Definition — Non-degenerate

A bilinear form $\varphi$ is non-degenerate if the only vector $u$ with $\varphi(u, v) = 0$ for all $v \in V$ is $u = 0$. Equivalently, $\Phi : V \to V^*$ is injective.

Theorem — Non-degeneracy and invertibility

Let $V$ be finite-dimensional. Then $\varphi$ is non-degenerate if and only if its matrix $M$ (in any basis) is invertible.

Proof

Step 1. Write $\varphi(u, v) = [u]^T M [v]$. Then $\varphi(u, v) = 0$ for all $v$ means $[u]^T M [v] = 0$ for all column vectors $[v]$.

Step 2. $[u]^T M [v] = 0$ for all $[v]$ if and only if the row vector $[u]^T M$ is the zero row, i.e. $M^T [u] = 0$, i.e. $[u] \in \ker(M^T)$.

Step 3. So $\varphi$ is non-degenerate iff $\ker(M^T) = \{0\}$, iff $M^T$ is invertible (Phase 6, page 04), iff $M$ is invertible.

∎

Non-degeneracy means the form can "tell vectors apart": if $u \neq u'$, there exists some $v$ with $\varphi(u, v) \neq \varphi(u', v)$. The dot product is non-degenerate (its matrix is $I_n$, which is certainly invertible). The zero form $\varphi \equiv 0$ is the extreme degenerate case.

Historical note

Gauss's Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (1801) gave the first systematic treatment of binary quadratic forms $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$, including their equivalence under integer substitutions and the notion of the discriminant $b^2 - ac$. Arthur Cayley (1858) introduced the abstract notion of a bilinear form and its matrix, while James Joseph Sylvester developed the theory of invariants under congruence transformations. The modern treatment — bilinear forms as maps $V \times V \to K$ with change of basis by congruence — emerged in the early twentieth century as linear algebra was axiomatised.

What breaks

Without symmetry. Skew-symmetric bilinear forms behave very differently. For a skew-symmetric form, $\varphi(v, v) = -\varphi(v, v)$, so $\varphi(v, v) = 0$ for all $v$ — the diagonal of the matrix is always zero. You cannot extract a "quadratic form" from a skew-symmetric bilinear form (the next page depends on symmetry).

Without non-degeneracy. The map $\Phi : V \to V^*$ has a nontrivial kernel: there exist "invisible" vectors $u \neq 0$ that are orthogonal to everything. The form cannot distinguish $u$ from $0$, and many theorems (e.g. the existence of orthogonal bases) require restricting to the quotient $V / \ker(\Phi)$.

A bilinear form is a dot-product-like machine on a vector space, encoded by a matrix that transforms by congruence $P^TMP$ under change of basis.
