Singular Value Decomposition

Phase 13 — Bilinear Algebra and Spectral Theory, Page 4

The problem

The spectral theorem (page 3) decomposes symmetric matrices into orthogonal eigenvectors. But what about rectangular matrices? A dataset with 1000 samples and 50 features is a $1000 \times 50$ matrix — it has no eigenvalues, no eigenvectors, no square structure at all.

What is the right generalisation of eigendecomposition to any matrix, any shape? The answer is the singular value decomposition (SVD): every $m \times n$ matrix factors as $A = U\Sigma V^T$, where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal and $\Sigma$ is diagonal. The trick is to reduce the problem to the spectral theorem by looking at $A^TA$ and $AA^T$.

1. The Gram matrices $A^TA$ and $AA^T$

Let $A$ be any real $m \times n$ matrix. Consider the two products $A^TA$ ($n \times n$) and $AA^T$ ($m \times m$). These are called the Gram matrices of $A$.

Why look at these products? The matrix $A$ itself may be rectangular, so it cannot have eigenvalues — eigenvalues require a square matrix (the equation $Av = \lambda v$ needs $A$ and $\lambda I$ to have the same dimensions). But $A^TA$ and $AA^T$ are always square, always symmetric, and always positive semi-definite. This means the spectral theorem applies to them, and we can use their eigenstructure to understand $A$.

Think of $A^TA$ as measuring "input correlations": the $(i,j)$ entry of $A^TA$ is the dot product of the $i$-th and $j$-th columns of $A$. Similarly, $AA^T$ measures "output correlations": its $(i,j)$ entry is the dot product of the $i$-th and $j$-th rows of $A$.

The eigenstructure of $A^TA$ reveals which input directions produce the largest outputs, and the eigenstructure of $AA^T$ reveals which output directions receive the most energy.

Lemma — Gram matrices are symmetric positive semi-definite

For any real $m \times n$ matrix $A$, both $A^TA$ and $AA^T$ are symmetric and positive semi-definite.

Proof

Step 1. Symmetry of $A^TA$: we compute the transpose directly.

$$(A^TA)^T = A^T(A^T)^T = A^TA$$

So $A^TA$ is symmetric. The same calculation gives $(AA^T)^T = (A^T)^TA^T = AA^T$.

Step 2. Positive semi-definiteness of $A^TA$: for any $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$x^T(A^TA)x = (Ax)^T(Ax) = \|Ax\|^2 \geq 0$$

The key step is the associativity $(A^TA)x = A^T(Ax)$, so $x^T(A^TA)x = x^TA^T(Ax) = (Ax)^T(Ax)$. This is a squared norm, hence non-negative.

Step 3. The same argument handles $AA^T$: for any $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$,

$$y^T(AA^T)y = (A^Ty)^T(A^Ty) = \|A^Ty\|^2 \geq 0$$
∎

Since both Gram matrices are symmetric, the spectral theorem (page 3) guarantees they can be orthogonally diagonalised with real eigenvalues. Since they are also positive semi-definite, all eigenvalues are $\geq 0$.

The remarkable fact is that $A^TA$ and $AA^T$ share the same non-zero eigenvalues, even though they live in different dimensions ($n \times n$ vs $m \times m$). This is the bridge that connects the "input space" $\mathbb{R}^n$ to the "output space" $\mathbb{R}^m$.

Lemma — shared non-zero eigenvalues

The non-zero eigenvalues of $A^TA$ and $AA^T$ are identical, with the same multiplicities.

Proof

Step 1. Suppose $A^TAv = \sigma^2 v$ with $v \neq 0$ and $\sigma^2 > 0$. Set $u = Av$.

Step 2. First, $u \neq 0$. Why? If $Av = 0$, then $A^TAv = A^T(0) = 0 = \sigma^2 v$, forcing $\sigma^2 = 0$ (since $v \neq 0$). This contradicts $\sigma^2 > 0$. So $u = Av \neq 0$.

Step 3. Now compute $AA^Tu$:

$$AA^Tu = AA^T(Av) = A(A^TAv) = A(\sigma^2 v) = \sigma^2(Av) = \sigma^2 u$$

So $u$ is an eigenvector of $AA^T$ with the same eigenvalue $\sigma^2$.

Step 4. The reverse direction is identical: if $AA^Tu = \sigma^2 u$ with $\sigma^2 > 0$, then $v = A^Tu \neq 0$ and $A^TAv = A^T(AA^Tu) = A^T(\sigma^2 u) = \sigma^2(A^Tu) = \sigma^2 v$.

Step 5. For multiplicities: the map $v \mapsto Av$ is a linear injection from the $\sigma^2$-eigenspace of $A^TA$ into the $\sigma^2$-eigenspace of $AA^T$ (injective because $Av = 0$ would force $\sigma^2 = 0$). The reverse map $u \mapsto A^Tu$ is also injective. So both eigenspaces have the same dimension.

∎

2. Singular values

Let $r = \operatorname{rank}(A)$. The rank of $A^TA$ equals the rank of $A$ — this is a standard result from linear algebra (Phase 6, page 4), proved by showing $\ker(A^TA) = \ker(A)$. Indeed, $A^TAx = 0$ implies $x^TA^TAx = \|Ax\|^2 = 0$, hence $Ax = 0$. Conversely, $Ax = 0$ trivially gives $A^TAx = 0$.

So $A^TA$ has exactly $r$ positive eigenvalues and $n - r$ zero eigenvalues.

Definition — singular values

The singular values of $A$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $A^TA$, ordered as

$$\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0 = \sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n$$

where $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^TA)}$.

The singular values are intrinsic to $A$ — they do not depend on any choice of basis. If you change the input basis by an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (replacing $A$ by $AQ$), then $(AQ)^T(AQ) = Q^TA^TAQ$, which is similar to $A^TA$ and therefore has the same eigenvalues. Likewise for a change of output basis. The singular values depend only on how $A$ acts as a linear map, not on how we represent it.

Note that singular values are always non-negative, unlike eigenvalues which can be negative or complex. A symmetric matrix with eigenvalues $3, -2, 1$ has singular values $3, 2, 1$ — the absolute values, reordered.

For a general (non-symmetric) matrix, singular values and eigenvalues are different quantities entirely. The singular values are properties of $A$ as a map between two spaces (how it stretches), while eigenvalues describe $A$ as a self-map of a single space (its fixed directions and scaling factors). The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalue $0$ (with multiplicity 2) but singular value $\sigma_1 = 1$ — it maps $e_2$ to $e_1$ with unit length, even though it has no eigenvectors other than $e_1$ (with eigenvalue 0).

3. The SVD theorem

Theorem — Singular Value Decomposition

For any real $m \times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$, there exist an orthogonal matrix $U$ ($m \times m$), an orthogonal matrix $V$ ($n \times n$), and an $m \times n$ matrix $\Sigma$ with $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$ on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere, such that

$$A = U\Sigma V^T$$

The matrix $\Sigma$ has the same shape as $A$: it is $m \times n$, with $\Sigma_{ii} = \sigma_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, \min(m,n)$ and all other entries zero. If $m > n$, the bottom $m - n$ rows of $\Sigma$ are all zeros. If $m < n$, the rightmost $n - m$ columns are all zeros.

Proof (constructive)

Step 1. Since $A^TA$ is symmetric positive semi-definite, the spectral theorem (page 3) gives an orthonormal eigenbasis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ with

$$A^TAv_i = \sigma_i^2 v_i$$

Arrange these so $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0 = \sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n$. Set $V = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$. This is an $n \times n$ orthogonal matrix.

Step 2. For $i = 1, \ldots, r$, define

$$u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}Av_i$$

We claim these are unit vectors. Compute:

$$\|u_i\|^2 = \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2}(Av_i)^T(Av_i) = \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2}v_i^T A^TA v_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2}\cdot\sigma_i^2\cdot\|v_i\|^2 = 1$$

Step 3. The vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ are mutually orthogonal. For $i \neq j$:

$$u_i^T u_j = \frac{1}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}(Av_i)^T(Av_j) = \frac{1}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}v_i^T A^TA v_j = \frac{\sigma_j^2}{\sigma_i\sigma_j}v_i^T v_j = 0$$

since $v_i^Tv_j = 0$ by orthonormality of the eigenbasis.

Step 4. The set $\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\}$ is an orthonormal set in $\mathbb{R}^m$ with $r \leq m$ elements. Extend it to a full orthonormal basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_m\}$ of $\mathbb{R}^m$ by the Gram-Schmidt procedure (Phase 6, page 7). The vectors $u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m$ span the orthogonal complement of $\operatorname{col}(A)$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$. Set $U = [u_1 \mid \cdots \mid u_m]$.

Step 5. We verify that $A = U\Sigma V^T$. Since $V$ is orthogonal, it suffices to check $AV = U\Sigma$, i.e., to check column by column that $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ for $i \leq r$ and $Av_i = 0$ for $i > r$.

For $i \leq r$: by construction, $u_i = Av_i / \sigma_i$, so $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$. This is the $i$-th column of $U\Sigma$.

For $i > r$: $\|Av_i\|^2 = v_i^T A^TA v_i = \sigma_i^2\|v_i\|^2 = 0$, so $Av_i = 0$. And $\sigma_i = 0$, so the $i$-th column of $U\Sigma$ is also zero.

∎

Notice how the entire proof reduces to one application of the spectral theorem (to $A^TA$) plus a normalisation step. The SVD does not require any new ideas — it is the spectral theorem in disguise, applied to the Gram matrix.

Let us trace through a small example to make the construction concrete. Take $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, a $3 \times 2$ matrix.

First, $A^TA = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, which has eigenvalues $3$ and $1$ (check: trace $= 4 = 3 + 1$, determinant $= 3 = 3 \times 1$). So $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{3}$ and $\sigma_2 = 1$.

The eigenvectors of $A^TA$ are $v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1)^T$ (for $\lambda = 3$) and $v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, -1)^T$ (for $\lambda = 1$). These form $V$.

Now build $U$: $u_1 = \frac{1}{\sigma_1}Av_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}A(1,1)^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(2, 1, 1)^T$ and $u_2 = \frac{1}{\sigma_2}Av_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}A(1,-1)^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0, -1, 1)^T$. These are orthogonal unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$. We extend to a full basis by finding $u_3$ orthogonal to both: $u_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(1, -1, -1)^T$. Then $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{3} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

The third row of zeros in $\Sigma$ reflects the fact that $A$ maps $\mathbb{R}^2$ into a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ — the column space of $A$. The vector $u_3$ spans the orthogonal complement of $\operatorname{col}(A)$ (the left null space), and $A$ produces no component in that direction.

This example also illustrates the four fundamental subspaces of $A$, all revealed by the SVD:

The column space $\operatorname{col}(A) = \operatorname{span}\{u_1, \ldots, u_r\}$: the first $r$ left singular vectors span the range of $A$. In our example, $\operatorname{col}(A) = \operatorname{span}\{u_1, u_2\}$ is a 2D plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The left null space $\ker(A^T) = \operatorname{span}\{u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m\}$: the remaining left singular vectors span the orthogonal complement of $\operatorname{col}(A)$. In our example, $\ker(A^T) = \operatorname{span}\{u_3\}$ is a line perpendicular to the column space.

The row space $\operatorname{row}(A) = \operatorname{span}\{v_1, \ldots, v_r\}$: the first $r$ right singular vectors span the row space. In our example, $r = n = 2$, so the row space is all of $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The null space $\ker(A) = \operatorname{span}\{v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n\}$: the remaining right singular vectors span the null space. In our example, the null space is $\{0\}$ (since $r = n$).

The SVD gives orthonormal bases for all four subspaces simultaneously. This is more than the rank-nullity theorem provides — it gives canonical bases that are geometrically natural (orthogonal and ordered by importance via the singular values).

This is the key difference between the SVD and eigendecomposition. Eigendecomposition works with a single space (it decomposes a square matrix as $A = PDP^{-1}$, mapping $\mathbb{R}^n$ to itself). The SVD works with two spaces: it decomposes a map $A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ using an orthonormal basis for the domain ($V$) and a separate orthonormal basis for the codomain ($U$). This is why it applies to rectangular matrices where eigendecomposition cannot.

For a square symmetric matrix, the SVD and eigendecomposition coincide: $A = P\Lambda P^T$ (spectral theorem) is the same as $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with $U = V = P$ and $\Sigma = |\Lambda|$ (singular values are absolute values of eigenvalues). More precisely: if $\lambda_i \geq 0$, then $u_i = v_i$; if $\lambda_i < 0$, then $u_i = -v_i$ (the sign flip absorbs the negative eigenvalue into the left singular vector, making the singular value $|\lambda_i|$ positive).

For a non-symmetric square matrix, the two decompositions are genuinely different. The eigendecomposition uses the same basis for input and output ($A = PDP^{-1}$, with $P$ generally not orthogonal and possibly complex), while the SVD uses different, always real, always orthogonal bases ($A = U\Sigma V^T$, with $U \neq V$ in general). Not every matrix has an eigendecomposition (it may lack a full set of eigenvectors — consider a non-trivial Jordan block from Phase 6, page 11), but every matrix has an SVD. This universality is the SVD's greatest strength.

4. Geometric meaning

The SVD reveals what $A$ does geometrically. Write $A = U\Sigma V^T$ and think of $A$ acting on a vector $x$ in three stages:

Stage 1: $V^T$ rotates the input. The matrix $V^T$ is orthogonal, so it acts as a rigid rotation (or rotation-reflection) of $\mathbb{R}^n$. It maps $x$ into the coordinate system defined by the right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$. The $i$-th component of $V^Tx$ is $v_i^Tx$ — the projection of $x$ onto the $i$-th right singular vector. No stretching happens here — all distances and angles are preserved.

Stage 2: $\Sigma$ stretches along coordinate axes. The diagonal matrix $\Sigma$ multiplies the $i$-th coordinate by $\sigma_i$. Directions with large singular values get amplified; directions with small singular values get compressed; directions with $\sigma_i = 0$ get annihilated entirely (the entire null space of $A$ consists of directions that $\Sigma$ maps to zero). The unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^n$ maps to an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$ (or a degenerate ellipsoid if some $\sigma_i = 0$) with semi-axis lengths $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_r$. If $m > n$, the ellipsoid lives in a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$.

Stage 3: $U$ rotates the output. The orthogonal matrix $U$ rigidly rotates the ellipsoid into its final position in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The columns of $U$ — the left singular vectors — point along the axes of the output ellipsoid. Again, no stretching — $U$ just orients the ellipsoid.

In summary: every linear map is a rotation, followed by an axis-aligned stretch, followed by another rotation. The singular values are the stretch factors. The columns of $V$ are the "input directions" (the radii of the sphere that get mapped to the axes of the ellipsoid), and the columns of $U$ are the "output directions" (the axes of the ellipsoid in the target space).

Two important consequences of this picture:

The largest singular value is the maximum stretch factor: $\sigma_1 = \max_{\|x\|=1} \|Ax\|$. This is the operator norm (or spectral norm) of $A$. The maximum is attained at $x = v_1$, where $\|Av_1\| = \sigma_1$.

The smallest non-zero singular value $\sigma_r$ is the minimum stretch factor on the row space of $A$: $\sigma_r = \min_{x \in \operatorname{row}(A), \|x\|=1} \|Ax\|$. The ratio $\kappa(A) = \sigma_1 / \sigma_r$ is the condition number of $A$, measuring how much $A$ distorts shapes. A matrix with $\kappa(A) = 1$ maps the unit sphere to a sphere (it is a scalar multiple of an orthogonal matrix). A matrix with $\kappa(A) = 10^{16}$ maps the unit sphere to a needle-thin ellipsoid — it is effectively singular for numerical purposes. When you solve $Ax = b$ on a computer, the condition number controls how much roundoff errors in $b$ get amplified in $x$: the relative error in $x$ can be up to $\kappa(A)$ times the relative error in $b$.

The pseudoinverse (or Moore-Penrose inverse) $A^+$ also follows immediately from the SVD. If $A = U\Sigma V^T$, then

$$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$$

where $\Sigma^+$ is the $n \times m$ matrix obtained from $\Sigma$ by transposing and inverting each non-zero diagonal entry: $\Sigma^+_{ii} = 1/\sigma_i$ for $i \leq r$ and zero otherwise. You can verify: $AA^+A = U\Sigma V^T \cdot V\Sigma^+ U^T \cdot U\Sigma V^T = U\Sigma\Sigma^+\Sigma V^T = U\Sigma V^T = A$, confirming $A^+$ is a generalised inverse.

The pseudoinverse solves the least-squares problem $\min_x \|Ax - b\|^2$: the minimum-norm solution is $x^* = A^+ b$. When $A$ is square and invertible, $A^+ = A^{-1}$ (since all singular values are non-zero, $\Sigma^+ = \Sigma^{-1}$). When $A$ is rectangular or singular, $A^+$ provides the best substitute for an inverse. This generalises matrix inversion to any matrix of any shape and any rank.

For our earlier example $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, the pseudoinverse $A^+$ is a $2 \times 3$ matrix. The least-squares solution $x^* = A^+b$ gives the best fit when you have more equations (3) than unknowns (2) — the standard situation in data fitting and linear regression.

The connection between SVD and least squares is fundamental. Suppose we want to solve $Ax = b$ where $A$ is $m \times n$ with $m > n$ (overdetermined system). The least-squares solution minimises $\|Ax - b\|^2$. Writing $A = U\Sigma V^T$ and substituting $y = V^Tx$, $c = U^Tb$:

$$\|Ax - b\|^2 = \|U\Sigma V^Tx - b\|^2 = \|\Sigma y - c\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^r (\sigma_i y_i - c_i)^2 + \sum_{i=r+1}^m c_i^2$$

The second sum is constant (it is $\|b_\perp\|^2$, the component of $b$ outside $\operatorname{col}(A)$, which cannot be reduced). Minimising over $y$ gives $y_i = c_i/\sigma_i$ for $i \leq r$. So $x^* = V\Sigma^+U^Tb = A^+b$. The SVD turns the least-squares problem into $r$ trivially solvable one-dimensional problems.

The residual $\|Ax^* - b\|^2 = \sum_{i=r+1}^m c_i^2$ is the squared distance from $b$ to $\operatorname{col}(A)$. If $b \in \operatorname{col}(A)$, the residual is zero and $x^*$ is an exact solution. If not, $x^*$ is the closest thing to a solution — it minimises the error $\|Ax - b\|$. This is the foundation of linear regression, curve fitting, and signal processing.

When $A$ is ill-conditioned (some $\sigma_i$ are very small), the pseudoinverse amplifies noise: dividing by a tiny $\sigma_i$ turns a small error in $b$ into a large error in $x$. The amplification factor is $1/\sigma_r$ — the reciprocal of the smallest singular value — which can be enormous.

Truncated SVD regularisation addresses this by setting $\Sigma^+_{ii} = 0$ for all $\sigma_i$ below some threshold $\tau$. This discards the components of the solution corresponding to the smallest singular values — precisely the components most contaminated by noise. You trade a small increase in the residual $\|Ax - b\|$ for a large decrease in the solution norm $\|x\|$ and noise amplification.

The closely related Tikhonov regularisation replaces $\Sigma^+_{ii} = 1/\sigma_i$ by $\sigma_i/(\sigma_i^2 + \alpha)$, where $\alpha > 0$ is a regularisation parameter. This smoothly damps the contribution of small singular values rather than cutting them off sharply. Both methods are essential tools in inverse problems (medical imaging, geophysics, machine learning).

To summarise the four fundamental objects the SVD reveals:

1. The singular values $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$ measure the "importance" of each direction — how much the matrix stretches along it.

2. The right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ (columns of $V$) form an orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$, with the first $r$ spanning the row space and the remaining $n - r$ spanning the null space.

3. The left singular vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_m$ (columns of $U$) form an orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{R}^m$, with the first $r$ spanning the column space and the remaining $m - r$ spanning the left null space.

4. The rank $r$ is the number of non-zero singular values, and the condition number $\kappa = \sigma_1/\sigma_r$ measures the "difficulty" of the matrix for numerical computation.

5. Rank and low-rank approximation

From the SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$, we can write $A$ as a sum of rank-one matrices:

$$A = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_r u_r v_r^T$$

This is the outer product form of the SVD, and it is arguably the most revealing way to write it. Each term is a rank-one "layer" of the matrix, weighted by its singular value.

Each term $\sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T$ is an $m \times n$ matrix of rank one: every column is a scalar multiple of $u_i$, and every row a scalar multiple of $v_i^T$. The weight $\sigma_i$ measures the "importance" of this term — how much of $A$'s action it captures.

The rank of $A$ is exactly $r$, the number of non-zero singular values. This gives a clean characterisation: $\operatorname{rank}(A)$ counts how many independent "stretch directions" $A$ has. A rank-one matrix maps all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ to a single line in $\mathbb{R}^m$; a rank-$r$ matrix maps $\mathbb{R}^n$ to an $r$-dimensional subspace. The SVD organises these contributions by size, from most important ($\sigma_1$) to least ($\sigma_r$).

Now comes the key application. If the singular values decay rapidly — $\sigma_1 \gg \sigma_2 \gg \cdots$ — then the first few terms of the sum capture most of the matrix's structure. Truncating the sum at $k < r$ terms gives an approximation:

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \, u_i v_i^T$$

How good is this approximation? Remarkably, the answer is: the best possible, in a very strong sense.

Theorem — Eckart-Young (1936)

Among all $m \times n$ matrices of rank at most $k$, the truncated SVD $A_k$ minimises the distance to $A$. Specifically:

$$\min_{\operatorname{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\| = \|A - A_k\| = \sigma_{k+1}$$

in the operator norm, and

$$\min_{\operatorname{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_F = \|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$$

in the Frobenius norm $\|M\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{ij} M_{ij}^2} = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i(M)^2}$.

This theorem is why the SVD is the workhorse of data science and applied mathematics.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is precisely the truncated SVD of the centred data matrix. Given $m$ data points in $\mathbb{R}^n$, arranged as rows of an $m \times n$ matrix $X$ (after subtracting the mean), PCA finds the $k$-dimensional subspace that captures the most variance. The right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ of $X$ span this subspace, and the singular values measure the standard deviation along each principal direction.

Image compression: a greyscale image is a matrix of pixel intensities. Store only the top $k$ singular values and the corresponding columns of $U$ and $V$. Instead of $mn$ numbers, you store $k(m + n + 1)$ numbers. For a $1000 \times 1000$ image with $k = 50$, that is 100,050 numbers instead of 1,000,000 — a 10x compression with usually imperceptible loss.

Recommender systems: a user-item rating matrix (mostly empty) is approximated by a low-rank matrix via truncated SVD. This fills in the missing entries with predictions. The Netflix Prize (2009) was won by methods built on this idea.

Latent semantic analysis: in natural language processing, a word-document matrix records how often each word appears in each document. The truncated SVD reveals "latent topics" — groups of words that co-occur, captured by the leading singular vectors. Two documents about the same topic will have similar projections onto the low-rank subspace, even if they use different words.

Noise reduction: if $A = A_0 + E$ where $A_0$ is a low-rank "signal" and $E$ is noise, then the truncated SVD $A_k$ (with $k = \operatorname{rank}(A_0)$) approximately recovers $A_0$. The Eckart-Young theorem guarantees this is optimal: no other rank-$k$ matrix is closer to $A$. The error is bounded by $\|A_k - A_0\| \leq 2\|E\|$ (triangle inequality: $\|A_k - A_0\| \leq \|A_k - A\| + \|A - A_0\| = \sigma_{k+1}(A) + \|E\| \leq 2\|E\|$, since the noise pushes singular values up by at most $\|E\|$ by Weyl's inequality applied to singular values).

The Frobenius norm formula also gives us $\|A\|_F^2 = \sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2 = \operatorname{tr}(A^TA)$, connecting singular values to the trace.

To see Eckart-Young in action: suppose a $1000 \times 500$ matrix has $\sigma_1 = 100$, $\sigma_2 = 50$, $\sigma_3 = 10$, and all remaining singular values below $1$. The rank-3 approximation $A_3$ captures a fraction $\frac{100^2 + 50^2 + 10^2}{\|A\|_F^2} = \frac{12600}{\|A\|_F^2}$ of the total energy. If $\|A\|_F^2 \approx 13000$, then $A_3$ captures over 96% of the matrix with only 3 rank-one components instead of 500. This is precisely the dimension reduction that makes PCA effective: most real-world data matrices have rapidly decaying singular values because the data lives near a low-dimensional subspace.

A subtlety: Eckart-Young gives the best approximation in a fixed-rank class. It does not tell you how to choose $k$. In practice, one plots the singular values and looks for a "gap" — a sharp drop between $\sigma_k$ and $\sigma_{k+1}$ — and truncates there. The singular values above the gap carry "signal"; those below carry "noise."

The Frobenius norm interpretation makes this precise. Define the explained variance ratio for rank $k$ as

$$\rho_k = \frac{\sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_k^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2} = 1 - \frac{\|A - A_k\|_F^2}{\|A\|_F^2}$$

This is the fraction of $A$'s total energy captured by the rank-$k$ approximation. In PCA, $\rho_k$ is called the "proportion of variance explained" by the first $k$ principal components. A common heuristic is to choose $k$ such that $\rho_k \geq 0.95$ (retain 95% of the energy). Making this rigorous requires tools from random matrix theory, which goes beyond CPGE-level material.

Historical note

Beltrami (1873) and Jordan (1874) independently discovered the SVD for square real matrices, motivated by problems in the theory of bilinear forms. Autonne (1915) and Eckart-Young (1936) extended it to rectangular matrices and proved the optimality of truncated SVD for low-rank approximation. The modern computational algorithm, due to Golub and Kahan (1965), reduced SVD to a sequence of bidiagonalisation steps followed by QR iterations, making it practical for large matrices. Today, variants of their algorithm run inside every major numerical library (LAPACK, NumPy, MATLAB).

What breaks

Without the spectral theorem for symmetric matrices (page 3): the entire SVD construction collapses. We need to orthogonally diagonalise $A^TA$, which requires the spectral theorem. No spectral theorem means no orthonormal eigenbasis for $A^TA$, no $V$ matrix, no singular values, no SVD.

Without symmetry of $A^TA$: this failure mode is impossible — $A^TA$ is always symmetric, regardless of $A$. This is precisely why the SVD always exists, even when eigendecomposition does not. A generic non-symmetric square matrix may have no real eigenvalues and cannot be diagonalised over $\mathbb{R}$. But it always has a real SVD, because its Gram matrix is always symmetric.

Without positive semi-definiteness of $A^TA$: also impossible. The identity $x^TA^TAx = \|Ax\|^2 \geq 0$ always holds, so eigenvalues of $A^TA$ are always $\geq 0$ and singular values are always real non-negative numbers. There is no analogue of "negative eigenvalues" or "complex eigenvalues" for singular values — they are always well-behaved.

Without the ordering convention: the SVD still exists but the Eckart-Young theorem requires the singular values to be sorted in decreasing order. If you scramble the order, the truncation $A_k$ (first $k$ terms) is no longer the best rank-$k$ approximation — you would be keeping small singular values and discarding large ones.

Every matrix — any shape, any rank — decomposes as a rotation, a stretch along orthogonal axes (the singular values), and another rotation.
