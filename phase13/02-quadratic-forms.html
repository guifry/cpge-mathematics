Quadratic Forms and Sylvester's Law of Inertia

Phase 13 — Bilinear Algebra and Spectral Theory, Page 2

The problem

The equation $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2 = 1$ describes a conic section in the plane. Depending on $a, b, c$, you get an ellipse, a hyperbola, or a degenerate curve (two lines, a point, or nothing). Rotate coordinates to eliminate the $xy$ term — the equation becomes $\lambda_1 X^2 + \lambda_2 Y^2 = 1$, clearly an ellipse if both $\lambda_i$ are positive, a hyperbola if they have opposite signs.

But different changes of variable give different values of $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$. If you scale the axes too, the actual numbers change. So what is intrinsic to the form? What cannot be altered by any invertible substitution?

Sylvester (1852) answered: the number of positive coefficients and the number of negative coefficients are invariant. You can make the positive ones all equal to $+1$ and the negative ones all $-1$ by rescaling, but you can never turn a positive coefficient into a negative one. This pair of counts — the signature — completely classifies real quadratic forms up to change of basis.

1. From bilinear to quadratic

On page 01, we defined symmetric bilinear forms. A quadratic form is what you get when you feed the same vector into both slots.

Definition — Quadratic form

Let $\varphi$ be a symmetric bilinear form on $V$. The quadratic form associated to $\varphi$ is the function $Q : V \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by:

$$Q(v) = \varphi(v, v)$$

In coordinates with matrix $M$: $Q(v) = [v]^T M [v] = \sum_{i,j} M_{ij} x_i x_j$. For instance, the form $M = \begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ b & c\end{pmatrix}$ gives $Q(v) = ax_1^2 + 2bx_1x_2 + cx_2^2$ — precisely Gauss's binary quadratic form.

The crucial fact is that you can go back from $Q$ to $\varphi$. This is the polarisation identity:

Theorem — Polarisation identity

If $Q(v) = \varphi(v,v)$ for a symmetric bilinear form $\varphi$, then:

$$\varphi(u, v) = \frac{1}{2}\bigl[Q(u + v) - Q(u) - Q(v)\bigr]$$
Proof

Step 1. Expand $Q(u + v) = \varphi(u + v, u + v)$.

Step 2. By bilinearity:

$$Q(u+v) = \varphi(u,u) + \varphi(u,v) + \varphi(v,u) + \varphi(v,v)$$

Step 3. Since $\varphi$ is symmetric, $\varphi(u,v) = \varphi(v,u)$, so:

$$Q(u+v) = Q(u) + 2\varphi(u,v) + Q(v)$$

Step 4. Rearrange: $\varphi(u,v) = \tfrac{1}{2}[Q(u+v) - Q(u) - Q(v)]$.

∎

So symmetric bilinear forms and quadratic forms are in perfect correspondence (over $\mathbb{R}$, or any field of characteristic $\neq 2$). The quadratic form contains all the information of the bilinear form.

2. Diagonalisation by completing the square

A quadratic form $Q$ is diagonal if its matrix is diagonal: $Q(v) = \sum_i d_i x_i^2$ with no cross-terms. We now show that every real quadratic form can be brought to diagonal form by a change of basis.

Theorem — Diagonalisation of quadratic forms

For every symmetric bilinear form $\varphi$ on a finite-dimensional real vector space $V$, there exists a basis $\mathcal{B}'$ in which the matrix of $\varphi$ is diagonal.

Proof (Gauss's method — completing the square)

We proceed by induction on $n = \dim V$.

Step 1. Base case. If $n = 1$, the matrix is already $1 \times 1$, hence diagonal.

Step 2. If $Q \equiv 0$. Then $\varphi \equiv 0$ by polarisation, and the zero matrix is diagonal. Done.

Step 3. Find a vector with $Q(v) \neq 0$. Since $Q$ is not identically zero, there exists $v$ with $Q(v) \neq 0$. (If $Q(e_i) = 0$ for all basis vectors but $Q$ is nonzero, then some $Q(e_i + e_j) \neq 0$. By polarisation, $\varphi(e_i, e_j) = \tfrac{1}{2}[Q(e_i + e_j) - Q(e_i) - Q(e_j)] = \tfrac{1}{2}Q(e_i + e_j) \neq 0$, and then $Q(e_i + e_j) \neq 0$.)

Step 4. Complete the square. Let $e_1' = v$ with $Q(v) \neq 0$. For any $w \in V$, write:

$$w = \frac{\varphi(w, e_1')}{\varphi(e_1', e_1')} e_1' + \left(w - \frac{\varphi(w, e_1')}{\varphi(e_1', e_1')} e_1'\right)$$

Call the remainder $w' = w - \frac{\varphi(w, e_1')}{Q(e_1')} e_1'$. Then $\varphi(w', e_1') = \varphi(w, e_1') - \frac{\varphi(w, e_1')}{Q(e_1')} Q(e_1') = 0$.

Step 5. Let $W = \{w \in V : \varphi(w, e_1') = 0\}$. Step 4 shows $V = \operatorname{span}(e_1') \oplus W$ (every vector decomposes uniquely). The restriction of $\varphi$ to $W$ is a symmetric bilinear form on a space of dimension $n - 1$.

Step 6. By the induction hypothesis, $\varphi|_W$ can be diagonalised in some basis $(e_2', \dots, e_n')$ of $W$. Since $\varphi(e_1', e_j') = 0$ for $j \geq 2$, the full matrix in basis $(e_1', \dots, e_n')$ is diagonal.

∎

Concretely, this is the familiar "completing the square" procedure. For $Q(x,y) = 2x^2 + 4xy + 3y^2$: factor out from the $x$-terms to get $2(x + y)^2 + y^2$. Set $X = x + y$, $Y = y$, and the form becomes $2X^2 + Y^2$.

3. Sylvester's law of inertia

After diagonalisation, we have $Q(v) = d_1 x_1^2 + \cdots + d_n x_n^2$ with each $d_i$ positive, negative, or zero. By rescaling each basis vector $e_i' \mapsto e_i'/\sqrt{|d_i|}$ (when $d_i \neq 0$), we can arrange $d_i \in \{+1, -1, 0\}$. After reordering:

$$Q(v) = x_1^2 + \cdots + x_p^2 - x_{p+1}^2 - \cdots - x_{p+q}^2$$

with $p$ positive terms, $q$ negative terms, and $n - p - q$ zero terms. The pair $(p, q)$ is the signature of $Q$, and $p + q$ is the rank.

Theorem — Sylvester's law of inertia

The signature $(p, q)$ of a real quadratic form is independent of the choice of diagonalising basis.

Proof

Suppose two diagonalisations give signatures $(p, q)$ and $(p', q')$ with $p' > p$ (seeking a contradiction).

Step 1. In the first diagonalisation, let $V^+ = \operatorname{span}(e_1, \dots, e_p)$ be the subspace on which $Q$ is positive (excluding zero), so $\dim V^+ = p$, and let $V^- = \operatorname{span}(e_{p+1}, \dots, e_n)$ be the subspace on which $Q \leq 0$. Then $\dim V^- = n - p$.

Step 2. In the second diagonalisation, let $W^+ = \operatorname{span}(f_1, \dots, f_{p'})$ be the subspace on which $Q$ is positive, with $\dim W^+ = p' > p$.

Step 3. Consider $W^+ \cap V^-$. By the dimension formula (Phase 6):

$$\dim(W^+ \cap V^-) \geq \dim W^+ + \dim V^- - n = p' + (n - p) - n = p' - p > 0$$

Step 4. So there exists a nonzero $v \in W^+ \cap V^-$. On $W^+$, the form $Q$ is strictly positive on nonzero vectors: $Q(v) > 0$. On $V^-$, the form satisfies $Q(v) \leq 0$. Contradiction.

Therefore $p' \leq p$. By symmetry of the argument, $p \leq p'$, so $p = p'$. The same reasoning applied to $-Q$ gives $q = q'$.

∎

4. Definite and indefinite forms

Definition — Definiteness

A quadratic form $Q$ (or its associated symmetric matrix $M$) is:

  • Positive definite if $Q(v) > 0$ for all $v \neq 0$ (signature $(n, 0)$).
  • Negative definite if $Q(v) < 0$ for all $v \neq 0$ (signature $(0, n)$).
  • Positive semi-definite if $Q(v) \geq 0$ for all $v$.
  • Indefinite if $Q$ takes both positive and negative values.

The dot product $\sum x_i^2$ is positive definite. The Lorentz form $x_1^2 - x_2^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$ is indefinite with signature $(1, 1)$ — it is fundamental to special relativity.

Equivalent conditions for positive definiteness of a symmetric matrix $M$:

  • All eigenvalues of $M$ are strictly positive (we prove on the next page that symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues).
  • All leading principal minors $\det(M_k)$ for $k = 1, \dots, n$ are strictly positive (Sylvester's criterion — this follows from the spectral theorem and the relationship between eigenvalues and minors; we state it here and defer the full proof).
  • There exists an invertible matrix $L$ with $M = L^T L$ (Cholesky decomposition).
Historical note

James Joseph Sylvester coined the term "law of inertia" in 1852, by analogy with physical inertia: the signature resists change, no matter what transformation you apply. Gauss's completing-the-square method (1801) provided the algorithmic foundation — he used it to reduce ternary quadratic forms in his number-theoretic investigations. The classification of conics and quadrics by signature was developed throughout the nineteenth century, connecting algebra to geometry in a way that foreshadowed modern algebraic geometry.

What breaks

Over $\mathbb{C}$ instead of $\mathbb{R}$. Every nonzero complex number is a square (including $-1$), so you can always turn a negative coefficient into a positive one. Over $\mathbb{C}$, every non-degenerate quadratic form has signature $(n, 0)$ — signature is no longer a useful invariant. The only invariant is the rank.

Without symmetry. If $\varphi$ is not symmetric, the "quadratic form" $Q(v) = \varphi(v, v)$ only sees the symmetric part $\tfrac{1}{2}(\varphi + \varphi^*)$: the skew-symmetric part contributes $\tfrac{1}{2}[\varphi(v,v) - \varphi(v,v)] = 0$. So $Q$ cannot detect skew-symmetry at all, and the correspondence between bilinear and quadratic forms breaks down.

Every real quadratic form can be diagonalised by completing the square, and its signature — the count of positive and negative terms — is an invariant.
