The Spectral Theorem

Phase 13 — Bilinear Algebra and Spectral Theory, Page 3

The problem

A vibrating membrane — a drum head, say — has natural frequencies of oscillation. Strike it, and it vibrates as a superposition of independent modes, each with its own pitch. Physically, these modes correspond to eigenvalues of a symmetric differential operator (the Laplacian restricted to the membrane's shape).

But does such an operator always have a full set of eigenvalues? We know from Phase 6 that not all matrices are diagonalisable: the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ has only one eigenvalue ($\lambda = 0$) with a one-dimensional eigenspace, so it cannot be diagonalised. It is "defective."

The spectral theorem says that symmetry prevents defects. Every real symmetric matrix has a full set of real eigenvalues, and the eigenvectors can be chosen mutually orthogonal. You can always decompose the space into independent eigendirections. This is why the drum always has a complete set of modes.

1. The diagonalisation problem

Recall from Phase 6 that a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is diagonalisable if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that $P^{-1}AP = \Lambda$ is diagonal. The columns of $P$ are eigenvectors, and the diagonal entries of $\Lambda$ are the corresponding eigenvalues.

Not all matrices are diagonalisable. The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ satisfies $A^2 = 0$, so its only eigenvalue is $0$, but $\ker(A) = \operatorname{span}\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ is one-dimensional — we cannot find two linearly independent eigenvectors.

What additional condition on $A$ guarantees diagonalisability? The answer: symmetry. If $A = A^T$, then $A$ is always diagonalisable, and something stronger is true — we can choose an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors.

2. Symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues

Before proving diagonalisability, we must show the eigenvalues are real. A priori, the characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ could have complex roots even for a real matrix (e.g. $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $\pm i$). Symmetry rules this out.

Theorem — Real eigenvalues

If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is symmetric ($A = A^T$), then all eigenvalues of $A$ are real.

Proof

Step 1. Let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue of $A$ (possibly complex), with eigenvector $v \in \mathbb{C}^n \setminus \{0\}$: $Av = \lambda v$.

Step 2. Compute $\bar{v}^T A v$ in two ways. First, since $Av = \lambda v$:

$$\bar{v}^T A v = \bar{v}^T (\lambda v) = \lambda \, \bar{v}^T v = \lambda \|v\|^2$$

where $\|v\|^2 = \bar{v}^T v = \sum_i |v_i|^2 > 0$.

Step 3. Second, since $A$ is real and symmetric ($A = A^T = \bar{A}^T$):

$$\bar{v}^T A v = \overline{(\bar{v}^T A v)}^T = \overline{v^T A^T \bar{v}} = \overline{v^T A \bar{v}} = \overline{\bar{\lambda} \|v\|^2} = \lambda \|v\|^2$$

Wait — let us be more careful. Take the conjugate transpose of the scalar $\bar{v}^T A v$. Since it is a $1 \times 1$ matrix:

$$\overline{\bar{v}^T A v} = v^T \bar{A}^T \bar{v} = v^T A^T \bar{v} = v^T A \bar{v}$$

Now $A\bar{v} = \overline{Av} = \overline{\lambda v} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{v}$, so $v^T A \bar{v} = \bar{\lambda} v^T \bar{v} = \bar{\lambda}\|v\|^2$.

Step 4. Combining: $\overline{\lambda \|v\|^2} = \bar{\lambda}\|v\|^2$. But also $\overline{\lambda \|v\|^2} = \bar{\lambda}\|v\|^2$ (since $\|v\|^2$ is real). From Steps 2 and 3: $\lambda \|v\|^2 = \bar{\lambda}\|v\|^2$. Since $\|v\|^2 \neq 0$, we get $\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$, so $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

∎

3. Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal

Theorem — Orthogonality of eigenvectors

If $A$ is symmetric and $u, v$ are eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues $\lambda \neq \mu$, then $u$ and $v$ are orthogonal: $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.

Proof

Step 1. Compute $\langle Au, v \rangle = \langle \lambda u, v \rangle = \lambda \langle u, v \rangle$.

Step 2. Since $A = A^T$, we have $\langle Au, v \rangle = u^T A^T v = u^T A v = \langle u, Av \rangle = \langle u, \mu v \rangle = \mu \langle u, v \rangle$.

Step 3. Therefore $\lambda \langle u, v \rangle = \mu \langle u, v \rangle$, so $(\lambda - \mu)\langle u, v \rangle = 0$. Since $\lambda \neq \mu$, we conclude $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.

∎

This is remarkable: the geometry (orthogonality) comes for free from the algebra (symmetry). For a non-symmetric matrix, eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent (Phase 6) but need not be orthogonal.

4. The spectral theorem

Theorem — Spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices

Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ be symmetric. Then there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ ($Q^TQ = I$) such that:

$$A = Q \Lambda Q^T$$

where $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n)$ with all $\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}$. The columns of $Q$ form an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors.

Proof (by induction on $n$)

Step 1. Base case ($n = 1$). A $1 \times 1$ matrix $(a)$ is already diagonal with $Q = (1)$.

Step 2. Existence of a real eigenvalue. The characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I)$ has degree $n$ with real coefficients. By the result of Section 2, all roots are real. Let $\lambda_1$ be one such root, and let $v_1$ be a corresponding eigenvector with $\|v_1\| = 1$.

Step 3. The orthogonal complement is $A$-invariant. Let $W = v_1^\perp = \{w \in \mathbb{R}^n : \langle w, v_1 \rangle = 0\}$. We claim that if $w \in W$, then $Aw \in W$. Indeed:

$$\langle Aw, v_1 \rangle = \langle w, A^T v_1 \rangle = \langle w, Av_1 \rangle = \langle w, \lambda_1 v_1 \rangle = \lambda_1 \langle w, v_1 \rangle = 0$$

so $Aw \perp v_1$, confirming $Aw \in W$. This is where symmetry is essential — we used $A = A^T$.

Step 4. Restriction to $W$. The restriction $A|_W : W \to W$ is a symmetric linear operator on the $(n-1)$-dimensional space $W$ (equipped with the restricted inner product). Choose any orthonormal basis of $W$; the matrix of $A|_W$ in this basis is symmetric (the entries are $\langle Aw_i, w_j \rangle = \langle w_i, Aw_j \rangle$).

Step 5. Induction. By the induction hypothesis, there exists an orthonormal basis $(v_2, \dots, v_n)$ of $W$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A|_W$. Then $(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n)$ is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.

Step 6. Matrix form. Set $Q = (v_1 \mid v_2 \mid \cdots \mid v_n)$. Since the columns are orthonormal, $Q^TQ = I$, so $Q$ is orthogonal. Then $AQ = Q\Lambda$ gives $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$.

∎

Note the contrast with page 02: there we diagonalised bilinear forms by congruence ($P^TMP$), and $P$ was merely invertible. Here we diagonalise by orthogonal similarity ($Q^TAQ = Q^{-1}AQ$), and the diagonalising matrix is orthogonal. Congruence and similarity coincide when the change-of-basis matrix is orthogonal.

5. Normal matrices

Symmetry is not the most general condition guaranteeing diagonalisability. A matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is normal if it commutes with its transpose:

$$A^T A = A A^T$$

Symmetric matrices ($A = A^T$) are normal, as are orthogonal matrices ($A^T = A^{-1}$) and skew-symmetric matrices ($A^T = -A$). Over $\mathbb{C}$, the condition becomes $A^*A = AA^*$ (where $A^* = \bar{A}^T$), and the relevant class is Hermitian matrices ($A = A^*$).

Theorem — Spectral theorem for normal matrices (stated without proof)

A complex matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$ is unitarily diagonalisable (i.e. $A = U\Lambda U^*$ with $U$ unitary) if and only if $A$ is normal ($A^*A = AA^*$).

We state this without proof — the argument requires Schur's triangularisation theorem (every complex matrix is unitarily triangularisable) and the observation that a normal upper-triangular matrix must be diagonal. The real symmetric case we proved above is the most important special case.

6. Spectral decomposition

The factorisation $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ can be rewritten in a revealing way. Let $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $A$, and let $E_i$ be the eigenspace for $\lambda_i$. Define $P_i$ as the orthogonal projection onto $E_i$ (Phase 8).

Theorem — Spectral decomposition

If $A$ is a real symmetric matrix with distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k$ and corresponding orthogonal projections $P_1, \dots, P_k$, then:

$$A = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \lambda_i P_i$$

with $P_i P_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$ and $P_1 + \cdots + P_k = I$.

Proof

Step 1. By the spectral theorem, $\mathbb{R}^n = E_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus E_k$ with the $E_i$ mutually orthogonal.

Step 2. Every $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$ decomposes uniquely as $v = v_1 + \cdots + v_k$ with $v_i \in E_i$. The orthogonal projection $P_i$ satisfies $P_i(v) = v_i$.

Step 3. Since $Av_i = \lambda_i v_i$:

$$Av = A(v_1 + \cdots + v_k) = \lambda_1 v_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k v_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i P_i(v)$$

This holds for all $v$, so $A = \sum \lambda_i P_i$.

Step 4. Since $E_i \perp E_j$ for $i \neq j$, projecting onto $E_i$ then $E_j$ gives zero: $P_j P_i = 0$. And $\sum P_i = I$ because every $v$ is the sum of its components.

∎

This is why the theorem is called "spectral": the eigenvalues $\{\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k\}$ form the spectrum of $A$, and the matrix decomposes into a weighted sum of projections onto the corresponding eigenspaces — like light decomposing into a spectrum of colours. The analogy is not accidental: in quantum mechanics, the spectrum of a Hermitian operator gives the observable values of a physical measurement.

Historical note

Augustin-Louis Cauchy proved in 1829 that real symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and are diagonalisable — the first version of the spectral theorem. Charles Hermite extended the result to complex Hermitian matrices in 1855. David Hilbert (1904) proved the spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators on infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, providing the mathematical foundation for quantum mechanics. The term "spectrum" was introduced by Hilbert, inspired by the discrete spectral lines of atomic emission.

What breaks

Without symmetry. A non-symmetric real matrix may have complex eigenvalues (e.g. rotations), or may fail to be diagonalisable entirely (e.g. $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$). Without diagonalisability, the best you can do is the Jordan normal form (Phase 6), which involves Jordan blocks rather than a clean diagonal.

Over $\mathbb{C}$ with $A = A^T$ instead of $A = A^*$. Complex symmetric matrices (satisfying $A = A^T$ but not $A = \bar{A}^T$) are not necessarily diagonalisable. For example, $A = \begin{pmatrix}1&i\\i&-1\end{pmatrix}$ satisfies $A = A^T$ but $A^2 = 0$ and $A \neq 0$, so $A$ is not diagonalisable. The correct condition over $\mathbb{C}$ is Hermitian: $A = A^* = \bar{A}^T$.

Without finite dimension. In infinite-dimensional spaces, the spectral theorem still holds for compact self-adjoint operators (Hilbert, 1904), but the spectrum may also contain a "continuous" part with no eigenvectors — only generalised eigenfunctions (e.g. the position operator in quantum mechanics has "eigenstates" $\delta(x - x_0)$ which are not proper functions).

Every real symmetric matrix is orthogonally diagonalisable: symmetry guarantees real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors, and a clean decomposition into independent spectral components.
