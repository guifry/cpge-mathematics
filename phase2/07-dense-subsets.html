Dense Subsets

$\mathbb{Q}$ is Everywhere and Nowhere — Phase 2, Page 7

The paradox

The problem

Over the course of Phase 0 and Phase 2, we've built a paradox.

On one hand (page 03 of Phase 0): $\mathbb{Q}$ has holes. $\sqrt{2}$ is missing, the IVT fails in $\mathbb{Q}$, Cauchy sequences can converge to nothing. You need $\mathbb{R}$ to do analysis.

On the other hand: between any two real numbers, there's a rational number. And between any two rational numbers, there's an irrational number. The rationals are "everywhere" on the number line — you can approximate any real number as closely as you want with fractions.

So $\mathbb{Q}$ is simultaneously full of holes and everywhere dense. How can both be true?

What "dense" means

A set $D \subset \mathbb{R}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$ if for every open interval $(a, b)$ with $a < b$, there exists an element of $D$ in $(a, b)$.

Equivalently: every real number can be approximated arbitrarily well by elements of $D$. For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $d \in D$ with $|x - d| < \varepsilon$.

The two formulations are the same statement wearing different clothes. The first says "no interval is empty of $D$." The second says "no real number is isolated from $D$." Pick whichever is more natural for the proof at hand.

The rationals are dense

Theorem 1 — Density of $\mathbb{Q}$

$\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$. Between any two distinct real numbers, there exists a rational number.

This is sometimes called a consequence of the Archimedean property, which states: for any positive real number $\varepsilon$, there exists a natural number $n$ with $1/n < \varepsilon$. That sounds obvious, but it's actually a consequence of completeness — it fails in certain ordered fields. We use it here without further comment.

Proof

Let $a < b$ be real numbers. We need to find a rational $p/q$ with $a < p/q < b$.

Step 1. Choose the denominator. Since $b - a > 0$, the Archimedean property gives us a positive integer $q$ with $1/q < b - a$. In other words, the spacing $1/q$ between consecutive multiples of $1/q$ is smaller than the width of the interval $(a, b)$.

Step 2. Choose the numerator. Consider the multiples of $1/q$: $\ldots, -2/q, -1/q, 0, 1/q, 2/q, \ldots$ These are spaced $1/q$ apart along the real line. Since the interval $(a, b)$ has width greater than $1/q$, it must contain at least one of these multiples.

Formally: let $p$ be the smallest integer with $p > aq$. Then $p/q > a$. And since $p$ is the smallest such integer, we have $p - 1 \leq aq$, so

$$\frac{p}{q} = \frac{p-1}{q} + \frac{1}{q} \leq a + \frac{1}{q} < a + (b - a) = b$$

Therefore $a < p/q < b$.

∎

Why the proof works

The strategy is simple: make the grid fine enough, and the interval can't dodge it. Choosing $q$ large enough ensures the rational grid $\{k/q : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ has spacing smaller than the interval width. Then some grid point must land inside.

Notice we never needed to know what $a$ and $b$ are. They could be irrational, transcendental, whatever. The argument works uniformly. That's the power of the Archimedean property: no matter how tiny $b - a$ is, you can always find a rational grid fine enough to thread a point through it.

The irrationals are dense too

Theorem 2 — Density of $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$

The irrationals are dense in $\mathbb{R}$. Between any two distinct real numbers, there exists an irrational number.

Proof

Let $a < b$. Consider the shifted interval $(a - \sqrt{2},\; b - \sqrt{2})$. By Theorem 1, there exists a rational $r$ with $a - \sqrt{2} < r < b - \sqrt{2}$.

Now set $x = r + \sqrt{2}$. Adding $\sqrt{2}$ to both sides of the inequality gives $a < x < b$. And $x$ is irrational, because a rational plus an irrational is always irrational (if $r + \sqrt{2}$ were rational, then $\sqrt{2} = (r + \sqrt{2}) - r$ would be rational, contradicting Phase 0 page 3).

∎

This proof is a lovely trick: to find an irrational in $(a, b)$, shift the window into $\mathbb{Q}$-territory, find a rational there, then shift back. The irrational $\sqrt{2}$ acts as a translation that moves every rational to an irrational.

Resolving the paradox

So $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense: rationals are everywhere, approximating everything, filling every interval. And $\mathbb{Q}$ is incomplete: it has holes where limits fall through. How can both be true?

Because density and completeness are different properties.

Density means "good enough for approximation." For any real number you name, I can find a rational as close as you like. Completeness means "good enough for limits." Every Cauchy sequence converges, every bounded monotone sequence has a limit, every supremum exists. These are not the same demand.

An analogy: think of a mesh net. It covers the whole area — everywhere you look, there's a strand of net nearby. But things fall through the holes. The net is dense (it's everywhere) but not complete (it can't hold everything). $\mathbb{Q}$ is the mesh net. $\mathbb{R}$ is solid fabric.

Or in sequence language: every real number is the limit of a sequence of rationals (that's density), but not every Cauchy sequence of rationals converges within $\mathbb{Q}$ (that's incompleteness). The sequence $1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, \ldots$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{Q}$, consists entirely of rationals, but its limit $\sqrt{2}$ is outside $\mathbb{Q}$. Dense: every point is approachable. Incomplete: some approaches lead nowhere.

Why density matters for analysis

Density is not a curiosity. It's what makes computation possible.

Decimal expansions work because of density. Writing $\pi = 3.14159\ldots$ means constructing a sequence of rationals ($3$, $3.1$, $3.14$, $3.141$, $\ldots$) that converges to $\pi$. This is density restated: every real number is the limit of a sequence of rationals.

Computers rely on density. No computer stores $\sqrt{2}$ or $\pi$ exactly. Every floating-point number is rational. Density guarantees that for any real quantity you need, a rational approximation is available and can be made as precise as your hardware allows.

Approximation theory is density in function space. The Weierstrass approximation theorem (Phase 4) says that polynomials — which use only rational arithmetic — can approximate any continuous function on a closed interval as closely as desired. That's the density idea lifted from numbers to functions.

Historical note

The density of $\mathbb{Q}$ was known implicitly since the Greeks. Eudoxus' theory of proportions (~370 BC) essentially handles the idea that rationals can approximate any magnitude. But the distinction between "dense" and "complete" remained invisible for over two thousand years. Mathematicians vaguely assumed $\mathbb{Q}$ was "enough" and only encountered trouble occasionally — $\sqrt{2}$, the irrationality of $\pi$, stubborn series that refused to converge to anything rational.

The rigorous separation of density from completeness came in the 1870s, when Dedekind and Cantor independently formalised the real numbers. Dedekind's construction (cuts) builds $\mathbb{R}$ by filling in exactly the holes that density leaves behind. Cantor's construction (equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences) makes the same repair from a different angle. Both constructions are, in essence, the answer to the paradox on this page: $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense but not complete, so we complete it and call the result $\mathbb{R}$.

  • Cauchy criterion: convergence without knowing the limit (page 05)
  • IVT: continuous functions don't skip values (page 06)
  • Dense subsets: $\mathbb{Q}$ approximates everything but can't hold limits (this page)

These are the foundations of analysis. You can reason about limits, convergence, continuity, and the structure of the real line. But you can't yet describe how fast things change. For that, you need rates of change — and that's what derivatives are.

What breaks

If $\mathbb{Q}$ weren't dense, you couldn't approximate real numbers with fractions. Decimal expansions wouldn't work. Numerical computation — everything from pocket calculators to finite element simulations — would be impossible, because every computed number is rational and you'd have no guarantee it's close to the real answer.

Conversely, if $\mathbb{Q}$ were dense and complete, it would be $\mathbb{R}$. Indeed, the completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ — the smallest complete ordered field containing it — is precisely $\mathbb{R}$. The reals are what you get when you take the rationals and demand that limits can't escape.

$\mathbb{Q}$ is everywhere on the number line — close enough to approximate anything — but full of invisible holes where limits fall through.
