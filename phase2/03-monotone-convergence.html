The Monotone Convergence Theorem

Why bounded monotone sequences must converge — Phase 2, Page 3

The problem

The problem

Define a sequence by $a_1 = 1$ and

$$a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(a_n + \frac{2}{a_n}\right)$$

This is the Babylonian method for computing $\sqrt{2}$. The idea is ancient: if $a_n$ is too big (bigger than $\sqrt{2}$), then $2/a_n$ is too small, and their average is a better approximation. Let's compute:

$n$$a_n$
$1$$1$
$2$$1.5$
$3$$1.41\overline{6}$
$4$$1.414215\ldots$

By step 4, we already have five correct decimal places of $\sqrt{2} = 1.41421356\ldots$. The terms are clearly homing in on $\sqrt{2}$. But how do you prove this sequence converges, without already knowing the limit?

Look at the pattern: after the first step, the terms are decreasing ($1.5 > 1.41\overline{6} > 1.414215\ldots$) and positive (bounded below by 0). Intuitively, a sequence that keeps going down but can't go below zero must eventually settle somewhere. But "must" needs a proof.

The vocabulary

Before the theorem, we need four definitions. None are deep — they just pin down the words precisely.

A sequence $(a_n)$ is monotone increasing if $a_{n+1} \geq a_n$ for all $n$. Each term is at least as big as the one before it. The sequence goes up (or stays flat), never dips.

A sequence $(a_n)$ is monotone decreasing if $a_{n+1} \leq a_n$ for all $n$. Each term is at most as big as the one before it. It goes down (or stays flat), never rises.

A sequence is bounded above if there exists a real number $M$ such that $a_n \leq M$ for all $n$. Think of $M$ as a ceiling — no term ever punches through it.

A sequence is bounded below if there exists a real number $m$ such that $a_n \geq m$ for all $n$. Think of $m$ as a floor.

A sequence that is both bounded above and bounded below is simply called bounded.

The theorem

Monotone Convergence Theorem

Every bounded monotone sequence converges. Specifically:

  • If $(a_n)$ is increasing and bounded above, then $\displaystyle\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \sup\{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.
  • If $(a_n)$ is decreasing and bounded below, then $\displaystyle\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \inf\{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.

The theorem doesn't just say "the limit exists" — it tells you exactly what the limit is. An increasing sequence converges to its supremum (the least upper bound of all its terms). A decreasing sequence converges to its infimum (the greatest lower bound).

Why this needs completeness

The proof will use the supremum — the least upper bound of the set of terms. And the supremum only exists because $\mathbb{R}$ is complete (page 01). This isn't a technicality. It's the entire point.

Consider our Babylonian sequence in $\mathbb{Q}$ instead of $\mathbb{R}$. Every term $a_n$ is rational (you can check: the formula only uses addition, division, and multiplication, starting from the integer 1). The sequence is bounded and monotone decreasing (after $a_2$). But its limit is $\sqrt{2}$, which is not in $\mathbb{Q}$. The sequence is bounded, monotone, and doesn't converge in $\mathbb{Q}$.

The Monotone Convergence Theorem is false in $\mathbb{Q}$. What makes it true in $\mathbb{R}$ is completeness — the guarantee that every non-empty set bounded above has a supremum. Remove completeness, and the theorem collapses. Completeness is doing the work.

The proof (increasing case)

Proof

Let $(a_n)$ be an increasing sequence that is bounded above. We must show it converges, and find its limit.

Step 1. Build the set. Let $S = \{a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots\}$ — the set of all terms. $S$ is non-empty (it contains at least $a_1$) and bounded above (by hypothesis).

Step 2. Take the supremum. By the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ (page 01), $L = \sup(S)$ exists. This $L$ is a real number. It is an upper bound for $S$ (meaning $a_n \leq L$ for all $n$), and it is the smallest such upper bound.

Step 3. Show $a_n \to L$. Fix any $\varepsilon > 0$, no matter how small. We need to find an $N$ such that $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$.

Since $L$ is the least upper bound, $L - \varepsilon$ is strictly less than $L$, so $L - \varepsilon$ is not an upper bound for $S$. (If it were, $L$ wouldn't be the least one.) Therefore some term exceeds it: there exists $N$ such that

$$a_N > L - \varepsilon$$

Now use monotonicity: since the sequence is increasing, for all $n \geq N$,

$$a_n \geq a_N > L - \varepsilon$$

And since $L$ is an upper bound for $S$,

$$a_n \leq L < L + \varepsilon$$

Combining these two inequalities: for all $n \geq N$,

$$L - \varepsilon < a_n \leq L < L + \varepsilon$$

which means $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$. Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, we have $a_n \to L = \sup(S)$.

∎

The decreasing case is identical, with $\inf$ replacing $\sup$ and all inequalities reversed. You can also reduce it to the increasing case by considering $(-a_n)$.

Unpacking the proof

The key move is in Step 3: "$L - \varepsilon$ is not an upper bound." This is the supremum doing its job. The supremum is the tightest possible ceiling — anything even slightly lower fails to contain the set. So there must be some term $a_N$ that pokes above $L - \varepsilon$.

From that point on, monotonicity takes over. Since the sequence only goes up, every term after $a_N$ is at least as large. So the entire tail of the sequence is trapped in the corridor $(L - \varepsilon, L]$. It can't escape upward (because $L$ is an upper bound) and it can't fall back (because the sequence is increasing).

That's it. The supremum provides the target, monotonicity locks the sequence into the corridor, and $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary. Three ingredients, one conclusion.

Historical note

Euler and the Bernoullis (18th century) freely computed with sequences, assuming limits existed whenever the terms "looked like they were settling down." They summed divergent series, interchanged limits, and generally treated convergence as obvious. It usually worked, because they had good instincts. But sometimes it didn't, and then the errors were baffling.

The Monotone Convergence Theorem was formalised as part of the great rigourisation of analysis by Bolzano (1817) and Weierstrass (1860s). Their programme replaced intuition with proofs: show that the limit exists, don't just assume it does because the terms are getting closer together. The MCT was a cornerstone of this programme — a clean, checkable criterion for convergence that doesn't require you to guess the limit first.

Back to the Babylonian method

Now we have the tools to prove the opening problem. The sequence $a_1 = 1$, $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 2/a_n)$ converges, and its limit is $\sqrt{2}$. The proof has four steps.

Step 1: $a_n > 0$ for all $n$. By induction. $a_1 = 1 > 0$. If $a_n > 0$, then $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 2/a_n)$ is a sum of two positive numbers divided by 2, so $a_{n+1} > 0$.

Step 2: $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$ for $n \geq 2$. By the AM-GM inequality: for any positive $x$,

$$\frac{x + 2/x}{2} \geq \sqrt{x \cdot \frac{2}{x}} = \sqrt{2}$$

So $a_{n+1} \geq \sqrt{2}$ whenever $a_n > 0$. Since $a_1 > 0$, this gives $a_n \geq \sqrt{2}$ for all $n \geq 2$.

Step 3: the sequence is decreasing for $n \geq 2$. We need $a_{n+1} \leq a_n$, i.e.

$$\frac{1}{2}\left(a_n + \frac{2}{a_n}\right) \leq a_n$$

Rearranging: $2/a_n \leq a_n$, which means $a_n^2 \geq 2$, which is true for $n \geq 2$ by Step 2.

Step 4: apply the MCT and find the limit. The sequence $(a_n)_{n \geq 2}$ is decreasing and bounded below by $\sqrt{2}$. By the Monotone Convergence Theorem, it converges to some limit $L$.

To find $L$: take the limit of both sides of the recurrence $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + 2/a_n)$. Since $a_n \to L$ and $a_{n+1} \to L$:

$$L = \frac{1}{2}\left(L + \frac{2}{L}\right)$$

Multiply both sides by $2L$: $2L^2 = L^2 + 2$, so $L^2 = 2$, giving $L = \sqrt{2}$ (positive, since all terms are positive).

Notice the structure: the MCT told us the limit exists without us knowing what it was. Only afterwards did we identify the limit by solving the fixed-point equation. This is the MCT's power — existence first, computation second.

What breaks without monotonicity

What breaks

The sequence $a_n = (-1)^n$ is bounded: every term is either $-1$ or $1$, so it sits inside $[-1, 1]$. But it's not monotone — it alternates up-down-up-down forever. And it doesn't converge. It oscillates between $-1$ and $1$ without ever settling.

Boundedness alone is not enough. The sequence needs to be headed somewhere — that's what monotonicity provides. Without a consistent direction, a bounded sequence can wander back and forth inside its bounds indefinitely.

What breaks without boundedness

What breaks

The sequence $a_n = n$ is monotone increasing: $1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots$ — every term is larger than the one before. But it's not bounded above. There is no ceiling, so it grows without limit. It diverges to infinity.

Monotonicity alone is not enough. A sequence with a consistent direction but no bound just marches off to infinity. The bound is the wall that forces the sequence to compress and converge rather than escape.

The workhorse of convergence proofs

Most convergence proofs you'll encounter in first-year analysis reduce to the MCT. The pattern is always the same:

  1. Show the sequence is monotone (usually by induction on $a_{n+1} - a_n$ or $a_{n+1}/a_n$).
  2. Show it's bounded (usually by induction, or by a clever inequality).
  3. Conclude: it converges, by the MCT.
  4. If you want the limit, pass the recurrence to the limit and solve.

The beauty is that step 3 is free — you don't need to guess the limit, construct an $\varepsilon$-$N$ argument, or do anything clever. The MCT hands you convergence the moment you've checked two properties. This is why it's called the workhorse: it does the heavy lifting so you don't have to.

It's also the reason completeness matters in practice, not just in theory. Every time you use the MCT, you're invoking the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$. Every time a student writes "bounded and monotone, therefore convergent," the supremum axiom is silently at work underneath.

A sequence that keeps going up but can't go past a ceiling must eventually settle — and the ceiling it settles at is the supremum.
