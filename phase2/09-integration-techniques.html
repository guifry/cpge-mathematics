Integration Techniques

Phase 2 — Calculus, Page 9

The problem

The Fundamental Theorem (page 8) says: to compute $\int_a^b f(x)\, dx$, find an antiderivative $G$ with $G' = f$, then evaluate $G(b) - G(a)$. Simple enough in principle. And sometimes in practice: $\int x^2\, dx = \frac{x^3}{3} + C$ because $\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{x^3}{3}\right) = x^2$. You just reverse the power rule.

But what about $\int 2x \cos(x^2)\, dx$? Or $\int x e^x\, dx$? Or $\int \sin^2(x)\, dx$? None of these match any derivative we know directly. We need systematic methods for finding antiderivatives — and the key insight is that those methods come from reading the differentiation rules (page 2) backwards. Substitution is the chain rule in reverse. Integration by parts is the product rule in reverse. There are no new ideas here — only old ones read through the FTC.

1. The power rule backwards

The simplest antiderivative formula comes from reversing the power rule for differentiation. Since $\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\right) = x^n$, we have immediately:

$$\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \qquad (n \neq -1)$$

The "$+ C$" is because the antiderivative is unique only up to an additive constant — a consequence of the Mean Value Theorem (page 4): if two functions have the same derivative on an interval, they differ by a constant.

The restriction $n \neq -1$ matters: at $n = -1$ the formula gives $\frac{x^0}{0}$, which is undefined. This is the one case the power rule doesn't cover, and it requires a separate answer:

$$\int \frac{1}{x}\, dx = \ln|x| + C$$

(IOU: the natural logarithm will be properly defined via its power series in Phase 3. For now, note that this gap in the power rule is precisely what defines the logarithm — it is the antiderivative that the power rule cannot produce.)

2. Integration by substitution

Theorem (Substitution Rule)

Let $g$ be differentiable on $[a,b]$ with continuous derivative $g'$, and let $f$ be continuous on the range of $g$. Then:

$$\int_a^b f\!\big(g(x)\big) \cdot g'(x)\, dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\, du$$
Proof

Since $f$ is continuous, by the FTC (Part 1, page 8) it has an antiderivative: let $F$ be a function satisfying $F' = f$.

Consider the composite function $F(g(x))$. By the chain rule (page 2):

$$\frac{d}{dx}\, F\!\big(g(x)\big) = F'\!\big(g(x)\big) \cdot g'(x) = f\!\big(g(x)\big) \cdot g'(x)$$

So $F(g(x))$ is an antiderivative of $f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$. Applying the FTC (Part 2):

$$\int_a^b f\!\big(g(x)\big) \cdot g'(x)\, dx = F\!\big(g(b)\big) - F\!\big(g(a)\big)$$

But $F$ is also an antiderivative of $f$, so applying the FTC again on the right-hand side:

$$F\!\big(g(b)\big) - F\!\big(g(a)\big) = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\, du$$
∎

The intuition: when you see a composite function $f(g(x))$ multiplied by $g'(x)$, the $g'(x)\, dx$ "becomes" $du$ under the substitution $u = g(x)$. This isn't a formal manipulation of differentials — it is the chain rule, which created that $g'(x)$ factor when we differentiated $F(g(x))$, now read in reverse. Substitution undoes exactly what the chain rule did.

In practice, you look at the integrand and ask: is there a function composed with something, multiplied by the derivative of that something? If so, set $u$ equal to the "inner" function. The $g'(x)\, dx$ factor gets absorbed into $du$, leaving a simpler integral in $u$ alone. After integrating, substitute back. For definite integrals, you can either substitute back at the end or transform the bounds directly (as in example (b) below) — the latter is cleaner.

3. Examples of substitution

Example (a). Compute $\int 2x \cos(x^2)\, dx$.

Let $u = x^2$, so $du = 2x\, dx$. The integral becomes:

$$\int \cos(u)\, du = \sin(u) + C = \sin(x^2) + C$$

Verification: $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x$. ✓

Example (b). Compute $\int_0^1 x\sqrt{1 - x^2}\, dx$.

Let $u = 1 - x^2$, so $du = -2x\, dx$, hence $x\, dx = -\frac{du}{2}$. The bounds transform: when $x = 0$, $u = 1$; when $x = 1$, $u = 0$. The integral becomes:

$$\int_1^0 \sqrt{u} \cdot \left(-\frac{du}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 \sqrt{u}\, du = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$

Example (c). Compute $\int \tan(x)\, dx$.

Write $\tan(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}$. Let $u = \cos(x)$, so $du = -\sin(x)\, dx$. Then:

$$\int \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\, dx = -\int \frac{du}{u} = -\ln|u| + C = -\ln|\cos(x)| + C$$

4. Integration by parts

Theorem (Integration by Parts)

Let $u$ and $v$ be differentiable on $[a,b]$ with continuous derivatives. Then:

$$\int_a^b u(x) \cdot v'(x)\, dx = \Big[u(x)\, v(x)\Big]_a^b - \int_a^b u'(x) \cdot v(x)\, dx$$

In shorthand: $\int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du$.

Proof

By the product rule (page 2):

$$\frac{d}{dx}\Big[u(x)\, v(x)\Big] = u'(x)\, v(x) + u(x)\, v'(x)$$

Integrate both sides from $a$ to $b$:

$$\int_a^b \frac{d}{dx}\Big[u(x)\, v(x)\Big]\, dx = \int_a^b u'(x)\, v(x)\, dx + \int_a^b u(x)\, v'(x)\, dx$$

By the FTC (Part 2), the left side evaluates to $\Big[u(x)\, v(x)\Big]_a^b$. Rearranging:

$$\int_a^b u(x)\, v'(x)\, dx = \Big[u(x)\, v(x)\Big]_a^b - \int_a^b u'(x)\, v(x)\, dx$$
∎

The strategy: integration by parts trades one integral for another. You split the integrand into $u$ (which you will differentiate) and $dv$ (which you will integrate). The hope is that the new integral $\int v\, du$ is simpler than the original $\int u\, dv$. The art lies in choosing $u$ and $dv$ so that differentiating $u$ simplifies it, and integrating $dv$ doesn't make things worse.

5. Examples of integration by parts

Example (a). Compute $\int x e^x\, dx$.

Choose $u = x$ (differentiating simplifies it to $1$) and $dv = e^x\, dx$ (integrating keeps it as $e^x$). Then $du = dx$ and $v = e^x$.

$$\int x e^x\, dx = x e^x - \int e^x\, dx = x e^x - e^x + C = e^x(x - 1) + C$$

Verification: $\frac{d}{dx}\big[e^x(x-1)\big] = e^x(x-1) + e^x = xe^x$. ✓

Example (b). Compute $\int \ln(x)\, dx$.

Choose $u = \ln(x)$ and $dv = dx$. Then $du = \frac{1}{x}\, dx$ and $v = x$.

$$\int \ln(x)\, dx = x\ln(x) - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x}\, dx = x\ln(x) - \int dx = x\ln(x) - x + C$$

(IOU on $\ln$ as before — Phase 3 will ground it via power series.)

Example (c). Compute $\int \sin^2(x)\, dx$.

Choose $u = \sin(x)$, $dv = \sin(x)\, dx$. Then $du = \cos(x)\, dx$, $v = -\cos(x)$.

$$\int \sin^2(x)\, dx = -\sin(x)\cos(x) + \int \cos^2(x)\, dx$$

The new integral looks no simpler — but use the identity $\cos^2(x) = 1 - \sin^2(x)$:

$$\int \sin^2(x)\, dx = -\sin(x)\cos(x) + \int \big(1 - \sin^2(x)\big)\, dx = -\sin(x)\cos(x) + x - \int \sin^2(x)\, dx$$

The original integral has reappeared on the right. Call it $I$:

$$I = -\sin(x)\cos(x) + x - I$$
$$2I = x - \sin(x)\cos(x)$$
$$I = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{\sin(2x)}{4} + C$$

(using the double-angle identity $\sin(2x) = 2\sin(x)\cos(x)$). This trick — applying integration by parts and then solving algebraically for the original integral — is a standard technique, not a one-off coincidence.

6. Differentiation rules read backwards

Every integration technique we have seen is a differentiation rule from page 2, reversed through the FTC:

Differentiation rule Integration technique
Power rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(x^{n+1}) = (n+1)x^n$ $\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$
Chain rule: $\frac{d}{dx}F(g(x)) = f(g(x))\, g'(x)$ Substitution
Product rule: $\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u'v + uv'$ Integration by parts

There are no "new" ideas in integration techniques. Every method is an old idea — a differentiation rule — read backwards through the Fundamental Theorem. The difficulty of integration is not conceptual but tactical: recognising which rule to read backwards, and choosing the right decomposition of the integrand.

7. Phase 2 recap

In nine pages we built calculus from scratch:

  1. The derivative, from the tangent problem and the limit definition.
  2. Differentiation rules — sum, product, quotient, chain — proved from that definition.
  3. Trigonometric derivatives, proved geometrically from the unit circle.
  4. The Mean Value Theorem, connecting derivatives to function behaviour.
  5. L'Hôpital's rule, turning the MVT into a limit-computation tool.
  6. The Riemann integral, from Archimedes' area problem and the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.
  7. Integrability — which functions have a well-defined integral.
  8. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — the single deepest result in the subject, connecting derivatives and integrals.
  9. Integration techniques — differentiation rules read backwards through the FTC.

Every formula was derived. Every theorem was proved. Nothing was assumed from lycée or taken on faith.

Phase 3 (Taylor series and power series) will use both derivatives and integrals heavily. Taylor's theorem requires repeated differentiation. The integral form of the remainder requires integration by parts. Power series define $e^x$, $\sin$, $\cos$, and $\ln$ rigorously — finally paying off the IOUs accumulated across Phase 2. All of this now stands on solid ground.

Historical note

Both the substitution rule and integration by parts were known to Euler (1730s-1760s) and used extensively by the Bernoulli family. They treated these as algebraic manipulations of "infinitesimals" — $dx$, $du$ — without worrying about rigour. The formal proofs we gave, deriving both rules from the FTC and the differentiation rules, are essentially Cauchy's approach from his 1823 Résumé des leçons. Cauchy was the first to bother proving these "obvious" manipulations — and in doing so, he discovered that they require hypotheses (continuity of $g'$ for substitution, differentiability of both $u$ and $v$ for parts) that the infinitesimal heuristics had silently assumed.

What breaks

Substitution without continuous $g'$: the proof uses the FTC to evaluate $\int_a^b \frac{d}{dx}F(g(x))\, dx$, which requires $F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ to be Riemann integrable. If $g'$ is not continuous, the composite $f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ may fail to be integrable, and the substitution formula breaks down.

Integration by parts with a bad choice of $u$ and $dv$: the formula is always true (it's a theorem, not a heuristic), but a poor choice makes the new integral harder than the original. For instance, choosing $u = e^x$ and $dv = x\, dx$ in $\int x e^x\, dx$ gives $\int \frac{x^2}{2} e^x\, dx$ — a true equation but a useless one. There is no algorithm for choosing the right split; it is a skill developed through practice.

Functions with no elementary antiderivative: some integrands — $e^{-x^2}$, $\frac{\sin x}{x}$, $\frac{1}{\ln x}$ — have no antiderivative expressible in terms of elementary functions (polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, trigonometric functions, and their compositions). No amount of substitution or parts will help. The integral exists (the FTC guarantees it for continuous functions) but cannot be written in closed form. These integrals require either numerical methods or new named functions (the error function $\operatorname{erf}$, the sine integral $\operatorname{Si}$, etc.).

Every integration technique is a differentiation rule read backwards — substitution undoes the chain rule, integration by parts undoes the product rule.
