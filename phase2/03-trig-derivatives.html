Derivatives of Sine and Cosine

Phase 2 — Calculus, Page 3

The problem

We can differentiate polynomials using the power rule. But sine and cosine are not polynomials — they're defined geometrically, via the unit circle (Phase 0, page 5). To differentiate $\sin(x)$, we must compute:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin(x)}{h}$$

Expanding $\sin(x+h)$ requires the angle addition formula, which produces terms involving $\frac{\sin h}{h}$ and $\frac{\cos h - 1}{h}$. Everything hinges on two limits. Can we compute them from what we already know?

1. The angle addition formulae

Before we touch derivatives, we need to expand $\sin(\alpha + \beta)$. The formula comes directly from the unit circle.

Consider two angles $\alpha$ and $\beta$. On the unit circle, the point at angle $\alpha + \beta$ has coordinates $(\cos(\alpha+\beta),\, \sin(\alpha+\beta))$. We can reach this point by first rotating to angle $\alpha$, then rotating by $\beta$. The rotation matrix for angle $\beta$ applied to the point $(\cos\alpha,\, \sin\alpha)$ gives:

$$\begin{pmatrix} \cos\beta & -\sin\beta \\ \sin\beta & \cos\beta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \cos\alpha \\ \sin\alpha \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta \\ \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta \end{pmatrix}$$

Reading off the coordinates:

Theorem — Angle addition formulae
$$\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$
$$\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$

These hold for all real $\alpha, \beta$. They follow from the unit circle definition of sine and cosine (Phase 0) and the geometry of rotation.

Note: In Phase 3, we will re-derive these algebraically from the power series definitions of sine and cosine. For now, the geometric proof is rigorous.

2. The key limit: $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$

This is the crux. Everything else follows from it. We prove it using the geometric squeeze argument — combining the unit circle (Phase 0) with the squeeze theorem (Phase 1).

Consider a small angle $\theta > 0$ from the positive $x$-axis on the unit circle. Let $O$ be the origin, $A = (1, 0)$, and $B = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ the point at angle $\theta$. Let $C = (1, \tan\theta)$ be where the tangent line at $A$ meets the ray $OB$ extended. Three regions nest inside each other:

  • Triangle $OAB$ (inscribed in the sector): area $= \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot \sin\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\sin\theta$.
  • Circular sector $OAB$: area $= \tfrac{\theta}{2\pi} \cdot \pi \cdot 1^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\theta$.
  • Triangle $OAC$ (circumscribing the sector): area $= \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot \tan\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}\tan\theta$.

Since triangle $OAB \subset$ sector $OAB \subset$ triangle $OAC$:

$$\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\tan\theta$$
Proof — $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$

For $0 < \theta < \frac{\pi}{2}$, we have $\sin\theta > 0$. Divide the inequality $\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\tan\theta$ throughout by $\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta$:

$$1 \leq \frac{\theta}{\sin\theta} \leq \frac{1}{\cos\theta}$$

All three expressions are positive, so we may invert (flipping inequalities):

$$\cos\theta \leq \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \leq 1$$

Now take $\theta \to 0^+$. The left bound: $\cos\theta \to \cos 0 = 1$ (continuity of cosine on the unit circle, Phase 0). The right bound is the constant $1$. Both bounds converge to $1$.

By the squeeze theorem (Phase 1):

$$\lim_{\theta \to 0^+} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$$

For $\theta \to 0^-$: let $\theta = -\phi$ with $\phi > 0$. Then $\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = \frac{\sin(-\phi)}{-\phi} = \frac{-\sin\phi}{-\phi} = \frac{\sin\phi}{\phi}$, using $\sin(-\phi) = -\sin\phi$ (odd symmetry from Phase 0). So the left-hand limit equals the right-hand limit.

$$\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$$
∎

Observe the dependency chain: unit circle geometry (Phase 0) gave us the area inequality. The squeeze theorem (Phase 1) converted that inequality into a limit. No black boxes.

3. The second key limit: $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\cos\theta - 1}{\theta} = 0$

Proof — $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\cos\theta - 1}{\theta} = 0$

Multiply numerator and denominator by $(\cos\theta + 1)$:

$$\frac{\cos\theta - 1}{\theta} \cdot \frac{\cos\theta + 1}{\cos\theta + 1} = \frac{\cos^2\theta - 1}{\theta(\cos\theta + 1)}$$

By the Pythagorean identity $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ (Phase 0), we have $\cos^2\theta - 1 = -\sin^2\theta$. So:

$$\frac{\cos\theta - 1}{\theta} = \frac{-\sin^2\theta}{\theta(\cos\theta + 1)} = -\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta + 1}$$

As $\theta \to 0$: the first factor $\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \to 1$ (just proved). The second factor $\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta + 1} \to \frac{0}{1 + 1} = 0$.

By the product rule for limits:

$$\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\cos\theta - 1}{\theta} = (-1) \cdot 0 = 0$$
∎

4. Derivative of sine

Theorem
$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos(x)$$
Proof

From the limit definition of the derivative:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin(x)}{h}$$

Apply the angle addition formula to $\sin(x+h)$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h - \sin x}{h}$$

Regroup:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \left[\sin x \cdot \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} + \cos x \cdot \frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$

As $h \to 0$: the first term gives $\sin x \cdot 0 = 0$. The second gives $\cos x \cdot 1 = \cos x$.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos(x)$$
∎

5. Derivative of cosine

Theorem
$$\frac{d}{dx}\cos(x) = -\sin(x)$$
Proof
$$\frac{d}{dx}\cos(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cos(x+h) - \cos(x)}{h}$$

Apply the angle addition formula to $\cos(x+h)$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\cos x \cos h - \sin x \sin h - \cos x}{h}$$

Regroup:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \left[\cos x \cdot \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} - \sin x \cdot \frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$

As $h \to 0$: the first term gives $\cos x \cdot 0 = 0$. The second gives $-\sin x \cdot 1 = -\sin x$.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\cos(x) = -\sin(x)$$
∎

6. Derivative of tangent

Since $\tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}$, we apply the quotient rule (Phase 2, page 2):

$$\frac{d}{dx}\tan(x) = \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x}$$
Theorem
$$\frac{d}{dx}\tan(x) = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2(x)$$

Valid wherever $\cos x \neq 0$, i.e. $x \neq \frac{\pi}{2} + k\pi$.

7. The dependency chain

This page used exactly four prior results:

  1. Unit circle definition of $\sin$ and $\cos$ (Phase 0, page 5) — gave us the area comparison and the Pythagorean identity.
  2. $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$ (Phase 0, page 5) — from $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$.
  3. Squeeze theorem (Phase 1, page 2) — converted the geometric inequality into $\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \to 1$.
  4. Limit definition of the derivative (Phase 2, page 1) — the framework within which $\frac{d}{dx}\sin x$ is even a meaningful question.

No circular reasoning. We did not assume the derivative of sine to prove any of the prerequisites. The angle addition formulae came from rotation geometry, the squeeze argument from area comparison, and the derivative from assembling these pieces.

The IOU: In Phase 3 (Taylor series), we will define $\sin$ and $\cos$ purely arithmetically as convergent power series. The derivative will then follow from term-by-term differentiation — no geometry at all. The geometric approach here is rigorous but depends on the unit circle. The series approach will be self-contained.

Historical note

The limit $\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \to 1$ was known intuitively to Euler, who freely manipulated infinitesimals like $\sin(d\theta) = d\theta$. But a rigorous proof had to wait for the 19th century, when Cauchy and Weierstrass replaced infinitesimals with $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ arguments. It is remarkable that this limit — comparing an arc length to a chord — requires the squeeze theorem, an analytical tool, to make rigorous. This is the exact point where geometry hands off to analysis.

What breaks if $\theta$ is in degrees?

If $\theta$ is measured in degrees, the sector area is $\frac{\theta}{360} \cdot \pi$, not $\frac{\theta}{2}$. The squeeze argument then gives $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin(\theta^\circ)}{\theta} = \frac{\pi}{180}$, not $1$.

The derivative of $\sin(x^\circ)$ would be $\frac{\pi}{180}\cos(x^\circ)$. Every differentiation formula for trigonometric functions acquires a factor of $\frac{\pi}{180}$. Radians are not a convention — they are the unique angle measure for which $\frac{d}{dx}\sin x = \cos x$ holds cleanly. This is why mathematicians use radians.

$\frac{d}{dx}\sin x = \cos x$ — proved by squeezing $\frac{\sin h}{h} \to 1$ between a triangle and a sector on the unit circle.
