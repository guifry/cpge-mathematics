L'Hôpital's Rule

Phase 2 — Calculus, Page 5

The problem

Compute $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$. We did this with the squeeze theorem (Phase 1 / Phase 2 page 3). It took a geometric argument and careful bounding. Now consider:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x}, \qquad \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2}.$$

Substituting $x = 0$ gives $\frac{0}{0}$ in every case — meaningless. These are indeterminate forms: both numerator and denominator vanish, and the value of the limit is not determined by this fact alone. Is there a systematic method that handles all such forms at once?

1. Indeterminate forms

If $f(a) = g(a) = 0$, the expression $f(x)/g(x)$ as $x \to a$ takes the form $0/0$. This tells you nothing about the limit. It could be anything:

$$\frac{\sin x}{x} \to 1, \qquad \frac{x^2}{x} \to 0, \qquad \frac{x}{x^2} \to \infty.$$

In each case, both numerator and denominator tend to $0$. The limit depends entirely on how fast each function approaches $0$. If the numerator vanishes at roughly the same rate as the denominator, the ratio stays finite. If the numerator vanishes faster, the ratio tends to $0$. If slower, it blows up.

Rates of change are derivatives. The idea: instead of comparing $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ directly, compare $f'(x)$ and $g'(x)$ — the speeds at which they vanish.

2. L'Hôpital's rule ($0/0$ form)

Theorem — L'Hôpital's rule ($0/0$)

Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable on an open interval containing $a$, except possibly at $a$ itself. Suppose:

  • $f(a) = g(a) = 0$ (or more precisely, $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0$),
  • $g'(x) \neq 0$ for $x$ near $a$ (except possibly at $a$),
  • $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} = L$.

Then:

$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = L.$$
Proof

If $f$ or $g$ is not defined at $a$, extend by setting $f(a) = 0$ and $g(a) = 0$. Since $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 0 = f(a)$ and likewise for $g$, both functions are continuous at $a$.

Take any $x \neq a$ in the interval. The functions $f$ and $g$ are continuous on the closed interval between $a$ and $x$, and differentiable on its interior. Also $g(x) \neq g(a) = 0$ for $x$ sufficiently close to $a$ (because $g'(x) \neq 0$ near $a$ and Rolle's theorem would force a zero of $g'$ between any two zeros of $g$). So we may apply Cauchy's generalised mean value theorem (page 4): there exists $c$ strictly between $a$ and $x$ such that

$$\frac{f(x) - f(a)}{g(x) - g(a)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}.$$

Since $f(a) = g(a) = 0$, this simplifies to

$$\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}.$$

Now let $x \to a$. The point $c$ lies strictly between $a$ and $x$, so $c$ is squeezed towards $a$ as well. By hypothesis, $f'(c)/g'(c) \to L$ as $c \to a$. Therefore

$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{c \to a} \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = L.$$
∎

The entire proof rests on Cauchy's MVT converting the ratio $f(x)/g(x)$ into the ratio $f'(c)/g'(c)$ at a single intermediate point. Everything else is bookkeeping.

A crucial warning: you are not differentiating the quotient $f/g$ using the quotient rule. You are replacing $f(x)$ by $f'(x)$ in the numerator and $g(x)$ by $g'(x)$ in the denominator, independently. The quotient rule would give $(f'g - fg')/g^2$ — a completely different expression. L'Hôpital's rule is not the quotient rule.

3. Examples

Example 1. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$.

Both $\sin 0 = 0$ and the denominator is $0$: this is $0/0$. Differentiate numerator and denominator separately:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{1} = \cos 0 = 1.$$

The same result we proved geometrically — now obtained mechanically in one line.

Example 2. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2}$.

Check: $1 - \cos 0 = 0$ and $0^2 = 0$. Apply L'Hôpital:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{2x}.$$

Still $0/0$. Apply L'Hôpital again (the conditions hold at each stage):

$$= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{2} = \frac{1}{2}.$$

You can apply the rule repeatedly, as long as the $0/0$ condition holds at each step.

Example 3. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x}$.

Both tend to $0$. Differentiate:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x}{1} = e^0 = 1.$$

This uses $\frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x$, which we have not yet proved. It will come from the power series definition of $e^x$ in Phase 3. For now, accept this as a preview of L'Hôpital's reach — it reduces unknown limits to known derivatives.

Example 4. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x - \sin x}{x^3}$.

Check: $0 - 0 = 0$ and $0^3 = 0$. Apply L'Hôpital:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{3x^2}.$$

Still $0/0$. Apply again:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{6x}.$$

Still $0/0$. Apply once more:

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{6} = \frac{1}{6}.$$

Three successive applications. At each step, verify that the $0/0$ condition still holds before differentiating again. If at any step the form ceases to be indeterminate, stop and evaluate directly.

4. L'Hôpital's rule ($\infty/\infty$ form)

Theorem — L'Hôpital's rule ($\infty/\infty$)

Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable near $a$ (or for $x$ sufficiently large, if $a = \infty$). Suppose $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \pm\infty$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = \pm\infty$, with $g'(x) \neq 0$ near $a$. If

$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} = L,$$

then $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = L$.

The full proof is more technical than the $0/0$ case. The standard reduction — write $f/g = (1/g)/(1/f)$ to convert $\infty/\infty$ into $0/0$ — works in principle but has sign subtleties. The cleaner approach proves it directly from Cauchy's MVT applied on an interval $[x, x_0]$ where both $f$ and $g$ are large, then lets $x_0 \to a$. We omit the details and use the result.

5. Example: logarithms grow slowly

$\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x}$.

Both $\ln x \to \infty$ and $x \to \infty$. This is $\infty/\infty$. Differentiate:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{1} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0.$$

Logarithms grow slower than any positive power of $x$. More generally, for any $\alpha > 0$:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x^\alpha} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{\alpha x^{\alpha - 1}} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{\alpha x^\alpha} = 0.$$

This uses $\frac{d}{dx}\ln x = 1/x$, which will be derived from the integral of $1/x$ (page 8) or from the power series of $\ln(1+x)$ (Phase 3).

What breaks

When the derivative ratio has no limit. Consider $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x + \sin x}{x}$. The answer is $1$ (divide numerator and denominator by $x$: $1 + \frac{\sin x}{x} \to 1$). But applying L'Hôpital gives

$$\frac{1 + \cos x}{1},$$

which oscillates between $0$ and $2$ — no limit exists. L'Hôpital says: if $\lim f'/g'$ exists, then it equals $\lim f/g$. The converse is false. The rule failing does not mean the original limit fails.

When the form is not indeterminate. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$. This is $0/0$, so L'Hôpital applies: $2x/1 \to 2$. Correct, but you could just factor: $(x-1)(x+1)/(x-1) = x+1 \to 2$. Don't reach for L'Hôpital when algebra suffices.

When $g'(x) = 0$ near the point. The hypothesis $g'(x) \neq 0$ near $a$ is essential. If $g'$ vanishes at or near $a$, the ratio $f'/g'$ is undefined and the rule collapses.

Applying to a non-indeterminate form. If $g(a) \neq 0$, then $f(x)/g(x) \to f(a)/g(a)$ by continuity — no rule needed. Blindly differentiating numerator and denominator when the form is not $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$ produces wrong answers. Always check the indeterminate condition first.

6. When L'Hôpital fails: a checklist

Before applying the rule, verify all three conditions:

  1. Indeterminate form. Is it genuinely $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$? If the denominator has a non-zero limit, just substitute.
  2. Differentiability. Are $f$ and $g$ differentiable near $a$?
  3. Derivative ratio converges. Does $\lim f'/g'$ exist (or equal $\pm\infty$)? If the derivative ratio oscillates, L'Hôpital is silent — try a different method.

If all three hold, the rule gives the answer. If any fails, it says nothing — the original limit may still exist, you just need another technique (factoring, conjugates, squeeze theorem, Taylor expansion).

Historical note

Despite the name, this rule was discovered by Johann Bernoulli around 1694. He communicated it to the Marquis de l'Hôpital, who published it in 1696 in Analyse des Infiniment Petits pour l'Intelligence des Lignes Courbes — the first calculus textbook ever printed. L'Hôpital had a contract with Bernoulli: an annual salary of 300 livres in exchange for mathematical discoveries, with the right to publish them under his own name. The arrangement was legal but ethically dubious. The attribution stuck, to Bernoulli's lasting irritation. The correspondence proving Bernoulli's priority was published only after both men had died.

When $0/0$ blocks you, compare rates of vanishing — L'Hôpital replaces the limit of a ratio with the ratio of derivatives.
