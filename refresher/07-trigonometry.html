Trigonometry Refresher

Refresher — High-school level essentials

What sine, cosine, and tangent are, the key values, the unit circle, the identities you need, and how to solve trig equations. The course covers trig rigorously from scratch in Phase 0 — this page is just to reactivate what you learnt in lycée.

1. What are sine, cosine, tangent?

In a right triangle with an angle $\theta$ (not the right angle):

$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$$

Mnemonic: SOH-CAH-TOA (Sine = Opposite/Hypotenuse, Cosine = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, Tangent = Opposite/Adjacent).

This definition only works for angles between $0$ and $90°$. For all other angles, we use the unit circle.

2. The unit circle

Draw a circle of radius 1 centred at the origin. For any angle $\theta$ (measured anticlockwise from the positive x-axis), the point on the circle is $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$.

This extends trig to all angles, not just $0$ to $90°$:

  • Quadrant I ($0$ to $\pi/2$): $\sin > 0$, $\cos > 0$
  • Quadrant II ($\pi/2$ to $\pi$): $\sin > 0$, $\cos < 0$
  • Quadrant III ($\pi$ to $3\pi/2$): $\sin < 0$, $\cos < 0$
  • Quadrant IV ($3\pi/2$ to $2\pi$): $\sin < 0$, $\cos > 0$

Mnemonic for signs: "All Students Take Calculus" — All positive in Q1, Sine in Q2, Tangent in Q3, Cosine in Q4.

3. Degrees vs radians

Radians are the standard in all mathematics beyond high school. The conversion:

$$\pi \text{ radians} = 180° \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad 1° = \frac{\pi}{180} \qquad\text{and}\qquad 1 \text{ rad} = \frac{180°}{\pi} \approx 57.3°$$
Degrees$0°$$30°$$45°$$60°$$90°$$180°$$270°$$360°$
Radians$0$$\pi/6$$\pi/4$$\pi/3$$\pi/2$$\pi$$3\pi/2$$2\pi$

From now on, always use radians. The entire course is in radians.

4. Key values to memorise

$\theta$$0$$\pi/6$$\pi/4$$\pi/3$$\pi/2$
$\sin\theta$$0$$\dfrac{1}{2}$$\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$$\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$1$
$\cos\theta$$1$$\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$$\dfrac{1}{2}$$0$
$\tan\theta$$0$$\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$$1$$\sqrt{3}$undef

Pattern for sine: $\frac{\sqrt{0}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{1}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{4}}{2}$. That's $0, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, 1$. Cosine is the same sequence reversed.

For other quadrants, use the unit circle signs. E.g. $\sin(5\pi/6) = \sin(\pi - \pi/6) = \sin(\pi/6) = 1/2$ (Q2, sine positive).

5. The fundamental identity

$$\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$$

This is the Pythagorean theorem on the unit circle. It's the single most important trig identity. From it:

  • $\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta$
  • $\cos^2\theta = 1 - \sin^2\theta$

Dividing through by $\cos^2\theta$: $\;\tan^2\theta + 1 = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2\theta}$.

6. Symmetry relations

These let you reduce any angle to the first quadrant:

Relation$\sin$$\cos$Meaning
$\pi - \theta$$\sin\theta$$-\cos\theta$Reflect across y-axis
$\pi + \theta$$-\sin\theta$$-\cos\theta$Rotate 180°
$-\theta$$-\sin\theta$$\cos\theta$Reflect across x-axis
$\pi/2 - \theta$$\cos\theta$$\sin\theta$Complementary angle

Example: $\cos(3\pi/4) = \cos(\pi - \pi/4) = -\cos(\pi/4) = -\sqrt{2}/2$.

7. Essential identities

Double angle

$$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$
$$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta$$

Addition formulae

$$\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$
$$\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$

These are proved rigorously in Phase 0, page 7. For now, just know them.

Half-angle (from double angle)

$$\cos^2\frac{\theta}{2} = \frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2} \qquad\qquad \sin^2\frac{\theta}{2} = \frac{1 - \cos\theta}{2}$$

8. Solving trig equations

$\sin(x) = a$

If $|a| \leq 1$, the solutions are:

$$x = \arcsin(a) + 2k\pi \qquad\text{or}\qquad x = \pi - \arcsin(a) + 2k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Example: $\sin(x) = 1/2$. $\arcsin(1/2) = \pi/6$. Solutions: $x = \pi/6 + 2k\pi$ or $x = 5\pi/6 + 2k\pi$.

$\cos(x) = a$

$$x = \pm\arccos(a) + 2k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Example: $\cos(x) = -\sqrt{2}/2$. $\arccos(-\sqrt{2}/2) = 3\pi/4$. Solutions: $x = \pm 3\pi/4 + 2k\pi$.

$\tan(x) = a$

$$x = \arctan(a) + k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Tangent has period $\pi$ (not $2\pi$), so only one family of solutions.

The substitution trick

Equations like $2\sin^2(x) - 3\sin(x) + 1 = 0$ are quadratics in disguise. Let $u = \sin(x)$, solve the quadratic for $u$, then solve $\sin(x) = u$ for each value.

9. Inverse trig functions

The inverse functions "undo" sine, cosine, tangent:

  • $\arcsin(x)$: takes $x \in [-1, 1]$, returns angle in $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$
  • $\arccos(x)$: takes $x \in [-1, 1]$, returns angle in $[0, \pi]$
  • $\arctan(x)$: takes $x \in \mathbb{R}$, returns angle in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$

Key point: these return one angle (the principal value). Trig equations have infinitely many solutions; the inverse function gives you the starting point, and you add $2k\pi$ (or $k\pi$ for tangent) to get all solutions.

10. Graphs

$\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x)$ are waves with:

  • Period: $2\pi$ (one full cycle)
  • Amplitude: $1$ (oscillates between $-1$ and $1$)
  • Phase: $\cos(x) = \sin(x + \pi/2)$ (cosine is sine shifted left by $\pi/2$)

For $A\sin(Bx + C) + D$:

  • $|A|$ = amplitude
  • $2\pi/|B|$ = period
  • $-C/B$ = phase shift (horizontal)
  • $D$ = vertical shift

$\tan(x)$ has period $\pi$ and vertical asymptotes at $x = \pi/2 + k\pi$.
