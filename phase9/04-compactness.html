Compactness

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 4

The problem

In Phase 2, page 4, we proved Bolzano-Weierstrass for $\mathbb{R}$: every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence. In Phase 2, page 6 and throughout Phase 3, we used a remarkable fact about closed intervals $[a,b]$: continuous functions on $[a,b]$ are bounded, attain their maximum and minimum, and are uniformly continuous. None of this works on open intervals — $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0,1)$ is continuous, unbounded, and not uniformly continuous.

What makes $[a,b]$ so special? Heine and Borel identified the key property: every open cover of $[a,b]$ can be reduced to a finite subcover. This property — compactness — turns out to be the single condition behind all those theorems about closed intervals.

1. Open Covers

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space and $K \subseteq X$. An open cover of $K$ is a collection $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ of open subsets of $X$ such that $K \subseteq \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha$. A subcover is a sub-collection that still covers $K$.

Consider $[0,1]$. Cover it by the intervals $(-0.1, 0.6)$ and $(0.4, 1.1)$. Two sets suffice — that's a finite cover. Now try something more extravagant: for each $x \in [0,1]$, take $U_x = (x - 0.01, x + 0.01)$. Uncountably many open sets. Yet you can always extract a finite subcover. No matter how you cover $[0,1]$ with open sets, finitely many suffice.

Now try $(0,1)$. Cover it by $U_n = (1/n, 1)$ for $n \geq 2$. Every point of $(0,1)$ lies in some $U_n$, so this is an open cover. But no finite subcollection covers $(0,1)$: the largest finite subcollection is $U_N = (1/N, 1)$ for some $N$, which misses $(0, 1/N]$. The open cover has no finite subcover. Something is fundamentally different about $(0,1)$.

2. Compactness Defined

Definition

A subset $K$ of a metric space $(X, d)$ is compact if every open cover of $K$ has a finite subcover.

This is the topological definition. It looks abstract — you must check every open cover, not just specific ones. But its power comes precisely from this universality. Any argument that produces an open cover automatically yields a finite subcover when $K$ is compact, and finiteness is something you can compute with.

3. Sequential Compactness

There is a second, more concrete characterisation in terms of sequences.

Definition

A metric space $K$ is sequentially compact if every sequence in $K$ has a subsequence that converges to a point in $K$.

In metric spaces, the two notions coincide. This is a deep theorem.

Theorem

In a metric space, $K$ is compact if and only if $K$ is sequentially compact.

We prove both directions. The backward direction needs a lemma.

Lemma (Lebesgue number)

Let $K$ be a sequentially compact metric space and $\{U_\alpha\}$ an open cover of $K$. Then there exists $\delta > 0$ (the Lebesgue number) such that every subset of $K$ with diameter less than $\delta$ is contained in some single $U_\alpha$.

Proof of Lebesgue number lemma

Suppose no such $\delta$ exists. Then for each $n \geq 1$, there exists a set $S_n$ with $\operatorname{diam}(S_n) < 1/n$ not contained in any single $U_\alpha$. Pick $x_n \in S_n$. By sequential compactness, $(x_n)$ has a subsequence $x_{n_k} \to L \in K$.

Since $\{U_\alpha\}$ covers $K$, we have $L \in U_\beta$ for some $\beta$. Since $U_\beta$ is open, there exists $r > 0$ with $B(L, r) \subseteq U_\beta$. Choose $k$ large enough that $d(x_{n_k}, L) < r/2$ and $1/n_k < r/2$. Then $S_{n_k}$ has diameter less than $r/2$ and contains a point within $r/2$ of $L$, so $S_{n_k} \subseteq B(L, r) \subseteq U_\beta$. This contradicts the choice of $S_{n_k}$.

∎
Proof (sequentially compact implies compact)

Let $\{U_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $K$. By the Lebesgue number lemma, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that every set of diameter less than $\delta$ sits inside some $U_\alpha$.

We claim $K$ can be covered by finitely many balls of radius $\delta/3$. If not, we could build a sequence: pick $x_1 \in K$ arbitrarily; given $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, the balls $B(x_i, \delta/3)$ don't cover $K$, so pick $x_{n+1}$ outside all of them. By construction, $d(x_i, x_j) \geq \delta/3$ for all $i \neq j$. No subsequence can be Cauchy, contradicting sequential compactness.

So $K \subseteq B(y_1, \delta/3) \cup \cdots \cup B(y_m, \delta/3)$ for finitely many $y_i$. Each ball has diameter less than $\delta$, so each sits inside some $U_{\alpha_i}$. Then $\{U_{\alpha_1}, \ldots, U_{\alpha_m}\}$ is a finite subcover.

∎
Proof (compact implies sequentially compact)

Suppose $K$ is compact and let $(x_n)$ be a sequence in $K$ with no subsequence converging in $K$. Then for every point $y \in K$, there is a ball $B(y, r_y)$ containing only finitely many terms of $(x_n)$ (otherwise $y$ would be a limit of a subsequence). These balls form an open cover of $K$. By compactness, finitely many cover $K$: $K \subseteq B(y_1, r_{y_1}) \cup \cdots \cup B(y_p, r_{y_p})$. But each ball contains finitely many terms, so $K$ contains only finitely many terms of the sequence — contradicting the fact that $(x_n)$ is an infinite sequence in $K$.

∎

4. Heine-Borel Theorem

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, compactness has a beautifully simple characterisation.

Theorem (Heine-Borel)

A subset $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

Proof

($\Longrightarrow$) Compact implies bounded. Cover $K$ by balls $B(\mathbf{0}, m)$ for $m = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ This is an open cover of $K$ (indeed of all $\mathbb{R}^n$). By compactness, finitely many suffice: $K \subseteq B(\mathbf{0}, M)$ for some $M$. So $K$ is bounded.

($\Longrightarrow$) Compact implies closed. We use sequential compactness. Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence in $K$ with $x_n \to L \in \mathbb{R}^n$. The constant subsequence trick isn't needed: the sequence itself converges, so every subsequence converges to $L$. By sequential compactness, some subsequence converges to a point of $K$. Limits are unique, so $L \in K$. Therefore $K$ contains all its limit points and is closed.

($\Longleftarrow$) Closed and bounded implies compact. We show sequential compactness. Let $(x_k)$ be a sequence in $K$. Since $K$ is bounded, all terms lie in some box $[-M, M]^n$.

Apply Bolzano-Weierstrass (Phase 2, page 4) to the first coordinates: extract a subsequence along which $x_k^{(1)}$ converges. From that subsequence, extract a further subsequence along which $x_k^{(2)}$ converges. Repeat for all $n$ coordinates. After $n$ extractions, we have a subsequence converging in every coordinate, hence converging in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Call the limit $L$. Since $(x_k)$ lies in $K$ and $K$ is closed, $L \in K$. So every sequence in $K$ has a subsequence converging in $K$.

∎

5. Compact Spaces Are Complete

Theorem

Every compact metric space is complete.

Proof

Let $(x_n)$ be Cauchy in the compact space $K$. By sequential compactness, it has a subsequence $x_{n_k} \to L \in K$. We show $x_n \to L$.

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $(x_n)$ is Cauchy, there exists $N_1$ with $d(x_m, x_n) < \varepsilon/2$ for all $m, n \geq N_1$. Since $x_{n_k} \to L$, there exists $k_0$ with $n_{k_0} \geq N_1$ and $d(x_{n_{k_0}}, L) < \varepsilon/2$. For any $n \geq N_1$:

$$d(x_n, L) \leq d(x_n, x_{n_{k_0}}) + d(x_{n_{k_0}}, L) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon$$
∎

The converse is false: $\mathbb{R}$ is complete but not compact. Compactness is strictly stronger than completeness — it adds boundedness (and in general, "total boundedness").

6. Properties of Compact Sets

Proposition

(a) A compact subset of a metric space is closed.
(b) A closed subset of a compact space is compact.
(c) The continuous image of a compact set is compact.

Part (a) follows from the Heine-Borel proof above (the argument works in any metric space, not just $\mathbb{R}^n$). Part (b): if $F \subseteq K$ is closed and $\{U_\alpha\}$ covers $F$, then $\{U_\alpha\} \cup \{X \setminus F\}$ covers $K$; extract a finite subcover of $K$ and remove $X \setminus F$ if present.

Part (c) is the most important for applications.

Proof of (c)

Let $f : K \to Y$ be continuous and $K$ compact. Let $\{V_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $f(K)$. Since $f$ is continuous, each $f^{-1}(V_\alpha)$ is open in $K$. The collection $\{f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\}$ covers $K$. By compactness, finitely many suffice: $K \subseteq f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_1}) \cup \cdots \cup f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_m})$. Applying $f$: $f(K) \subseteq V_{\alpha_1} \cup \cdots \cup V_{\alpha_m}$.

∎

This single result is the engine behind half the theorems in analysis. A continuous function on a compact set is bounded (its image is compact, hence bounded). A continuous function on a compact set attains its maximum (its image is compact, hence closed and bounded, hence contains its supremum). We will develop these consequences on the next page.

Historical note

Heine (1872) proved that continuous functions on $[a,b]$ are uniformly continuous — the first result that implicitly used the finite subcover property. Borel (1895) introduced the open cover formulation explicitly, proving that any countable cover of $[a,b]$ by open intervals has a finite subcover. Lebesgue extended this to arbitrary open covers.

Fréchet (1906), who invented metric spaces, established the equivalence of open-cover compactness and sequential compactness in this setting. He also coined the term compact. The general topological definition (open covers without a metric) came later, from Alexandroff and Urysohn in the 1920s. In that generality, sequential compactness and compactness diverge — they are equivalent only in metric spaces.

What breaks

Closed but not bounded: not compact. $\mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{R}$ is closed. Cover it by $U_n = (n - 1, n + 1)$ for each $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. Each $U_n$ covers only the integer $n$, so no finite subcollection covers $\mathbb{Z}$.

Bounded but not closed: not compact. $(0,1) \subset \mathbb{R}$ is bounded. The sequence $x_n = 1/n$ lies in $(0,1)$ and converges to $0 \notin (0,1)$. Every subsequence also converges to $0$, so no subsequence converges in $(0,1)$. Sequential compactness fails.

In infinite dimensions, Heine-Borel fails. Consider $\ell^2$, the space of square-summable sequences with the norm $\|(x_n)\| = (\sum x_n^2)^{1/2}$. The closed unit ball $\overline{B}(\mathbf{0}, 1)$ is closed and bounded. But the standard basis vectors $e_1 = (1,0,0,\ldots)$, $e_2 = (0,1,0,\ldots)$, etc. satisfy $\|e_i - e_j\| = \sqrt{2}$ for $i \neq j$. No subsequence of $(e_n)$ is Cauchy, let alone convergent. The closed unit ball is not compact. Heine-Borel is a theorem about $\mathbb{R}^n$, not about metric spaces in general.

Compactness is the next best thing to finiteness — every open cover, every sequence, is forced to behave as if the space were finite.
