Open and Closed Sets

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 2

The problem

In $\mathbb{R}$, the closed interval $[a, b]$ has a remarkable property: if a sequence $(x_n)$ lives entirely inside $[a, b]$ and converges, its limit stays in $[a, b]$. The open interval $(a, b)$ does not enjoy this — the sequence $x_n = a + 1/n$ lies in $(a, b)$ for large $n$ but converges to $a$, which is outside.

Conversely, every point of $(a, b)$ has "breathing room": you can draw a small interval around it that stays inside $(a, b)$. For $[a, b]$, the endpoints $a$ and $b$ have no such room — any interval around them spills outside.

Cantor (1884) realised these two properties — "contains all its limit points" versus "every point has room around it" — are the fundamental distinction. They have nothing to do with square or round brackets. They make sense in any metric space.

1. Open sets

Definition — Open set

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space. A subset $U \subseteq X$ is open if for every $x \in U$, there exists $r > 0$ such that $B(x, r) \subseteq U$.

In words: every point of $U$ is an interior point — it sits strictly inside $U$ with room to spare. No point of $U$ is on the "edge."

Examples in $\mathbb{R}$. The interval $(a, b)$ is open: for any $x \in (a, b)$, take $r = \min(x - a, b - x) > 0$, then $(x - r, x + r) \subseteq (a, b)$. The interval $[a, b]$ is not open: the point $a$ has no open ball $B(a, r) = (a - r, a + r)$ contained in $[a, b]$, since $a - r/2 < a$ lies outside.

Example in a general metric space. Every open ball $B(x_0, R)$ is itself an open set. To see this, take any $y \in B(x_0, R)$ and set $r = R - d(x_0, y) > 0$. For any $z \in B(y, r)$, the triangle inequality gives

$$d(x_0, z) \leq d(x_0, y) + d(y, z) < d(x_0, y) + r = R$$

so $z \in B(x_0, R)$. Thus $B(y, r) \subseteq B(x_0, R)$.

2. Properties of open sets

Theorem — Open set axioms

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space. Then:

  1. $\varnothing$ and $X$ are open.
  2. Any union of open sets is open: if $(U_i)_{i \in I}$ are open, so is $\bigcup_{i \in I} U_i$.
  3. Any finite intersection of open sets is open: if $U_1, \ldots, U_n$ are open, so is $U_1 \cap \cdots \cap U_n$.
Proof

(1) $\varnothing$ is open vacuously: it contains no points, so the condition "for every $x \in \varnothing$, ..." is satisfied with nothing to check. $X$ is open because every ball $B(x, r)$ is a subset of $X$.

(2) Let $x \in \bigcup_{i \in I} U_i$. Then $x \in U_j$ for some $j \in I$. Since $U_j$ is open, there exists $r > 0$ with $B(x, r) \subseteq U_j \subseteq \bigcup_{i \in I} U_i$.

(3) Let $x \in U_1 \cap \cdots \cap U_n$. For each $k$, since $U_k$ is open, there exists $r_k > 0$ with $B(x, r_k) \subseteq U_k$. Take $r = \min(r_1, \ldots, r_n) > 0$ (this is where finiteness is essential — a minimum of finitely many positive numbers is positive). Then $B(x, r) \subseteq B(x, r_k) \subseteq U_k$ for every $k$, so $B(x, r) \subseteq U_1 \cap \cdots \cap U_n$.

∎

The restriction to finite intersections is not a technicality — it is essential. Consider the open sets $U_n = (-1/n, 1/n)$ in $\mathbb{R}$. Each is open, but

$$\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} U_n = \{0\}$$

and $\{0\}$ is not open in $\mathbb{R}$: no interval $(0 - r, 0 + r)$ is contained in $\{0\}$.

3. Closed sets

Definition — Closed set

A subset $F \subseteq X$ is closed if its complement $X \setminus F$ is open.

"Closed" is not the opposite of "open." A set can be both open and closed (like $\varnothing$ and $X$), or neither (like $[0, 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}$). The terminology is unfortunate but entrenched.

Theorem — Sequential characterisation of closed sets

$F \subseteq X$ is closed if and only if whenever $(x_n)$ is a sequence in $F$ that converges to some $x \in X$, then $x \in F$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $F$ is closed and $(x_n)$ is a sequence in $F$ converging to $x$. We show $x \in F$ by contradiction. If $x \notin F$, then $x \in X \setminus F$, which is open. So there exists $r > 0$ with $B(x, r) \subseteq X \setminus F$. But $x_n \to x$ means eventually $d(x_n, x) < r$, so $x_n \in B(x, r) \subseteq X \setminus F$ — contradicting $x_n \in F$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $F$ contains all limits of its convergent sequences. We show $X \setminus F$ is open. Take $x \in X \setminus F$. If no ball $B(x, r)$ were contained in $X \setminus F$, then for each $n \geq 1$ the ball $B(x, 1/n)$ would contain some $x_n \in F$. This gives a sequence $(x_n)$ in $F$ with $d(x_n, x) < 1/n \to 0$, so $x_n \to x$. By hypothesis, $x \in F$ — contradicting $x \in X \setminus F$.

∎

This is the precise version of Cantor's insight: a closed set is one that "holds on to all its limit points."

4. Properties of closed sets

Theorem — Closed set axioms

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space. Then:

  1. $\varnothing$ and $X$ are closed.
  2. Any intersection of closed sets is closed.
  3. Any finite union of closed sets is closed.

These are exactly dual to the open set properties: complement each statement and apply De Morgan's laws ($X \setminus \bigcup F_i = \bigcap (X \setminus F_i)$ and vice versa). Since complements of open sets are closed, unions become intersections and the finiteness condition migrates accordingly.

5. Interior, closure, and boundary

Definition — Interior, closure, boundary

Let $A \subseteq X$.

  • The interior $\mathring{A}$ is the largest open set contained in $A$: the union of all open subsets of $A$.
  • The closure $\overline{A}$ is the smallest closed set containing $A$: the intersection of all closed supersets of $A$.
  • The boundary $\partial A = \overline{A} \setminus \mathring{A}$.

Equivalently, $x \in \mathring{A}$ iff there exists $r > 0$ with $B(x, r) \subseteq A$, and $x \in \overline{A}$ iff every ball $B(x, r)$ meets $A$ (contains at least one point of $A$).

Theorem — Closure via limit points

$\overline{A} = A \cup A'$, where $A'$ is the set of limit points of $A$: points $x \in X$ such that every ball $B(x, r)$ contains a point of $A$ distinct from $x$.

Proof

Let $C = A \cup A'$. We show $C$ is closed and is the smallest closed set containing $A$.

$C$ is closed. Take $x \notin C$. Then $x \notin A$ and $x \notin A'$, so there exists $r > 0$ with $B(x, r) \cap (A \setminus \{x\}) = \varnothing$. Since $x \notin A$, we actually have $B(x, r) \cap A = \varnothing$. We claim $B(x, r) \cap A' = \varnothing$ too: if $y \in B(x, r) \cap A'$, choose $\rho = r - d(x, y) > 0$; then $B(y, \rho) \subseteq B(x, r)$, so $B(y, \rho)$ contains a point of $A$ — but $B(x, r) \cap A = \varnothing$, contradiction. Hence $B(x, r) \subseteq X \setminus C$, so $X \setminus C$ is open.

$C$ is the smallest. Any closed set $F \supseteq A$ must contain $A'$: if $x$ is a limit point of $A$, build a sequence in $A \subseteq F$ converging to $x$; since $F$ is closed, $x \in F$. So $F \supseteq A \cup A' = C$.

∎

Example. The closure of $\mathbb{Q}$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is $\mathbb{R}$. Every real number is a limit of rationals — we proved this density property in Phase 2, page 7. Equivalently, every ball $(x - r, x + r)$ contains a rational number.

6. Neither open nor closed

The half-open interval $[0, 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is neither open nor closed. It is not open because $0$ has no ball $(0 - r, 0 + r) \subseteq [0, 1)$. It is not closed because the sequence $x_n = 1 - 1/n$ lies in $[0, 1)$ but converges to $1 \notin [0, 1)$.

Similarly, $\mathbb{Q}$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is neither open nor closed. It is not open because every ball around a rational contains irrationals. It is not closed because $\mathbb{Q}$ does not contain all its limit points — every irrational is a limit of rationals.

Historical note

Georg Cantor developed point-set theory in the 1870s and 1880s while studying a concrete problem: under what conditions is a function's trigonometric series representation unique? He needed to understand the structure of "exceptional sets" where uniqueness might fail, and this forced him to define limit points, derived sets, and the distinction between open and closed.

The precise definitions and the terminology we use today — "open set" (offene Menge), "closed set" (abgeschlossene Menge), "neighbourhood" — were standardised by Hausdorff in Grundzüge der Mengenlehre (1914). Hausdorff recognised that the three properties of open sets (the "open set axioms" we proved in Section 2) could serve as the foundation for a theory of topology that does not require a metric at all — but that is a story for another phase.

What breaks

Infinite intersections of open sets need not be open. We saw this already: $\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1/n, 1/n) = \{0\}$, a single point, which is not open in $\mathbb{R}$. The proof of finite intersections used $r = \min(r_1, \ldots, r_n) > 0$; with infinitely many $r_k$, the infimum can be zero.

Infinite unions of closed sets need not be closed. Take $F_n = [1/n, 1 - 1/n]$ in $\mathbb{R}$. Each $F_n$ is closed, but

$$\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} F_n = (0, 1)$$

which is open, not closed. The limit points $0$ and $1$ escape every finite stage but are limits of the whole union.

The topology depends on the metric. Equip $\mathbb{R}$ with the discrete metric ($d(x,y) = 1$ for $x \neq y$). Then $B(x, 1/2) = \{x\}$, so every singleton is open, hence every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ is a union of singletons and therefore open. Every set is also closed (its complement is open). In the discrete metric, every set is simultaneously open and closed — clopen. The concepts of "open" and "closed" are not intrinsic to the set $\mathbb{R}$; they depend on which metric you choose.

Open sets give every point breathing room; closed sets hold on to all their limit points — the two notions are complementary.
