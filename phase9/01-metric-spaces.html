Metric Spaces

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 1

The problem

By the late 19th century, analysis had outgrown $\mathbb{R}^n$. Mathematicians were routinely working with sequences of functions, sets of continuous maps, spaces of probability distributions. These objects are not points in Euclidean space, yet analysts applied convergence arguments, continuity reasoning, and limit constructions to them as though they were.

The machinery worked — but why? What do all these settings have in common? Fréchet (1906) isolated the answer in his doctoral thesis: the only structure you truly need is a notion of distance. Give a set a function that measures how far apart two elements are, subject to three natural axioms, and the entire apparatus of limits and continuity follows. He called the result an espace métrique — a metric space.

1. The definition of a metric

A metric distils the essence of "distance" into three properties. Strip away coordinates, strip away geometry — these three axioms are what remains.

Definition — Metric space

A metric space is a pair $(X, d)$ where $X$ is a non-empty set and $d : X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ is a function satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in X$:

  1. Positivity with identity of indiscernibles: $d(x, y) \geq 0$, and $d(x, y) = 0$ if and only if $x = y$.
  2. Symmetry: $d(x, y) = d(y, x)$.
  3. Triangle inequality: $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$.

The function $d$ is called a metric (or distance function) on $X$.

Axiom 1 says distance is never negative and only zero between a point and itself. Axiom 2 says the distance from $x$ to $y$ is the same as from $y$ to $x$. Axiom 3 says the direct route is never longer than a detour through a third point — the most geometrically loaded of the three.

2. Examples

The power of the definition is its generality. All of the following are metric spaces.

(a) The real line. Take $X = \mathbb{R}$ with $d(x, y) = |x - y|$. Positivity and symmetry are immediate from properties of absolute value. The triangle inequality $|x - z| \leq |x - y| + |y - z|$ was proved in Phase 2, page 1. This is the metric that underlies all of single-variable analysis.

(b) Euclidean $\mathbb{R}^n$. For $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ and $\mathbf{y} = (y_1, \ldots, y_n)$, define

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^{n}(x_k - y_k)^2}$$

Positivity and symmetry are clear. The triangle inequality follows from the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality (Phase 6, page 7): for vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$\|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 + 2\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = (\|\mathbf{u}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|)^2$$

so $\|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\| + \|\mathbf{v}\|$. Setting $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{z}$ gives $d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z}) \leq d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) + d(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z})$.

(c) The discrete metric. On any set $X$, define $d(x, y) = 0$ if $x = y$ and $d(x, y) = 1$ if $x \neq y$. All three axioms are trivially satisfied. The triangle inequality holds because the right-hand side is at least 1 whenever $x \neq z$, and 1 is the largest value $d$ ever takes. This metric contains no geometric information — it is the coarsest possible distance.

(d) Function spaces: the sup metric. Let $X = C([0, 1])$, the set of continuous functions $f : [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$. Define

$$d(f, g) = \sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f(x) - g(x)| = \|f - g\|_\infty$$

This supremum exists because $f - g$ is continuous on a closed bounded interval, hence bounded (IOU: the extreme value theorem will be proved in this phase, page 5). The metric axioms follow from those of $|\cdot|$ on $\mathbb{R}$, applied pointwise then taking the supremum.

(e) The $\ell^2$ space. Let $X$ be the set of all real sequences $(x_n)_{n \geq 1}$ with $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x_n^2 < \infty$. Define

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \sqrt{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(x_n - y_n)^2}$$

This extends Euclidean distance to infinitely many dimensions. The triangle inequality follows from Cauchy–Schwarz for series, exactly as in example (b).

3. Convergence in metric spaces

Definition — Convergence

A sequence $(x_n)$ in a metric space $(X, d)$ converges to $x \in X$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n \geq N \implies d(x_n, x) < \varepsilon$.

This is exactly the $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition from Phase 2, page 2, with $|x_n - x|$ replaced by $d(x_n, x)$. In $(\mathbb{R}, |\cdot|)$, the two definitions are identical.

Now consider $C([0, 1])$ with the sup metric. A sequence of functions $(f_n)$ converges to $f$ in this metric precisely when

$$\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0$$

This says $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $[0, 1]$. We defined uniform convergence in Phase 5, page 6 and saw its importance for preserving continuity. Now we see it is simply convergence in the metric space $(C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_\infty)$. The abstract definition of convergence in a metric space unifies all these cases.

A natural question: can a sequence converge to two different limits? No — limits in metric spaces are unique.

Theorem — Uniqueness of limits

If $x_n \to x$ and $x_n \to y$ in a metric space $(X, d)$, then $x = y$.

Proof

By the triangle inequality, $0 \leq d(x, y) \leq d(x, x_n) + d(x_n, y)$ for all $n$. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ large enough that $d(x, x_n) < \varepsilon/2$ and $d(x_n, y) < \varepsilon/2$ for all $n \geq N$. Then $d(x, y) < \varepsilon$. Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $d(x, y) = 0$, so $x = y$ by axiom 1.

∎

4. Balls and boundedness

Definition — Open and closed balls

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space, $x \in X$, and $r > 0$. The open ball of radius $r$ centred at $x$ is

$$B(x, r) = \{y \in X : d(x, y) < r\}$$

The closed ball is

$$\overline{B}(x, r) = \{y \in X : d(x, y) \leq r\}$$

In $\mathbb{R}$, $B(x, r) = (x - r, x + r)$: the open interval of radius $r$. In $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the Euclidean metric, balls are discs. In $C([0,1])$ with the sup metric, $B(f, \varepsilon)$ is the set of all continuous functions whose graph lies within a vertical band of width $\varepsilon$ around the graph of $f$.

In the discrete metric, $B(x, 1) = \{x\}$ (just the centre) and $B(x, r) = X$ for any $r > 1$. Balls can look very different depending on the metric.

Definition — Bounded set, diameter

A subset $A \subseteq X$ is bounded if there exist $x \in X$ and $r > 0$ with $A \subseteq B(x, r)$. The diameter of $A$ is

$$\text{diam}(A) = \sup\{d(a, b) : a, b \in A\}$$

with the convention $\text{diam}(\varnothing) = 0$.

A set is bounded if and only if its diameter is finite. This is a short exercise: if $A \subseteq B(x, r)$ then for any $a, b \in A$ the triangle inequality gives $d(a, b) \leq d(a, x) + d(x, b) < 2r$, so $\text{diam}(A) \leq 2r < \infty$. Conversely, if $\text{diam}(A) = M < \infty$, pick any $a_0 \in A$; then $A \subseteq B(a_0, M + 1)$.

Historical note

Maurice Fréchet introduced metric spaces in his 1906 doctoral thesis Sur quelques points du calcul fonctionnel, supervised by Hadamard. He originally called the concept a "classe (D)" — a set equipped with a distance-like function (écart). The name "metric space" (metrischer Raum) and the systematic development of the theory came from Felix Hausdorff's landmark 1914 book Grundzüge der Mengenlehre.

The abstraction was not idle generalisation. By 1900, mathematicians were already computing with function spaces — Volterra's work on integral equations, Hilbert's work on $\ell^2$ — but lacked a common language. Fréchet's three axioms gave that language: one framework in which points in $\mathbb{R}$, vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, continuous functions, and square-summable sequences all live on equal footing.

What breaks

Drop symmetry. Define $d(x, y) = |y - x|$ if $y \geq x$ and $d(x, y) = 2|y - x|$ if $y < x$. Going "uphill" costs less than going "downhill." This is a quasimetric: it models directed distances, like travel time in a city with one-way streets. Without symmetry, $d(x, y) < \varepsilon$ does not imply $d(y, x) < \varepsilon$, so convergence $x_n \to x$ is no longer equivalent to $x \to x_n$ being "close." The theory becomes asymmetric and much harder.

Drop the triangle inequality. On $\mathbb{R}$, set $d(x, y) = (x - y)^2$. Take $x = 0$, $y = 2$, $z = 1$: then $d(x, z) = 1$, $d(x, y) + d(y, z) = 4 + 1 = 5$ — fine so far. But take $x = 0$, $y = 1$, $z = 3$: then $d(x, z) = 9$ while $d(x, y) + d(y, z) = 1 + 4 = 5$. The triangle inequality fails. Without it, "closeness" is not transitive: $x$ close to $y$ and $y$ close to $z$ no longer guarantees $x$ close to $z$.

Drop $d(x,y) = 0 \implies x = y$. On $C([0, 1])$, define $d(f, g) = |f(0) - g(0)|$. Then $d$ satisfies all axioms except this one: any two functions agreeing at $x = 0$ have distance zero, even if they differ wildly elsewhere. This is a pseudometric. It identifies distinct objects, destroying the ability to distinguish points.

A metric space is any set where you can measure distance — three axioms abstract the essential properties of $|x - y|$ on $\mathbb{R}$.
