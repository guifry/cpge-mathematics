Continuity on Compact Sets

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 5

The problem

We proved in Phase 3, page 7 that continuous functions on $[a, b]$ are integrable, and in Phase 2, page 6 that the intermediate value theorem holds on closed intervals. Both proofs relied on two special properties of $[a, b]$: every open cover has a finite subcover, and every sequence has a convergent subsequence. These are precisely the two faces of compactness, which we defined on page 4.

Now we state the general versions. What can you guarantee about a continuous function when its domain is compact?

1. Continuous maps between metric spaces

Definition — Continuity

Let $(X, d_X)$ and $(Y, d_Y)$ be metric spaces. A function $f : X \to Y$ is continuous at $x_0 \in X$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$d_X(x, x_0) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x), f(x_0)) < \varepsilon$$

We say $f$ is continuous if it is continuous at every point of $X$.

This is the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition from Phase 2, page 5, with absolute values replaced by metrics. There are two equivalent characterisations.

Theorem — Equivalent characterisations of continuity

For $f : (X, d_X) \to (Y, d_Y)$, the following are equivalent:

  1. $f$ is continuous ($\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition).
  2. For every open set $U \subseteq Y$, the preimage $f^{-1}(U)$ is open in $X$.
  3. $f$ preserves convergent sequences: $x_n \to x$ in $X$ implies $f(x_n) \to f(x)$ in $Y$.
Proof

(1) $\Rightarrow$ (2). Let $U \subseteq Y$ be open and take $x_0 \in f^{-1}(U)$. Then $f(x_0) \in U$, so there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ with $B(f(x_0), \varepsilon) \subseteq U$. By continuity at $x_0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ with $f(B(x_0, \delta)) \subseteq B(f(x_0), \varepsilon) \subseteq U$. Hence $B(x_0, \delta) \subseteq f^{-1}(U)$, so $f^{-1}(U)$ is open.

(2) $\Rightarrow$ (3). Suppose $x_n \to x$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$. The set $U = B(f(x), \varepsilon)$ is open in $Y$, so $f^{-1}(U)$ is open in $X$. Since $x \in f^{-1}(U)$, there exists $\delta > 0$ with $B(x, \delta) \subseteq f^{-1}(U)$. Since $x_n \to x$, eventually $x_n \in B(x, \delta)$, so $f(x_n) \in U$, i.e. $d_Y(f(x_n), f(x)) < \varepsilon$.

(3) $\Rightarrow$ (1). Suppose $f$ is not continuous at $x_0$. Then there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that for every $n \geq 1$, there is $x_n$ with $d_X(x_n, x_0) < 1/n$ but $d_Y(f(x_n), f(x_0)) \geq \varepsilon$. Then $x_n \to x_0$ but $f(x_n) \not\to f(x_0)$, contradicting (3).

∎

2. The extreme value theorem (general)

On $\mathbb{R}$, a continuous function on $[a, b]$ is bounded and attains its bounds. The general version replaces $[a, b]$ with any compact metric space.

Theorem — Continuous image of compact is compact

Let $f : X \to Y$ be continuous and $K \subseteq X$ compact. Then $f(K)$ is compact in $Y$.

Proof

Let $\{U_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $f(K)$. Each $f^{-1}(U_\alpha)$ is open in $X$ (by characterisation (2) above), and $\{f^{-1}(U_\alpha)\}$ covers $K$. Since $K$ is compact, finitely many $f^{-1}(U_{\alpha_1}), \ldots, f^{-1}(U_{\alpha_n})$ cover $K$. Then $U_{\alpha_1}, \ldots, U_{\alpha_n}$ cover $f(K)$.

∎
Theorem — Extreme value theorem

If $f : K \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous and $K$ is a compact metric space, then $f$ attains its maximum and minimum: there exist $x_{\min}, x_{\max} \in K$ with $f(x_{\min}) \leq f(x) \leq f(x_{\max})$ for all $x \in K$.

Proof

$f(K)$ is compact in $\mathbb{R}$. By Heine–Borel (page 4), $f(K)$ is closed and bounded. Since $f(K)$ is bounded, $M = \sup f(K)$ is finite. Since $f(K)$ is closed, $M \in f(K)$, so there exists $x_{\max} \in K$ with $f(x_{\max}) = M$. The argument for the minimum is identical.

∎

3. Uniform continuity

Definition — Uniform continuity

A function $f : (X, d_X) \to (Y, d_Y)$ is uniformly continuous if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in X$,

$$d_X(x, y) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x), f(y)) < \varepsilon$$

The crucial distinction: in ordinary continuity, $\delta$ may depend on both $\varepsilon$ and the point $x_0$. In uniform continuity, a single $\delta$ works for every pair of points simultaneously. Uniform continuity is a global property of the function; ordinary continuity is local.

Every uniformly continuous function is continuous (take the same $\delta$ at each point). The converse is false in general — but it becomes true on compact sets.

4. Heine's theorem

Theorem — Heine's theorem

Let $K$ be a compact metric space and $f : K \to Y$ continuous. Then $f$ is uniformly continuous.

Proof

Suppose $f$ is not uniformly continuous. Then there exists $\varepsilon_0 > 0$ such that for every $n \geq 1$, there exist $x_n, y_n \in K$ with $d_X(x_n, y_n) < 1/n$ but $d_Y(f(x_n), f(y_n)) \geq \varepsilon_0$.

Since $K$ is compact (hence sequentially compact, page 4), $(x_n)$ has a subsequence $x_{n_k} \to x^* \in K$. Since $d_X(y_{n_k}, x^*) \leq d_X(y_{n_k}, x_{n_k}) + d_X(x_{n_k}, x^*) < 1/n_k + d_X(x_{n_k}, x^*) \to 0$, we also have $y_{n_k} \to x^*$.

By continuity of $f$, $f(x_{n_k}) \to f(x^*)$ and $f(y_{n_k}) \to f(x^*)$. So $d_Y(f(x_{n_k}), f(y_{n_k})) \to 0$, contradicting $d_Y(f(x_{n_k}), f(y_{n_k})) \geq \varepsilon_0$.

∎

This generalises the result from Phase 3, page 7, where we proved uniform continuity on $[a, b]$. The proof there used the same contradiction argument, but relied on Bolzano–Weierstrass specifically in $\mathbb{R}$. Now we see the real engine was sequential compactness.

5. Continuous bijections on compact sets

A continuous bijection need not have a continuous inverse — unless the domain is compact.

Theorem — Continuous bijection from compact space is a homeomorphism

Let $K$ be a compact metric space, $Y$ a metric space, and $f : K \to Y$ a continuous bijection. Then $f^{-1} : Y \to K$ is continuous, so $f$ is a homeomorphism.

Proof

It suffices to show $f$ maps closed sets to closed sets (then $f^{-1}$ pulls back closed sets to closed sets, which is continuity by the open-set characterisation applied to complements).

Let $C \subseteq K$ be closed. Since $K$ is compact and $C$ is closed, $C$ is compact (page 4: a closed subset of a compact set is compact). Since $f$ is continuous, $f(C)$ is compact (proved above). A compact subset of a metric space is closed (page 4). So $f(C)$ is closed.

∎

The proof is a beautiful chain: closed $\Rightarrow$ compact $\Rightarrow$ compact $\Rightarrow$ closed, with compactness of $K$ and continuity of $f$ doing the heavy lifting at each step.

Historical note

Eduard Heine proved uniform continuity on closed intervals in 1872, building on ideas of Dirichlet. The generalisation to compact metric spaces came naturally once Fréchet introduced abstract metric spaces in 1906 and the notion of compactness was formalised by Alexandroff and Urysohn in the 1920s.

The extreme value theorem in $\mathbb{R}$ is due to Weierstrass (1861), who also gave the first rigorous $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definitions. Its extension to general metric spaces was part of the Bourbaki programme of recasting analysis in topological language.

What breaks

Without compactness, the extreme value theorem fails. The function $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0, 1)$ is continuous but unbounded — $\sup f = +\infty$, never attained. The domain is bounded but not closed, hence not compact.

Without compactness, uniform continuity fails. The function $f(x) = x^2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is continuous but not uniformly continuous: for any $\delta > 0$, take $x = 1/\delta$ and $y = x + \delta/2$. Then $|x - y| = \delta/2 < \delta$ but $|f(x) - f(y)| = |2x + \delta/2|\cdot\delta/2 > 1$. The domain is closed but not bounded, hence not compact.

Without compactness, a continuous bijection need not be a homeomorphism. Define $f : [0, 2\pi) \to S^1$ by $t \mapsto (\cos t, \sin t)$. This is a continuous bijection, but $f^{-1}$ is not continuous: as points on $S^1$ approach $(1, 0)$ from above, their preimages approach $0$, but from below their preimages approach $2\pi$. The domain $[0, 2\pi)$ is not compact (not closed in $\mathbb{R}$).

On a compact set, continuous functions cannot misbehave — they are bounded, attain their extremes, and are uniformly continuous.
