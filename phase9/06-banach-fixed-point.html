The Banach Fixed Point Theorem

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 6

The problem

In Phase 8, page 2, we proved the Cauchy–Lipschitz theorem: if $f$ is Lipschitz in $y$, the ODE $y' = f(t, y)$ has a unique local solution. The proof used Picard iteration: define an operator $T$, show $T$ brings functions closer together, iterate, and argue the iterates converge to a fixed point.

That argument felt specific to ODEs, but the pattern is universal. Banach (1922) isolated the abstract principle: any map that shrinks distances on a complete metric space has exactly one fixed point, and iteration from anywhere converges to it.

1. Fixed points

Definition — Fixed point

Let $T : X \to X$ be a map from a set to itself. A point $x \in X$ is a fixed point of $T$ if $T(x) = x$.

Fixed points are where a transformation does nothing. The map $T(x) = x/2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ has the unique fixed point $x = 0$. The map $T(x) = x + 1$ has no fixed point. The identity map fixes everything.

Finding fixed points is equivalent to solving equations: $T(x) = x$ is the same as $T(x) - x = 0$. The power of Banach's theorem is that it gives existence, uniqueness, and a constructive algorithm (iteration) all at once.

2. Contractions

Definition — Contraction mapping

Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space. A map $T : X \to X$ is a contraction (or contraction mapping) if there exists a constant $k \in [0, 1)$ such that

$$d(T(x), T(y)) \leq k \cdot d(x, y) \quad \text{for all } x, y \in X$$

The constant $k$ is called the contraction constant.

A contraction is Lipschitz continuous with Lipschitz constant $k < 1$, so in particular it is (uniformly) continuous. The key point is the strict inequality $k < 1$: every application of $T$ shrinks all distances by at least a factor of $k$.

The map $T(x) = x/2$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is a contraction with $k = 1/2$: we have $|T(x) - T(y)| = |x - y|/2$. The map $T(x) = x/2 + 3$ is also a contraction with $k = 1/2$ (the additive constant drops out when computing $|T(x) - T(y)|$). The map $T(x) = x$ satisfies $|T(x) - T(y)| = |x - y|$ — it is non-expansive ($k = 1$) but not a contraction, because we require $k$ strictly less than 1.

Geometrically, iterating a contraction is like repeatedly zooming in: each application compresses the entire space by a factor of $k$, pulling all points closer together. After $n$ iterations, points that were initially at distance $D$ are at most $k^n D$ apart. Since $k^n \to 0$, everything collapses toward a single point — the fixed point.

3. The theorem

Theorem — Banach fixed point theorem

Let $(X, d)$ be a complete metric space and $T : X \to X$ a contraction with constant $k \in [0, 1)$. Then:

  1. $T$ has exactly one fixed point $x^* \in X$.
  2. For any starting point $x_0 \in X$, the sequence $x_{n+1} = T(x_n)$ converges to $x^*$.

4. Proof

Proof

Existence. Pick any $x_0 \in X$ and define $x_n = T^n(x_0)$ (i.e. $x_{n+1} = T(x_n)$). We show $(x_n)$ is Cauchy.

First, by induction on the contraction property:

$$d(x_{n+1}, x_n) = d(T(x_n), T(x_{n-1})) \leq k \cdot d(x_n, x_{n-1}) \leq \cdots \leq k^n \cdot d(x_1, x_0)$$

For $m > n$, the triangle inequality gives:

$$d(x_m, x_n) \leq \sum_{j=n}^{m-1} d(x_{j+1}, x_j) \leq \sum_{j=n}^{m-1} k^j \cdot d(x_1, x_0) \leq \frac{k^n}{1 - k} \cdot d(x_1, x_0)$$

Since $k < 1$, we have $k^n \to 0$, so the right-hand side tends to zero as $n \to \infty$. Hence $(x_n)$ is Cauchy.

Since $X$ is complete, there exists $x^* \in X$ with $x_n \to x^*$. We show $T(x^*) = x^*$. Since $T$ is continuous (being Lipschitz),

$$T(x^*) = T\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} x_n\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} T(x_n) = \lim_{n \to \infty} x_{n+1} = x^*$$

Uniqueness. Suppose $T(x^*) = x^*$ and $T(y^*) = y^*$. Then

$$d(x^*, y^*) = d(T(x^*), T(y^*)) \leq k \cdot d(x^*, y^*)$$

So $(1 - k) \cdot d(x^*, y^*) \leq 0$. Since $1 - k > 0$, this forces $d(x^*, y^*) = 0$, hence $x^* = y^*$.

∎

5. Error estimate

The proof gives a quantitative convergence rate. From the Cauchy estimate, letting $m \to \infty$:

Corollary — A priori error bound
$$d(x_n, x^*) \leq \frac{k^n}{1 - k} \cdot d(x_1, x_0)$$

Convergence is geometric with ratio $k$. If $k = 1/2$, each iteration roughly doubles the number of correct digits. If $k = 0.99$, convergence is painfully slow — you need about 230 iterations to reduce the error by a factor of 10.

There is also an a posteriori bound, useful when you can measure the last step but not the first:

$$d(x_n, x^*) \leq \frac{k}{1 - k} \cdot d(x_n, x_{n-1})$$

This follows from the same geometric series argument, starting from $d(x_n, x_{n-1})$ instead of $d(x_1, x_0)$.

6. Application: Cauchy–Lipschitz revisited

Consider the ODE $y' = f(t, y)$ with $y(0) = y_0$, where $f$ is continuous and Lipschitz in $y$ with constant $L$. The equivalent integral equation is

$$y(t) = y_0 + \int_0^t f(s, y(s)) \, ds$$

Define the Picard operator $T$ on $C([0, \delta])$ by

$$T(\varphi)(t) = y_0 + \int_0^t f(s, \varphi(s)) \, ds$$

For two functions $\varphi, \psi \in C([0, \delta])$:

$$|T(\varphi)(t) - T(\psi)(t)| \leq \int_0^t |f(s, \varphi(s)) - f(s, \psi(s))| \, ds \leq L \int_0^t |\varphi(s) - \psi(s)| \, ds \leq L\delta \cdot \|\varphi - \psi\|_\infty$$

So $\|T(\varphi) - T(\psi)\|_\infty \leq L\delta \cdot \|\varphi - \psi\|_\infty$. Choosing $\delta < 1/L$ makes $T$ a contraction on the complete metric space $(C([0, \delta]), \|\cdot\|_\infty)$. Banach's theorem gives a unique fixed point — the solution to the ODE.

7. Application: Newton's method

To solve $f(x) = 0$ where $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is smooth, Newton's iteration is

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} = T(x_n)$$

Near a simple root $x^*$ (where $f(x^*) = 0$ and $f'(x^*) \neq 0$), the map $T$ is a contraction. Indeed, $T'(x) = f(x) f''(x) / [f'(x)]^2$, so $T'(x^*) = 0$ since $f(x^*) = 0$. By continuity, $|T'(x)| < 1$ in a neighbourhood of $x^*$, and the mean value theorem gives $|T(x) - T(y)| \leq k|x - y|$ with $k < 1$ on that neighbourhood.

Restricting to a closed ball $\overline{B}(x^*, r)$ (which is complete as a closed subset of $\mathbb{R}$) and verifying $T$ maps this ball into itself, Banach's theorem guarantees convergence. In practice, the convergence is even better: since $T'(x^*) = 0$, Newton's method converges quadratically (the error squares at each step).

Historical note

Stefan Banach proved this theorem in his 1920 doctoral thesis at the University of Lwów (now Lviv), published in 1922. The result was part of his broader programme of founding functional analysis — the same thesis introduced Banach spaces.

Banach was a central figure of the Lwów school of mathematics, which gathered at the Scottish Café (Kawiarnia Szkocka). Problems were recorded in the famous Scottish Book. Despite working under German occupation during World War II (surviving partly by feeding lice for a typhus research institute), Banach's ideas became foundational to modern analysis.

The fixed point theorem unified iteration arguments that had appeared independently in Picard's work on ODEs (1890), Fredholm's integral equations (1903), and many other contexts. It remains one of the most widely applied theorems in mathematics.

What breaks

Without completeness. Define $T(x) = x/2$ on the open interval $(0, 1)$. Then $|T(x) - T(y)| = |x - y|/2$, so $T$ is a contraction with $k = 1/2$. But the fixed point is $x^* = 0$, which does not belong to $(0, 1)$. The space is not complete, and the Cauchy sequence of iterates escapes.

Without strict contraction ($k = 1$). The map $T(x) = x + 1/x$ on $[1, \infty)$ satisfies $|T(x) - T(y)| \leq |x - y|$ for large $x, y$ (it is non-expansive), but has no fixed point: $T(x) = x$ would require $1/x = 0$. More simply, the identity map $T(x) = x$ satisfies $|T(x) - T(y)| = |x-y|$ but every point is a fixed point — uniqueness fails.

The theorem is global. The fixed point is unique in all of $X$, not just in some neighbourhood. And iteration converges from any starting point, not just from nearby ones. This is much stronger than local fixed-point theorems.

A contraction on a complete space has exactly one fixed point, and iteration from anywhere converges to it — the abstract engine behind Picard, Newton, and countless existence proofs.
