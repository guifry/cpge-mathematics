Completeness in Metric Spaces

Phase 9 — Topology of Metric Spaces, Page 3

The problem

In Phase 2, page 5, we proved the Cauchy criterion for $\mathbb{R}$: a sequence converges if and only if its terms get arbitrarily close to each other. The "if" direction — Cauchy implies convergent — worked because $\mathbb{R}$ has no holes. Every cluster point is already a real number.

But $\mathbb{Q}$ with the usual metric is not like this. The sequence $1,\; 1.4,\; 1.41,\; 1.414,\; \ldots$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{Q}$ — the terms get arbitrarily close — yet it converges to $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$. The limit has escaped the space.

Which metric spaces have the property that Cauchy sequences can never escape? And when a space fails to have it, can we repair the damage?

1. Cauchy Sequences in Metric Spaces

We generalise the definition from $\mathbb{R}$ to any metric space $(X, d)$ by replacing $|a_m - a_n|$ with $d(a_m, a_n)$.

Definition

A sequence $(x_n)$ in a metric space $(X, d)$ is a Cauchy sequence if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $m, n \geq N$,

$$d(x_m, x_n) < \varepsilon$$

The key relationship between convergence and the Cauchy property carries over immediately.

Proposition

Every convergent sequence in a metric space is Cauchy.

Proof

Suppose $x_n \to L$ in $(X, d)$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. By convergence, there exists $N$ such that for all $n \geq N$: $d(x_n, L) < \varepsilon / 2$. For any $m, n \geq N$, the triangle inequality gives:

$$d(x_m, x_n) \leq d(x_m, L) + d(L, x_n) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon$$
∎

The converse — Cauchy implies convergent — is not true in general. It depends on the space. That dependence is exactly what completeness captures.

2. Complete Metric Spaces

Definition

A metric space $(X, d)$ is complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a limit in $X$.

The phrase "in $X$" is doing all the work. Cauchy sequences always want to converge — their terms are clustering. Completeness means the target is present.

$\mathbb{R}$ is complete. We proved this in Phase 2, page 5 as the Cauchy criterion. That proof used Bolzano-Weierstrass, which itself rests on the completeness axiom (least upper bound property) of $\mathbb{R}$.

Theorem

$\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean metric is complete.

Proof

Let $(\mathbf{x}_k)$ be a Cauchy sequence in $\mathbb{R}^n$, where $\mathbf{x}_k = (x_k^{(1)}, \ldots, x_k^{(n)})$. For each coordinate $i$:

$$|x_m^{(i)} - x_k^{(i)}| \leq \|\mathbf{x}_m - \mathbf{x}_k\| = d(\mathbf{x}_m, \mathbf{x}_k)$$

So the sequence $(x_k^{(i)})_{k \geq 1}$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$ for each $i$. Since $\mathbb{R}$ is complete, each coordinate sequence converges: $x_k^{(i)} \to L^{(i)}$. Set $\mathbf{L} = (L^{(1)}, \ldots, L^{(n)})$. Then:

$$d(\mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{L}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_k^{(i)} - L^{(i)}|^2} \to 0$$

since each term under the square root tends to zero. Therefore $\mathbf{x}_k \to \mathbf{L}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

∎
Theorem

The space $\mathcal{C}([a,b])$ of continuous functions $[a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$, equipped with the sup metric $d(f,g) = \sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x) - g(x)|$, is complete.

Proof

Let $(f_n)$ be Cauchy in $(\mathcal{C}([a,b]),\, d)$. For each fixed $x \in [a,b]$:

$$|f_m(x) - f_n(x)| \leq \sup_{t \in [a,b]} |f_m(t) - f_n(t)| = d(f_m, f_n)$$

So $(f_n(x))$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$, hence converges to some value $f(x)$. This defines a function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ pointwise.

We need $f$ to be continuous and $d(f_n, f) \to 0$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $(f_n)$ is Cauchy, there exists $N$ such that for all $m, n \geq N$: $d(f_m, f_n) < \varepsilon / 2$. That is, for all $x$: $|f_m(x) - f_n(x)| < \varepsilon / 2$. Letting $m \to \infty$ (with $x$ fixed):

$$|f(x) - f_n(x)| \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{2} < \varepsilon \quad \text{for all } x \in [a,b], \; n \geq N$$

So $d(f_n, f) \leq \varepsilon / 2 < \varepsilon$ for $n \geq N$: the convergence $f_n \to f$ is uniform. The uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous (Phase 5, page 6), so $f \in \mathcal{C}([a,b])$.

∎

3. Incomplete Spaces

$\mathbb{Q}$ is not complete. Define the sequence $(q_n)$ by truncating the decimal expansion of $\sqrt{2}$: $q_1 = 1$, $q_2 = 1.4$, $q_3 = 1.41$, $q_4 = 1.414$, and so on. Each $q_n \in \mathbb{Q}$, and $|q_m - q_n| \leq 10^{-\min(m,n)}$ for $m \neq n$, so the sequence is Cauchy. But $q_n \to \sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$.

$(0,1)$ is not complete. The sequence $x_n = 1/n$ lies in $(0,1)$ for $n \geq 2$. It is Cauchy: $|1/m - 1/n| \leq 1/n \to 0$. But $x_n \to 0 \notin (0,1)$. The limit has escaped through the boundary.

Here is a subtlety worth pausing on. The interval $(0,1)$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}$ via, say, $\varphi(x) = \tan(\pi(x - 1/2))$. And $\mathbb{R}$ is complete. So completeness is not a topological property. It depends on the specific metric, not just on which sets are open or closed. Two spaces can be topologically identical yet differ in completeness.

4. Completion

When a space has holes, we can fill them in — systematically.

Theorem (existence of completion)

Every metric space $(X, d)$ has a completion: a complete metric space $(\hat{X}, \hat{d})$ together with an isometric embedding $\iota : X \hookrightarrow \hat{X}$ such that $\iota(X)$ is dense in $\hat{X}$. The completion is unique up to isometry.

We state this without full proof — the construction mirrors Cantor's. You take equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences in $X$, declaring two sequences equivalent when their distance tends to zero, then define a metric on these classes. The original space embeds as the "constant" sequences.

The archetype: $\mathbb{R}$ is the completion of $\mathbb{Q}$. Every real number is (or can be represented as) an equivalence class of Cauchy sequences of rationals. This was Cantor's 1872 construction.

5. Closed Subsets of Complete Spaces

Completeness and closedness are deeply connected. A closed subset "keeps all its limit points," which is exactly what completeness needs.

Theorem

Let $(X, d)$ be a complete metric space and $A \subseteq X$. Then $A$ (with the restricted metric) is complete if and only if $A$ is closed in $X$.

Proof

($\Longrightarrow$) Complete implies closed. Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence in $A$ converging to some $L \in X$. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy (Section 1). Since $A$ is complete, this Cauchy sequence converges in $A$. Limits are unique in metric spaces (Phase 9, page 1), so $L \in A$. Therefore $A$ contains all its limit points, i.e. $A$ is closed.

($\Longleftarrow$) Closed implies complete. Let $(x_n)$ be Cauchy in $A$. Since $A \subseteq X$ and $X$ is complete, $(x_n)$ converges to some $L \in X$. Since $A$ is closed and $(x_n) \subseteq A$ with $x_n \to L$, we have $L \in A$. So the Cauchy sequence converges in $A$.

∎

This is powerful. To check completeness of a subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, you just check closedness — because $\mathbb{R}^n$ is already complete. For instance: $[a,b]$ is closed in $\mathbb{R}$, hence complete. The set $\{1/n : n \geq 1\}$ is not closed (its limit point $0$ is missing), hence not complete.

Historical note

Cantor (1872) constructed $\mathbb{R}$ as equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences of rationals — the first completion. Charles Méray independently gave an essentially identical construction in 1869, though with less influence. Cantor's motivation was not abstract topology but trigonometric series: he needed to understand limit points of sets of real numbers, which led him to develop both the completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ and the beginnings of set theory.

The abstract notion of completeness for metric spaces was formalised by Fréchet (1906), who introduced metric spaces themselves, and by Hausdorff (1914), who systematised the theory in his Grundzüge der Mengenlehre. Banach (1920s) made completeness central to functional analysis: a Banach space is a complete normed vector space, and the completeness of $\mathcal{C}([a,b])$ under the sup norm is the prototype.

What breaks

Without completeness: the intermediate value theorem fails. In $\mathbb{Q}$, the function $f(x) = x^2 - 2$ satisfies $f(1) = -1 < 0$ and $f(2) = 2 > 0$, yet there is no $c \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $f(c) = 0$. The IVT (Phase 2, page 6) relies on the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ — without it, continuous functions can jump over values.

Completeness is not a topological invariant. The spaces $(0,1)$ and $\mathbb{R}$ are homeomorphic (via $x \mapsto \tan(\pi(x - 1/2))$), yet $(0,1)$ with the standard metric is incomplete while $\mathbb{R}$ is complete. A homeomorphism preserves open sets, closed sets, convergence, continuity — but not completeness. Completeness depends on the metric, not the topology.

A complete subspace need not be open. $\mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{R}$ is complete (every Cauchy sequence of integers is eventually constant), yet $\mathbb{Z}$ is not open in $\mathbb{R}$. Completeness and openness are unrelated properties.

A metric space is complete when Cauchy sequences have nowhere to escape to — every would-be limit is already present.
