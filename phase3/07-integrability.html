Integrability and Properties

Phase 3 — Calculus, Page 7

The problem

On the previous page we defined the Riemann integral using upper and lower sums. We showed $x^2$ is integrable by explicit computation. We showed Dirichlet’s function is not integrable. But computing upper and lower sums by hand for every function is impractical — we need a general theorem telling us which functions are integrable.

Once we know a function is integrable, we need algebraic properties: can we integrate sums? Pull out constants? Compare integrals? Split the interval? All of these must be proved from the definition, not assumed. The key tool is uniform continuity — a strengthening of continuity that only works on closed bounded intervals.

1. Uniform continuity

Ordinary continuity says: at each point $x$, for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|y - x| < \delta$ implies $|f(y) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$. The crucial detail is that $\delta$ can depend on the point $x$. Near a steep part of the graph, $\delta$ might be tiny; near a flat part, $\delta$ might be large.

Uniform continuity removes this dependence: one $\delta$ works for all points simultaneously.

Definition — Uniform continuity

A function $f$ is uniformly continuous on $[a, b]$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in [a, b]$ with $|x - y| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon$.

The quantifier order matters. In ordinary continuity: $\forall x,\; \forall \varepsilon,\; \exists \delta$. In uniform continuity: $\forall \varepsilon,\; \exists \delta,\; \forall x, y$. The $\delta$ is chosen before the points are, so it cannot depend on them.

On a closed bounded interval, continuity automatically upgrades to uniform continuity. This is Heine’s theorem.

Theorem — Heine’s theorem

If $f$ is continuous on a closed bounded interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $[a, b]$.

Proof (by contradiction, using Bolzano–Weierstrass)

Suppose $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ but not uniformly continuous. Then there exists $\varepsilon_0 > 0$ such that for every $\delta > 0$, there exist points $x, y \in [a, b]$ with $|x - y| < \delta$ but $|f(x) - f(y)| \geq \varepsilon_0$.

Taking $\delta = 1/n$ for each $n \in \mathbb{N}$, we get sequences $(x_n)$ and $(y_n)$ in $[a, b]$ with

$$|x_n - y_n| < \frac{1}{n} \quad \text{but} \quad |f(x_n) - f(y_n)| \geq \varepsilon_0$$

The sequence $(x_n)$ is bounded (it lives in $[a, b]$), so by Bolzano–Weierstrass (Phase 2, page 4) it has a convergent subsequence: $x_{n_k} \to c$ for some $c \in [a, b]$.

Since $|x_{n_k} - y_{n_k}| < 1/n_k \to 0$, we also have $y_{n_k} \to c$.

By continuity of $f$ at $c$: $f(x_{n_k}) \to f(c)$ and $f(y_{n_k}) \to f(c)$. So $|f(x_{n_k}) - f(y_{n_k})| \to 0$. But we required $|f(x_{n_k}) - f(y_{n_k})| \geq \varepsilon_0 > 0$ for all $k$ — contradiction.

∎

2. Continuous functions are Riemann integrable

This is the main theorem of the page. The proof combines uniform continuity with the Riemann integrability criterion: $f$ is integrable if and only if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a partition $P$ with $U(f, P) - L(f, P) < \varepsilon$.

Theorem — Integrability of continuous functions

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$.

Proof

Let $\varepsilon > 0$. We need a partition $P$ with $U(f, P) - L(f, P) < \varepsilon$.

Step 1. Get a uniform $\delta$. Since $f$ is continuous on the closed bounded interval $[a, b]$, Heine’s theorem gives uniform continuity. So there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x, y \in [a, b]$ with $|x - y| < \delta$:

$$|f(x) - f(y)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{b - a}$$

Step 2. Choose a fine partition. Pick any partition $P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ of $[a, b]$ with every subinterval of width less than $\delta$: $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1} < \delta$ for all $i$.

Step 3. Bound the oscillation on each subinterval. On $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, let $M_i = \sup f$ and $m_i = \inf f$. Any two points in this subinterval are within $\delta$ of each other (since the subinterval has width $< \delta$). By uniform continuity, the values of $f$ at any two such points differ by less than $\varepsilon / (b - a)$. In particular, the oscillation satisfies

$$M_i - m_i \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{b - a}$$

Step 4. Sum.

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (M_i - m_i)\,\Delta x_i \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{b - a} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \Delta x_i = \frac{\varepsilon}{b - a} \cdot (b - a) = \varepsilon$$

So for any $\varepsilon > 0$, a fine enough partition makes $U - L < \varepsilon$. By the integrability criterion, $f$ is Riemann integrable.

∎

The proof is clean because uniform continuity gives us a single $\delta$ that controls the oscillation on every subinterval simultaneously. With ordinary continuity, the $\delta$ at each point might shrink unpredictably, and we couldn’t guarantee a uniform bound on $M_i - m_i$.

3. Monotone functions are integrable

Continuous functions aren’t the only integrable ones. Monotone functions are integrable too — even if they have jump discontinuities.

Theorem — Integrability of monotone functions

If $f$ is monotone (increasing or decreasing) on $[a, b]$, then $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$.

Proof (for $f$ increasing; decreasing is identical)

Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Use the uniform partition with $n$ subintervals: $x_i = a + i(b - a)/n$, so $\Delta x_i = (b - a)/n$ for all $i$.

Since $f$ is increasing, on $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ the supremum is $M_i = f(x_i)$ and the infimum is $m_i = f(x_{i-1})$. So

$$U(f, P) - L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})\bigr) \cdot \frac{b - a}{n}$$

The sum is telescoping:

$$= \frac{b - a}{n} \cdot \bigl(f(b) - f(a)\bigr)$$

For any $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $n$ large enough that $(b - a)(f(b) - f(a))/n < \varepsilon$. Then $U(f, P) - L(f, P) < \varepsilon$.

∎

Notice: we never assumed continuity. A monotone function on $[a, b]$ can have countably many jump discontinuities (for instance, a staircase function with jumps at every rational) and is still integrable. The telescoping trick makes the total oscillation depend only on $f(b) - f(a)$, regardless of what happens in between.

4. Properties of the integral

We now know that continuous and monotone functions are integrable. For the rest of this section, assume all functions mentioned are integrable on $[a, b]$. We prove the algebraic properties directly from the definition.

Linearity

Theorem — Linearity of the integral

If $f$ and $g$ are integrable on $[a, b]$ and $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$, then $\alpha f + \beta g$ is integrable on $[a, b]$ and

$$\int_a^b \bigl(\alpha f + \beta g\bigr) = \alpha \int_a^b f + \beta \int_a^b g$$
Proof (in two steps)

Step 1. Constant multiple: $\int \alpha f = \alpha \int f$. If $\alpha \geq 0$: on any subinterval, $\sup(\alpha f) = \alpha \sup f$ and $\inf(\alpha f) = \alpha \inf f$. So $U(\alpha f, P) = \alpha\, U(f, P)$ and $L(\alpha f, P) = \alpha\, L(f, P)$. Taking the infimum of upper sums and supremum of lower sums gives $\int \alpha f = \alpha \int f$.

If $\alpha < 0$: then $\sup(\alpha f) = \alpha \inf f$ and $\inf(\alpha f) = \alpha \sup f$ (multiplying by a negative number reverses the order). So $U(\alpha f, P) = \alpha\, L(f, P)$ and $L(\alpha f, P) = \alpha\, U(f, P)$. Taking infimum and supremum again gives $\int \alpha f = \alpha \int f$.

Step 2. Sum: $\int (f + g) = \int f + \int g$. On each subinterval, $\sup(f + g) \leq \sup f + \sup g$ (the sum of two functions can’t exceed the sum of their individual suprema — the suprema might occur at different points). So $U(f + g, P) \leq U(f, P) + U(g, P)$.

Similarly, $\inf(f + g) \geq \inf f + \inf g$, giving $L(f + g, P) \geq L(f, P) + L(g, P)$.

Therefore:

$$L(f, P) + L(g, P) \leq L(f + g, P) \leq \int_a^b (f + g) \leq U(f + g, P) \leq U(f, P) + U(g, P)$$

Since $f$ and $g$ are integrable, for any $\varepsilon > 0$ we can find a common refinement $P$ making $U(f, P) - L(f, P) < \varepsilon$ and $U(g, P) - L(g, P) < \varepsilon$. The squeeze gives $\int (f + g) = \int f + \int g$.

∎

Monotonicity

Theorem — Monotonicity of the integral

If $f(x) \leq g(x)$ for all $x \in [a, b]$, then $\displaystyle\int_a^b f \leq \int_a^b g$.

Proof

On each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, $\inf f \leq \inf g$ (since $f \leq g$ pointwise). So $L(f, P) \leq L(g, P)$ for every partition $P$. Taking the supremum over all partitions: $\int_a^b f = \sup_P L(f, P) \leq \sup_P L(g, P) = \int_a^b g$.

∎

Absolute value inequality

Theorem — Triangle inequality for integrals

If $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$, then $|f|$ is integrable and $\displaystyle\left|\int_a^b f\right| \leq \int_a^b |f|$.

Proof

Since $-|f(x)| \leq f(x) \leq |f(x)|$ for all $x$, monotonicity gives

$$-\int_a^b |f| \leq \int_a^b f \leq \int_a^b |f|$$

which is exactly $\left|\int_a^b f\right| \leq \int_a^b |f|$.

∎

Additivity over intervals

Theorem — Additivity (Chasles’s relation)

If $f$ is integrable on $[a, c]$ and $a < b < c$, then

$$\int_a^c f = \int_a^b f + \int_b^c f$$
Proof

Let $P$ be any partition of $[a, c]$. Let $P^* = P \cup \{b\}$ be the refinement that includes the splitting point $b$. Refinement can only decrease $U - L$, so $U(f, P^*) \leq U(f, P)$ and $L(f, P^*) \geq L(f, P)$.

The partition $P^*$ splits naturally into a partition $P_1$ of $[a, b]$ and a partition $P_2$ of $[b, c]$. Their upper and lower sums add:

$$U(f, P^*) = U(f, P_1) + U(f, P_2), \qquad L(f, P^*) = L(f, P_1) + L(f, P_2)$$

Taking infimum of upper sums and supremum of lower sums over all such split partitions gives $\int_a^c f = \int_a^b f + \int_b^c f$.

∎

5. The integral as a function of its upper limit

Everything so far treats the integral as a number. Now we let the upper limit vary. Fix $a$ and define, for each $x \in [a, b]$:

$$F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$$

This is a function of $x$. As $x$ increases from $a$ to $b$, $F(x)$ accumulates the signed area under $f$ from $a$ to $x$. Note $F(a) = 0$.

Theorem — $F$ is continuous

If $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$, then $F(x) = \int_a^x f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$.

Proof

Since $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$, it is bounded: there exists $M > 0$ with $|f(t)| \leq M$ for all $t \in [a, b]$. For any $x, x + h \in [a, b]$:

$$|F(x + h) - F(x)| = \left|\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\, dt\right| \leq \int_x^{x+h} |f(t)|\, dt \leq M \cdot |h|$$

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, take $\delta = \varepsilon / M$. Then $|h| < \delta$ implies $|F(x + h) - F(x)| \leq M \cdot |h| < \varepsilon$. So $F$ is continuous (in fact, Lipschitz continuous).

∎

This result is stronger than it looks. The function $f$ itself might have jump discontinuities (as long as it remains integrable). Yet $F$ is always continuous — integration smooths out jumps. On the next page, we go further: if $f$ is continuous at a point $x_0$, then $F$ is differentiable at $x_0$ with $F'(x_0) = f(x_0)$. That is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Historical note

Eduard Heine proved in 1872 that continuity on a closed interval implies uniform continuity. His proof used what we now call the Heine–Borel theorem: every open cover of $[a, b]$ has a finite subcover. The sequential proof we gave above (via Bolzano–Weierstrass) is equivalent — both rely on compactness of $[a, b]$ (closed + bounded in $\mathbb{R}$). In topology, compactness is the property that does the heavy lifting whenever you need to extract a finite amount of control from infinitely many local conditions.

What breaks on open intervals

On an open interval $(a, b)$, continuous does not imply uniformly continuous. Consider $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ on $(0, 1)$. This function is continuous, but near $x = 0$ it oscillates between $-1$ and $+1$ with increasing rapidity. For any $\delta > 0$, there exist points $x, y \in (0, \delta)$ with $|x - y| < \delta$ but $|f(x) - f(y)| = 2$. No single $\delta$ works for $\varepsilon = 1$.

On the closed interval $[a, b]$, the boundary points are trapped: Bolzano–Weierstrass forces the subsequence to converge inside the interval. On an open interval, the sequences can escape towards the boundary, where continuity provides no control.

Continuous on a closed interval means integrable — uniform continuity forces the upper and lower sums to agree.
