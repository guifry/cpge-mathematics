Variation of Parameters

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 5

The problem

You know how to solve $y'' + y = 0$: the solutions are $c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t$ (we built these from their power series). But what about $y'' + y = \sec t$? The right-hand side is not zero — it is a forcing term pushing the system away from its natural oscillation.

Lagrange (1774) invented a beautifully simple trick: take the homogeneous solutions and let their "constants" vary. Instead of $y = c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t$, try $y = u_1(t)\cos t + u_2(t)\sin t$ and determine the functions $u_1, u_2$. The method works for any forcing term — no guessing required.

1. The non-homogeneous problem

Consider a second-order linear ODE with a forcing term:

$$y'' + p(t)\,y' + q(t)\,y = g(t).$$

Write $L[y] = y'' + p(t)\,y' + q(t)\,y$ for the linear differential operator. Linearity means $L[\alpha y_1 + \beta y_2] = \alpha\, L[y_1] + \beta\, L[y_2]$.

Suppose $y_h$ solves the homogeneous equation $L[y_h] = 0$ and $y_p$ solves $L[y_p] = g(t)$. Then $y = y_h + y_p$ solves the full equation:

$$L[y_h + y_p] = L[y_h] + L[y_p] = 0 + g(t) = g(t).$$

So the general solution of the non-homogeneous equation is: homogeneous general solution plus any one particular solution. All that remains is to find $y_p$.

2. The ansatz

Let $y_1, y_2$ be a fundamental system of $L[y] = 0$ — two linearly independent solutions, so the homogeneous general solution is $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ (see page 4).

The idea: seek a particular solution in the form

$$y_p(t) = u_1(t)\, y_1(t) + u_2(t)\, y_2(t)$$

where $u_1$ and $u_2$ are unknown functions. We have two unknowns but only one equation ($L[y_p] = g$). This gives us freedom to impose an extra condition.

3. The key trick

Differentiate the ansatz:

$$y_p' = u_1'\, y_1 + u_1\, y_1' + u_2'\, y_2 + u_2\, y_2'.$$

This has four terms, two of which involve $u_1'$ and $u_2'$. To keep $y_p''$ manageable, we impose the Lagrange condition:

$$u_1'\, y_1 + u_2'\, y_2 = 0.$$

With this constraint, the derivative simplifies to

$$y_p' = u_1\, y_1' + u_2\, y_2'.$$

Differentiate again:

$$y_p'' = u_1\, y_1'' + u_2\, y_2'' + u_1'\, y_1' + u_2'\, y_2'.$$

4. Substitution into the ODE

Substitute $y_p$, $y_p'$, $y_p''$ into $L[y_p] = g$:

$$\bigl(u_1\, y_1'' + u_2\, y_2'' + u_1'\, y_1' + u_2'\, y_2'\bigr) + p\bigl(u_1\, y_1' + u_2\, y_2'\bigr) + q\bigl(u_1\, y_1 + u_2\, y_2\bigr) = g.$$

Regroup by factor:

$$u_1\underbrace{(y_1'' + p\,y_1' + q\,y_1)}_{L[y_1] = 0} + u_2\underbrace{(y_2'' + p\,y_2' + q\,y_2)}_{L[y_2] = 0} + u_1'\,y_1' + u_2'\,y_2' = g.$$

Since $y_1$ and $y_2$ are homogeneous solutions, $L[y_1] = L[y_2] = 0$. What survives is:

$$u_1'\, y_1' + u_2'\, y_2' = g(t).$$

5. The system and Cramer's rule

We now have two equations in two unknowns $u_1'$ and $u_2'$:

$$\begin{cases} u_1'\, y_1 + u_2'\, y_2 = 0 \\ u_1'\, y_1' + u_2'\, y_2' = g(t) \end{cases}$$

The determinant of the coefficient matrix is the Wronskian $W(t) = y_1\, y_2' - y_1'\, y_2$, which is nonzero because $y_1, y_2$ are linearly independent (page 4). By Cramer's rule:

$$u_1' = \frac{\begin{vmatrix} 0 & y_2 \\ g & y_2' \end{vmatrix}}{W} = \frac{-y_2\, g}{W}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{\begin{vmatrix} y_1 & 0 \\ y_1' & g \end{vmatrix}}{W} = \frac{y_1\, g}{W}.$$

Integrate to obtain $u_1$ and $u_2$.

6. The variation of parameters formula

Theorem — Variation of parameters

Let $y_1, y_2$ be a fundamental system of $y'' + p(t)\,y' + q(t)\,y = 0$ with Wronskian $W$. A particular solution of $y'' + p(t)\,y' + q(t)\,y = g(t)$ is

$$y_p(t) = -y_1(t)\int_{t_0}^{t} \frac{y_2(s)\,g(s)}{W(s)}\,ds \;+\; y_2(t)\int_{t_0}^{t} \frac{y_1(s)\,g(s)}{W(s)}\,ds.$$
Proof

Step 1. We seek $y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2$ with the Lagrange condition $u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 = 0$. As derived above, substituting into the ODE and using $L[y_1] = L[y_2] = 0$ yields the system

$$u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 = 0, \qquad u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2' = g.$$

Step 2. Since $W = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2 \neq 0$, Cramer's rule gives $u_1' = -y_2 g / W$ and $u_2' = y_1 g / W$.

Step 3. Integrate: $u_1(t) = -\int_{t_0}^{t} y_2(s)\,g(s)/W(s)\,ds$ and $u_2(t) = \int_{t_0}^{t} y_1(s)\,g(s)/W(s)\,ds$.

Step 4. The particular solution is $y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2$, which gives the stated formula. We verify directly: $y_p(t_0) = 0$ and $y_p'(t_0) = 0$ (from the Lagrange condition and the fundamental theorem of calculus), and $L[y_p] = g$ by construction.

□

7. Example: $y'' + y = \sec t$

The homogeneous equation $y'' + y = 0$ has fundamental system $y_1 = \cos t$, $y_2 = \sin t$. The Wronskian is

$$W = \cos t \cdot \cos t - (-\sin t)\cdot \sin t = \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1.$$

Apply the formula with $g(t) = \sec t$:

$$u_1' = -\frac{\sin t \cdot \sec t}{1} = -\frac{\sin t}{\cos t} = -\tan t.$$
$$u_1 = -\int \tan t\, dt = \ln|\cos t|.$$
$$u_2' = \frac{\cos t \cdot \sec t}{1} = 1.$$
$$u_2 = \int 1\, dt = t.$$

Therefore the particular solution is

$$y_p = \cos t \ln|\cos t| + t\sin t.$$

The general solution is $y = c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t + \cos t \ln|\cos t| + t \sin t$.

Note the $t \sin t$ term: the forcing function $\sec t$ creates a response that grows linearly in amplitude — a secular term. This kind of growth is invisible to methods like undetermined coefficients, which cannot handle $\sec t$ at all (it is not a polynomial, exponential, or sinusoid). Variation of parameters handles it without difficulty.

Let us verify: $y_p' = -\sin t \ln|\cos t| + \cos t \cdot \frac{-\sin t}{-\cos t} \cdot (-1) + \sin t + t\cos t$. Simplifying carefully: $y_p' = -\sin t \ln|\cos t| + \sin t + t\cos t$. Then $y_p'' = -\cos t \ln|\cos t| + \frac{\sin^2 t}{\cos t} + \cos t - t\sin t + \cos t$. We get $y_p'' + y_p = \frac{\sin^2 t}{\cos t} + \cos t = \frac{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t}{\cos t} = \sec t$. Correct.

8. Example: $y'' - y = e^{2t}$

The homogeneous equation $y'' - y = 0$ has solutions $y_1 = e^t$, $y_2 = e^{-t}$, with Wronskian $W = e^t(-e^{-t}) - e^t \cdot e^{-t} = -1 - 1 = -2$.

By variation of parameters:

$$u_1' = -\frac{e^{-t} \cdot e^{2t}}{-2} = \frac{e^t}{2}, \qquad u_1 = \frac{e^t}{2}.$$
$$u_2' = \frac{e^t \cdot e^{2t}}{-2} = -\frac{e^{3t}}{2}, \qquad u_2 = -\frac{e^{3t}}{6}.$$
$$y_p = \frac{e^t}{2} \cdot e^t - \frac{e^{3t}}{6} \cdot e^{-t} = \frac{e^{2t}}{2} - \frac{e^{2t}}{6} = \frac{e^{2t}}{3}.$$

You can verify: $y_p'' - y_p = 4e^{2t}/3 - e^{2t}/3 = e^{2t}$. Correct.

For this particular forcing term, the method of undetermined coefficients is faster: guess $y_p = Ae^{2t}$, substitute, get $4A - A = 1$, so $A = 1/3$. But undetermined coefficients only works when $g(t)$ is a polynomial, exponential, sine, cosine, or a product of these. Variation of parameters works for any continuous $g(t)$.

9. Extension to $n$-th order

For an $n$-th order equation $L[y] = g$ with fundamental system $y_1, \ldots, y_n$, the variation of parameters formula generalises. Write $y_p = \sum_{k=1}^{n} u_k\, y_k$ with conditions $\sum_k u_k'\, y_k^{(j)} = 0$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, n-2$ and $\sum_k u_k'\, y_k^{(n-1)} = g$. The system is

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1 & \cdots & y_n \\ y_1' & \cdots & y_n' \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ y_1^{(n-1)} & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u_1' \\ u_2' \\ \vdots \\ u_n' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ g \end{pmatrix}.$$

The coefficient matrix is the Wronskian matrix. Its determinant $W \neq 0$ (linear independence), so Cramer's rule gives each $u_k'$ as a ratio of determinants. Then integrate to find $u_k$.

Historical note

Lagrange introduced variation of parameters in 1774, in his work on celestial mechanics. The name comes from the idea of letting the "parameters" (constants of integration) in the homogeneous solution become functions of time. Euler had used similar ideas earlier for specific equations, but Lagrange gave the general method. It extends naturally to systems of ODEs: the matrix version replaces the Wronskian scalar with the fundamental matrix. Unlike undetermined coefficients (which requires guessing the form of $y_p$ and only works for special forcing functions), variation of parameters handles any continuous right-hand side.

What breaks

Remove the fundamental system: the method requires knowing $y_1, y_2$ explicitly. If you cannot solve the homogeneous equation, you cannot apply variation of parameters. The formula shifts the difficulty from "solve the non-homogeneous equation" to "solve the homogeneous equation and then integrate."

Non-integrable forcing terms: the integrals $\int y_i\, g / W\, dt$ may not have closed-form antiderivatives. For instance, $y'' + y = e^{-t^2}$ leads to integrals involving the error function. The formula still gives an integral representation of the solution, which is valuable for analysis (bounding, asymptotics) even when you cannot write a neat formula.

Remove linearity: for nonlinear ODEs $y'' + f(y) = g(t)$, the superposition principle fails — $y_h + y_p$ is no longer a solution. The entire method collapses.

Let the constants vary: if you know the homogeneous solutions, the Wronskian and an integral give you the particular solution for any forcing term.
