The Poincaré-Bendixson Theorem

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 8

The problem

Poincaré (1881–1886) studied the long-term behaviour of ODEs in the plane. A remarkable rigidity appears in two dimensions: if a solution stays bounded forever and does not settle at an equilibrium, it must approach a periodic orbit. There is no other possibility — no chaos, no quasi-periodicity, no strange attractors. Chaos requires at least three dimensions. Why is the plane so special?

1. Omega-limit sets

Definition — Omega-limit set

For a solution $\mathbf{y}(t)$ of $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ defined for all $t \geq 0$, the omega-limit set is

$$\omega(\mathbf{y}) = \bigcap_{T \geq 0} \overline{\{\mathbf{y}(t) : t \geq T\}}.$$

Equivalently, $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$ iff there exists a sequence $t_n \to \infty$ with $\mathbf{y}(t_n) \to p$.

The omega-limit set captures where the trajectory "ends up" as $t \to \infty$. It is the set of all accumulation points of the orbit in forward time.

Theorem — Properties of omega-limit sets

If the trajectory $\{\mathbf{y}(t) : t \geq 0\}$ is bounded, then $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is nonempty, compact, and connected.

Proof

Step 1. (Nonempty.) The trajectory lies in a bounded set. By Bolzano-Weierstrass (Phase 2, page 4, generalised to $\mathbb{R}^2$), any sequence $\mathbf{y}(n)$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ has a convergent subsequence. Its limit is in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$.

Step 2. (Closed.) $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is an intersection of closed sets (closures), hence closed.

Step 3. (Bounded.) $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ lies in the closure of the (bounded) trajectory, hence is bounded.

Step 4. (Compact.) Closed and bounded in $\mathbb{R}^2$, so compact by Heine-Borel.

Step 5. (Connected.) Suppose $\omega(\mathbf{y}) = U_1 \cup U_2$ with $U_1, U_2$ nonempty, disjoint, and closed (in the subspace topology). Since they are compact and disjoint, $d = \inf\{\|p_1 - p_2\| : p_1 \in U_1, p_2 \in U_2\} > 0$. For large $t$, the trajectory $\mathbf{y}(t)$ must visit within $d/3$ of both $U_1$ and $U_2$ infinitely often. By continuity of $\mathbf{y}(t)$, the intermediate value theorem shows $\mathbf{y}$ must pass through the "gap" between $U_1$ and $U_2$, producing accumulation points outside $U_1 \cup U_2$ — a contradiction.

□

2. Flow invariance

Lemma — Invariance of omega-limit sets

$\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is invariant under the flow: if $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$, then the entire orbit through $p$ lies in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$.

Proof

Step 1. Let $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$. Then there exists $t_n \to \infty$ with $\mathbf{y}(t_n) \to p$.

Step 2. Let $\phi_s$ denote the flow map (the solution at time $s$ starting from a given point). By continuous dependence on initial conditions (page 2): $\phi_s(\mathbf{y}(t_n)) \to \phi_s(p)$ as $n \to \infty$.

Step 3. But $\phi_s(\mathbf{y}(t_n)) = \mathbf{y}(t_n + s)$. Since $t_n + s \to \infty$, the point $\phi_s(p)$ is a limit of $\mathbf{y}$ along a sequence of times tending to infinity. So $\phi_s(p) \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$.

Step 4. This holds for every $s \in \mathbb{R}$ (both forward and backward time), so the entire orbit through $p$ lies in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$.

□

3. The Jordan curve theorem

The key topological ingredient is a fact specific to the plane.

Theorem — Jordan curve theorem

A simple closed curve (continuous, injective on $[0,1)$ with endpoints identified) in $\mathbb{R}^2$ separates the plane into exactly two connected components: a bounded interior and an unbounded exterior.

This is a deep topological result. Its proof requires tools from algebraic topology (IOU: accessible after Phase 9). We use it as a black box here. The critical point: this theorem is false in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — a closed curve in 3D does not separate space. This is why the Poincaré-Bendixson theorem is fundamentally two-dimensional.

4. The transversal argument

A transversal $\Sigma$ at a point $q$ is a short line segment through $q$ that crosses the flow (i.e. $f(q)$ is not tangent to $\Sigma$). In two dimensions, a trajectory that returns to the same transversal must do so monotonically.

Here is why. Suppose the trajectory leaves $q_1 \in \Sigma$, travels through the plane, and returns to $q_2 \in \Sigma$. The arc of trajectory from $q_1$ to $q_2$, together with the segment of $\Sigma$ between $q_1$ and $q_2$, forms a simple closed curve. By the Jordan curve theorem, this curve separates the plane into inside and outside. The next return to $\Sigma$ must hit a point $q_3$ on the same side of $q_2$ as $q_2$ is of $q_1$ (because the trajectory cannot cross itself — uniqueness of solutions from Cauchy-Lipschitz). So the successive return points $q_1, q_2, q_3, \ldots$ form a monotone sequence on $\Sigma$.

A monotone bounded sequence converges (monotone convergence theorem from Phase 2, page 3). So the return points converge to a limit, and the trajectory through that limit point is periodic (it returns to exactly the same point on $\Sigma$).

5. The theorem

Theorem — Poincaré-Bendixson

Let $f \in C^1(\mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R}^2)$ and let $\mathbf{y}(t)$ be a solution of $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ with bounded forward orbit. If $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ contains no equilibria, then $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is a periodic orbit (a limit cycle).

Proof

Step 1. Since the orbit is bounded, $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is nonempty, compact, and connected (section 1). It is flow-invariant (section 2). Since it contains no equilibria, $f(p) \neq 0$ for every $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$.

Step 2. Pick any $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$ and construct a transversal $\Sigma$ through $p$. This is possible because $f(p) \neq 0$.

Step 3. Since $p \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$, the trajectory $\mathbf{y}(t)$ returns near $p$ infinitely often, hence crosses $\Sigma$ infinitely often (for $\Sigma$ short enough, the flow is transverse and must cross rather than miss). Let the crossing points be $q_1, q_2, q_3, \ldots$ along $\Sigma$.

Step 4. By the monotonicity argument of section 4, the sequence $(q_n)$ is monotone on $\Sigma$ and bounded, so it converges to some point $q^* \in \Sigma$.

Step 5. The limit $q^*$ lies in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ (it is a limit of points $\mathbf{y}(t_n)$ with $t_n \to \infty$). By flow invariance, the orbit through $q^*$ lies in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$.

Step 6. The orbit through $q^*$ must also return to $\Sigma$ (since $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is compact and invariant, the orbit cannot escape to infinity). Let it return to $q^{**} \in \Sigma$. But $q^{**} \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$, and by the same monotonicity argument applied to the orbit in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$, the return points must also converge — but since $q^*$ is already the limit, we get $q^{**} = q^*$.

Step 7. So the orbit through $q^*$ returns to $q^*$: it is periodic. Call it $\gamma$.

Step 8. We claim $\omega(\mathbf{y}) = \gamma$. We already know $\gamma \subseteq \omega(\mathbf{y})$. For the reverse inclusion: take any $p' \in \omega(\mathbf{y})$. The orbit through $p'$ lies in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ (invariance). Using transversals and the monotonicity argument at various points of $\gamma$, one shows that $p'$ must lie on $\gamma$ — otherwise, the orbit through $p'$ would produce crossings of a transversal that violate monotonicity.

□

6. No chaos in two dimensions

Corollary — Trichotomy in 2D

For $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $f \in C^1$, every bounded forward orbit has one of three long-term behaviours:

  • Convergence to an equilibrium.
  • Convergence to a periodic orbit (limit cycle).
  • The trajectory is itself periodic.

There are no other possibilities.

This is extraordinary. In two dimensions, the qualitative dynamics are completely classified: equilibria, periodic orbits, and nothing else. No chaotic wandering, no quasi-periodic motion on tori, no fractal attractors.

7. Example: the van der Pol oscillator

The van der Pol equation $x'' - \mu(1 - x^2)x' + x = 0$ (with $\mu > 0$) models a self-exciting oscillator. Write as a system:

$$x_1' = x_2, \qquad x_2' = \mu(1 - x_1^2)\,x_2 - x_1.$$

Step 1. The only equilibrium is the origin. Setting $x_2 = 0$ and $\mu(1 - x_1^2) \cdot 0 - x_1 = 0$ gives $x_1 = 0$.

Step 2. The origin is unstable. The Jacobian at the origin is $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & \mu \end{pmatrix}$. Its eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda^2 - \mu\lambda + 1 = 0$, giving $\lambda = \frac{\mu \pm \sqrt{\mu^2 - 4}}{2}$. For any $\mu > 0$, the real part of both eigenvalues is $\mu/2 > 0$ (check: the sum of eigenvalues is $\mu > 0$, and for $\mu < 2$ the eigenvalues are complex with real part $\mu/2$). So the origin is unstable by page 7.

Step 3. Trajectories are bounded. For $|x_1|$ and $|x_2|$ large enough, one can construct a trapping region — a closed curve that trajectories can enter but not leave. The damping term $-\mu(1 - x_1^2)x_2$ is strongly negative for $|x_1| > 1$ and large $|x_2|$, which prevents unbounded growth. (The full construction uses a Liénard plane transformation and is standard but lengthy; we omit the details.)

Step 4. Poincaré-Bendixson applies. The only equilibrium (the origin) is unstable, so trajectories starting near it move away. They are bounded, so their omega-limit sets are nonempty. If the omega-limit set contained the origin, the trajectory would have to approach it — but the origin is unstable, a contradiction. So $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ contains no equilibria, and by Poincaré-Bendixson, it is a limit cycle.

This was one of the first rigorous proofs of the existence of a self-sustained oscillation: the system settles into a periodic motion regardless of initial conditions. Van der Pol discovered this experimentally in radio circuits in the 1920s.

8. Why three dimensions are different

The Lorenz system (1963):

$$\dot{x} = \sigma(y - x), \qquad \dot{y} = x(\rho - z) - y, \qquad \dot{z} = xy - \beta z$$

with parameters $\sigma = 10$, $\rho = 28$, $\beta = 8/3$, has bounded trajectories that never settle to an equilibrium or periodic orbit. They wander chaotically on a strange attractor — a fractal set with sensitive dependence on initial conditions.

Why does Poincaré-Bendixson fail here? The Jordan curve theorem is the key: a closed curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$ separates the plane, forcing trajectories to cross transversals monotonically. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, a closed curve does not separate space — trajectories can wind around and past each other freely. The monotonicity argument collapses, and chaotic behaviour becomes possible.

This is a profound dimensional threshold: two dimensions are too constrained for chaos, while three dimensions are rich enough. The Lorenz system was the first concrete demonstration that deterministic ODEs can produce apparently random behaviour — a cornerstone of chaos theory.

Historical note

Poincaré proved the theorem as part of his monumental study of curves defined by differential equations (1881–1886), motivated by celestial mechanics and the three-body problem. He introduced the concepts of limit cycles, omega-limit sets, and the qualitative theory of ODEs — studying the geometry of solutions rather than seeking explicit formulas. Bendixson (1901) sharpened and clarified the result. The Lorenz attractor, discovered numerically by the meteorologist Edward Lorenz (1963) while modelling atmospheric convection, demonstrated that Poincaré-Bendixson genuinely fails in three dimensions. Lorenz's paper "Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow" launched chaos theory.

What breaks

Dimension $\geq 3$: the Lorenz attractor shows that bounded trajectories in $\mathbb{R}^3$ can be chaotic. The Jordan curve theorem fails (curves do not separate $\mathbb{R}^3$), so the transversal monotonicity argument is unavailable.

Equilibria in $\omega(\mathbf{y})$: if the omega-limit set contains equilibria, more complex structures are possible. Homoclinic orbits (trajectories that leave an equilibrium and return to it) and heteroclinic connections (trajectories connecting different equilibria) can appear. The Poincaré-Bendixson theorem has a generalisation covering this case: $\omega(\mathbf{y})$ is then a union of equilibria and orbits connecting them.

Discontinuous vector fields: uniqueness of solutions is essential. If trajectories can cross (because the vector field is not Lipschitz), the monotonicity on transversals breaks down. Consider $f$ with a discontinuity along a line: trajectories arriving from both sides can merge, destroying the one-to-one correspondence that the proof requires.

Unbounded trajectories: if the orbit is not bounded, the omega-limit set may be empty (the trajectory escapes to infinity), and the theorem says nothing.

In the plane, bounded trajectories must converge to an equilibrium or a periodic orbit — the Jordan curve theorem forbids anything more complex.
