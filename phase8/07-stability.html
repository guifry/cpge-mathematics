Stability and Lyapunov's Method

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 7

The problem

A pendulum hanging straight down is stable: push it slightly and it oscillates back. A pencil balanced on its tip is unstable: the tiniest perturbation sends it falling. Both are equilibria — states where nothing changes — yet they respond to disturbances in opposite ways.

How do you formalise "stable" and "unstable" for a general ODE $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$? Lyapunov (1892) found a remarkable answer: you can prove stability without solving the equation, using an "energy-like" function that decreases along solutions.

1. Equilibria

Definition — Equilibrium point

A point $\bar{\mathbf{y}} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an equilibrium (or fixed point) of $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ if $f(\bar{\mathbf{y}}) = 0$.

If $\mathbf{y}(0) = \bar{\mathbf{y}}$, the constant function $\mathbf{y}(t) = \bar{\mathbf{y}}$ solves the ODE for all $t$. The system sits there forever. The question is: what happens when you start near $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$?

2. Definitions of stability

Definition — Lyapunov stability

The equilibrium $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ is:

  • Stable if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|\mathbf{y}(0) - \bar{\mathbf{y}}\| < \delta$ implies $\|\mathbf{y}(t) - \bar{\mathbf{y}}\| < \varepsilon$ for all $t \geq 0$.
  • Asymptotically stable if it is stable and additionally $\mathbf{y}(t) \to \bar{\mathbf{y}}$ as $t \to \infty$.
  • Unstable if it is not stable.

Stability means nearby trajectories stay nearby forever. Asymptotic stability means they actually converge back. The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ structure mirrors the definition of continuity from Phase 2 — stability is a form of continuous dependence on initial conditions, but stronger because it holds for all future time.

3. Linear stability

For the linear system $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$, the origin is the equilibrium. We can classify its stability entirely from the eigenvalues of $A$.

Theorem — Stability of linear systems

Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$ be the eigenvalues of $A$ (counted with multiplicity, possibly complex). The equilibrium $\mathbf{0}$ is:

  • Asymptotically stable iff $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_k) < 0$ for all $k$.
  • Stable iff $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_k) \leq 0$ for all $k$ and every eigenvalue with $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_k) = 0$ has trivial Jordan blocks (i.e. its geometric multiplicity equals its algebraic multiplicity).
  • Unstable if any eigenvalue has $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_k) > 0$.
Proof

Step 1. The solution is $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}_0$ (page 6). Write $A = PJP^{-1}$ in Jordan normal form (Phase 6, page 11). Then $e^{At} = P\,e^{Jt}\,P^{-1}$.

Step 2. For a Jordan block $J_m(\lambda)$, the entries of $e^{J_m(\lambda)t}$ are $\frac{t^k}{k!}e^{\lambda t}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, m-1$ (page 6, section 7).

Step 3. If $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) < 0$: the exponential decay $|e^{\lambda t}| = e^{\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)t} \to 0$ dominates any polynomial $t^k$, so $t^k e^{\lambda t} \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$. Therefore $e^{At} \to 0$, giving $\mathbf{y}(t) \to 0$. This is asymptotic stability.

Step 4. If $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) = 0$ and the Jordan block is $1 \times 1$: $|e^{\lambda t}| = 1$, so the contribution is bounded. If the block is larger ($m \geq 2$): terms $t^k e^{\lambda t}$ with $k \geq 1$ satisfy $|t^k e^{\lambda t}| = t^k \to \infty$. So the solution grows unboundedly — instability.

Step 5. If $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) > 0$: $|e^{\lambda t}| \to \infty$ and $\mathbf{y}(t)$ diverges. Unstable.

□

4. Linearisation

For a nonlinear system $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ near an equilibrium $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$, Taylor-expand $f$ around $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$:

$$f(\mathbf{y}) = f(\bar{\mathbf{y}}) + Df(\bar{\mathbf{y}})(\mathbf{y} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}) + O(\|\mathbf{y} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}\|^2).$$

Since $f(\bar{\mathbf{y}}) = 0$, the linearisation is $\mathbf{z}' = J\mathbf{z}$ where $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{y} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}$ and $J = Df(\bar{\mathbf{y}})$ is the Jacobian matrix.

Theorem — Linearisation principle (Hartman-Grobman)

Suppose $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ is a hyperbolic equilibrium: every eigenvalue of $J = Df(\bar{\mathbf{y}})$ has nonzero real part. Then the nonlinear system has the same stability type as its linearisation near $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$.

Proof

Step 1. Write $\mathbf{z}' = J\mathbf{z} + r(\mathbf{z})$ where $r(\mathbf{z}) = O(\|\mathbf{z}\|^2)$, i.e. $\|r(\mathbf{z})\| \leq C\|\mathbf{z}\|^2$ for $\|\mathbf{z}\|$ small.

Step 2. The solution of the linearised system is $\mathbf{z}_L(t) = e^{Jt}\mathbf{z}_0$. By the variation of constants formula (the vector analogue of page 5), the nonlinear solution satisfies

$$\mathbf{z}(t) = e^{Jt}\mathbf{z}_0 + \int_0^t e^{J(t-s)}r(\mathbf{z}(s))\,ds.$$

Step 3. If all eigenvalues of $J$ have $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) < 0$, there exist constants $M, \alpha > 0$ such that $\|e^{Jt}\| \leq Me^{-\alpha t}$ for $t \geq 0$.

Step 4. Set $\varphi(t) = \|\mathbf{z}(t)\|$. Then

$$\varphi(t) \leq Me^{-\alpha t}\|\mathbf{z}_0\| + \int_0^t Me^{-\alpha(t-s)}C\,\varphi(s)^2\,ds.$$

Step 5. For $\|\mathbf{z}_0\|$ sufficiently small, Gronwall's inequality (page 3) applied to $e^{\alpha t}\varphi(t)$ shows that $\varphi(t) \to 0$ exponentially. The nonlinear remainder $r$ is too small to overcome the linear decay.

Step 6. If any eigenvalue has $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) > 0$, solutions along the corresponding eigenspace grow exponentially for the linear system, and the nonlinear remainder cannot prevent this growth for sufficiently small perturbations.

□

5. Lyapunov's direct method

Linearisation requires computing eigenvalues. Lyapunov found a way to prove stability using a single clever function — no eigenvalues, no explicit solutions.

Definition — Lyapunov function

Let $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ be an equilibrium of $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$. A continuously differentiable function $V: U \to \mathbb{R}$ defined on a neighbourhood $U$ of $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ is a Lyapunov function if:

  • $V(\bar{\mathbf{y}}) = 0$ and $V(\mathbf{y}) > 0$ for $\mathbf{y} \neq \bar{\mathbf{y}}$ in $U$ (positive definite).
  • $\dot{V}(\mathbf{y}) = \nabla V(\mathbf{y}) \cdot f(\mathbf{y}) \leq 0$ in $U$ (non-increasing along solutions).

The quantity $\dot{V}$ is the time derivative of $V$ along trajectories: $\frac{d}{dt}V(\mathbf{y}(t)) = \nabla V \cdot \mathbf{y}' = \nabla V \cdot f(\mathbf{y})$. If $V$ represents "energy," then $\dot{V} \leq 0$ means energy never increases.

Theorem — Lyapunov stability theorem

If a Lyapunov function $V$ exists for the equilibrium $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$, then $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ is stable. If additionally $\dot{V}(\mathbf{y}) < 0$ for $\mathbf{y} \neq \bar{\mathbf{y}}$ (strict decrease), then $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ is asymptotically stable.

Proof

Step 1. (Stability.) Without loss of generality, take $\bar{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{0}$. Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given, small enough that the closed ball $\overline{B}_\varepsilon = \{\|\mathbf{y}\| \leq \varepsilon\}$ lies in $U$.

Step 2. On the sphere $\|\mathbf{y}\| = \varepsilon$, $V$ is continuous and positive, so it attains a minimum $m = \min_{\|\mathbf{y}\| = \varepsilon} V(\mathbf{y}) > 0$.

Step 3. Since $V$ is continuous and $V(\mathbf{0}) = 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|\mathbf{y}\| < \delta$ implies $V(\mathbf{y}) < m$.

Step 4. Take $\|\mathbf{y}(0)\| < \delta$. Then $V(\mathbf{y}(0)) < m$. Since $\dot{V} \leq 0$, the function $t \mapsto V(\mathbf{y}(t))$ is non-increasing, so $V(\mathbf{y}(t)) \leq V(\mathbf{y}(0)) < m$ for all $t \geq 0$.

Step 5. If $\mathbf{y}(t)$ ever reached $\|\mathbf{y}(t)\| = \varepsilon$, we would have $V(\mathbf{y}(t)) \geq m$, contradicting Step 4. So $\|\mathbf{y}(t)\| < \varepsilon$ for all $t \geq 0$. This is stability.

Step 6. (Asymptotic stability.) Suppose $\dot{V} < 0$ for $\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{0}$. The function $V(\mathbf{y}(t))$ is strictly decreasing and bounded below by $0$, so it converges to some limit $\ell \geq 0$.

Step 7. Suppose $\ell > 0$. Then $\mathbf{y}(t)$ stays in the compact annulus $\{V(\mathbf{y}) \geq \ell\} \cap \overline{B}_\varepsilon$, which does not contain $\mathbf{0}$ (since $V(\mathbf{0}) = 0 < \ell$). On this compact set, $\dot{V}$ is continuous, negative, and bounded away from zero: $\dot{V} \leq -\mu < 0$ for some $\mu > 0$.

Step 8. Then $V(\mathbf{y}(t)) \leq V(\mathbf{y}(0)) - \mu t \to -\infty$, contradicting $V \geq 0$. So $\ell = 0$, meaning $V(\mathbf{y}(t)) \to 0$. Since $V$ is positive definite, $\mathbf{y}(t) \to \mathbf{0}$.

□

6. Example: the damped pendulum

The equation $\theta'' + b\,\theta' + \sin\theta = 0$ (with damping $b > 0$) models a pendulum with friction. Write as a system: $x_1 = \theta$, $x_2 = \theta'$, so

$$x_1' = x_2, \qquad x_2' = -\sin x_1 - b\,x_2.$$

The origin $(0, 0)$ is an equilibrium (pendulum hanging straight down). Define the total energy:

$$V(x_1, x_2) = (1 - \cos x_1) + \frac{1}{2}x_2^2.$$

This is the sum of potential energy $(1 - \cos\theta \geq 0)$ and kinetic energy $(\frac{1}{2}\dot{\theta}^2 \geq 0)$, with $V(0, 0) = 0$. For $(x_1, x_2)$ near the origin with $|x_1| < \pi$, we have $V > 0$ unless $x_1 = x_2 = 0$. So $V$ is positive definite near the origin.

Compute the orbital derivative:

$$\dot{V} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_1}\,x_1' + \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_2}\,x_2' = (\sin x_1)(x_2) + x_2(-\sin x_1 - b\,x_2) = -b\,x_2^2 \leq 0.$$

Since $\dot{V} \leq 0$, Lyapunov's theorem gives stability. Note that $\dot{V} = 0$ when $x_2 = 0$ (zero velocity), not just at the origin. So $\dot{V}$ is not strictly negative, and we cannot directly conclude asymptotic stability from the theorem above.

However, by LaSalle's invariance principle: if $\dot{V} = 0$ only on a set where the largest invariant subset is $\{\mathbf{0}\}$, then the origin is asymptotically stable. Here $\dot{V} = 0$ forces $x_2 = 0$, and if $x_2(t) = 0$ for all $t$, then $x_2' = -\sin x_1 = 0$, forcing $x_1 = 0$ (near the origin). So the origin is indeed asymptotically stable. (LaSalle's principle is a refinement we state without full proof; it is not part of the standard CPGE curriculum.)

7. The stable manifold theorem

Near a hyperbolic equilibrium, the eigenspaces of the Jacobian organise the geometry of trajectories.

Theorem — Stable manifold theorem

Let $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ be a hyperbolic equilibrium of $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ with Jacobian $J = Df(\bar{\mathbf{y}})$. Let $E^s$ be the eigenspace of $J$ corresponding to eigenvalues with $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) < 0$, and $E^u$ the eigenspace for $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda) > 0$. Then there exist smooth manifolds $W^s$ (the stable manifold) and $W^u$ (the unstable manifold) such that:

  • $W^s$ is tangent to $E^s$ at $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ and $W^u$ is tangent to $E^u$.
  • Solutions starting on $W^s$ approach $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ exponentially as $t \to +\infty$.
  • Solutions starting on $W^u$ approach $\bar{\mathbf{y}}$ exponentially as $t \to -\infty$ (equivalently, they depart exponentially as $t \to +\infty$).

The full proof uses the contraction mapping principle on a suitable function space and requires tools from topology (Phase 9). (IOU: the complete proof will be accessible after Phase 9.) The key insight: near a hyperbolic equilibrium, the nonlinear flow is a "bent" version of the linear flow, with the bending controlled by the higher-order terms.

Historical note

Lyapunov developed his stability theory in his 1892 doctoral thesis, one of the most influential theses in the history of mathematics. The direct method (using energy-like functions rather than explicit solutions) was revolutionary: it works for nonlinear systems where no closed-form solution exists. Poincaré had studied stability of orbits in celestial mechanics in the 1880s, focusing on geometric and topological methods. Hadamard (1901) and Perron (1928) proved the stable manifold theorem. LaSalle refined Lyapunov's approach in the 1960s for cases where $\dot{V}$ is only non-negative definite.

What breaks

Remove hyperbolicity (eigenvalues on the imaginary axis): linearisation fails completely. The system $\dot{x} = -y + x(x^2 + y^2)$, $\dot{y} = x + y(x^2 + y^2)$ has linearisation at the origin with eigenvalues $\pm i$ (a centre). But the nonlinear system spirals outward — the origin is unstable despite the linearisation being (neutrally) stable. In the centre case, the nonlinear terms decide everything.

Wrong Lyapunov function: failure to find a Lyapunov function does not prove instability. The method is not an "if and only if" — it is sufficient but not necessary (though converse Lyapunov theorems exist under stronger hypotheses). Finding the right $V$ is an art: there is no systematic procedure for nonlinear systems.

Stability $\neq$ asymptotic stability: the harmonic oscillator $y'' + y = 0$ has the origin as a stable but not asymptotically stable equilibrium. Solutions orbit forever without converging. Energy $V = \frac{1}{2}(y^2 + y'^2)$ has $\dot{V} = 0$ — energy is conserved, giving stability, but no dissipation means no convergence.

Find a function that decreases along trajectories and you have proved stability without solving the equation.
