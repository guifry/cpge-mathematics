Linear ODE Solution Structure

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 4

The problem

A vibrating spring satisfies $x'' + \omega^2 x = 0$. Both $\cos(\omega t)$ and $\sin(\omega t)$ are solutions. So is any linear combination $A\cos(\omega t) + B\sin(\omega t)$. The set of all solutions forms a two-dimensional vector space — it has the same algebraic structure as $\mathbb{R}^2$.

This is where linear algebra meets differential equations. The linearity of differentiation means the solution set of a linear ODE inherits the structure of a vector space. Finding all solutions reduces to finding a basis — $n$ independent solutions for an $n$-th order equation — and taking linear combinations.

1. Definitions

Definition — Linear ODE

An $n$-th order linear ODE has the form

$$y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(t)\, y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(t)\, y' + a_0(t)\, y = g(t)$$

where $a_0, \ldots, a_{n-1}$ and $g$ are continuous functions on an interval $I$. The equation is homogeneous if $g(t) = 0$ for all $t$. Otherwise it is non-homogeneous.

Define the linear differential operator $\mathcal{L}[y] = y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_0 y$. Then the homogeneous equation is $\mathcal{L}[y] = 0$ and the non-homogeneous equation is $\mathcal{L}[y] = g$.

The operator $\mathcal{L}$ is linear: $\mathcal{L}[c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2] = c_1 \mathcal{L}[y_1] + c_2 \mathcal{L}[y_2]$. This follows from the linearity of differentiation, which we proved in Phase 3, page 2.

2. The solution space is a vector space

Theorem — Superposition principle

The set $S = \{y \in C^n(I) : \mathcal{L}[y] = 0\}$ of all solutions to the homogeneous equation is a vector subspace of $C^n(I)$.

Proof

Step 1. The zero function satisfies $\mathcal{L}[0] = 0$, so $0 \in S$.

Step 2. Let $y_1, y_2 \in S$ and $c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{R}$. Then $\mathcal{L}[c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2] = c_1 \mathcal{L}[y_1] + c_2 \mathcal{L}[y_2] = c_1 \cdot 0 + c_2 \cdot 0 = 0$. So $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \in S$.

By the subspace criterion, $S$ is a vector subspace.

∎

3. Dimension of the solution space

Theorem — Dimension equals order

The solution space of an $n$-th order homogeneous linear ODE with continuous coefficients on an interval $I$ is exactly $n$-dimensional.

Proof

Step 1. Fix $t_0 \in I$. Define the map $\Phi : S \to \mathbb{R}^n$ by

$$\Phi(y) = (y(t_0),\; y'(t_0),\; \ldots,\; y^{(n-1)}(t_0))$$

This sends each solution to its initial data at $t_0$.

Step 2. $\Phi$ is linear. $\Phi(c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2) = c_1 \Phi(y_1) + c_2 \Phi(y_2)$ because differentiation and evaluation are linear.

Step 3. $\Phi$ is injective. Suppose $\Phi(y) = 0$, meaning $y(t_0) = y'(t_0) = \cdots = y^{(n-1)}(t_0) = 0$. The zero function also satisfies these initial conditions. By the Cauchy–Lipschitz theorem (which extends to $n$-th order linear systems — the Lipschitz condition holds because the coefficients are continuous and the equation is linear), the solution is unique. So $y = 0$. Thus $\ker \Phi = \{0\}$.

Step 4. $\Phi$ is surjective. For any $(c_0, c_1, \ldots, c_{n-1}) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the IVP $\mathcal{L}[y] = 0$ with $y(t_0) = c_0, y'(t_0) = c_1, \ldots, y^{(n-1)}(t_0) = c_{n-1}$ has a solution by Cauchy–Lipschitz. That solution maps to $(c_0, \ldots, c_{n-1})$ under $\Phi$.

Step 5. So $\Phi$ is a linear isomorphism from $S$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$. Therefore $\dim S = \dim \mathbb{R}^n = n$.

∎

This is a remarkable fact. Finding all solutions of an $n$-th order homogeneous linear ODE amounts to finding $n$ linearly independent solutions. Every other solution is a linear combination of these.

4. The Wronskian

How do you check whether $n$ solutions are linearly independent? The answer is a determinant.

Definition — Wronskian

Given $n$ functions $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ that are $(n-1)$ times differentiable, the Wronskian is

$$W(y_1, \ldots, y_n)(t) = \det \begin{pmatrix} y_1(t) & y_2(t) & \cdots & y_n(t) \\ y_1'(t) & y_2'(t) & \cdots & y_n'(t) \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ y_1^{(n-1)}(t) & y_2^{(n-1)}(t) & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)}(t) \end{pmatrix}$$
Theorem — Wronskian test for linear independence

Let $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ be solutions of $\mathcal{L}[y] = 0$ on $I$. Then:

(a) If $W(t_0) \neq 0$ for some $t_0 \in I$, then $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ are linearly independent (they form a basis of $S$).

(b) If $W(t_0) = 0$ for some $t_0 \in I$, then $W(t) = 0$ for ALL $t \in I$, and the solutions are linearly dependent.

Proof

Step 1. Suppose $c_1 y_1 + \cdots + c_n y_n = 0$ on $I$. Differentiating $k$ times ($k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$) and evaluating at $t_0$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1(t_0) & \cdots & y_n(t_0) \\ y_1'(t_0) & \cdots & y_n'(t_0) \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ y_1^{(n-1)}(t_0) & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)}(t_0) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_n \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

If $W(t_0) \neq 0$, the matrix is invertible (Phase 6, page 5), so $c_1 = \cdots = c_n = 0$. The solutions are linearly independent.

Step 2. Part (b) follows from Abel's identity (below), which shows that $W$ is either always zero or never zero on $I$.

∎

5. Abel's identity

Theorem — Abel's identity (second-order case)

If $y_1, y_2$ are solutions of $y'' + p(t)y' + q(t)y = 0$ on $I$, then their Wronskian satisfies

$$W(t) = W(t_0) \exp\!\left(-\int_{t_0}^t p(s)\, ds\right)$$

for any $t_0, t \in I$.

Proof

Step 1. The Wronskian is $W = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2$. Differentiate:

$$W' = y_1 y_2'' - y_1'' y_2$$

(The cross terms $y_1' y_2'$ cancel: $y_1' y_2' + y_1 y_2'' - y_1'' y_2 - y_1' y_2' = y_1 y_2'' - y_1'' y_2$.)

Step 2. Since $y_1$ and $y_2$ solve the ODE: $y_1'' = -p y_1' - q y_1$ and $y_2'' = -p y_2' - q y_2$. Substitute:

$$W' = y_1(-p y_2' - q y_2) - (-p y_1' - q y_1) y_2$$
$$= -p y_1 y_2' - q y_1 y_2 + p y_1' y_2 + q y_1 y_2$$
$$= -p(y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2) = -p\, W$$

Step 3. So $W$ satisfies the first-order linear ODE $W' = -p(t) W$. By the method of page 1 (separable equation):

$$W(t) = W(t_0) \exp\!\left(-\int_{t_0}^t p(s)\, ds\right)$$
∎

Since the exponential is never zero, $W(t) = 0$ for some $t$ if and only if $W(t_0) = 0$ if and only if $W(t) = 0$ for all $t$. The Wronskian is either identically zero or never zero. This completes the proof of part (b) of the Wronskian test.

6. Constant-coefficient equations

The most important special case: all coefficients $a_i$ are constants. Consider the second-order equation

$$y'' + by' + cy = 0$$

The exponential ansatz: try $y = e^{rt}$. Then $y' = re^{rt}$, $y'' = r^2 e^{rt}$. Substituting:

$$r^2 e^{rt} + b r e^{rt} + c e^{rt} = (r^2 + br + c) e^{rt} = 0$$

Since $e^{rt} \neq 0$, we need $r^2 + br + c = 0$. This is the characteristic equation. Its roots determine the form of the solution.

Case 1: Two distinct real roots $r_1 \neq r_2$.

Both $e^{r_1 t}$ and $e^{r_2 t}$ are solutions. Their Wronskian at $t = 0$ is $W(0) = r_2 - r_1 \neq 0$ (compute the $2 \times 2$ determinant), so they are linearly independent. The general solution is

$$y = c_1 e^{r_1 t} + c_2 e^{r_2 t}$$

Case 2: Repeated root $r_1 = r_2 = r = -b/2$.

We have one solution $e^{rt}$. For a second, try $y = te^{rt}$. Then $y' = e^{rt} + rte^{rt} = (1 + rt)e^{rt}$ and $y'' = (2r + r^2 t)e^{rt}$. Substitute into $y'' + by' + cy = 0$:

$$(2r + r^2 t + b + brt + ct) e^{rt} = ((r^2 + br + c)t + (2r + b)) e^{rt}$$

The coefficient of $t$ vanishes since $r$ is a root. The constant term is $2r + b = 0$ since $r = -b/2$ (the repeated root condition $b^2 = 4c$ gives $2r + b = -b + b = 0$). So $te^{rt}$ is indeed a solution. The Wronskian of $e^{rt}$ and $te^{rt}$ at $t = 0$ is $1 \neq 0$. General solution:

$$y = (c_1 + c_2 t) e^{rt}$$

Case 3: Complex conjugate roots $r = \alpha \pm i\beta$ ($\beta \neq 0$).

Formally, $e^{(\alpha + i\beta)t}$ is a solution. Using the exponential of a complex number: $e^{(\alpha + i\beta)t} = e^{\alpha t}(\cos\beta t + i\sin\beta t)$. Since the equation has real coefficients, the real and imaginary parts are separately solutions (substitute and separate). So $e^{\alpha t}\cos\beta t$ and $e^{\alpha t}\sin\beta t$ are real solutions. Their Wronskian at $t = 0$ is $\beta \neq 0$. General solution:

$$y = e^{\alpha t}(c_1 \cos\beta t + c_2 \sin\beta t)$$

Example. $y'' + 4y' + 5y = 0$. Characteristic equation: $r^2 + 4r + 5 = 0$, roots $r = -2 \pm i$. So $\alpha = -2$, $\beta = 1$, and the general solution is $y = e^{-2t}(c_1 \cos t + c_2 \sin t)$. The factor $e^{-2t}$ causes the oscillations to decay — a damped vibration.

7. Higher-order constant-coefficient equations

For $y^{(n)} + a_{n-1} y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_0 y = 0$, the same ansatz $y = e^{rt}$ gives the characteristic polynomial $r^n + a_{n-1} r^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0 = 0$. By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (IOU: this will be proved properly in a later phase on complex analysis), it has $n$ roots over $\mathbb{C}$ (counted with multiplicity).

Each simple real root $r$ contributes $e^{rt}$. A real root of multiplicity $m$ contributes $e^{rt}, te^{rt}, \ldots, t^{m-1}e^{rt}$. Complex conjugate roots $\alpha \pm i\beta$ of multiplicity $m$ contribute $t^k e^{\alpha t}\cos\beta t$ and $t^k e^{\alpha t}\sin\beta t$ for $k = 0, \ldots, m-1$. Together, these give exactly $n$ linearly independent solutions.

This is deeply connected to eigenvalues. Rewrite the $n$-th order equation as a first-order system $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$ where $\mathbf{y} = (y, y', \ldots, y^{(n-1)})^T$ and $A$ is the companion matrix. The characteristic polynomial of the ODE is exactly the characteristic polynomial of $A$ (Phase 6, page 6), and the roots $r$ are the eigenvalues. The exponential solutions $e^{rt}$ correspond to eigenvectors, and the $t^k e^{rt}$ terms arise from Jordan blocks.

Historical note

Euler (1743) solved constant-coefficient ODEs using the exponential ansatz — guess $y = e^{rt}$ and see what happens. D'Alembert (1748) handled the complex root case, extracting real solutions from complex exponentials. The Wronskian is named after Józef Hoene-Wroński (1812), a Polish mathematician with grand philosophical ambitions who introduced the determinant in a much broader (and often confused) context. Its systematic use in ODE theory is due to later mathematicians. Abel's identity (1827) reveals that the Wronskian satisfies its own first-order ODE — connecting the independence of solutions to the coefficients of the equation without needing to solve it.

What breaks

Remove linearity. The sum of two solutions of $y' = y^2$ is generally not a solution: if $y_1' = y_1^2$ and $y_2' = y_2^2$, then $(y_1 + y_2)' = y_1^2 + y_2^2 \neq (y_1 + y_2)^2$ in general. The vector space structure — and the reduction to finding a basis — is a gift of linearity. Without it, there is no systematic method for finding all solutions.

Non-constant coefficients. The exponential ansatz fails completely. For $y'' + t\, y = 0$ (the Airy equation), solutions cannot be expressed in terms of elementary functions. The solution space is still two-dimensional (by the theorem above), but finding a basis requires power series or special functions.

The Wronskian test requires the functions to be solutions. For arbitrary (non-solution) functions, $W = 0$ at a point does not imply $W = 0$ everywhere. The functions $f(t) = t^2$ and $g(t) = t|t|$ have $W(0) = 0$ but $W(t) \neq 0$ for $t \neq 0$. Abel's identity — and the "all or nothing" property of $W$ — depends on the functions actually satisfying a linear ODE.

The solution space of an $n$-th order homogeneous linear ODE is an $n$-dimensional vector space; for constant coefficients, a basis comes from the roots of the characteristic polynomial.
