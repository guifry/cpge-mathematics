Systems and the Matrix Exponential

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 6

The problem

Two springs are coupled together. Their displacements $x_1$ and $x_2$ satisfy simultaneous ODEs: $x_1'' = -k_1 x_1 + k(x_2 - x_1)$ and $x_2'' = -k_2 x_2 - k(x_2 - x_1)$. Each equation involves both unknowns.

More generally, any system of linear ODEs can be written as $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$ where $A$ is a matrix and $\mathbf{y}$ is a vector. For a scalar ODE, the solution of $y' = ay$ is $y(t) = e^{at}y(0)$. By analogy, the solution should be $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}(0)$. But what does it mean to exponentiate a matrix?

1. Systems of linear ODEs

Definition — Linear system

A first-order linear system is $\mathbf{y}'(t) = A\,\mathbf{y}(t)$, where $A$ is an $n \times n$ real matrix and $\mathbf{y}: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n$.

Any $n$-th order linear ODE can be rewritten as a first-order system. Given $y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_0 y = 0$, set $\mathbf{y} = (y,\, y',\, \ldots,\, y^{(n-1)})^T$. Then $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$ where $A$ is the companion matrix:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\ -a_0 & -a_1 & -a_2 & \cdots & -a_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}.$$

So the theory of systems subsumes the theory of higher-order linear ODEs.

2. The matrix exponential

Definition — Matrix exponential

For an $n \times n$ matrix $A$, the matrix exponential is

$$e^{A} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k}{k!} = I + A + \frac{A^2}{2!} + \frac{A^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

where $A^0 = I$ (the identity matrix).

This is the power series for $e^x$ from Phase 4, page 3 with $x$ replaced by $A$. We write $e^{At}$ for the exponential of the matrix $tA$.

3. Convergence

Theorem — Convergence of the matrix exponential

The series $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} A^k t^k / k!$ converges absolutely for every $n \times n$ matrix $A$ and every $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

Proof

Step 1. Use any submultiplicative matrix norm $\|\cdot\|$ (for instance, the operator norm). Then $\|A^k\| \leq \|A\|^k$ by induction on $k$.

Step 2. Therefore $\left\|\frac{A^k t^k}{k!}\right\| \leq \frac{\|A\|^k |t|^k}{k!}$.

Step 3. The series $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\|A\|^k |t|^k}{k!}$ equals $e^{\|A\| |t|}$, which converges (it is the scalar exponential series).

Step 4. By the Weierstrass M-test (Phase 5, page 7), the matrix series converges absolutely and uniformly on every bounded interval $|t| \leq R$.

□

4. The solution formula

Theorem — Solution of linear systems

The unique solution of $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$, $\mathbf{y}(0) = \mathbf{y}_0$ is $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}_0$.

Proof

Step 1. (It solves the ODE.) Differentiate term by term. Since the series converges uniformly on bounded intervals, term-by-term differentiation is justified (Phase 4, page 5):

$$\frac{d}{dt} e^{At} = \frac{d}{dt}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^k t^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{A^k \cdot k \cdot t^{k-1}}{k!} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{A^k t^{k-1}}{(k-1)!} = A\sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^j t^j}{j!} = A\,e^{At}.$$

So $\mathbf{y}'(t) = A\,e^{At}\mathbf{y}_0 = A\,\mathbf{y}(t)$.

Step 2. (Initial condition.) At $t = 0$: $e^{A \cdot 0} = e^0 = I$, so $\mathbf{y}(0) = I \cdot \mathbf{y}_0 = \mathbf{y}_0$.

Step 3. (Uniqueness.) The function $f(\mathbf{y}) = A\mathbf{y}$ is Lipschitz with constant $\|A\|$. By the Cauchy-Lipschitz theorem (page 2), the solution is unique.

□

5. Properties of the matrix exponential

The matrix exponential inherits several properties from the scalar exponential, but with an important caveat about commutativity.

Theorem — Properties of $e^{At}$

(i) $e^{0} = I$.

(ii) $e^{(s+t)A} = e^{sA}\, e^{tA}$ for all $s, t \in \mathbb{R}$.

(iii) $(e^{At})^{-1} = e^{-At}$.

(iv) If $A = PBP^{-1}$, then $e^{At} = P\,e^{Bt}\,P^{-1}$.

Proof

(i) Direct: $e^0 = \sum A^k \cdot 0^k / k! = I + 0 + 0 + \cdots = I$.

(ii) Since $sA$ and $tA$ commute (both are multiples of $A$), we can multiply the series by the Cauchy product. The same argument as for the scalar functional equation in Phase 4, page 3 gives

$$e^{sA}\,e^{tA} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\sum_{j=0}^{m} \frac{(sA)^j}{j!}\frac{(tA)^{m-j}}{(m-j)!} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{A^m}{m!}\sum_{j=0}^{m}\binom{m}{j}s^j t^{m-j} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{A^m (s+t)^m}{m!} = e^{(s+t)A}.$$

(iii) Set $s = t$ and $s = -t$ in (ii): $e^{At}\,e^{-At} = e^{0} = I$.

(iv) Since $A^k = PB^kP^{-1}$ (by induction), we have $\sum A^k t^k/k! = P\left(\sum B^k t^k/k!\right)P^{-1} = P\,e^{Bt}\,P^{-1}$.

□

Warning: in general, $e^{A+B} \neq e^A\,e^B$ unless $AB = BA$. The proof of (ii) used commutativity of $sA$ and $tA$ crucially. For non-commuting matrices, the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula gives the correction: $e^A e^B = e^{A + B + \frac{1}{2}[A,B] + \cdots}$ where $[A,B] = AB - BA$ is the commutator.

6. Computing $e^{At}$: the diagonalisable case

If $A$ is diagonalisable (Phase 6, page 6), write $A = PDP^{-1}$ where $D = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$. By property (iv):

$$e^{At} = P\,e^{Dt}\,P^{-1} = P\begin{pmatrix}e^{\lambda_1 t} & & 0 \\ & \ddots & \\ 0 & & e^{\lambda_n t}\end{pmatrix}P^{-1}.$$

This is the spectral decomposition of the matrix exponential. Each eigenvalue $\lambda_k$ contributes a mode $e^{\lambda_k t}$: exponential growth if $\text{Re}(\lambda_k) > 0$, decay if $\text{Re}(\lambda_k) < 0$, oscillation if $\lambda_k$ is purely imaginary.

7. Computing $e^{At}$: the Jordan form

When $A$ is not diagonalisable, use the Jordan normal form (Phase 6, page 11): $A = PJP^{-1}$ where $J$ is block-diagonal with Jordan blocks. For a single Jordan block $J_m(\lambda) = \lambda I + N$ where $N$ is the $m \times m$ nilpotent shift matrix ($N^m = 0$), we have:

$$e^{J_m(\lambda)t} = e^{\lambda t}\, e^{Nt} = e^{\lambda t}\left(I + Nt + \frac{N^2 t^2}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{N^{m-1}t^{m-1}}{(m-1)!}\right).$$

This is a finite sum because $N^k = 0$ for $k \geq m$. Note that $\lambda I$ and $N$ commute, so $e^{(\lambda I + N)t} = e^{\lambda I t}\,e^{Nt}$ is valid here. Explicitly:

$$e^{J_m(\lambda)t} = e^{\lambda t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t & t^2/2! & \cdots & t^{m-1}/(m-1)! \\ 0 & 1 & t & \cdots & t^{m-2}/(m-2)! \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & t \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The polynomial factors $t^k$ multiplying $e^{\lambda t}$ are what distinguish the Jordan case from the diagonal case: repeated eigenvalues with nontrivial Jordan blocks produce polynomial-times-exponential solutions.

8. Example: rotation

Solve $\mathbf{y}' = A\mathbf{y}$ with $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 1. Find the eigenvalues: $\det(A - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 + 1 = 0$, so $\lambda = \pm i$.

Step 2. Compute $e^{At}$ directly from the series. Note that $A^2 = -I$, $A^3 = -A$, $A^4 = I$, and the pattern repeats with period 4. Grouping:

$$e^{At} = \left(1 - \frac{t^2}{2!} + \frac{t^4}{4!} - \cdots\right)I + \left(t - \frac{t^3}{3!} + \frac{t^5}{5!} - \cdots\right)A = (\cos t)\,I + (\sin t)\,A.$$
$$e^{At} = \begin{pmatrix}\cos t & \sin t \\ -\sin t & \cos t\end{pmatrix}.$$

This is the rotation matrix through angle $t$. The solution $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}_0$ traces a circle — which makes sense, since the system $y_1' = y_2$, $y_2' = -y_1$ describes simple harmonic oscillation.

Historical note

The matrix exponential was introduced by Laguerre (1867) and developed systematically by Poincaré and Lyapunov in the late 19th century. It sits at the intersection of three major threads: power series (Phase 4), linear algebra (Phase 6), and differential equations (this phase). The formula $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}_0$ is the cornerstone of linear systems theory and appears throughout physics — in quantum mechanics, the time evolution operator is $U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar}$ where $H$ is the Hamiltonian matrix.

What breaks

Non-commuting matrices: $e^{A+B} \neq e^A e^B$ unless $AB = BA$. This is the single most common error. The Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula gives the correction terms involving iterated commutators $[A,B]$, $[A,[A,B]]$, etc. — far more complex than the scalar case.

Non-linear systems: for $\mathbf{y}' = f(\mathbf{y})$ with nonlinear $f$, no matrix exponential exists. The solution operator (the flow map $\phi_t(\mathbf{y}_0)$) is still well-defined by Cauchy-Lipschitz (page 2), but there is no explicit formula for it in general. The matrix exponential is a luxury of linearity.

Non-constant $A(t)$: if the matrix depends on time, $\mathbf{y}' = A(t)\mathbf{y}$, then $e^{\int_0^t A(s)\,ds}$ is generally not the solution (because $A(t_1)$ and $A(t_2)$ may not commute). The solution requires the time-ordered exponential (Dyson series), which is considerably harder.

$e^{At}$ is a matrix-valued power series that solves every linear constant-coefficient system at once — diagonalise to compute it, or use Jordan blocks.
