First-Order Ordinary Differential Equations

Phase 8 — Differential Equations, Page 1

The problem

A colony of bacteria doubles every hour. If $P(t)$ is the population at time $t$, the rate of growth is proportional to the current size: $P'(t) = kP(t)$. Find the function $P$. This is the simplest differential equation: a relationship between a function and its derivative, and you must recover the function itself.

Newton invented calculus largely to solve such equations — the motion of planets under gravity, the cooling of a hot object, the flow of water through a pipe. Each of these phenomena is described not by giving $y(t)$ directly, but by saying how fast $y$ changes. The challenge: go from the rate of change back to the function.

1. Definitions

An ordinary differential equation (ODE) is an equation involving a function $y(t)$ of a single variable and one or more of its derivatives $y', y'', \ldots$. The word "ordinary" distinguishes these from partial differential equations, which involve functions of several variables and partial derivatives.

The order of the ODE is the highest derivative that appears. A first-order ODE has the general form

$$y' = f(t, y)$$

where $f$ is a given function of two variables. A solution is a differentiable function $y(t)$ defined on some interval $I$ such that $y'(t) = f(t, y(t))$ for every $t \in I$.

An initial value problem (IVP) specifies in addition an initial condition $y(t_0) = y_0$. We seek a solution passing through the point $(t_0, y_0)$.

Definition — General and particular solutions

The general solution of a first-order ODE is the family of all solutions, typically involving one arbitrary constant. A particular solution is obtained by fixing the constant via an initial condition.

2. Separable equations

The simplest class. A first-order ODE is separable if it can be written as

$$y' = f(t)\, g(y)$$

where the right-hand side factors into a function of $t$ alone times a function of $y$ alone. When $g(y) \neq 0$, we separate variables:

$$\frac{dy}{g(y)} = f(t)\, dt$$

and integrate both sides. The left side is integrated with respect to $y$, the right with respect to $t$. This works because of the chain rule and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: if $G$ is an antiderivative of $1/g$ and $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$, then $G(y(t)) = F(t) + C$.

Example. Solve $y' = ky$ where $k$ is a constant.

Separate: $dy/y = k\, dt$. Integrate: $\ln|y| = kt + C$. Exponentiate: $|y| = e^{kt + C} = e^C \cdot e^{kt}$. Writing $A = \pm e^C$ (which absorbs the sign and the constant), we get

$$y(t) = A e^{kt}$$

The initial condition $y(0) = y_0$ forces $A = y_0$, giving $y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}$. This is exponential growth ($k > 0$) or decay ($k < 0$). Note that $y = 0$ is also a solution (the case $A = 0$), which we lost when dividing by $y$. Always check for this.

Example. Solve $y' = t y^2$, $y(0) = 1$.

Separate: $dy/y^2 = t\, dt$. Integrate: $-1/y = t^2/2 + C$. Initial condition: $-1/1 = 0 + C$, so $C = -1$. Thus $y = -1/(t^2/2 - 1) = 2/(2 - t^2)$. This solution blows up at $t = \sqrt{2}$ — it does not exist for all time.

Example. Newton's law of cooling: $T' = -k(T - T_{\text{env}})$.

Let $u = T - T_{\text{env}}$. Then $u' = T' = -ku$, so $u(t) = u(0) e^{-kt}$. The temperature approaches the environment exponentially: $T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{env}}) e^{-kt}$. The rate of cooling is proportional to the temperature difference — a larger gap means faster cooling, exactly matching physical intuition.

3. Linear first-order equations

A first-order ODE is linear if it has the form

$$y' + p(t)\, y = q(t)$$

where $p$ and $q$ are given continuous functions. The key idea is the integrating factor.

Theorem — Integrating factor method

Let $p$ and $q$ be continuous on an interval $I$. Define $\mu(t) = e^{\int_{t_0}^t p(s)\, ds}$. Then the general solution of $y' + p(t)y = q(t)$ on $I$ is

$$y(t) = \frac{1}{\mu(t)} \left[ y_0 + \int_{t_0}^t \mu(s)\, q(s)\, ds \right]$$

where $y(t_0) = y_0$.

Proof

Step 1. Multiply both sides of $y' + p(t)y = q(t)$ by $\mu(t) = e^{\int_{t_0}^t p(s)\, ds}$. Note that $\mu'(t) = p(t)\mu(t)$ by the chain rule and the Fundamental Theorem.

Step 2. The left side becomes $\mu y' + \mu p y = \mu y' + \mu' y$. By the product rule, this equals $(\mu y)'$.

Step 3. So $(\mu y)' = \mu q$. Integrate from $t_0$ to $t$:

$$\mu(t) y(t) - \mu(t_0) y(t_0) = \int_{t_0}^t \mu(s)\, q(s)\, ds$$

Since $\mu(t_0) = e^0 = 1$, we get $\mu(t) y(t) = y_0 + \int_{t_0}^t \mu q\, ds$. Divide by $\mu(t)$ (which is never zero since it is an exponential).

∎

Example. Solve $y' + 2t\, y = t$, $y(0) = 1$.

Here $p(t) = 2t$, so $\mu(t) = e^{\int_0^t 2s\, ds} = e^{t^2}$. Then

$$e^{t^2} y = 1 + \int_0^t s\, e^{s^2}\, ds = 1 + \frac{1}{2}(e^{t^2} - 1) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} e^{t^2}$$

(using the substitution $u = s^2$ for the integral). Dividing by $e^{t^2}$:

$$y(t) = \frac{1}{2} e^{-t^2} + \frac{1}{2}$$

4. Bernoulli equations

A Bernoulli equation has the form

$$y' + p(t)\, y = q(t)\, y^n$$

where $n \neq 0, 1$. This is nonlinear because of $y^n$, but a substitution linearises it.

Set $v = y^{1-n}$. Then $v' = (1-n) y^{-n} y'$. Multiply the Bernoulli equation by $(1-n) y^{-n}$:

$$(1-n) y^{-n} y' + (1-n) p(t)\, y^{1-n} = (1-n) q(t)$$

The left side is $v' + (1-n) p(t)\, v$, which is linear in $v$. Solve by the integrating factor method, then recover $y = v^{1/(1-n)}$.

Example. Solve $y' + y = y^2$.

Here $n = 2$, so $v = y^{-1}$, $v' = -y^{-2} y'$. Dividing the equation by $y^2$: $y^{-2}y' + y^{-1} = 1$, i.e. $-v' + v = 1$, or $v' - v = -1$. The integrating factor is $e^{-t}$: $(e^{-t}v)' = -e^{-t}$, so $e^{-t}v = e^{-t} + C$, giving $v = 1 + Ce^t$. Thus $y = 1/v = 1/(1 + Ce^t)$.

Notice that as $t \to \infty$, if $C > 0$ then $y \to 0$, and if $C < 0$ then $y$ blows up at $t = -\ln|C|$. The equilibrium solutions are $y = 0$ (stable) and $y = 1$ (unstable) — set $y' + y = y^2$, i.e. $y' = y^2 - y = y(y-1) = 0$.

5. Existence and uniqueness: the question

Does every IVP have a solution? Is it unique? Consider $y' = \sqrt{y}$ with $y(0) = 0$.

The constant function $y(t) = 0$ is a solution. But so is

$$y(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } t \leq c \\ \frac{(t - c)^2}{4} & \text{if } t > c \end{cases}$$

for any $c \geq 0$. Check: for $t > c$, $y' = (t-c)/2$ and $\sqrt{y} = (t-c)/2$. At $t = c$, $y = 0$ and $y' = 0 = \sqrt{0}$. So uniqueness fails — there are infinitely many solutions through the origin.

In contrast, $y' = y$ with $y(0) = 1$ has the unique solution $y = e^t$. What distinguishes these two cases? The answer lies in a condition on $f(t,y)$ called the Lipschitz condition. For $f(t,y) = y$, the function is "well-behaved" in $y$. For $f(t,y) = \sqrt{y}$, the derivative $\partial f / \partial y = 1/(2\sqrt{y})$ blows up at $y = 0$, so the function changes too fast near the initial point. Making this precise is the subject of the next page.

Historical note

Newton (1660s–80s) and Leibniz (1680s) developed techniques for solving specific ODEs alongside the invention of calculus itself. The Bernoulli family — Jakob, Johann, and Daniel — solved numerous types throughout the late 17th and early 18th centuries, often in competitive public challenges. The brachistochrone problem (1696), posed by Johann Bernoulli, led to the calculus of variations, an entire field built on differential equations. The systematic theory of when solutions exist and when they are unique had to wait for Cauchy and Lipschitz in the 19th century.

What breaks

Not every ODE has a closed-form solution. Even the innocent-looking $y' = e^{y^2}$ cannot be solved in terms of elementary functions. The solution exists (by the Peano existence theorem) but cannot be written down.

Solutions may not exist for all time. The equation $y' = y^2$ with $y(0) = 1$ gives $y = 1/(1-t)$ by separation. This blows up at $t = 1$: the solution escapes to infinity in finite time. The equation is perfectly well-defined, but no solution exists on the interval $[0, \infty)$.

Dividing by zero in separation. When we write $dy/g(y) = f(t)\, dt$, we implicitly assume $g(y) \neq 0$. The roots of $g$ give constant solutions ($y = c$ where $g(c) = 0$) that the separation method misses. Always check for these equilibrium solutions separately.

A first-order ODE asks you to recover a function from its rate of change; separable and linear equations have systematic solution methods, but existence and uniqueness are not guaranteed without further conditions.
