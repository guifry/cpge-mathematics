Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 1

The problem

Temperature in a room depends on position: $T(x, y, z)$ gives the temperature at each point. If you walk east, the temperature changes at one rate. If you walk north, at another. If you climb, at yet another. You already know how to differentiate a function of one variable (Phase 3, page 1). But $T$ depends on three variables simultaneously. How do you measure its rate of change in a single direction?

Euler, working on fluid mechanics in the 1730s, introduced the idea: hold all variables fixed except one, and differentiate with respect to that one. This gives a partial derivative — the rate of change in one coordinate direction. Collect all partial derivatives into a vector and you get the gradient, which points in the direction of steepest ascent.

1. Functions of several variables

A function of several variables is a map $f: U \to \mathbb{R}$ where $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is an open set. The input is a point $a = (a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$; the output is a real number $f(a)$.

Examples you already know in disguise: the distance from the origin, $f(x, y) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, is a function $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$. A polynomial like $g(x, y) = x^2 y + 3xy^2 - 7$ is another. Temperature $T(x, y, z)$, pressure $P(V, T)$ in thermodynamics — these are all functions of several variables.

2. Partial derivatives

Definition — Partial derivative

Let $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and $a \in U$. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x_i$ at $a$ is

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h e_i) - f(a)}{h}$$

where $e_i = (0, \ldots, 0, 1, 0, \ldots, 0)$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector. If this limit exists, we say $f$ has a partial derivative with respect to $x_i$ at $a$.

In plain language: freeze every variable except $x_i$, then differentiate with respect to $x_i$ as a single-variable function. You already know how to do this — it is exactly the derivative from Phase 3, page 1, applied to the function $t \mapsto f(a_1, \ldots, a_{i-1}, t, a_{i+1}, \ldots, a_n)$.

Standard notations: $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, $f_x$, $\partial_x f$, $D_x f$. All mean the same thing.

3. A concrete computation

Let $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(xy)$. We compute both partial derivatives at a general point $(x, y)$.

Partial derivative with respect to $x$: treat $y$ as a constant.

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2 y) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(\sin(xy)) = 2xy + y\cos(xy)$$

For $x^2 y$: $y$ is a constant, so this is $y \cdot 2x$. For $\sin(xy)$: the chain rule (Phase 3, page 2) gives $\cos(xy) \cdot y$, since $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy) = y$.

Partial derivative with respect to $y$: treat $x$ as a constant.

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + x\cos(xy)$$

4. The gradient

Definition — Gradient

If all partial derivatives of $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ exist at $a$, the gradient of $f$ at $a$ is the vector

$$\nabla f(a) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(a), \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(a)\right)$$

For our example: $\nabla f(x,y) = (2xy + y\cos(xy),\; x^2 + x\cos(xy))$.

The gradient is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — it lives in the same space as the input, not the output. Geometrically, it points in the direction of steepest increase of $f$, and its magnitude $\|\nabla f\|$ is the rate of increase in that direction. We will make this precise with directional derivatives.

5. Directional derivatives

Definition — Directional derivative

Let $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a unit vector ($\|v\| = 1$). The directional derivative of $f$ at $a$ in the direction $v$ is

$$D_v f(a) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(a + tv) - f(a)}{t}$$

Partial derivatives are the special case $v = e_i$. The directional derivative measures the rate of change along any direction, not just along coordinate axes.

When $f$ is differentiable (defined below), the directional derivative has a clean formula: $D_v f(a) = \nabla f(a) \cdot v$. This is why the gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent: the dot product $\nabla f \cdot v$ is maximised when $v$ points in the same direction as $\nabla f$, by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (Phase 6, page 7).

6. Differentiability — the total derivative

Having all partial derivatives is not enough to guarantee that $f$ behaves well. The correct multivariable notion of "differentiable" is stronger.

Definition — Differentiability (total derivative)

A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at $a$ if there exists a linear map $L: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ such that

$$f(a + h) = f(a) + L(h) + o(\|h\|)$$

meaning $\displaystyle\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a) - L(h)}{\|h\|} = 0$. The linear map $L$ is called the total derivative of $f$ at $a$, written $Df(a)$.

If $f$ is differentiable, then $Df(a)$ is the linear map $h \mapsto \nabla f(a) \cdot h$. As a matrix (a $1 \times n$ row vector), $Df(a)$ is simply the gradient written as a row. This connects to the Jacobian matrix on page 2.

The key point: differentiability means $f$ is well-approximated by a linear function near $a$. The partial derivatives existing only means the function behaves linearly along each axis — it says nothing about what happens in between.

7. Partial derivatives existing does not imply differentiability

This is the most important subtlety on this page. Consider:

$$f(x, y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} & \text{if } (x,y) \neq (0,0) \\ 0 & \text{if } (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

Both partial derivatives exist at the origin. Along the $x$-axis ($y = 0$): $f(x, 0) = 0$ for all $x$, so $f_x(0,0) = 0$. Similarly $f_y(0,0) = 0$. So $\nabla f(0,0) = (0, 0)$.

If $f$ were differentiable at the origin, we would need $f(h_1, h_2) = 0 + 0 + o(\|(h_1, h_2)\|)$, i.e. $f(h_1, h_2)/\|(h_1, h_2)\| \to 0$. But along the line $y = x$:

$$\frac{f(t, t)}{\|(t, t)\|} = \frac{t^2 / \sqrt{2t^2}}{|t|\sqrt{2}} = \frac{|t|/\sqrt{2}}{|t|\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This does not tend to $0$. So $f$ is not differentiable at the origin, despite both partial derivatives existing there. The partial derivatives capture behaviour along the axes; differentiability captures behaviour in all directions simultaneously.

8. A sufficient condition for differentiability

Theorem — Continuous partials imply differentiability

Let $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$. If all partial derivatives $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$ exist and are continuous in a neighbourhood of $a$, then $f$ is differentiable at $a$.

Proof

We prove the case $n = 2$; the general case follows by the same argument applied to each variable in turn. Write $h = (h_1, h_2)$ and consider:

$$f(a_1 + h_1, a_2 + h_2) - f(a_1, a_2)$$

Step 1. Split into two increments:

$$= \bigl[f(a_1 + h_1, a_2 + h_2) - f(a_1, a_2 + h_2)\bigr] + \bigl[f(a_1, a_2 + h_2) - f(a_1, a_2)\bigr]$$

Step 2. Apply the single-variable mean value theorem (Phase 3, page 4) to each bracket. For the first bracket, $g(t) = f(t, a_2 + h_2)$ is a function of $t$ alone. By the MVT, there exists $\theta_1$ between $a_1$ and $a_1 + h_1$ such that:

$$f(a_1 + h_1, a_2 + h_2) - f(a_1, a_2 + h_2) = h_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\theta_1, a_2 + h_2)$$

For the second bracket, similarly there exists $\theta_2$ between $a_2$ and $a_2 + h_2$:

$$f(a_1, a_2 + h_2) - f(a_1, a_2) = h_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(a_1, \theta_2)$$

Step 3. Combine:

$$f(a + h) - f(a) = h_1 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\theta_1, a_2 + h_2) + h_2 \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(a_1, \theta_2)$$

Step 4. Add and subtract the gradient term $h_1 f_{x_1}(a) + h_2 f_{x_2}(a)$:

$$f(a+h) - f(a) - \nabla f(a) \cdot h = h_1\bigl[f_{x_1}(\theta_1, a_2+h_2) - f_{x_1}(a)\bigr] + h_2\bigl[f_{x_2}(a_1, \theta_2) - f_{x_2}(a)\bigr]$$

Step 5. Divide by $\|h\|$ and bound. Since $|h_1| \leq \|h\|$ and $|h_2| \leq \|h\|$:

$$\frac{|f(a+h) - f(a) - \nabla f(a) \cdot h|}{\|h\|} \leq |f_{x_1}(\theta_1, a_2+h_2) - f_{x_1}(a)| + |f_{x_2}(a_1, \theta_2) - f_{x_2}(a)|$$

As $h \to 0$, both $(\theta_1, a_2 + h_2) \to a$ and $(a_1, \theta_2) \to a$. Since $f_{x_1}$ and $f_{x_2}$ are continuous at $a$, both terms tend to $0$. Therefore $f(a+h) = f(a) + \nabla f(a) \cdot h + o(\|h\|)$, so $f$ is differentiable.

∎

Functions whose partial derivatives are all continuous are called $C^1$ functions. The theorem says: $C^1$ implies differentiable. The converse is false — a function can be differentiable everywhere without having continuous partial derivatives, but such examples are pathological.

Historical note

Euler introduced partial derivatives in the 1730s to study fluid flow and vibrating strings. He used notation like $\left(\frac{dP}{dx}\right)$ with the understanding that other variables are held fixed. The modern $\partial$ symbol was introduced by Legendre (1786) and popularised by Jacobi (1841), who systematised the calculus of several variables. The distinction between existence of partial derivatives and differentiability was clarified in the 19th century, as analysts (Weierstrass, Schwarz) encountered pathological functions that violated naive expectations.

What breaks

Without continuity of partial derivatives: the counterexample in section 7 has partial derivatives at the origin, but they are not continuous there, and $f$ is not differentiable. The theorem's hypothesis is genuinely needed.

Partial derivatives without differentiability: the formula $D_v f = \nabla f \cdot v$ fails. In the counterexample, $\nabla f(0,0) = (0,0)$ predicts $D_v f = 0$ in every direction. But $D_{(1/\sqrt{2},\, 1/\sqrt{2})} f(0,0) = 1/\sqrt{2} \neq 0$. The gradient formula requires differentiability, not just existence of partials.

Without differentiability for the total derivative: if $f$ is not differentiable, there is no linear approximation $L$ with $f(a+h) = f(a) + L(h) + o(\|h\|)$. The function bends too sharply near $a$ for any single linear map to approximate it. This is the multivariable analogue of a corner in one dimension.

Partial derivatives measure change along each axis; the gradient collects them into a vector pointing uphill — but only differentiability guarantees the linear approximation works.
