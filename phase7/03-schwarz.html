Schwarz's Theorem

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 3

The problem

If $f(x, y)$ has second-order partial derivatives, you can differentiate in two orders: first with respect to $x$ then $y$ (giving $f_{xy}$), or first $y$ then $x$ (giving $f_{yx}$). Are these always equal? In every polynomial or trig function you try, they are. Euler used this freely without proof throughout the 18th century. But is it actually a theorem, or just a coincidence?

Schwarz (1873) found the precise answer: the mixed partials are equal whenever they are continuous. Without continuity, they can differ — Peano (1890) gave a clean counterexample. This page proves the theorem and exhibits the counterexample.

1. Second-order partial derivatives

If $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ has partial derivatives $f_x$ and $f_y$, and these are themselves partially differentiable, we get four second-order partial derivatives:

$$f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}, \quad f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}, \quad f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \, \partial x}, \quad f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \, \partial y}$$

Notation: $f_{xy}$ means differentiate first with respect to $x$, then with respect to $y$. In Leibniz notation, $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \, \partial x}$ reads right to left: first $x$, then $y$. The two notations agree on the order of differentiation.

2. An example where mixed partials agree

Let $f(x, y) = x^3 y + x y^4$. Compute $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$.

First route: $f_x = 3x^2 y + y^4$, then $f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2 y + y^4) = 3x^2 + 4y^3$.

Second route: $f_y = x^3 + 4xy^3$, then $f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3 + 4xy^3) = 3x^2 + 4y^3$.

They agree: $f_{xy} = f_{yx} = 3x^2 + 4y^3$. This always happens for polynomials. Let us prove why.

3. Schwarz's theorem

Theorem — Schwarz (Clairaut–Schwarz)

Let $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ where $U$ is open. If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ both exist and are continuous in a neighbourhood of $(a, b)$, then

$$f_{xy}(a, b) = f_{yx}(a, b)$$
Proof

Step 1. Define the second difference. For small $h, k \neq 0$:

$$\Delta(h, k) = f(a+h, b+k) - f(a+h, b) - f(a, b+k) + f(a, b)$$

We will compute $\Delta$ two ways — by grouping along $x$ first, then along $y$ first — and compare.

Step 2. Group along $x$ first. Define $\varphi(x) = f(x, b+k) - f(x, b)$. Then $\Delta = \varphi(a+h) - \varphi(a)$.

By the mean value theorem (Phase 3, page 4), there exists $\alpha$ between $a$ and $a+h$ such that:

$$\Delta = h \, \varphi'(\alpha) = h \bigl[f_x(\alpha, b+k) - f_x(\alpha, b)\bigr]$$

Apply the MVT again to $t \mapsto f_x(\alpha, t)$: there exists $\beta$ between $b$ and $b+k$ such that:

$$\Delta = hk \, f_{xy}(\alpha, \beta) \qquad \text{where } \alpha \in (a, a+h),\; \beta \in (b, b+k) \tag{I}$$

Step 3. Group along $y$ first. Define $\psi(y) = f(a+h, y) - f(a, y)$. Then $\Delta = \psi(b+k) - \psi(b)$.

By the MVT, there exists $\beta'$ between $b$ and $b+k$ with $\Delta = k \, \psi'(\beta') = k[f_y(a+h, \beta') - f_y(a, \beta')]$. Apply MVT again: there exists $\alpha'$ between $a$ and $a+h$ with:

$$\Delta = hk \, f_{yx}(\alpha', \beta') \qquad \text{where } \alpha' \in (a, a+h),\; \beta' \in (b, b+k) \tag{II}$$

Step 4. From (I) and (II):

$$f_{xy}(\alpha, \beta) = f_{yx}(\alpha', \beta')$$

Step 5. Let $h, k \to 0$. Then $\alpha, \alpha' \to a$ and $\beta, \beta' \to b$ (since they lie between $a$ and $a+h$, and $b$ and $b+k$ respectively). By the continuity of $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ at $(a, b)$:

$$f_{xy}(a, b) = \lim_{h,k \to 0} f_{xy}(\alpha, \beta) = \lim_{h,k \to 0} f_{yx}(\alpha', \beta') = f_{yx}(a, b)$$
∎

4. Peano's counterexample

The continuity hypothesis is essential. Consider:

$$f(x, y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy(x^2 - y^2)}{x^2 + y^2} & \text{if } (x, y) \neq (0, 0) \\ 0 & \text{if } (x, y) = (0, 0) \end{cases}$$

We will show $f_{xy}(0, 0) = -1$ and $f_{yx}(0, 0) = 1$.

Computing $f_x(0, y)$. For $y \neq 0$:

$$f_x(0, y) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h, y) - f(0, y)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{hy(h^2 - y^2)/(h^2 + y^2)}{h} = \frac{y(0 - y^2)}{0 + y^2} = -y$$

And $f_x(0, 0) = \lim_{h \to 0} f(h, 0)/h = 0$. So $f_x(0, y) = -y$ for all $y$.

Computing $f_{yx}(0, 0)$. Differentiate $f_x(0, y) = -y$ with respect to $y$:

$$f_{yx}(0, 0) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\bigl[f_x(0, y)\bigr]\Big|_{y=0} = \frac{d}{dy}(-y)\Big|_{y=0} = -1$$

Computing $f_y(x, 0)$. By symmetry of the original function (swapping $x$ and $y$ flips the sign of $x^2 - y^2$): for $x \neq 0$,

$$f_y(x, 0) = \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{f(x, k) - f(x, 0)}{k} = \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{xk(x^2 - k^2)/(x^2 + k^2)}{k} = \frac{x \cdot x^2}{x^2} = x$$

And $f_y(0, 0) = 0$. So $f_y(x, 0) = x$ for all $x$.

Computing $f_{xy}(0, 0)$. Differentiate $f_y(x, 0) = x$ with respect to $x$:

$$f_{xy}(0, 0) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\bigl[f_y(x, 0)\bigr]\Big|_{x=0} = \frac{d}{dx}(x)\Big|_{x=0} = 1$$

So $f_{xy}(0, 0) = 1 \neq -1 = f_{yx}(0, 0)$. The mixed partials differ at the origin. The point is that $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ exist everywhere but are discontinuous at $(0, 0)$ — exactly the hypothesis Schwarz's theorem requires.

5. Why the mixed partials are discontinuous

Let us verify that $f_{xy}$ is indeed discontinuous at the origin. Away from the origin, we can compute $f_{xy}$ by standard differentiation. First, expanding $f(x,y)$:

$$f(x, y) = \frac{x^3 y - xy^3}{x^2 + y^2}$$

Differentiating (a lengthy but routine quotient rule computation) gives:

$$f_x(x, y) = \frac{y(x^4 + 4x^2 y^2 - y^4)}{(x^2 + y^2)^2} \quad \text{for } (x,y) \neq (0,0)$$

and then:

$$f_{xy}(x, y) = \frac{x^6 + 9x^4 y^2 - 9x^2 y^4 - y^6}{(x^2 + y^2)^3} \quad \text{for } (x,y) \neq (0,0)$$

Along the $x$-axis ($y = 0$): $f_{xy}(x, 0) = x^6/x^6 = 1$. Along the $y$-axis ($x = 0$): $f_{xy}(0, y) = -y^6/y^6 = -1$. So $f_{xy}$ takes the values $1$ and $-1$ arbitrarily close to the origin. It has no limit there — it is genuinely discontinuous. This is exactly the gap that Schwarz's theorem exploits: continuity of the mixed partials is what forces them to agree.

6. The Hessian matrix

Definition — Hessian matrix

For $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with continuous second partial derivatives, the Hessian matrix of $f$ at $a$ is

$$H_f(a) = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_n} \\[6pt] \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_2} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \end{pmatrix}$$

By Schwarz's theorem, $H_f$ is symmetric: $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j \partial x_i}$ when the second partials are continuous. This is a real symmetric matrix, so the spectral theorem (Phase 6, page 9) applies: $H_f$ has real eigenvalues and an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors. This will be essential for classifying critical points (second derivative test, coming in a later page).

7. Higher-order generalisation

Schwarz's theorem generalises: if $f$ is $C^k$ (all partial derivatives up to order $k$ exist and are continuous), then the order of differentiation does not matter. For instance, $f_{xyz} = f_{xzy} = f_{yxz} = f_{yzx} = f_{zxy} = f_{zyx}$ when $f$ is $C^3$. The proof is by induction, applying the two-variable version at each step.

This means that for $C^k$ functions, the $k$-th order partial derivatives are determined by the number of times you differentiate with respect to each variable, not the order. There are $\binom{n+k-1}{k}$ distinct $k$-th order partial derivatives of a function of $n$ variables.

8. A concrete Hessian computation

Let $f(x, y) = x^3 - 3xy^2$. The gradient is $\nabla f = (3x^2 - 3y^2,\; -6xy)$. The Hessian is:

$$H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 6x & -6y \\ -6y & -6x \end{pmatrix}$$

Check: $f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2 - 3y^2) = -6y$ and $f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-6xy) = -6y$. They agree, as Schwarz guarantees (all partials here are polynomials, hence continuous). The Hessian is symmetric.

At the origin: $H_f(0,0) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. This zero matrix gives no information about the nature of the critical point — the second derivative test is inconclusive. Indeed, $(0,0)$ is a saddle point of $f$ (the function is $x^3$ along the $x$-axis and $-3xy^2$ otherwise), but you cannot see this from the Hessian alone. Higher-order terms matter when the Hessian vanishes.

Historical note

Clairaut (1740) first stated that mixed partials commute for specific cases he encountered in mechanics. Euler used the result freely and without proof throughout his prolific output. Schwarz (1873) gave the first rigorous proof with the correct hypothesis (continuity of the mixed partials). The counterexample above is due to Peano (1890), who specialised in finding pathological functions that showed exactly where classical assumptions break down. The history reflects a recurring pattern: a result is used for a century, then someone identifies the precise conditions under which it holds.

What breaks

Without continuity of mixed partials: Peano's counterexample gives $f_{xy}(0,0) = 1$ and $f_{yx}(0,0) = -1$. The mixed partials exist everywhere, but are discontinuous at the origin. Schwarz's theorem genuinely needs continuity — mere existence is not enough.

Without the Hessian being symmetric: the spectral theorem does not apply. You cannot diagonalise $H_f$ with an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors, and the second derivative test (which relies on the signs of the eigenvalues) becomes inapplicable. The entire machinery of classifying critical points depends on Schwarz's theorem making the Hessian symmetric.

Without second partial derivatives existing: there is no Hessian at all. The function is only $C^1$ at best, and you cannot determine the curvature or the nature of critical points from first-order information alone.

Mixed partial derivatives commute when they are continuous — and this symmetry makes the Hessian a real symmetric matrix, unlocking the spectral theorem for optimisation.
