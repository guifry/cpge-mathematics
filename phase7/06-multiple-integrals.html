Multiple Integrals and Fubini's Theorem

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 6

The problem

Archimedes computed the area under a curve by slicing it into thin rectangles. But what about the volume under a surface $z = f(x,y)$? You need to integrate over a two-dimensional region. The natural approach: slice the region into thin strips, integrate along each strip, then integrate the results. But can you always reduce a double integral to two nested single integrals? And does the order matter — integrate first in $x$ then $y$, or vice versa?

Fubini (1907) answered: yes, the double integral equals either iterated integral, provided the function is integrable. This is the theorem that makes multivariable integration computable.

1. The double integral

Definition — Double integral over a rectangle

Let $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$ and $f: R \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded. Partition $[a,b]$ into $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_m = b$ and $[c,d]$ into $c = y_0 < y_1 < \cdots < y_n = d$. This gives $mn$ sub-rectangles $R_{ij} = [x_{i-1}, x_i] \times [y_{j-1}, y_j]$. Choose sample points $(x_i^*, y_j^*) \in R_{ij}$. The Riemann sum is

$$S = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} f(x_i^*, y_j^*)\, \Delta x_i\, \Delta y_j.$$

If $S$ converges to a limit as the mesh $\max(\Delta x_i, \Delta y_j) \to 0$, independent of the choice of sample points, we say $f$ is integrable on $R$ and write $\iint_R f(x,y)\, dA$ for the limit.

This directly generalises the single-variable Riemann integral (Phase 3, page 6) from intervals to rectangles. Continuous functions on $R$ are integrable, as are bounded functions with discontinuities confined to a set of measure zero (IOU: measure theory, Phase 14).

2. Fubini's theorem

Theorem — Fubini's theorem

Let $f: R \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous on the rectangle $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$. Then

$$\iint_R f(x,y)\, dA = \int_a^b \left(\int_c^d f(x,y)\, dy\right) dx = \int_c^d \left(\int_a^b f(x,y)\, dx\right) dy.$$

The double integral equals either iterated integral, and the order of integration does not matter.

Proof

Step 1. Since $f$ is continuous on the compact set $R$, it is uniformly continuous: for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|f(x,y) - f(x', y')| < \varepsilon$ whenever $\|(x,y) - (x',y')\| < \delta$.

Step 2. Define $F(x) = \int_c^d f(x,y)\, dy$. This is well-defined for each $x \in [a,b]$ since $y \mapsto f(x,y)$ is continuous. We claim $F$ is continuous. Fix $x_0$ and let $|x - x_0| < \delta$. Then for every $y$, $|f(x,y) - f(x_0,y)| < \varepsilon$ (uniform continuity). So

$$|F(x) - F(x_0)| = \left|\int_c^d [f(x,y) - f(x_0,y)]\, dy\right| \leq \varepsilon(d - c).$$

Step 3. Take a partition of $R$ with mesh $< \delta$. Each Riemann sum for the double integral can be written as

$$S = \sum_{i=1}^m \left(\sum_{j=1}^n f(x_i^*, y_j^*)\, \Delta y_j\right) \Delta x_i.$$

The inner sum $\sum_j f(x_i^*, y_j^*)\, \Delta y_j$ is a Riemann sum for $\int_c^d f(x_i^*, y)\, dy = F(x_i^*)$, approximating it with error at most $\varepsilon(d-c)$ (by uniform continuity of $f$, each term $f(x_i^*, y_j^*)$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $f(x_i^*, y)$ for $y \in [y_{j-1}, y_j]$).

Step 4. So $S = \sum_i F(x_i^*)\, \Delta x_i + E$ where the total error satisfies $|E| \leq \varepsilon(d-c)(b-a)$. As mesh $\to 0$, $\sum_i F(x_i^*)\, \Delta x_i \to \int_a^b F(x)\, dx$. Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $S \to \int_a^b F(x)\, dx = \int_a^b \left(\int_c^d f(x,y)\, dy\right) dx$.

Step 5. But $S$ also converges to $\iint_R f\, dA$ (by definition of the double integral). So $\iint_R f\, dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y)\, dy\, dx$. Swapping the roles of $x$ and $y$ gives the other iterated integral.

∎

The full measure-theoretic version (Tonelli–Fubini theorem) extends this to Lebesgue integrable functions and $\sigma$-finite measure spaces. (IOU: Lebesgue integration, Phase 14.)

3. Integration over general regions

For a region $D$ that is not a rectangle, we use iterated integrals with variable limits. If $D = \{(x,y) : a \leq x \leq b,\; g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x)\}$ (a type I region), then

$$\iint_D f\, dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y)\, dy\, dx.$$

Similarly, if $D = \{(x,y) : c \leq y \leq d,\; h_1(y) \leq x \leq h_2(y)\}$ (a type II region), integrate first in $x$ then $y$.

4. Change of variables

Theorem — Change of variables for double integrals

Let $T: U \to V$ be a $C^1$ bijection between open sets in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $\det(J_T) \neq 0$ on $U$. If $f$ is integrable on $V$, then

$$\iint_V f(x,y)\, dx\, dy = \iint_U f(T(u,v))\, |\det(J_T(u,v))|\, du\, dv.$$

The factor $|\det(J_T)|$ is the local area scaling — the absolute value of the determinant (Phase 6, page 5) of the Jacobian matrix. It measures how much $T$ stretches or compresses area near each point.

The intuition: a small rectangle $[u, u + du] \times [v, v + dv]$ in the $(u,v)$-plane maps to a small parallelogram in the $(x,y)$-plane, spanned by $\frac{\partial T}{\partial u}\, du$ and $\frac{\partial T}{\partial v}\, dv$. The area of this parallelogram is $|\det(J_T)|\, du\, dv$. So the Jacobian determinant converts "area in the new coordinates" to "area in the old coordinates."

This is the multivariable generalisation of the single-variable substitution rule $\int f(g(t))\, |g'(t)|\, dt$, where $|g'(t)|$ is the one-dimensional "Jacobian."

5. Example: area of an ellipse

Compute the area of the ellipse $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 \leq 1$. Use the substitution $x = au$, $y = bv$, which maps the unit disc $u^2 + v^2 \leq 1$ to the ellipse. The Jacobian is $|\det J_T| = ab$. So

$$\text{Area} = \iint_{u^2+v^2 \leq 1} ab\, du\, dv = ab \cdot \pi = \pi ab.$$

6. Example: the Gaussian integral via polar coordinates

Compute $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\, dx$. The trick: consider $I^2$.

$$I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\, dx \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2}\, dy = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)}\, dx\, dy.$$

Switch to polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$. The Jacobian is

$$J_T = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\det(J_T)| = r.$$

The integral becomes

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2}\, r\, dr\, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^{\infty} r\, e^{-r^2}\, dr.$$

Substitute $u = r^2$, $du = 2r\, dr$:

$$\int_0^{\infty} r\, e^{-r^2}\, dr = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-u}\, du = \frac{1}{2}.$$

So $I^2 = \pi$, giving the famous result $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\, dx = \sqrt{\pi}$.

7. Triple integrals and higher dimensions

Everything generalises. A triple integral $\iiint_V f\, dV$ sums over a three-dimensional region. Fubini reduces it to three nested integrals. The change of variables formula uses $|\det(J_T)|$ for a map $T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$.

Cylindrical coordinates: $(x, y, z) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z)$ with Jacobian $r$. Use when the region has circular symmetry about the $z$-axis.

Spherical coordinates: $(x, y, z) = (\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \rho\cos\phi)$ with Jacobian $\rho^2 \sin\phi$. Use for regions with full spherical symmetry.

8. Example: volume of a sphere via spherical coordinates

Compute the volume of the ball $B = \{(x,y,z) : x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq R^2\}$. In spherical coordinates, the ball is $\{(\rho, \phi, \theta) : 0 \leq \rho \leq R,\; 0 \leq \phi \leq \pi,\; 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\}$:

$$\iiint_B dV = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^{R} \rho^2 \sin\phi\, d\rho\, d\phi\, d\theta.$$

The integral factors: $\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = 2\pi$, $\int_0^{\pi} \sin\phi\, d\phi = [-\cos\phi]_0^{\pi} = 2$, $\int_0^{R} \rho^2\, d\rho = R^3/3$. The volume is $2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot R^3/3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$.

This is Archimedes' formula, now derived cleanly from the change of variables theorem. The factor $\rho^2 \sin\phi$ in the Jacobian is doing all the geometric work — it accounts for the fact that thin shells at larger radius enclose more volume, and that slices near the poles are thinner than slices near the equator.

Historical note

Fubini proved his theorem rigorously in 1907, but the idea of computing double integrals as iterated single integrals was used throughout the 18th century by Euler and Lagrange without formal justification. Jacobi (1841) formalised the role of the Jacobian determinant in change of variables, explaining why $r$ appears in polar coordinates and $r^2 \sin\phi$ in spherical coordinates. The Gaussian integral $\int e^{-x^2}\, dx = \sqrt{\pi}$ was first evaluated by Laplace (1774) using the same polar coordinate trick; Poisson popularised it in 1809.

What breaks

Fubini without integrability: there exist bounded functions on $[0,1]^2$ where the two iterated integrals both exist but are not equal. The classic construction uses a function that is not Riemann integrable as a function of two variables (it has too many discontinuities). In these cases, there is no well-defined double integral, and the iterated integrals disagree. (The Lebesgue version of Fubini avoids this for $L^1$ functions — IOU: Phase 14.)

Change of variables at singular points: the formula requires $\det(J_T) \neq 0$. At the origin in polar coordinates, $r = 0$ and the Jacobian vanishes — the map collapses the entire line $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$ to a single point. This is harmless here (a single point has measure zero), but in other situations singularities of the transformation can cause genuine problems.

Wrong orientation: forgetting the absolute value $|\det J_T|$ flips the sign of the integral when $T$ reverses orientation. In single-variable calculus, reversing limits negates the integral; the absolute value in the $n$-dimensional formula accounts for this automatically.

Fubini's theorem turns a multidimensional integral into nested one-dimensional integrals; the Jacobian determinant corrects for how a coordinate change distorts area.
