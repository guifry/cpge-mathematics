Lagrange Multipliers and the Second Derivative Test

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 5

The problem

You want to maximise a function $f(x,y)$ — but you are not free to roam all of $\mathbb{R}^2$. You must stay on a curve $g(x,y) = c$. Setting $\nabla f = 0$ will not help: the critical point of $f$ may lie far from the constraint.

Lagrange (1788) faced exactly this in mechanics: maximise energy subject to conservation laws. His insight was geometric. At a constrained extremum, you cannot improve $f$ by moving along the constraint. This means the component of $\nabla f$ tangent to the constraint must vanish — so $\nabla f$ must be perpendicular to the constraint surface. But $\nabla g$ is also perpendicular to that surface. Therefore $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ for some scalar $\lambda$.

1. Unconstrained optimisation: critical points

Recall from single-variable calculus (Phase 3, page 4) that a local extremum of $f$ at an interior point forces $f'(a) = 0$. The multivariable analogue: if $f: U \to \mathbb{R}$ (with $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ open) has a local extremum at $a$, then $\nabla f(a) = 0$. The proof is the same — restrict $f$ to each coordinate axis through $a$ and apply the one-variable result to get each partial derivative equal to zero.

A point where $\nabla f(a) = 0$ is a critical point. But not every critical point is an extremum — it could be a saddle point. We need a way to classify them.

2. The Hessian matrix

The Hessian of $f$ at $a$ is the matrix of second partial derivatives:

$$H_f(a) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2}(a) & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_n}(a) \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1}(a) & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2}(a) \end{pmatrix}$$

By Schwarz's theorem (page 3), if $f$ is $C^2$ then mixed partials commute, so $H_f(a)$ is symmetric. The spectral theorem (Phase 6, page 9) then guarantees $H_f(a)$ has real eigenvalues and is orthogonally diagonalisable.

3. The second derivative test

Theorem — Second derivative test

Let $f: U \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^2$ with $\nabla f(a) = 0$. Then:

(i) If $H_f(a)$ is positive definite (all eigenvalues $> 0$), then $a$ is a strict local minimum.

(ii) If $H_f(a)$ is negative definite (all eigenvalues $< 0$), then $a$ is a strict local maximum.

(iii) If $H_f(a)$ is indefinite (eigenvalues of both signs), then $a$ is a saddle point.

Proof

Step 1. Since $f$ is $C^2$ and $\nabla f(a) = 0$, Taylor's theorem gives

$$f(a + h) = f(a) + \tfrac{1}{2} h^T H_f(a)\, h + o(\|h\|^2)$$

Step 2. Suppose $H_f(a)$ is positive definite. Let $\lambda_{\min} > 0$ be its smallest eigenvalue. For any unit vector $u$, we have $u^T H_f(a)\, u \geq \lambda_{\min}$ (this follows from the spectral decomposition). So $h^T H_f(a)\, h \geq \lambda_{\min} \|h\|^2$.

Step 3. The remainder satisfies $|o(\|h\|^2)| \leq \varepsilon \|h\|^2$ for $\|h\|$ small enough. Choose $\varepsilon = \lambda_{\min}/4$. Then for small $h$:

$$f(a + h) - f(a) = \tfrac{1}{2} h^T H_f(a)\, h + o(\|h\|^2) \geq \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_{\min}\|h\|^2 - \tfrac{\lambda_{\min}}{4}\|h\|^2 = \tfrac{\lambda_{\min}}{4}\|h\|^2 > 0$$

So $f(a+h) > f(a)$ for all small $h \neq 0$: strict local minimum.

Step 4. Negative definite: apply the argument to $-f$.

Step 5. Indefinite: there exist unit vectors $u, v$ with $u^T H_f(a)\, u > 0$ and $v^T H_f(a)\, v < 0$. Along $h = tu$, for small $t > 0$, $f(a + tu) > f(a)$; along $h = tv$, $f(a + tv) < f(a)$. So $a$ is neither a max nor a min.

∎

In two variables, positive definite means $\det(H_f) > 0$ and $f_{xx} > 0$; negative definite means $\det(H_f) > 0$ and $f_{xx} < 0$; indefinite means $\det(H_f) < 0$. When $\det(H_f) = 0$ the test is inconclusive.

Example. Consider $f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$. The gradient is $\nabla f = (2x, -2y)$, which vanishes at the origin. The Hessian is

$$H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The eigenvalues are $2$ and $-2$: both signs, so $H_f$ is indefinite. The origin is a saddle point. Along the $x$-axis, $f$ increases; along the $y$-axis, $f$ decreases. The surface $z = x^2 - y^2$ looks like a horse saddle at the origin.

4. Constrained optimisation: the geometric insight

Now restrict to a constraint $g(x,y) = c$. At a constrained extremum $a$, you cannot improve $f$ by moving along the constraint surface. Any tangent direction $v$ to the level set $g = c$ at $a$ satisfies $\nabla g(a) \cdot v = 0$ (the gradient is perpendicular to level sets). And if $a$ is a constrained extremum, the directional derivative of $f$ along $v$ must be zero: $\nabla f(a) \cdot v = 0$.

So $\nabla f(a)$ is orthogonal to every tangent direction of the constraint. But $\nabla g(a)$ is also orthogonal to every tangent direction (assuming $\nabla g(a) \neq 0$, so the tangent space is $(n-1)$-dimensional). In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the orthogonal complement of an $(n-1)$-dimensional subspace is one-dimensional, spanned by $\nabla g(a)$. Therefore $\nabla f(a) = \lambda \nabla g(a)$ for some $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

5. The Lagrange multiplier theorem

Theorem — Lagrange multipliers

Let $f, g: U \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^1$ on an open set $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$. If $f$ has a local extremum at $a$ subject to the constraint $g(a) = c$, and $\nabla g(a) \neq 0$, then there exists $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ such that

$$\nabla f(a) = \lambda\, \nabla g(a).$$
Proof

Step 1. Since $\nabla g(a) \neq 0$, some partial derivative $\partial g / \partial x_j(a) \neq 0$. Without loss of generality, say $j = n$: we have $\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}(a) \neq 0$.

Step 2. By the implicit function theorem (page 4), near $a$ we can solve $g(x, x_n) = c$ for $x_n = \varphi(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1})$, where $\varphi$ is $C^1$.

Step 3. Define $F(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}) = f(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}, \varphi(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}))$. Since $a$ is a constrained extremum of $f$, the point $(a_1, \ldots, a_{n-1})$ is an unconstrained extremum of $F$. So $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x_i} = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$.

Step 4. By the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial x_i} = 0 \quad (i = 1, \ldots, n-1).$$

Step 5. Differentiating $g(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}, \varphi) = c$ with respect to $x_i$ gives $\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i} + \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}\frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial x_i} = 0$, so $\frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial x_i} = -\frac{\partial g / \partial x_i}{\partial g / \partial x_n}$.

Step 6. Substituting into Step 4: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\frac{\partial g / \partial x_i}{\partial g / \partial x_n} = 0$. Set $\lambda = \frac{\partial f / \partial x_n}{\partial g / \partial x_n}$. Then $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} = \lambda \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$. And by definition of $\lambda$, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} = \lambda \frac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}$. So $\nabla f(a) = \lambda \nabla g(a)$.

∎

6. Multiple constraints

With $k$ constraints $g_1 = c_1, \ldots, g_k = c_k$ (where $k < n$), the constraint surface has dimension $n - k$. At a constrained extremum, $\nabla f$ must be orthogonal to this $(n-k)$-dimensional tangent space. The gradients $\nabla g_1, \ldots, \nabla g_k$ span the normal space (provided they are linearly independent). So:

$$\nabla f(a) = \lambda_1 \nabla g_1(a) + \cdots + \lambda_k \nabla g_k(a).$$

7. Example: maximise $xyz$ on a plane

Maximise $f(x,y,z) = xyz$ subject to $g(x,y,z) = x + y + z = 1$ with $x, y, z > 0$.

$\nabla f = (yz, xz, xy)$ and $\nabla g = (1, 1, 1)$. Setting $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$: $yz = \lambda$, $xz = \lambda$, $xy = \lambda$. From the first two: $yz = xz$, so $y = z$ (since $z > 0$). Similarly $x = y$. With the constraint: $3x = 1$, so $x = y = z = 1/3$. The maximum value is $f = 1/27$.

To confirm it is a maximum rather than a minimum: $f > 0$ at the critical point, and $f \to 0$ as any variable approaches $0$ (which is the boundary of our domain). So the interior critical point must be the global maximum on this domain.

8. Example: closest point on an ellipse

Find the point on the ellipse $x^2/4 + y^2 = 1$ closest to $(1, 0)$. Minimise $f(x,y) = (x-1)^2 + y^2$ subject to $g(x,y) = x^2/4 + y^2 = 1$.

$\nabla f = (2(x-1), 2y)$, $\nabla g = (x/2, 2y)$. Setting $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$:

$$2(x - 1) = \lambda x/2, \qquad 2y = 2\lambda y.$$

From the second equation, either $y = 0$ or $\lambda = 1$. If $\lambda = 1$: the first equation gives $2(x-1) = x/2$, so $4x - 4 = x$, hence $x = 4/3$. Then $y^2 = 1 - (4/3)^2/4 = 1 - 4/9 = 5/9$. Distance squared: $(4/3 - 1)^2 + 5/9 = 1/9 + 5/9 = 2/3$.

If $y = 0$: the constraint gives $x = \pm 2$. Distances squared: $(2-1)^2 = 1$ and $(-2-1)^2 = 9$. The minimum distance squared is $2/3$, achieved at $(4/3, \pm\sqrt{5}/3)$.

Historical note

Lagrange introduced the multiplier method in Mécanique analytique (1788), motivated by constrained problems in classical mechanics — finding equilibria of systems with physical constraints (rigid rods, surfaces, etc.). The multiplier $\lambda$ is not merely a computational trick: it has physical meaning. In economics, $\lambda$ measures the rate of change of the optimal value with respect to the constraint level $c$ — the shadow price. In mechanics, it gives the constraint force.

What breaks

If $\nabla g(a) = 0$: the theorem gives no information. Consider $g(x,y) = x^2 - y^2 = 0$ at the origin. The level set is two crossing lines $y = \pm x$, not a smooth curve — the constraint surface has a singularity. Any function $f$ can have a constrained extremum there without the multiplier equation being satisfied.

If you forget to check the boundary: Lagrange multipliers find interior critical points of $f$ on the constraint surface. If the constraint region is not compact (e.g. the constraint is $x + y \leq 1$ rather than $= 1$), you must also check boundary behaviour and behaviour at infinity.

If constraints are dependent: with multiple constraints, if $\nabla g_1, \ldots, \nabla g_k$ are linearly dependent at $a$, the normal space has lower dimension than expected and the multiplier equation may fail. This is the constraint qualification condition.

At a constrained extremum, $\nabla f$ is a linear combination of the constraint gradients — you cannot improve $f$ by moving along the constraint surface.
