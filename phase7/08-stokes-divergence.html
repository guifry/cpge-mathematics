The Divergence Theorem and Stokes' Theorem

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 8

The problem

Green's theorem relates a line integral around a boundary to a double integral over the interior — but only in 2D. What about 3D? If a vector field represents fluid flow, how much fluid escapes through a closed surface? Gauss (1813) discovered that the total flux through the surface equals the integral of the divergence (source strength) inside the volume.

And there is something even deeper. Stokes found that Green's theorem, Gauss's theorem, and even the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus are all instances of a single principle: integrating a "derivative" over a region equals integrating the original quantity over the boundary. This page proves both the divergence theorem and Stokes' theorem, then shows how everything unifies.

1. Divergence

Let $\mathbf{F} = (F_1, F_2, F_3)$ be a $C^1$ vector field on $\mathbb{R}^3$. The divergence of $\mathbf{F}$ is the scalar field

$$\text{div}\, \mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}.$$

Geometrically, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(a)$ measures the rate at which the field "expands" at $a$. Positive divergence means a source (flow radiates outward); negative means a sink (flow converges inward); zero means the field is incompressible.

2. Surface integrals

Let $S$ be a smooth surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with unit outward normal $\hat{n}$. The flux of $\mathbf{F}$ through $S$ is

$$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{n}\, dA.$$

If $S$ is parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ for $(u,v) \in D$, then the normal is $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$ and $d\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v)\, du\, dv$. The flux integral becomes

$$\iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v)\, du\, dv.$$

3. The divergence theorem

Theorem — Divergence theorem (Gauss–Ostrogradsky)

Let $V$ be a bounded region in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with piecewise smooth boundary $\partial V$ (outward normal). If $\mathbf{F}$ is $C^1$ on an open set containing $V$, then

$$\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\, dV.$$

4. Proof for simple regions

Proof

It suffices to prove $\oiint_{\partial V} F_3\, \hat{n}_3\, dA = \iiint_V \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\, dV$; the analogous identities for $F_1$ and $F_2$ follow by the same argument with permuted coordinates, and adding the three gives the full theorem.

Step 1. Assume $V$ is a $z$-simple region: $V = \{(x,y,z) : (x,y) \in D,\; \alpha(x,y) \leq z \leq \beta(x,y)\}$ where $D$ is a region in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\alpha, \beta$ are $C^1$.

Step 2. Compute the volume integral using Fubini (page 6):

$$\iiint_V \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\, dV = \iint_D \left[\int_{\alpha(x,y)}^{\beta(x,y)} \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\, dz\right] dA.$$

Step 3. By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Phase 3, page 8):

$$\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\, dz = F_3(x, y, \beta(x,y)) - F_3(x, y, \alpha(x,y)).$$

Step 4. The boundary $\partial V$ has three parts: the top surface $S_{\text{top}}: z = \beta(x,y)$, the bottom surface $S_{\text{bot}}: z = \alpha(x,y)$, and the lateral surface $S_{\text{lat}}$ (vertical sides).

On $S_{\text{top}}$, the outward normal points upward. Writing $z = \beta(x,y)$, we have $d\mathbf{S} = (-\beta_x, -\beta_y, 1)\, dx\, dy$, so the $z$-component of $\hat{n}\, dA$ contributes $+dA$:

$$\iint_{S_{\text{top}}} F_3\, \hat{n}_3\, dA = \iint_D F_3(x, y, \beta(x,y))\, dx\, dy.$$

On $S_{\text{bot}}$, the outward normal points downward (negative $z$-direction), so

$$\iint_{S_{\text{bot}}} F_3\, \hat{n}_3\, dA = -\iint_D F_3(x, y, \alpha(x,y))\, dx\, dy.$$

On $S_{\text{lat}}$, the normal is horizontal ($\hat{n}_3 = 0$), so the lateral surface contributes nothing to the $F_3$ component.

Step 5. Adding top and bottom:

$$\oiint_{\partial V} F_3\, \hat{n}_3\, dA = \iint_D [F_3(x,y,\beta) - F_3(x,y,\alpha)]\, dx\, dy = \iiint_V \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial z}\, dV.$$

This matches Step 3. The same argument for $F_1$ (using an $x$-simple decomposition) and $F_2$ (using a $y$-simple decomposition) completes the proof.

∎

5. Example: flux of $\mathbf{F} = (x, y, z)$ through the unit sphere

Via the divergence theorem: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3$. So

$$\oiint_{S^2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_{B^3} 3\, dV = 3 \cdot \frac{4\pi}{3} = 4\pi.$$

Direct computation: on the unit sphere, $\hat{n} = (x,y,z)$ (the position vector is the outward normal). So $\mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{n} = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$. The surface area of $S^2$ is $4\pi$, giving flux $= 4\pi$. Both methods agree.

6. Curl

The curl of $\mathbf{F} = (F_1, F_2, F_3)$ is the vector field

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial F_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial z},\; \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_3}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}\right).$$

It measures the local rotation of the field. Note that the third component $\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}$ is exactly the 2D curl from Green's theorem.

Two fundamental identities (verified by direct computation of mixed partials, using Schwarz's theorem):

$$\nabla \times (\nabla \phi) = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0.$$

The curl of a gradient is zero; the divergence of a curl is zero. "Exact implies closed" in the language of differential forms (IOU: beyond CPGE scope).

7. Stokes' theorem

Theorem — Stokes' theorem

Let $S$ be an oriented smooth surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with piecewise smooth boundary $\partial S$ (oriented consistently via the right-hand rule). If $\mathbf{F}$ is $C^1$ on an open set containing $S$, then

$$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}.$$
Proof

We prove the theorem when $S$ is a graph $z = h(x,y)$ over a region $D$ in the $xy$-plane, with $\partial S$ projecting to $\partial D$.

Step 1. Parametrise $S$ by $(x, y) \mapsto (x, y, h(x,y))$. The upward normal is $d\mathbf{S} = (-h_x, -h_y, 1)\, dx\, dy$.

Step 2. Write $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q, R)$. The right side becomes

$$\iint_D [(R_y - Q_z)(-h_x) + (P_z - R_x)(-h_y) + (Q_x - P_y)]\, dx\, dy$$

where all functions are evaluated at $(x, y, h(x,y))$.

Step 3. For the left side, parametrise $\partial S$ as $\gamma(t) = (x(t), y(t), h(x(t), y(t)))$ where $(x(t), y(t))$ traces $\partial D$. Then

$$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \oint_{\partial D} [P + R h_x]\, dx + [Q + R h_y]\, dy$$

using $dz = h_x\, dx + h_y\, dy$.

Step 4. Apply Green's theorem (page 7) to the 2D integral on the right. Set $\tilde{P} = P + Rh_x$ and $\tilde{Q} = Q + Rh_y$. Green's theorem gives

$$\oint_{\partial D} \tilde{P}\, dx + \tilde{Q}\, dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial \tilde{Q}}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \tilde{P}}{\partial y}\right) dA.$$

Step 5. Compute $\frac{\partial \tilde{Q}}{\partial x}$, using the chain rule (all of $Q, R, h_y$ depend on $x$ both directly and through $z = h(x,y)$):

$$\frac{\partial \tilde{Q}}{\partial x} = Q_x + Q_z h_x + R_x h_y + R_z h_x h_y + R h_{yx}.$$

Similarly:

$$\frac{\partial \tilde{P}}{\partial y} = P_y + P_z h_y + R_y h_x + R_z h_y h_x + R h_{xy}.$$

Step 6. Subtract. The terms $R_z h_x h_y$ and $R h_{xy} = R h_{yx}$ (Schwarz) cancel:

$$\frac{\partial \tilde{Q}}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial \tilde{P}}{\partial y} = (Q_x - P_y) + (Q_z h_x - P_z h_y) + (R_x h_y - R_y h_x) - (Q_z h_x - Q_z h_x).$$

Simplifying (the spurious terms above were written to show the cancellation pattern):

$$= (Q_x - P_y) + h_x(Q_z - R_y) + h_y(R_x - P_z)$$

Wait — let us be more careful. Rearranging:

$$= (R_y - Q_z)(-h_x) + (P_z - R_x)(-h_y) + (Q_x - P_y).$$

This is exactly $(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot (-h_x, -h_y, 1)$, which is $(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} / (dx\, dy)$. So the Green's theorem integral equals the surface integral of the curl.

∎

8. The grand unification

These theorems are all the same theorem in different dimensions:

Dimension 1 (the FTC): $\int_a^b f'(x)\, dx = f(b) - f(a)$. The "boundary" of $[a,b]$ is the two-point set $\{a, b\}$. The integral of the derivative over the interior equals the function evaluated on the boundary.

Dimension 2 (Green's theorem): $\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \iint_D (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})_3\, dA$. The integral of the "derivative" (curl) over a 2D region equals the integral of $\mathbf{F}$ over the 1D boundary.

Dimension 3 — Stokes: $\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}$. The integral of the curl over a 2D surface equals the integral of $\mathbf{F}$ over the 1D boundary.

Dimension 3 — Divergence: $\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\, dV$. The integral of the divergence over a 3D volume equals the integral of $\mathbf{F}$ over the 2D boundary.

The unifying statement in the language of differential forms (due to Élie Cartan) is $\int_{\partial M} \omega = \int_M d\omega$: the integral of a form over the boundary equals the integral of its exterior derivative over the interior. (IOU: differential forms are beyond CPGE scope, but this is the conceptual endpoint of vector calculus.)

9. Example: verifying Stokes' theorem

Let $\mathbf{F} = (-y, x, 0)$ and let $S$ be the upper hemisphere $z = \sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2}$, bounded by the unit circle $\partial S$ in the $xy$-plane.

Line integral: parametrise $\partial S$ as $\gamma(t) = (\cos t, \sin t, 0)$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$:

$$\oint \mathbf{F} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \int_0^{2\pi} [(-\sin t)(-\sin t) + (\cos t)(\cos t)]\, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 1\, dt = 2\pi.$$

Surface integral: compute $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial 0}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial z},\; \frac{\partial (-y)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial 0}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (-y)}{\partial y}\right) = (0, 0, 2).$$

The curl points straight up with magnitude 2. On the hemisphere with upward normal, the flux of $(0,0,2)$ through $S$ equals $2$ times the area of the projection of $S$ onto the $xy$-plane (since $(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = 2\, dx\, dy$ when the normal has a positive $z$-component). The projection is the unit disc, area $\pi$. So the surface integral is $2\pi$.

Both sides give $2\pi$. Stokes' theorem is confirmed.

Historical note

Gauss stated the divergence theorem in 1813, working on gravitational attraction. Ostrogradsky gave the first rigorous proof in 1826. Stokes' theorem appeared as a problem on the 1854 Cambridge Mathematical Tripos examination, set by Stokes based on a result communicated to him by Kelvin in a letter. Neither Stokes nor Kelvin published a proof; the first appeared later. The modern formulation using differential forms is due to Élie Cartan (1899), and the definitive topological framework — de Rham cohomology — was completed by Georges de Rham in 1931.

What breaks

Singularities inside the volume: the divergence theorem requires $\mathbf{F}$ to be $C^1$ throughout $V$. The gravitational field $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{r}/\|\mathbf{r}\|^3$ satisfies $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0$ away from the origin, yet the flux through any sphere enclosing the origin is $4\pi$ (not zero). The singularity at $\mathbf{r} = 0$ violates the hypothesis. To handle this, excise a small sphere around the origin, apply the theorem to the punctured region, and account for the flux through the small sphere separately.

Non-smooth boundaries: both theorems require the boundary to be piecewise smooth. Fractal boundaries (e.g. Koch snowflake) have infinite length, and the line integral may not converge even though the enclosed area is finite.

Wrong orientation: Stokes' theorem requires the boundary orientation to be consistent with the surface orientation via the right-hand rule. Reversing the orientation of either the surface or the boundary flips the sign, and the equation fails.

The divergence theorem, Stokes' theorem, Green's theorem, and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus are all the same idea: integrating a derivative over a region equals integrating the original over the boundary.
