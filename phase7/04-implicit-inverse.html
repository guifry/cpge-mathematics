The Implicit and Inverse Function Theorems

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 4

The problem

The equation $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ defines a circle. Near the point $(0, 1)$, you can solve for $y$: $y = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$. Near $(-1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})$, you can too. But at $(1, 0)$, the circle is vertical — any neighbourhood of that point contains two values of $y$ for each $x$, and no single function $y = g(x)$ can describe the curve there.

More generally: given an equation $F(x, y) = 0$, when can you "solve for $y$" as a smooth function of $x$? The implicit function theorem gives the precise answer: you can do it whenever $\partial F/\partial y \neq 0$. Its close relative, the inverse function theorem, tells you when a map is locally invertible.

1. Motivating example

Take $F(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - 1$. The level set $F = 0$ is the unit circle. At the point $(0, 1)$:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(0, 1) = 2y\Big|_{(0,1)} = 2 \neq 0$$

The implicit function theorem will guarantee that near $(0, 1)$, we can write $y = g(x)$ with $g(0) = 1$ and $F(x, g(x)) = 0$. Indeed, $g(x) = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$.

At the point $(1, 0)$:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(1, 0) = 2y\Big|_{(1,0)} = 0$$

The theorem gives no guarantee — and indeed, the circle is vertical there. Near $(1, 0)$, the curve cannot be written as $y = g(x)$. (You can write $x = h(y)$ there, since $\partial F/\partial x = 2 \neq 0$ at that point.)

2. The implicit function theorem

Theorem — Implicit function theorem

Let $F: \mathbb{R}^{n+m} \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be $C^1$, and let $(a, b) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^m$ with $F(a, b) = 0$. If the $m \times m$ matrix

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a, b) = \left(\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial y_j}(a, b)\right)_{1 \leq i, j \leq m}$$

is invertible, then there exist open sets $U \ni a$ and $V \ni b$ and a unique $C^1$ function $g: U \to V$ such that

$$F(x, g(x)) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in U, \qquad g(a) = b$$

Moreover, the derivative of $g$ is given by:

$$Dg(a) = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a, b)\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}(a, b)$$

3. Proof of the derivative formula

Assuming $g$ exists and is $C^1$, we can derive its derivative by differentiating the identity $F(x, g(x)) = 0$.

Proof

Step 1. The identity $F(x, g(x)) = 0$ holds for all $x \in U$. Define $\Phi(x) = F(x, g(x))$. Then $\Phi \equiv 0$, so $D\Phi(x) = 0$.

Step 2. Apply the chain rule (page 2) to $\Phi(x) = F(x, g(x))$. Write $H(x) = (x, g(x))$, so $\Phi = F \circ H$. The Jacobian of $H$ is:

$$J_H(x) = \begin{pmatrix} I_n \\ Dg(x) \end{pmatrix}$$

where $I_n$ is the $n \times n$ identity. The chain rule gives:

$$0 = D\Phi(x) = DF(x, g(x)) \cdot J_H(x) = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \cdot I_n + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \cdot Dg(x)$$

Step 3. Solve for $Dg(x)$:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \cdot Dg(x) = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}$$

Since $\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}$ is invertible (by hypothesis, at $(a, b)$, and by continuity in a neighbourhood):

$$Dg(x) = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}$$
∎

For the circle example: $F(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - 1$, $\partial F/\partial x = 2x$, $\partial F/\partial y = 2y$. So $g'(x) = -2x / (2y) = -x/y$. At $(0, 1)$: $g'(0) = 0$, consistent with $g(x) = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$ having a horizontal tangent at $x = 0$.

4. Proof of existence

The existence of $g$ is the hard part. The deep engine is the Banach fixed point theorem (contraction mapping principle), which we will prove in full in Phase 9. Here we outline the argument with the key steps justified. (IOU: the Banach fixed point theorem itself will be proved in Phase 9 from the completeness of $\mathbb{R}^n$.)

Proof (existence, key steps)

Step 1. Reformulate as a fixed point problem. We want to find $y$ with $F(x, y) = 0$. Define the iteration:

$$T_x(y) = y - \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a, b)\right)^{-1} F(x, y)$$

This is Newton's method with a frozen derivative (evaluated at the base point $(a, b)$ rather than at the current iterate). A fixed point of $T_x$ satisfies $T_x(y) = y$, i.e. $F(x, y) = 0$.

Step 2. Show $T_x$ is a contraction near $(a, b)$. Write $A = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a, b)\right)^{-1}$. The derivative of $T_x$ with respect to $y$ is:

$$\frac{\partial T_x}{\partial y}(y) = I_m - A \cdot \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(x, y)$$

At $(x, y) = (a, b)$: this is $I_m - A \cdot \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(a, b) = I_m - I_m = 0$. Since $F$ is $C^1$, $\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}$ is continuous, so $\frac{\partial T_x}{\partial y}$ is continuous and equals $0$ at $(a, b)$. Therefore, in a small ball around $(a, b)$:

$$\left\|\frac{\partial T_x}{\partial y}\right\| \leq \frac{1}{2}$$

By the mean value inequality, for $y_1, y_2$ in this ball:

$$\|T_x(y_1) - T_x(y_2)\| \leq \frac{1}{2}\|y_1 - y_2\|$$

So $T_x$ is a contraction with ratio $1/2$.

Step 3. Apply the contraction mapping principle. A contraction on a complete metric space has a unique fixed point (Banach, 1922). We work in a closed ball $\overline{B}(b, r) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$, which is complete. We also need $T_x$ to map this ball into itself; this holds for $x$ sufficiently close to $a$ because $T_a(b) = b - A \cdot F(a, b) = b - A \cdot 0 = b$, and by continuity of $T_x$ in $x$.

Step 4. Conclude. For each $x$ near $a$, the contraction mapping principle produces a unique $g(x)$ with $F(x, g(x)) = 0$ and $g(a) = b$. The $C^1$ regularity of $g$ follows from the derivative formula in section 3 and the continuity of all the quantities involved.

∎

5. The inverse function theorem

Theorem — Inverse function theorem

Let $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be $C^1$ and let $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$. If $Df(a)$ is invertible (equivalently, $\det J_f(a) \neq 0$), then:

(i) $f$ is locally invertible: there exist open sets $U \ni a$ and $V \ni f(a)$ such that $f: U \to V$ is a bijection.

(ii) The inverse $f^{-1}: V \to U$ is $C^1$.

(iii) $D(f^{-1})(f(a)) = [Df(a)]^{-1}$.

6. Deriving the inverse function theorem from the implicit function theorem

Proof

Step 1. Define $F: \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ by $F(x, y) = f(y) - x$. Then $F(f(a), a) = f(a) - f(a) = 0$.

Step 2. Compute $\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}(f(a), a) = Df(a)$, which is invertible by hypothesis.

Step 3. Apply the implicit function theorem to $F(x, y) = 0$ with "$x$" playing the role of the free variable and "$y$" the variable to solve for. The IFT gives a $C^1$ function $g$ defined near $f(a)$ with $F(x, g(x)) = 0$, i.e. $f(g(x)) = x$. This $g$ is the local inverse of $f$.

Step 4. The derivative formula from the IFT gives:

$$Dg(f(a)) = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = -(Df(a))^{-1} \cdot (-I_n) = [Df(a)]^{-1}$$

since $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = -I_n$.

∎

7. Example: the exponential-polar map

Define $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ by $f(x, y) = (e^x \cos y,\; e^x \sin y)$. The Jacobian matrix is:

$$J_f = \begin{pmatrix} e^x \cos y & -e^x \sin y \\ e^x \sin y & e^x \cos y \end{pmatrix}$$

The determinant is $e^{2x}\cos^2 y + e^{2x}\sin^2 y = e^{2x}$, which is positive everywhere. By the inverse function theorem, $f$ is locally invertible at every point.

But $f$ is not globally invertible: $f(x, y) = f(x, y + 2\pi)$ since $\cos$ and $\sin$ are $2\pi$-periodic. The inverse function theorem is a local statement — it guarantees invertibility in some neighbourhood, not globally. The image of $f$ is $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}$, and the map wraps around infinitely many times.

8. Connection to the single-variable case

In one variable, the inverse function theorem says: if $f'(a) \neq 0$, then $f$ is locally invertible and $(f^{-1})'(f(a)) = 1/f'(a)$ (Phase 3, page 2). The multivariable version generalises this: "$f'(a) \neq 0$" becomes "$Df(a)$ is invertible," which means "$\det J_f(a) \neq 0$" (Phase 6, page 5). The scalar reciprocal $1/f'(a)$ becomes the matrix inverse $[Df(a)]^{-1}$.

The condition "$\det J_f(a) \neq 0$" has a geometric meaning: the linear map $Df(a)$ does not collapse any direction to zero. It preserves dimension locally. When the determinant is zero, some direction gets crushed, and you lose information — the map is not invertible.

Historical note

Cauchy (1831) proved early versions of both theorems for analytic functions. Dini (1878) gave a rigorous proof of the implicit function theorem for $C^1$ functions, which is why it is sometimes called the Dini theorem in Italian-speaking mathematics. The modern proofs using the contraction mapping principle originate in the functional analysis tradition — Banach's fixed point theorem (1922) provides the abstract engine. This is not just elegance: the contraction mapping approach works in infinite-dimensional spaces (Banach spaces), which is essential for proving existence of solutions to partial differential equations. The finite-dimensional case we proved here is the prototype.

What breaks

When $\partial F/\partial y$ is singular (IFT): consider $F(x, y) = y^2 - x^2$ at the origin. Here $\partial F/\partial y = 2y = 0$ at $(0, 0)$. The level set $F = 0$ is the pair of lines $y = x$ and $y = -x$ — two branches crossing at the origin. There is no single-valued function $y = g(x)$ near $(0, 0)$. The level set has a node (a self-intersection), not a smooth curve.

When $Df(a)$ is singular (inverse FT): consider $f(x, y) = (x^2, y)$ at the origin. $J_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2x & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, which has $\det = 0$ at $x = 0$. Indeed $f$ is not locally invertible there: both $(x, y)$ and $(-x, y)$ map to the same point $(x^2, y)$ for $x$ near $0$.

Local vs global: invertibility of $Df(a)$ everywhere does not guarantee global invertibility. The exponential-polar map (section 7) has nonzero Jacobian determinant everywhere but is not injective. The theorem is inherently local.

You can locally solve $F(x, y) = 0$ for $y$ precisely when $\partial F / \partial y$ is invertible — the implicit function theorem turns a nonlinear constraint into a smooth function.
