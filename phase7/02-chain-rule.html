The Multivariable Chain Rule

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 2

The problem

A particle moves through a temperature field: its position at time $t$ is $\gamma(t) = (x(t), y(t), z(t))$, and the temperature at each point is $T(x, y, z)$. The temperature the particle experiences is $T(x(t), y(t), z(t))$ — a function of $t$ alone. How fast does it change?

In one variable, the chain rule says $(f \circ g)'(t) = f'(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)$ (Phase 3, page 2). But here $T$ takes three inputs, each depending on $t$. The answer turns out to be the dot product $\nabla T \cdot \dot{\gamma}(t)$ — the chain rule becomes matrix multiplication.

1. Recall: the single-variable chain rule

If $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at $t$ and $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at $g(t)$, then

$$(f \circ g)'(t) = f'(g(t)) \cdot g'(t)$$

The key insight: the derivative of a composition is the product of the derivatives. In the multivariable world, "product" becomes "matrix product."

2. The multivariable setup

Now let $g: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$ and $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be differentiable maps. The composition $f \circ g: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^m$ sends $x \mapsto f(g(x))$. Each of these maps has a total derivative — a linear map. The total derivative of $g$ at $a$ is $Dg(a): \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$, and the total derivative of $f$ at $g(a)$ is $Df(g(a)): \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$.

3. The Jacobian matrix

Definition — Jacobian matrix

If $f = (f_1, \ldots, f_m): \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is differentiable at $a$, the Jacobian matrix of $f$ at $a$ is the $m \times n$ matrix

$$J_f(a) = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(a) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(a) \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1}(a) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n}(a) \end{pmatrix}$$

Row $i$ is the gradient of $f_i$; column $j$ is the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x_j$. This matrix is the total derivative $Df(a)$, written in the standard basis (Phase 6, page 4).

For a scalar-valued function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the Jacobian is a $1 \times n$ row matrix — the gradient transposed. For a map $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$, the Jacobian is a square matrix.

4. The multivariable chain rule

Theorem — Multivariable chain rule

Let $g: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be differentiable at $a$, and $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be differentiable at $g(a)$. Then $f \circ g$ is differentiable at $a$ and

$$D(f \circ g)(a) = Df(g(a)) \circ Dg(a)$$

In matrix form: $J_{f \circ g}(a) = J_f(g(a)) \cdot J_g(a)$.

Proof

Step 1. Write the differentiability conditions. Since $g$ is differentiable at $a$:

$$g(a + h) = g(a) + Dg(a) \cdot h + \|h\| \varepsilon_1(h)$$

where $\varepsilon_1(h) \to 0$ as $h \to 0$. Since $f$ is differentiable at $b = g(a)$:

$$f(b + k) = f(b) + Df(b) \cdot k + \|k\| \varepsilon_2(k)$$

where $\varepsilon_2(k) \to 0$ as $k \to 0$.

Step 2. Set $k = g(a+h) - g(a) = Dg(a) \cdot h + \|h\|\varepsilon_1(h)$. Then:

$$f(g(a+h)) = f(b + k) = f(b) + Df(b) \cdot k + \|k\|\varepsilon_2(k)$$

Step 3. Substitute $k$:

$$f(g(a+h)) = f(g(a)) + Df(b)\bigl[Dg(a) \cdot h + \|h\|\varepsilon_1(h)\bigr] + \|k\|\varepsilon_2(k)$$
$$= f(g(a)) + Df(b) \circ Dg(a) \cdot h + \|h\| Df(b) \cdot \varepsilon_1(h) + \|k\|\varepsilon_2(k)$$

Step 4. Show the remainder is $o(\|h\|)$. We need to check two terms.

First term: $\|h\| Df(b) \cdot \varepsilon_1(h)$. Since $Df(b)$ is a fixed linear map (hence bounded) and $\varepsilon_1(h) \to 0$, this is $\|h\| \cdot o(1) = o(\|h\|)$.

Second term: $\|k\|\varepsilon_2(k)$. We have $\|k\| = \|Dg(a) \cdot h + \|h\|\varepsilon_1(h)\| \leq (\|Dg(a)\| + \|\varepsilon_1(h)\|)\|h\|$, so $\|k\| \leq C\|h\|$ for some constant $C$ when $h$ is small enough. As $h \to 0$, $k \to 0$, so $\varepsilon_2(k) \to 0$. Thus $\|k\|\varepsilon_2(k) \leq C\|h\| \cdot o(1) = o(\|h\|)$.

Step 5. Combine: $f(g(a+h)) = f(g(a)) + [Df(b) \circ Dg(a)] \cdot h + o(\|h\|)$. This is exactly the definition of differentiability with $D(f \circ g)(a) = Df(g(a)) \circ Dg(a)$.

∎

5. A concrete example before the special cases

Let $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ and $g(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$. Then $f(g(t)) = \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1$, a constant. The chain rule should give zero. Let us check. The Jacobian of $f$ is the gradient $\nabla f = (2x, 2y)$. The Jacobian of $g$ is $g'(t) = (-\sin t, \cos t)^T$. Their product:

$$J_f \cdot J_g = (2\cos t, 2\sin t) \begin{pmatrix} -\sin t \\ \cos t \end{pmatrix} = -2\cos t \sin t + 2\sin t \cos t = 0$$

Correct. The particle moves along a level set of $f$ (the unit circle), so the temperature does not change. The gradient $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the level set, and the velocity $g'(t)$ is tangent to it — their dot product is zero.

A second example: $f(x, y) = xy$ and $g(t) = (e^t, e^{-t})$. Then $f(g(t)) = e^t \cdot e^{-t} = 1$ again. The chain rule: $\nabla f = (y, x) = (e^{-t}, e^t)$, $g'(t) = (e^t, -e^{-t})$, dot product $= e^{-t} \cdot e^t + e^t \cdot (-e^{-t}) = 1 - 1 = 0$. The hyperbola $xy = 1$ is a level set of $f$, and the curve $(e^t, e^{-t})$ traces along it.

6. Special cases

Curve through a scalar field. Let $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ and $\gamma: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^2$ with $\gamma(t) = (x(t), y(t))$. The Jacobian of $f$ is $1 \times 2$: the gradient $(\partial f/\partial x,\; \partial f/\partial y)$. The Jacobian of $\gamma$ is $2 \times 1$: the velocity $(x'(t),\; y'(t))^T$. Their product is a $1 \times 1$ matrix:

$$\frac{d}{dt} f(\gamma(t)) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} x'(t) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} y'(t) = \nabla f \cdot \gamma'(t)$$

This is the formula from the problem box. The rate of temperature change equals the gradient dotted with the velocity.

Change of variables. Let $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ and $g: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ (e.g. a coordinate transformation). The chain rule gives $J_{f \circ g} = J_f \cdot J_g$, a $1 \times 2$ matrix times a $2 \times 2$ matrix. In terms of partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial (f \circ g)}{\partial u} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$

This formula has a beautiful geometric interpretation. If $\gamma$ moves along a level set of $f$ (where $f$ is constant), then $\frac{d}{dt}f(\gamma(t)) = 0$, so $\nabla f \cdot \gamma'(t) = 0$: the gradient is perpendicular to level sets. This is why the gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent — it is orthogonal to the contour lines, pointing directly from one level set to the next.

7. Example: polar coordinates

Let $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$. This defines a map $g(r, \theta) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$. Its Jacobian is:

$$J_g = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

For any $f(x,y)$, the chain rule gives:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\cos\theta + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\sin\theta$$
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} = -\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} r\sin\theta + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} r\cos\theta$$

These are just the two entries of the $1 \times 2$ matrix $J_f \cdot J_g = (\partial f/\partial x,\; \partial f/\partial y) \cdot J_g$.

Note the Jacobian determinant: $\det(J_g) = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r$. This is the factor $r$ that appears in polar area integrals $\iint f\, r\, dr\, d\theta$ — it measures how much the coordinate transformation stretches area. We will return to this when we study multiple integrals.

8. The chain rule in three variables — explicit form

A common situation in applications: $f(x, y, z)$ where $x = x(s, t)$, $y = y(s, t)$, $z = z(s, t)$. The chain rule gives a $1 \times 2$ Jacobian via a $1 \times 3$ times $3 \times 2$ matrix product:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}$$
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}$$

Each formula sums over all intermediate variables. The pattern is always the same: the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to an outer variable equals the sum over all intermediate variables of $(\text{partial of } f) \times (\text{partial of the intermediate variable})$. Leibniz notation makes this look like fraction cancellation — $\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \cdots$ — which is suggestive but not literally true (you cannot cancel $\partial x$). The matrix product formulation is what makes it rigorous.

Historical note

Jacobi (1841) systematically studied the matrix of partial derivatives that now bears his name. He recognised that the chain rule for compositions of multivariable functions is simply matrix multiplication — a profound insight that unifies calculus and linear algebra. The total derivative $Df(a)$ is a linear map (Phase 6, page 3); composition of differentiable functions corresponds to composition of their linearisations, and composition of linear maps is matrix multiplication (Phase 6, page 4). This viewpoint, where calculus reduces to linear algebra at each point, is the foundation of differential geometry.

What breaks

Without differentiability (only partial derivatives): the chain rule can fail. From page 1, the function $f(x,y) = xy/\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ has partial derivatives at the origin but is not differentiable there. If $\gamma(t) = (t, t)$, then $f(\gamma(t)) = t^2/(\sqrt{2}\,|t|) = |t|/\sqrt{2}$, which is not differentiable at $t = 0$. The naive formula $\nabla f(0,0) \cdot \gamma'(0) = (0,0) \cdot (1,1) = 0$ predicts a derivative of $0$, but the actual left and right derivatives are $\pm 1/\sqrt{2}$. The chain rule requires differentiability, not just existence of partial derivatives.

Without differentiability of the outer function: even if $g$ is smooth, composing with a non-differentiable $f$ gives a non-differentiable result. The linear approximation step in the proof fails: there is no $Df(b)$ to multiply by.

The multivariable chain rule is matrix multiplication: the Jacobian of a composition is the product of the Jacobians.
