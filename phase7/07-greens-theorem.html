Green's Theorem

Phase 7 — Multivariable Calculus, Page 7

The problem

A vector field $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q)$ represents a force or flow in the plane. You walk along a closed curve $\partial D$. How much work does the field do on you? You could parametrise the curve and compute a line integral — but this can be painful for complicated curves.

Green (1828) discovered a remarkable shortcut: the line integral around the boundary equals a double integral over the enclosed region. Circulation around the boundary = total curl inside. This connects a one-dimensional computation (along the boundary) to a two-dimensional one (over the interior), and it is the first glimpse of a deep pattern that will culminate in Stokes' theorem.

1. Line integrals

Let $\gamma: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be a piecewise $C^1$ curve, $\gamma(t) = (x(t), y(t))$. Given a vector field $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q)$, the line integral of $\mathbf{F}$ along $\gamma$ is

$$\int_\gamma P\, dx + Q\, dy = \int_a^b \bigl[P(\gamma(t))\, x'(t) + Q(\gamma(t))\, y'(t)\bigr]\, dt.$$

This measures the work done by the force $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle travelling along $\gamma$. The integrand $\mathbf{F} \cdot \gamma'$ is the component of the force in the direction of motion.

Key properties: the line integral is independent of the parametrisation (only the direction matters, not the speed). Reversing the direction of traversal negates the integral. If $\gamma$ is a closed curve (i.e. $\gamma(a) = \gamma(b)$), we write $\oint_\gamma$ and call it a circulation integral.

If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi$ is a gradient field, then the line integral from point $A$ to point $B$ depends only on the endpoints: $\int_\gamma \nabla\phi \cdot d\gamma = \phi(B) - \phi(A)$. This follows from the chain rule: $\frac{d}{dt}\phi(\gamma(t)) = \nabla\phi \cdot \gamma'(t)$, and integrating gives the FTC (Phase 3, page 8). In particular, the integral around any closed curve is zero.

2. Example: line integral around the unit circle

Let $\mathbf{F} = (y, x)$ and let $\gamma(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$ for $t \in [0, 2\pi]$. Then $x'(t) = -\sin t$, $y'(t) = \cos t$, so

$$\int_\gamma y\, dx + x\, dy = \int_0^{2\pi} [\sin t \cdot (-\sin t) + \cos t \cdot \cos t]\, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} (\cos^2 t - \sin^2 t)\, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} \cos 2t\, dt = 0.$$

Zero work. This is not a coincidence — $(y, x) = \nabla(xy)$, and a gradient field does zero work around any closed curve. We will prove this shortly as a corollary of Green's theorem.

3. Green's theorem

Theorem — Green's theorem

Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ be a bounded region with piecewise smooth boundary $\partial D$ oriented counterclockwise. If $P, Q$ are $C^1$ on an open set containing $D$, then

$$\oint_{\partial D} P\, dx + Q\, dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA.$$

4. Proof for simple regions

Proof

We prove the theorem when $D$ is both a type I and type II region. We show $\oint P\, dx = -\iint \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\, dA$ and $\oint Q\, dy = \iint \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\, dA$ separately; adding them gives the result.

Step 1. Write $D$ as a type I region: $D = \{(x,y) : a \leq x \leq b,\; g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x)\}$. Compute the right side:

$$-\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\, dA = -\int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}(x,y)\, dy\, dx.$$

Step 2. Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Phase 3, page 8) to the inner integral:

$$\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}(x,y)\, dy = P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x)).$$

So

$$-\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\, dA = -\int_a^b [P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x))]\, dx = \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x))\, dx - \int_a^b P(x, g_2(x))\, dx.$$

Step 3. Now compute $\oint_{\partial D} P\, dx$. The boundary consists of four pieces: the bottom curve $y = g_1(x)$ from $x = a$ to $x = b$ (left to right), the right side, the top curve $y = g_2(x)$ from $x = b$ to $x = a$ (right to left), and the left side. On the vertical sides, $x$ is constant so $dx = 0$ and those pieces contribute nothing. On the bottom:

$$\int_{\text{bottom}} P\, dx = \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x))\, dx.$$

On the top (traversed from right to left):

$$\int_{\text{top}} P\, dx = \int_b^a P(x, g_2(x))\, dx = -\int_a^b P(x, g_2(x))\, dx.$$

Step 4. Adding: $\oint P\, dx = \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x))\, dx - \int_a^b P(x, g_2(x))\, dx$. This equals the expression in Step 2. So $\oint P\, dx = -\iint \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\, dA$.

Step 5. Similarly, writing $D$ as a type II region $\{(x,y) : c \leq y \leq d,\; h_1(y) \leq x \leq h_2(y)\}$ and applying the FTC to the $x$-integral gives $\oint Q\, dy = \iint \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}\, dA$. The argument is identical with the roles of $x$ and $y$ swapped.

Step 6. Adding both equations: $\oint P\, dx + Q\, dy = \iint \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA$.

∎

For more general regions, decompose $D$ into pieces that are both type I and type II. The boundary integrals along internal cuts cancel (each internal edge is traversed twice in opposite directions), leaving only the integral along $\partial D$.

5. The 2D curl

The quantity $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ is the curl of $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q)$ in two dimensions. It measures how much the field "rotates" at each point. Green's theorem says: the total circulation around $\partial D$ equals the total curl inside $D$.

6. Conservative fields

Theorem — Conservative fields on simply connected domains

Let $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q)$ be $C^1$ on a simply connected open set $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$. The following are equivalent:

(i) $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ everywhere in $U$.

(ii) $\oint_\gamma \mathbf{F} \cdot d\gamma = 0$ for every closed curve $\gamma$ in $U$.

(iii) $\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi$ for some $C^2$ function $\phi: U \to \mathbb{R}$.

Proof

(iii) $\Rightarrow$ (i): If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi = (\phi_x, \phi_y)$, then $P = \phi_x$, $Q = \phi_y$, and $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \phi_{xy} = \phi_{yx} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$ by Schwarz's theorem (page 3).

(i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii): by Green's theorem, the integrand of the double integral vanishes, so $\oint \mathbf{F} \cdot d\gamma = 0$.

(ii) $\Rightarrow$ (iii): fix a point $a \in U$. For any $x \in U$, define $\phi(x) = \int_\gamma \mathbf{F} \cdot d\gamma$ where $\gamma$ is any path from $a$ to $x$ in $U$. This is well-defined: if $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ are two such paths, $\gamma_1$ followed by $\gamma_2^{-1}$ is a closed curve, so $\int_{\gamma_1} - \int_{\gamma_2} = 0$ by (ii). One verifies $\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x_i}(x) = F_i(x)$ by differentiating under the integral along a short segment in the $x_i$-direction.

∎

7. Area via Green's theorem

Theorem — Area formula

If $D$ is a bounded region with piecewise smooth boundary $\partial D$ oriented counterclockwise, then

$$\text{Area}(D) = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial D} x\, dy - y\, dx.$$

Take $P = -y/2$, $Q = x/2$. Then $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$. Green's theorem gives $\oint P\, dx + Q\, dy = \iint_D 1\, dA = \text{Area}(D)$.

8. Verification: $\mathbf{F} = (-y, x)$ on the unit disc

Take $P = -y$, $Q = x$ on the unit disc $D$. The line integral around $\partial D$ (the unit circle, $\gamma(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$):

$$\oint (-y)\, dx + x\, dy = \int_0^{2\pi} [-\sin t \cdot (-\sin t) + \cos t \cdot \cos t]\, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 1\, dt = 2\pi.$$

The double integral: $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1 - (-1) = 2$. So

$$\iint_D 2\, dA = 2 \cdot \pi (1)^2 = 2\pi.$$

Both sides equal $2\pi$. Green's theorem checks out.

Historical note

George Green (1793–1841) was a self-taught British mathematician. His father was a baker and miller; Green had only one year of formal schooling. In 1828 he published An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism, containing what we now call Green's theorem, Green's functions, and foundational ideas in potential theory. He printed 51 copies, distributed mainly to friends and subscribers of his local library in Nottingham. The work was virtually unknown until William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) rediscovered it in the 1840s and brought it to the attention of the mathematical world.

What breaks

Without simple connectedness: the equivalence (i) $\Leftrightarrow$ (ii) fails. Consider $\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2},\, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$. Direct computation gives $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ everywhere in the domain. Yet around the unit circle, $\oint \mathbf{F} \cdot d\gamma = \int_0^{2\pi} 1\, dt = 2\pi \neq 0$. The hole at the origin prevents us from applying Green's theorem — the region enclosed by the curve does not lie entirely in the domain.

Without smoothness of the boundary: if $\partial D$ has cusps or self-intersections, the orientation is ill-defined and the theorem may fail. The piecewise smooth condition ensures each piece has a well-defined tangent (and hence normal) direction.

Without $C^1$ on the field: if $P$ or $Q$ has a discontinuity inside $D$, the double integral may not equal the line integral. The proof relied on applying the FTC to continuous partial derivatives.

Green's theorem converts a line integral around a closed curve into a double integral of the curl over the interior — boundary circulation equals interior rotation.
