The Open Mapping and Closed Graph Theorems

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 6

The problem

A continuous function maps open sets to... well, not necessarily open sets. The image of $(-1, 1)$ under $f(x) = x^2$ is $[0, 1)$, which is not open. But Banach (1929) proved something remarkable: if $T : X \to Y$ is a surjective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces, then $T$ does map open sets to open sets.

This "open mapping theorem" has a powerful equivalent formulation: if a linear map between Banach spaces has a closed graph, it is automatically continuous. These two results, together with Banach-Steinhaus (page 5), form the "holy trinity" of functional analysis.

1. The Open Mapping Theorem

Open mapping theorem (Banach, 1929)

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces and $T \in \mathcal{L}(X, Y)$ be surjective. Then $T$ is an open map: for every open set $U \subset X$, the image $T(U)$ is open in $Y$.

Equivalently: $T(B_X(0, 1))$ contains an open ball around $0$ in $Y$. If the image of the unit ball has interior, then by linearity, the image of any open ball has interior, and $T$ is open.

Proof

We show $T(B_X(0, 1))$ contains a ball $B_Y(0, \delta)$ for some $\delta > 0$. The proof has two stages: first show the closure of $T(B_X(0, 1))$ has interior, then bootstrap to the image itself.

Step 1. The closure has interior. Since $T$ is surjective, $Y = T(X) = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} T(B_X(0, n))$. Each $\overline{T(B_X(0, n))}$ is closed, and their union covers $Y$. By Baire's category theorem (Phase 9, page 7), applied to the Banach space $Y$, some $\overline{T(B_X(0, N))}$ has non-empty interior. By linearity ($T(B_X(0, N)) = N \cdot T(B_X(0, 1))$), we get that $\overline{T(B_X(0, 1))}$ has non-empty interior. A symmetric-set argument shows it contains a ball $B_Y(0, 2\varepsilon)$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$.

Step 2. From closure to image. We show $B_Y(0, \varepsilon) \subset T(B_X(0, 1))$. Take any $y \in B_Y(0, \varepsilon)$. Since $y \in \overline{T(B_X(0, 1/2))}$ (by the rescaled Step 1), there exists $x_1 \in B_X(0, 1/2)$ with $\|y - Tx_1\| < \varepsilon/2$.

Now $y - Tx_1 \in B_Y(0, \varepsilon/2) \subset \overline{T(B_X(0, 1/4))}$, so there exists $x_2 \in B_X(0, 1/4)$ with $\|y - Tx_1 - Tx_2\| < \varepsilon/4$.

Step 3. Continue inductively: at stage $k$, choose $x_k \in B_X(0, 2^{-k})$ with $\|y - T(x_1 + \cdots + x_k)\| < \varepsilon \cdot 2^{-k}$.

Step 4. The series $x = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} x_k$ converges absolutely in $X$ (since $\sum \|x_k\| < \sum 2^{-k} = 1$), and $X$ is complete, so $x \in X$ with $\|x\| < 1$. By continuity of $T$, $Tx = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} Tx_k = y$. Hence $y \in T(B_X(0, 1))$.

∎

Notice that completeness of both spaces is used: Baire applies to $Y$ in Step 1, and the series convergence in Step 4 uses completeness of $X$.

The symmetric-set argument in Step 1 deserves a word. If $\overline{T(B_X(0,1))}$ contains some ball $B_Y(y_0, 2\varepsilon)$, then since $T(B_X(0,1))$ is symmetric (if $x$ is in the ball, so is $-x$, and $T(-x) = -Tx$), its closure is symmetric too. So $B_Y(-y_0, 2\varepsilon) \subset \overline{T(B_X(0,1))}$. For any $y$ with $\|y\| < 2\varepsilon$, write $y = \frac{1}{2}(y_0 + y) + \frac{1}{2}(-y_0 + y)$. Both summands lie in $\overline{T(B_X(0,1))}$, and by convexity of the closure, $y$ does too. Hence $B_Y(0, 2\varepsilon) \subset \overline{T(B_X(0,1))}$.

The approximation argument in Steps 2-4 is sometimes called the "Banach iteration." It is a geometric series trick: at each stage we halve the error and halve the correction size, so the total correction converges. This pattern appears repeatedly in analysis — it is the same idea behind the contraction mapping theorem and Newton's method.

2. The Bounded Inverse Theorem

Bounded inverse theorem

If $X$ and $Y$ are Banach spaces and $T \in \mathcal{L}(X, Y)$ is bijective, then $T^{-1} \in \mathcal{L}(Y, X)$ — the inverse is automatically bounded.

Proof

Step 1. Since $T$ is bijective and surjective, the open mapping theorem applies: $T$ is an open map.

Step 2. A map is continuous if and only if the preimage of every open set is open. For $T^{-1}$, the preimage of an open set $U \subset X$ is $T(U)$, which is open since $T$ is an open map. Hence $T^{-1}$ is continuous, i.e. bounded.

∎

This is a remarkable rigidity result: in the Banach space world, a bounded bijective linear map is automatically an isomorphism. You never need to check that the inverse is bounded — it comes for free.

A practical consequence: if $X$ is a vector space that is Banach under two norms $\|\cdot\|_1$ and $\|\cdot\|_2$, and one norm dominates the other ($\|x\|_2 \leq C\|x\|_1$ for all $x$), then the norms are equivalent. The identity map $(X, \|\cdot\|_1) \to (X, \|\cdot\|_2)$ is bounded and bijective between Banach spaces, so its inverse is bounded — giving $\|x\|_1 \leq C'\|x\|_2$. This is how norm equivalence is often established in practice.

3. The Closed Graph Theorem

The graph of a linear map $T : X \to Y$ is the set $\operatorname{Gr}(T) = \{(x, Tx) : x \in X\} \subset X \times Y$, where $X \times Y$ carries the product norm $\|(x, y)\| = \|x\| + \|y\|$.

Closed graph theorem

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces and $T : X \to Y$ a linear map. Then $T$ is bounded if and only if $\operatorname{Gr}(T)$ is closed in $X \times Y$.

The forward direction is easy: if $T$ is continuous and $(x_n, Tx_n) \to (x, y)$, then $Tx_n \to Tx$ by continuity and $Tx_n \to y$ by assumption, so $y = Tx$ and $(x, y) \in \operatorname{Gr}(T)$. The reverse is the deep part.

Proof (closed graph implies bounded)

Step 1. Since $\operatorname{Gr}(T)$ is a closed subspace of the Banach space $X \times Y$, it is itself a Banach space (a closed subset of a complete space is complete).

Step 2. Consider the projection $\pi_1 : \operatorname{Gr}(T) \to X$ defined by $\pi_1(x, Tx) = x$. This is linear, bounded ($\|\pi_1(x, Tx)\| = \|x\| \leq \|x\| + \|Tx\|$), and bijective (every $x \in X$ gives exactly one pair $(x, Tx)$).

Step 3. By the bounded inverse theorem (Section 2), $\pi_1^{-1} : X \to \operatorname{Gr}(T)$ is bounded. That is, $\pi_1^{-1}(x) = (x, Tx)$ satisfies $\|x\| + \|Tx\| \leq C\|x\|$ for some constant $C$.

Step 4. Therefore $\|Tx\| \leq (C - 1)\|x\|$ for all $x$, so $T$ is bounded.

∎

4. Using the Closed Graph Theorem

The closed graph theorem is a powerful tool for proving continuity. Instead of finding an explicit bound $\|Tx\| \leq C\|x\|$, you only need to verify:

If $x_n \to x$ and $Tx_n \to y$, then $Tx = y$.

This is often much easier than a direct norm estimate.

Example. Consider differentiation $D : C^1([0,1]) \to C([0,1])$, where $C^1([0,1])$ carries the $C^1$ norm $\|f\|_{C^1} = \|f\|_\infty + \|f'\|_\infty$. Suppose $f_n \to f$ in $C^1$ and $f_n' \to g$ in $C([0,1])$. Then $f_n \to f$ uniformly and $f_n' \to g$ uniformly. By the classical theorem on uniform limits of derivatives (Phase 4, page 5), $f' = g$. So $D$ has a closed graph, hence $D$ is bounded.

Contrast this with $D : (C^1([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_\infty) \to (C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_\infty)$, where the domain carries only the sup norm. Here $D$ is not bounded (consider $f_n(x) = x^n/n$, which satisfies $\|f_n\|_\infty = 1/n \to 0$ but $\|f_n'\|_\infty = 1$). And indeed, $(C^1([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_\infty)$ is not a Banach space — it is not complete — so the closed graph theorem does not apply.

The moral: when proving a linear map is bounded, always ask whether the closed graph approach is simpler. The condition "$x_n \to x$ and $Tx_n \to y$ implies $Tx = y$" is a statement about limits, and limit arguments are often easier than explicit norm estimates. The theorem converts a topological problem (closedness of a set) into an analytic conclusion (boundedness of an operator).

Remark. The three theorems on pages 5-6 are logically interconnected. We proved the closed graph theorem from the bounded inverse theorem, which follows from the open mapping theorem. One can also derive the open mapping theorem from the closed graph theorem, or deduce Banach-Steinhaus from the closed graph theorem. The three are essentially equivalent in the presence of Baire — they are different faces of the same completeness phenomenon.

Historical note

Banach proved the open mapping theorem in 1929 in his paper "Sur les opérations dans les ensembles abstraits et leur application aux équations intégrales." Schauder contributed the closed graph formulation around the same time. Together with the uniform boundedness principle (Banach-Steinhaus, page 5), these three results form the "holy trinity" of functional analysis — and all three rest on Baire's category theorem (1899). The remarkable fact is that a single topological tool (Baire) yields three fundamental analytic results.

What breaks

Without surjectivity: the inclusion $\iota : \ell^1 \hookrightarrow \ell^2$ (mapping each sequence to itself) is bounded and injective, but not open. The image of the open unit ball in $\ell^1$ is not open in $\ell^2$ — the $\ell^1$ ball is "much smaller" than the $\ell^2$ ball. Surjectivity is essential.

Without completeness of both spaces: consider the identity map $\operatorname{id} : (C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_{L^1}) \to (C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_{L^1})$. The domain space is the same set but with a different, incomplete topology. There exist bijective bounded linear maps between incomplete normed spaces whose inverses are unbounded. Both spaces must be Banach for the bounded inverse theorem to hold.

Without linearity: surjective continuous maps between complete metric spaces need not be open. Consider $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = x^3$, which is a bijective continuous map, but in the nonlinear setting there is no general open mapping principle — you must verify openness directly.

A surjective bounded linear map between Banach spaces is automatically open — and a linear map with a closed graph is automatically continuous.
