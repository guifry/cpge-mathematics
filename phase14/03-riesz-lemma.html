Riesz's Lemma and Finite-Dimensional Characterisation

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 3

The problem

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the closed unit ball $\overline{B}(0, 1) = \{x : \|x\| \leq 1\}$ is compact (Heine–Borel, Phase 9 page 4). This is the engine behind the extreme value theorem: continuous functions on compact sets attain their bounds.

Does this extend to infinite-dimensional Banach spaces? Can we still extract convergent subsequences from bounded sequences? Riesz (1918) proved the devastating answer: no. The closed unit ball is compact if and only if the space is finite-dimensional. Infinite-dimensional analysis is fundamentally harder than anything we have seen so far.

Recall from Phase 9, page 4 that a set is compact if every sequence in it has a convergent subsequence (sequential compactness, equivalent to compactness in metric spaces). In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the Heine–Borel theorem says: closed and bounded $\Leftrightarrow$ compact. This is the tool behind almost every "maximum exists" argument in finite-dimensional analysis.

To prove that this fails in infinite dimensions, we need a way to build sequences in the unit ball whose elements are "spread apart" — so far apart that no subsequence can converge. Riesz's lemma is the tool that builds such sequences.

1. Riesz's lemma

Lemma — Riesz (1918)

Let $X$ be a normed vector space and $Y \subsetneq X$ a proper closed subspace. For every $\varepsilon \in (0, 1)$, there exists $x \in X$ with $\|x\| = 1$ and

$$\text{dist}(x, Y) = \inf_{y \in Y} \|x - y\| \geq 1 - \varepsilon.$$
Proof

Step 1. Since $Y \subsetneq X$, pick $z \in X \setminus Y$. Since $Y$ is closed, $d = \text{dist}(z, Y) = \inf_{y \in Y} \|z - y\| > 0$ (the infimum over a closed set not containing $z$).

Step 2. By definition of infimum, there exists $y_0 \in Y$ with $\|z - y_0\| < d / (1 - \varepsilon)$. (Since $\varepsilon < 1$, we have $d / (1 - \varepsilon) > d$, so such a $y_0$ exists.)

Step 3. Set $x = (z - y_0) / \|z - y_0\|$. Then $\|x\| = 1$. For any $y \in Y$:

$$\|x - y\| = \frac{\|z - y_0 - \|z - y_0\|\,y\|}{\|z - y_0\|} = \frac{\|z - (y_0 + \|z - y_0\|\,y)\|}{\|z - y_0\|}.$$

Since $y_0 + \|z - y_0\|\,y \in Y$ (a subspace), the numerator is at least $d$. So

$$\|x - y\| \geq \frac{d}{\|z - y_0\|} > \frac{d}{d/(1 - \varepsilon)} = 1 - \varepsilon.$$

Since this holds for every $y \in Y$, we have $\text{dist}(x, Y) \geq 1 - \varepsilon$.

∎

The lemma says: given a proper closed subspace, you can always find a unit vector that is "nearly as far as possible" from the subspace. The word "nearly" is encoded by the parameter $\varepsilon$. The closer $\varepsilon$ is to 0, the closer the distance is to 1 — but you cannot in general reach distance exactly 1 (more on this in the break-box below).

2. Constructing a sequence with no convergent subsequence

We now use Riesz's lemma to show that in any infinite-dimensional normed space, the closed unit ball fails to be compact. The strategy: build a sequence of unit vectors that are all pairwise at distance at least $1/2$, so no subsequence can be Cauchy.

Construction

Step 1. Let $X$ be infinite-dimensional. Pick any $x_1 \in X$ with $\|x_1\| = 1$ and set $Y_1 = \text{span}(x_1)$. Since $\dim X = \infty$, $Y_1$ is a proper subspace. It is finite-dimensional, hence closed (Phase 14, page 2: finite-dimensional subspaces are complete, hence closed).

Step 2. Apply Riesz's lemma with $\varepsilon = 1/2$: there exists $x_2 \in X$ with $\|x_2\| = 1$ and $\text{dist}(x_2, Y_1) \geq 1/2$. In particular, $\|x_2 - x_1\| \geq 1/2$.

Step 3. Set $Y_2 = \text{span}(x_1, x_2)$, again a proper closed subspace. Apply Riesz's lemma: there exists $x_3$ with $\|x_3\| = 1$ and $\text{dist}(x_3, Y_2) \geq 1/2$, so $\|x_3 - x_j\| \geq 1/2$ for $j = 1, 2$.

Step 4. Continuing inductively, we build a sequence $(x_n)$ in the closed unit ball with $\|x_m - x_n\| \geq 1/2$ for all $m \neq n$. No subsequence can be Cauchy (elements stay at least $1/2$ apart), so no subsequence converges.

∎

3. The compactness characterisation

Theorem — Riesz (1918)

The closed unit ball of a normed vector space $X$ is compact if and only if $X$ is finite-dimensional.

Proof

Step 1. ($\Leftarrow$) If $\dim X = n < \infty$, fix a basis and identify $X$ with $\mathbb{R}^n$. By norm equivalence (Phase 14, page 1), the closed unit ball in $\|\cdot\|$ is closed and bounded in $\|\cdot\|_2$. By Heine–Borel (Phase 9, page 4), it is compact.

Step 2. ($\Rightarrow$) Equivalently, if $X$ is infinite-dimensional, Section 2 constructs a sequence in the closed unit ball with no convergent subsequence. Hence the closed unit ball is not sequentially compact, hence not compact (in metric spaces, compactness and sequential compactness are equivalent — Phase 9, page 4).

∎

4. Consequences

This theorem is a watershed. In infinite dimensions:

The extreme value theorem needs compactness. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, a continuous function on a compact set attains its maximum (Phase 9, page 5). In infinite dimensions, the closed unit ball is not compact, so this guarantee vanishes. Consider the normed space $c_0$ of sequences converging to zero (with $\|\cdot\|_\infty$), and the linear functional $\varphi((x_n)) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x_n / 2^n$. This is bounded with $\|\varphi\| = 1$. But for any $x$ with $\|x\|_\infty \leq 1$, we have $\varphi(x) \leq \sum |x_n|/2^n \leq 1$, and equality would require $x_n = 1$ for all $n$ — but then $x \notin c_0$. So $\varphi$ does not attain its norm on the closed unit ball of $c_0$.

Compactness must be replaced. In infinite-dimensional analysis, we use substitutes: weak compactness (Banach–Alaoglu theorem), equicontinuity criteria (Arzelà–Ascoli, Phase 9 page 7), and tight families (Prokhorov's theorem in probability). Each captures a piece of what compactness gave us for free in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Finite-dimensional subspaces are always closed. A by-product of the proof: any finite-dimensional subspace of a normed space is complete (Phase 14, page 2), hence closed. This is used constantly — for instance, the kernel of a bounded linear map with finite-dimensional range is automatically closed.

The Bolzano–Weierstrass property is lost. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence (Bolzano–Weierstrass, Phase 9 page 4). In infinite dimensions, this is exactly what Riesz's construction destroys: bounded sequences can have elements that stay permanently far apart. Every technique that relied on extracting convergent subsequences from bounded data must be rethought.

This is why infinite-dimensional analysis splits into subspecialties: Hilbert spaces (where an inner product gives extra geometric structure), reflexive Banach spaces (where weak compactness partially restores Bolzano–Weierstrass), and general Banach spaces (where the bare minimum of completeness is all you have).

5. A concrete example in $\ell^2$

To make the abstract construction vivid, consider $X = \ell^2$ with the standard orthonormal basis $e_n = (0, \ldots, 0, 1, 0, \ldots)$ (a 1 in position $n$, zeros elsewhere). Each $e_n$ has norm 1, and for $m \neq n$:

$$\|e_m - e_n\|_2 = \sqrt{|1|^2 + |{-1}|^2} = \sqrt{2}.$$

So $(e_n)$ is a sequence in the closed unit ball with all pairwise distances equal to $\sqrt{2}$. No subsequence can be Cauchy. This is a direct, elementary proof that the unit ball of $\ell^2$ is not compact — no Riesz lemma needed. But Riesz's lemma gives the result for every infinite-dimensional normed space, not just those with an orthonormal basis.

Historical note

Frigyes Riesz proved this characterisation in 1918, in the same paper where he introduced the abstract notion of a normed space. The result shocked the mathematical community: it meant that the geometric intuition built in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — where closed bounded sets are compact — fails completely in the function spaces that analysis actually needs.

This failure drove the development of weak topologies (Banach–Alaoglu, 1940) and compactness substitutes that remain central to modern analysis. Riesz himself went on to develop the $L^p$ spaces (independently of Fischer) and prove the Riesz representation theorem, further cementing the importance of distinguishing finite from infinite dimensions.

What breaks

Without "closed." The open unit ball is never compact, even in $\mathbb{R}$: the sequence $x_n = 1 - 1/n$ has no limit in the open ball.

Without "proper subspace" in Riesz's lemma. If $Y = X$, then every unit vector has distance 0 from $Y$, and the conclusion is trivially false.

The $\varepsilon$ gap. In infinite dimensions, one generally cannot take $\varepsilon = 0$ in Riesz's lemma — that is, one cannot always find a unit vector at distance exactly 1 from $Y$. This is possible in some spaces (reflexive Banach spaces, and in particular all Hilbert spaces), but not in general. The gap $\varepsilon > 0$ is essential to the argument. In a Hilbert space, orthogonal projection onto $Y$ gives you the nearest point exactly, so you can take $\varepsilon = 0$. This is one reason Hilbert spaces are so much better behaved than general Banach spaces.

Without "normed space." In a general metric space, there is no notion of "unit ball" (there is no scaling), and the very statement of the theorem makes no sense. The interplay between the linear structure (subspaces, dimension) and the metric structure (distance, compactness) is what gives Riesz's theorem its content.

The closed unit ball is compact iff the space is finite-dimensional — Riesz's lemma kills compactness in infinite dimensions.
