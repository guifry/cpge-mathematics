Banach Spaces

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 2

The problem

In Phase 9 (page 3), we saw that completeness — every Cauchy sequence converges — is what makes $\mathbb{R}$ work. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ are not complete, and analysis breaks down there: Cauchy sequences can "aim" at irrational limits that do not exist in the space.

Now we ask the same question for normed vector spaces. When we pass from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to infinite-dimensional spaces of functions, which ones are complete? Banach (1920s) showed that completeness in normed spaces is not a luxury — it is the minimum requirement for a coherent theory of operators and functionals.

1. Definition

Definition — Banach space

A Banach space is a normed vector space $(X, \|\cdot\|)$ that is complete: every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a limit in $X$.

Recall from Phase 9 that a sequence $(x_n)$ is Cauchy if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that $\|x_m - x_n\| < \varepsilon$ for all $m, n \geq N$. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy (triangle inequality), but the converse can fail: Cauchy sequences may "aim" at a limit that does not belong to the space. Completeness is precisely the condition that this cannot happen — every Cauchy sequence has somewhere to go.

Why does this matter? Because the most natural constructions in analysis — taking limits of approximations, summing infinite series, iterating operators — all produce Cauchy sequences. If the space is not complete, these constructions can "fall out" of the space, and theorems fail silently.

2. Examples

The definition is simple, but which spaces actually satisfy it? The answer determines which function spaces are suitable for analysis.

$\mathbb{R}^n$ with any norm. We proved in Phase 14, page 1 that all norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent. Since $(\mathbb{R}^n, \|\cdot\|_2)$ is complete (Phase 9, page 3), and equivalent norms preserve Cauchy sequences and convergence, $\mathbb{R}^n$ is Banach under every norm. This is the simplest and most reassuring example: finite-dimensional spaces always work.

$C([a,b])$ with $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. This is the space of continuous functions $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ with the supremum norm $\|f\|_\infty = \max_{x \in [a,b]} |f(x)|$.

Proof of completeness

Step 1. Let $(f_n)$ be Cauchy in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. For each fixed $x \in [a, b]$, $|f_m(x) - f_n(x)| \leq \|f_m - f_n\|_\infty$, so $(f_n(x))$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$. By completeness of $\mathbb{R}$, define $f(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x)$.

Step 2. The convergence is uniform: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ so that $\|f_m - f_n\|_\infty < \varepsilon$ for $m, n \geq N$. Fix $x$ and let $m \to \infty$: $|f(x) - f_n(x)| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$ and all $x$. So $\|f - f_n\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon$.

Step 3. The uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous (Phase 5, page 6). So $f \in C([a,b])$ and $f_n \to f$ in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$.

∎

$\ell^p$ spaces. For $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, the space $\ell^p$ of sequences $(x_n)$ with $\|(x_n)\|_p = \left(\sum |x_n|^p\right)^{1/p} < \infty$ (or $\sup |x_n| < \infty$ for $p = \infty$) is a Banach space. We state this without proof; the argument is analogous to the one above, using completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ coordinate-by-coordinate and dominated convergence to exchange limit and sum.

3. A non-example

$C([0,1])$ with the $\|\cdot\|_1$ norm ($\|f\|_1 = \int_0^1 |f(x)|\,dx$) is not complete.

Proof of incompleteness

Step 1. Define a sequence of continuous functions that "want" to converge to a step function. For each $n \geq 1$, let

$$f_n(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } 0 \leq x \leq \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{n}, \\ \tfrac{n}{2}\left(x - \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{n}\right) & \text{if } \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{n} < x < \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{n}, \\ 1 & \text{if } \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{n} \leq x \leq 1. \end{cases}$$

Each $f_n$ is continuous, rising linearly from 0 to 1 over an interval of width $2/n$ centred at $1/2$.

Step 2. For $m > n$, $f_m$ and $f_n$ differ only on an interval of width at most $2/n$, and the difference is bounded by 1. So $\|f_m - f_n\|_1 \leq 2/n \to 0$. The sequence is Cauchy in $\|\cdot\|_1$.

Step 3. The pointwise limit is $f(x) = \mathbf{1}_{(1/2, 1]}(x)$, a step function that is discontinuous at $x = 1/2$. If $(f_n)$ converged in $\|\cdot\|_1$ to some $g \in C([0,1])$, then $g = f$ almost everywhere, but no continuous function equals this step function a.e. So there is no limit in $C([0,1])$.

∎

The "completion" of $(C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_1)$ is the Lebesgue space $L^1([0,1])$, which includes all (equivalence classes of) integrable functions, not just continuous ones. This is a general phenomenon: every normed space has a unique completion (up to isometric isomorphism), obtained by adding limits of all Cauchy sequences. The completion is always a Banach space.

The lesson: the same vector space $C([0,1])$ can be Banach or not depending on the norm. Completeness is a joint property of the space and the norm, not of the space alone. Choosing the "right" norm for a function space is one of the central decisions in functional analysis.

4. The absolute convergence criterion

Theorem — Characterisation of Banach spaces

A normed space $(X, \|\cdot\|)$ is Banach if and only if every absolutely convergent series converges: $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \|x_n\| < \infty \implies \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} x_n$ converges in $X$.

Proof

Step 1. ($\Rightarrow$) Assume $X$ is Banach and $\sum \|x_n\| < \infty$. The partial sums $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_n$ form a Cauchy sequence: for $M > N$,

$$\|S_M - S_N\| = \left\|\sum_{n=N+1}^{M} x_n\right\| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^{M} \|x_n\| \to 0$$

as $N \to \infty$ (tail of a convergent series). By completeness, $(S_N)$ converges.

Step 2. ($\Leftarrow$) Assume the criterion holds. Let $(y_n)$ be Cauchy. Extract a subsequence $(y_{n_k})$ with $\|y_{n_{k+1}} - y_{n_k}\| < 2^{-k}$ (possible since $(y_n)$ is Cauchy). Define $x_k = y_{n_{k+1}} - y_{n_k}$. Then $\sum \|x_k\| < \sum 2^{-k} = 1 < \infty$, so $\sum x_k$ converges. Its partial sums are $y_{n_{K+1}} - y_{n_1}$, so the subsequence $(y_{n_k})$ converges. Since the original sequence is Cauchy and has a convergent subsequence, it converges (Phase 9, page 3).

∎

5. Finite-dimensional spaces are always Banach

Theorem

Every finite-dimensional normed vector space is complete (hence Banach).

Proof

Step 1. Let $(X, \|\cdot\|)$ have dimension $n$. Fix a basis $(e_1, \ldots, e_n)$ and define the coordinate isomorphism $\Phi : \mathbb{R}^n \to X$ by $\Phi(\alpha) = \sum \alpha_i e_i$.

Step 2. By norm equivalence (Phase 14, page 1), there exist $c, C > 0$ with $c\|\alpha\|_2 \leq \|\Phi(\alpha)\| \leq C\|\alpha\|_2$.

Step 3. Let $(x_k)$ be Cauchy in $(X, \|\cdot\|)$. Write $x_k = \Phi(\alpha^{(k)})$. Then $c\|\alpha^{(m)} - \alpha^{(n)}\|_2 \leq \|x_m - x_n\|$, so $(\alpha^{(k)})$ is Cauchy in $(\mathbb{R}^n, \|\cdot\|_2)$. Since $\mathbb{R}^n$ is complete, $\alpha^{(k)} \to \alpha$ for some $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

Step 4. Set $x = \Phi(\alpha)$. Then $\|x_k - x\| = \|\Phi(\alpha^{(k)} - \alpha)\| \leq C\|\alpha^{(k)} - \alpha\|_2 \to 0$.

∎
Historical note

Stefan Banach's 1920 doctoral thesis (published 1922) axiomatised complete normed spaces and proved the foundational theorems of the theory that now bears his name. Remarkably, Banach came to mathematics informally: he was largely self-taught, spotted by Hugo Steinhaus who overheard him discussing the Lebesgue integral in a Kraków park in 1916.

His monograph Théorie des opérations linéaires (1932) was the first textbook on functional analysis and remains remarkably modern in its outlook. The Lwów School — Banach, Steinhaus, Mazur, Schauder — worked at the Scottish Café, recording open problems in the legendary "Scottish Book." Many of these problems remained open for decades; some are still unsolved.

The name "Banach space" was coined by Fréchet in 1928. Before that, complete normed spaces were called "spaces of type (B)" — the "(B)" standing for Banach.

What breaks

Without completeness. The Banach fixed-point theorem (Phase 9, page 6) requires completeness: the iterates of a contraction form a Cauchy sequence, and without completeness, the limit may not exist in the space. Operator theory collapses: limits of operator sequences need somewhere to land. Even basic existence results for ODEs (Picard–Lindelöf) rely on completeness of the underlying function space. Completeness is not optional — it is the minimum for doing analysis.

Without the norm. A complete metric space that is not a vector space (e.g. a closed subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$) still has some good properties (Baire category, fixed-point theorems) but lacks the linear structure needed for operator theory. Banach spaces combine both: completeness lets you take limits, linearity lets you add and scale. The combination is what makes a theory of operators possible.

Without absolute convergence criterion. In an incomplete space, you can have $\sum \|x_n\| < \infty$ but $\sum x_n$ diverges. For instance, in $(C([0,1]), \|\cdot\|_1)$, consider a series of bump functions whose partial sums approximate the step function from Section 3. The series is absolutely convergent in $\|\cdot\|_1$ but has no limit in the space of continuous functions. This is not merely a theoretical curiosity — Fourier series of continuous functions can fail to converge uniformly, and the distinction between different notions of convergence is what drives the entire theory of $L^p$ spaces.

A Banach space is a normed vector space where Cauchy sequences converge — the minimum setting for serious analysis.
