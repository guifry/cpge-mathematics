The Uniform Boundedness Principle

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 5

The problem

Consider a family of continuous linear functionals $\{f_\alpha\}$ on a Banach space $X$. Suppose each point $x$ is "not too big" under this family: $\sup_\alpha |f_\alpha(x)| < \infty$ for every $x$. Does this pointwise bound imply a uniform bound $\sup_\alpha \|f_\alpha\| < \infty$?

In general, pointwise bounds say nothing about uniform bounds. But Banach and Steinhaus (1927) proved that in Banach spaces, this miracle happens — pointwise bounded implies uniformly bounded. The secret ingredient is completeness, working through Baire's category theorem.

1. Statement of the Theorem

Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed vector space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ a family of bounded linear operators $T_\alpha \in \mathcal{L}(X, Y)$. Suppose the family is pointwise bounded: for every $x \in X$,

$$\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty.$$
Banach-Steinhaus theorem (uniform boundedness principle)

If $X$ is a Banach space, $Y$ is a normed space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subset \mathcal{L}(X, Y)$ satisfies $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty$ for every $x \in X$, then

$$\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\| < \infty.$$

Read it again: the hypothesis is about each individual point $x$ (every point has a finite supremum, but that supremum could vary wildly with $x$). The conclusion is about the operators collectively — a single finite number bounds all of them simultaneously.

Note what is not assumed: we do not require $A$ to be countable, nor do we assume anything about $Y$ beyond it being normed. The only completeness requirement is on $X$, the domain. Also note that pointwise boundedness is a condition on each individual $x$ — it does not say the bound is the same for every $x$. A priori, $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\|$ could equal $1$ for one $x$ and $10^{100}$ for another.

To appreciate why the theorem is surprising, consider the following finite-dimensional analogy that fails to be surprising. If $T_1, \ldots, T_m$ are finitely many operators, pointwise boundedness trivially implies uniform boundedness (take the max of finitely many norms). The theorem says the same conclusion holds for arbitrary families — possibly uncountable — provided the domain is Banach.

2. Proof via Baire's Category Theorem

The proof rests entirely on the Baire category theorem (Phase 9, page 7): in a complete metric space, a countable union of closed sets with empty interior cannot cover the whole space. Equivalently, if closed sets $F_1, F_2, \ldots$ cover the space, at least one $F_n$ has non-empty interior.

Proof

Step 1. Define the closed sets. For each $n \in \mathbb{N}$, let

$$F_n = \{x \in X : \sup_{\alpha} \|T_\alpha x\| \leq n\}.$$

Each $F_n$ is closed: if $x_k \to x$ with $x_k \in F_n$, then for each $\alpha$, $\|T_\alpha x\| = \lim_k \|T_\alpha x_k\| \leq n$ by continuity of $T_\alpha$, so $x \in F_n$.

Step 2. The union covers $X$. By hypothesis, every $x \in X$ satisfies $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty$, so $x \in F_n$ for some sufficiently large $n$. Hence $X = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} F_n$.

Step 3. Apply Baire. Since $X$ is a Banach space (complete), the Baire category theorem guarantees that some $F_N$ has non-empty interior. So there exist $x_0 \in X$ and $r > 0$ with $B(x_0, r) \subset F_N$.

Step 4. Extract the uniform bound. For any $x \in X$ with $\|x\| \leq 1$, we have $x_0 + rx \in B(x_0, r) \subset F_N$, so $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha(x_0 + rx)\| \leq N$. By the triangle inequality,

$$r\|T_\alpha x\| = \|T_\alpha(rx)\| = \|T_\alpha(x_0 + rx) - T_\alpha x_0\| \leq \|T_\alpha(x_0 + rx)\| + \|T_\alpha x_0\| \leq N + N = 2N.$$

The last inequality uses $x_0 \in F_N$ (so $\|T_\alpha x_0\| \leq N$). Therefore $\|T_\alpha x\| \leq 2N/r$ for all $\alpha$ and all $\|x\| \leq 1$, giving

$$\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| \leq \frac{2N}{r} < \infty.$$
∎

The entire analytic content is in Step 4 — the "ball trick" of centering at $x_0$ and using linearity. Baire's theorem provides the ball; linearity lets us shift it to the origin.

Let us unpack the logic once more. Why does Baire apply? Because $X$ is complete — that is the only place completeness enters. Why can we shift the ball to the origin in Step 4? Because $T_\alpha$ is linear: $T_\alpha(x_0 + rx) = T_\alpha(x_0) + rT_\alpha(x)$. The triangle inequality then gives $r\|T_\alpha x\| \leq \|T_\alpha(x_0 + rx)\| + \|T_\alpha x_0\|$. Both terms are bounded by $N$ (the first because $x_0 + rx \in F_N$, the second because $x_0 \in F_N$).

The constant $2N/r$ in the final bound is not optimal — we have been generous with the triangle inequality. But the existence of some finite bound is what matters. The precise constant is rarely important in applications.

3. Condensation of Singularities

The uniform boundedness principle has a striking contrapositive, known as the principle of condensation of singularities.

Condensation of singularities

If $\{T_\alpha\} \subset \mathcal{L}(X, Y)$ with $X$ Banach satisfies $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$, then the set

$$S = \{x \in X : \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty\}$$

is residual (a countable intersection of dense open sets). In the sense of Baire category, "most" points are singularities.

This is the contrapositive at work: if the family is not uniformly bounded, then the pointwise bound must fail somewhere. Baire tells us it fails not just at one point, but on a comeagre set — the singularities are generic, not exceptional.

To see why $S$ is residual, note that $S = X \setminus \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} F_n$ where $F_n$ is as in the proof. If $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$, then no $F_n$ has interior (if some $F_N$ had interior, the proof above would give a finite uniform bound — contradiction). So each $F_n$ is closed with empty interior, i.e. nowhere dense. Their union is meagre, and $S$ — its complement — is comeagre (residual).

The dichotomy is absolute: either the family is uniformly bounded, or the set where it blows up is "generic" in the topological sense. There is no middle ground — you cannot have unbounded operators that are only pointwise infinite on a "thin" set.

4. Application: Divergence of Fourier Series

Du Bois-Reymond (1876) proved that there exists a continuous function whose Fourier series diverges at a point. Banach-Steinhaus turns this from a hard construction into a soft argument.

The $n$-th partial sum of the Fourier series of $f \in C([-\pi, \pi])$ at $x = 0$ is

$$S_n f(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t) D_n(t)\, dt,$$

where $D_n$ is the Dirichlet kernel (Phase 11, page 2). Each $S_n$ is a bounded linear functional on $C([-\pi, \pi])$ (with the sup norm), and its operator norm is

$$\|S_n\| = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |D_n(t)|\, dt = \|D_n\|_1 / (2\pi).$$

One can show $\|D_n\|_1 \sim \frac{4}{\pi^2}\log n \to \infty$ (the Dirichlet kernel is not an approximate identity — its $L^1$ norm blows up). So $\sup_n \|S_n\| = \infty$.

By the condensation of singularities, the set of continuous functions $f$ for which $\sup_n |S_n f(0)| = \infty$ is residual in $C([-\pi, \pi])$. In particular, it is non-empty. There exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at $0$.

Better still: the set of functions whose Fourier series converges at every point is meagre. "Most" continuous functions (in the Baire category sense) have Fourier series that diverge somewhere.

This is a beautiful example of the condensation principle in action. The "hard" part of du Bois-Reymond's original 1876 proof was constructing one specific pathological function. Banach-Steinhaus shows that no construction is needed — the mere fact that $\|S_n\| \to \infty$ guarantees that divergence occurs, and occurs generically. The existence proof is entirely non-constructive.

The argument generalises: whenever you have a sequence of bounded linear operators on a Banach space whose norms blow up, the condensation principle produces a residual set of "bad" inputs. This meta-argument has been applied to divergence of interpolation, divergence of series expansions in various bases, and unboundedness phenomena in approximation theory.

Historical note

Stefan Banach and Hugo Steinhaus published the uniform boundedness principle in 1927, in their paper on operations in abstract sets. The theorem relies essentially on Baire's category theorem (1899) — completeness is doing all the heavy lifting. Banach recognised that the same Baire-based argument underpins several seemingly different results in analysis, unifying them under one principle.

Du Bois-Reymond's 1876 construction of a continuous function with divergent Fourier series was a shock — Dirichlet had conjectured (1829) that continuity would suffice for convergence. Banach-Steinhaus not only recovers du Bois-Reymond's result but shows divergence is the generic behaviour.

What breaks

Without completeness of $X$: take $X$ to be the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ with the usual absolute value. Define $f_n : \mathbb{Q} \cap [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f_n(x) = nx$. Restricted to any irrational-avoiding subset, these can be pointwise bounded but not uniformly bounded. More precisely, Baire's category theorem fails in incomplete spaces — $\mathbb{Q}$ is a countable union of singletons (closed, empty interior), yet it equals itself. The proof collapses at Step 3.

Without linearity: pointwise bounded families of nonlinear maps need not be uniformly bounded. Consider $f_n : [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f_n(x) = x^n$. For each $x \in [0, 1)$, $\sup_n |f_n(x)| \leq 1$, and $f_n(1) = 1$. So the family is pointwise bounded. But $\sup_n \|f_n\|_\infty = 1$, which is fine — the point is that the linear structure is used in Step 4 to shift the ball to the origin. Without it, the argument has no purchase.

In a Banach space, pointwise bounded families of operators are automatically uniformly bounded — completeness, through Baire, promotes local control to global control.
