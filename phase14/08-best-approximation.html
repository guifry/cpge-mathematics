Best Approximation in Hilbert Spaces

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 8

The problem

Given a point $x$ in a Hilbert space and a closed convex set $C$, is there a unique nearest point in $C$ to $x$? In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the answer is yes — project $x$ onto $C$. But in infinite dimensions, even closed bounded sets need not be compact (Riesz's lemma, page 3), so the infimum of distances might not be attained — a minimising sequence could converge to a point outside $C$.

In Hilbert spaces — complete inner product spaces — the parallelogram law saves the day. It forces minimising sequences to be Cauchy, giving both existence and uniqueness of the best approximation.

1. Hilbert Spaces and the Parallelogram Law

A Hilbert space is a Banach space whose norm comes from an inner product: $\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}$. The inner product gives Hilbert spaces a geometric structure that general Banach spaces lack — angles, orthogonality, and projections all make sense.

Parallelogram law

In any inner product space, for all $x, y$:

$$\|x + y\|^2 + \|x - y\|^2 = 2\|x\|^2 + 2\|y\|^2.$$
Proof

Step 1. Expand $\|x + y\|^2 = \langle x + y, x + y \rangle = \|x\|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle x, y \rangle + \|y\|^2$.

Step 2. Expand $\|x - y\|^2 = \langle x - y, x - y \rangle = \|x\|^2 - 2\operatorname{Re}\langle x, y \rangle + \|y\|^2$.

Step 3. Add: $\|x + y\|^2 + \|x - y\|^2 = 2\|x\|^2 + 2\|y\|^2$. The cross terms cancel.

∎

Geometrically: the sum of the squares of the diagonals of a parallelogram equals the sum of the squares of the four sides. This is a Euclidean identity, and it characterises inner product norms: a norm satisfies the parallelogram law if and only if it comes from an inner product (the von Neumann-Jordan theorem, 1935).

The $\ell^p$ norm on $\mathbb{R}^2$ for $p \neq 2$ violates the parallelogram law. Take $x = (1, 0)$ and $y = (0, 1)$: then $\|x + y\|_p^2 + \|x - y\|_p^2 = 2 \cdot 2^{2/p}$, while $2\|x\|_p^2 + 2\|y\|_p^2 = 4$. These are equal only when $p = 2$.

2. The Projection Theorem

Projection theorem

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space, $C \subset H$ a closed convex set, and $x \in H$. Then there exists a unique $p \in C$ such that

$$\|x - p\| = \inf_{c \in C} \|x - c\| =: d(x, C).$$
Proof

Step 1. Existence. Let $d = \inf_{c \in C}\|x - c\|$. Choose a minimising sequence $(c_n)$ in $C$ with $\|x - c_n\| \to d$.

Step 2. Apply the parallelogram law to $u = x - c_n$ and $v = x - c_m$:

$$\|u + v\|^2 + \|u - v\|^2 = 2\|u\|^2 + 2\|v\|^2,$$

i.e. $\|2x - c_n - c_m\|^2 + \|c_m - c_n\|^2 = 2\|x - c_n\|^2 + 2\|x - c_m\|^2$.

Step 3. Since $C$ is convex, $(c_n + c_m)/2 \in C$, so $\|x - (c_n + c_m)/2\| \geq d$. Therefore $\|2x - c_n - c_m\|^2 = 4\|x - (c_n + c_m)/2\|^2 \geq 4d^2$.

Step 4. Substituting:

$$\|c_m - c_n\|^2 = 2\|x - c_n\|^2 + 2\|x - c_m\|^2 - \|2x - c_n - c_m\|^2 \leq 2\|x - c_n\|^2 + 2\|x - c_m\|^2 - 4d^2.$$

As $n, m \to \infty$, the right side tends to $2d^2 + 2d^2 - 4d^2 = 0$. So $(c_n)$ is Cauchy.

Step 5. Since $H$ is complete and $C$ is closed, $(c_n)$ converges to some $p \in C$ with $\|x - p\| = d$.

Step 6. Uniqueness. Suppose $p, q \in C$ both achieve the minimum $d$. Apply the same parallelogram argument with $c_n = p$ and $c_m = q$ for all $n, m$:

$$\|p - q\|^2 \leq 2d^2 + 2d^2 - 4d^2 = 0,$$

so $p = q$.

∎

The parallelogram law is used twice: once to show the minimising sequence is Cauchy (existence), and once to show two minimisers must coincide (uniqueness). This is why the theorem fails in general Banach spaces — without the parallelogram law, neither step works.

3. Orthogonal Projection onto Closed Subspaces

When the closed convex set $C$ is a closed subspace $M$, the projection has an elegant characterisation in terms of orthogonality.

Orthogonal projection

Let $M$ be a closed subspace of a Hilbert space $H$ and $x \in H$. The nearest point $p \in M$ to $x$ is the unique element satisfying $x - p \perp M$, i.e. $\langle x - p, m \rangle = 0$ for all $m \in M$.

Proof

Step 1. Let $p$ be the best approximation from Section 2. For any $m \in M$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$, $p + tm \in M$, so $\|x - p\|^2 \leq \|x - p - tm\|^2$.

Step 2. Expanding: $\|x - p\|^2 \leq \|x - p\|^2 - 2t\operatorname{Re}\langle x - p, m \rangle + t^2\|m\|^2$.

Step 3. This gives $0 \leq -2t\operatorname{Re}\langle x - p, m \rangle + t^2\|m\|^2$ for all $t$. Setting $t = \operatorname{Re}\langle x - p, m \rangle / \|m\|^2$ forces $\operatorname{Re}\langle x - p, m \rangle = 0$. Replacing $m$ by $im$ (in the complex case) gives the imaginary part too, so $\langle x - p, m \rangle = 0$.

∎

This gives the orthogonal decomposition: every $x \in H$ can be written uniquely as $x = p + (x - p)$ where $p \in M$ and $x - p \in M^\perp$. That is,

$$H = M \oplus M^\perp.$$

This generalises the Gram-Schmidt orthogonal projection from finite dimensions (Phase 6, page 8) to the infinite-dimensional setting. It also connects to conditional expectation in probability (Phase 12, page 8): if $H = L^2(\Omega)$ and $M$ is the subspace of $\sigma$-algebra-measurable functions, the orthogonal projection onto $M$ is precisely the conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[\cdot \mid \mathcal{G}]$.

4. The Riesz Representation Theorem

In a general normed space, the dual $X^*$ can be mysterious — Hahn-Banach (page 7) tells us it is rich, but gives no concrete description of its elements. In a Hilbert space, the dual has a beautifully simple characterisation.

Riesz representation theorem (for Hilbert spaces)

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space. For every bounded linear functional $f \in H^*$, there exists a unique $y \in H$ such that

$$f(x) = \langle x, y \rangle \quad \text{for all } x \in H.$$

Moreover, $\|f\| = \|y\|$.

Proof

Step 1. If $f = 0$, take $y = 0$. Otherwise, let $M = \ker(f)$. Since $f$ is continuous, $M$ is a closed subspace of $H$, and $M \neq H$ (since $f \neq 0$).

Step 2. By the orthogonal decomposition, $M^\perp \neq \{0\}$. Pick any $z \in M^\perp$ with $z \neq 0$.

Step 3. For any $x \in H$, the element $x - \frac{f(x)}{f(z)}z$ lies in $\ker(f) = M$. So $\langle x - \frac{f(x)}{f(z)}z, z \rangle = 0$, giving

$$\langle x, z \rangle = \frac{f(x)}{f(z)}\|z\|^2, \quad \text{hence} \quad f(x) = \left\langle x, \frac{\overline{f(z)}}{\|z\|^2}z\right\rangle.$$

Step 4. Set $y = \frac{\overline{f(z)}}{\|z\|^2}z$. Then $f(x) = \langle x, y \rangle$ for all $x$.

Step 5. Uniqueness: if $\langle x, y_1 \rangle = \langle x, y_2 \rangle$ for all $x$, then $\langle x, y_1 - y_2 \rangle = 0$ for all $x$; setting $x = y_1 - y_2$ gives $y_1 = y_2$.

Step 6. Norm: $|f(x)| = |\langle x, y \rangle| \leq \|x\|\,\|y\|$ by Cauchy-Schwarz, so $\|f\| \leq \|y\|$. Equality is achieved at $x = y/\|y\|$.

∎

The Riesz representation theorem says $H^* \cong H$ (isometrically, via the conjugate-linear map $y \mapsto \langle \cdot, y \rangle$). A Hilbert space is its own dual. This is a luxury that general Banach spaces do not enjoy.

5. Phase 14 Recap

Eight pages have built functional analysis from the ground up. Here is the arc:

Pages 1-2: Norms and equivalence. All norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent — a finite-dimensional luxury. In infinite dimensions, different norms give genuinely different topologies.

Page 3: Riesz's lemma and the failure of compactness. The closed unit ball is compact if and only if the space is finite-dimensional. This is why infinite-dimensional analysis is hard.

Page 4: Bounded linear maps. Continuity equals boundedness for linear maps. The operator norm makes $\mathcal{L}(X, Y)$ a normed space — Banach when $Y$ is Banach.

Pages 5-6: The Baire-powered theorems. Banach-Steinhaus (pointwise bounded implies uniformly bounded), the open mapping theorem (surjective bounded maps are open), and the closed graph theorem (closed graph implies bounded). All three require completeness, all three use Baire.

Page 7: Hahn-Banach. Bounded functionals extend without norm inflation. Uses Zorn's lemma, not Baire. Does not require completeness. Makes the dual space rich.

Page 8: Hilbert spaces. The inner product gives geometry — orthogonality, projections, best approximation. The parallelogram law rescues existence and uniqueness. The Riesz representation theorem identifies $H^*$ with $H$.

The grand unifying theme: infinite-dimensional spaces require new tools because finite-dimensional intuition fails — compactness breaks, norms diverge, bases are uncountable. But completeness (Baire) and the inner product structure (parallelogram law) restore enough geometry to do analysis.

Historical note

David Hilbert developed the theory of integral equations between 1904 and 1910, working with what we now call $\ell^2$ — sequences of square-summable reals. The abstract axioms for Hilbert spaces were given by John von Neumann in 1929, who recognised that the inner product structure (not the specific representation as sequences or functions) was the essential feature.

Frigyes Riesz proved the representation theorem in 1907 for $L^2$ spaces, before the abstract framework existed. The projection theorem generalises the finite-dimensional Gram-Schmidt orthogonalisation (Phase 6, page 8) to infinite dimensions — the same geometric idea, but completeness is now needed to ensure the projected point exists.

What breaks

Without completeness: in an incomplete inner product space, the infimum of $\|x - c\|$ over $c \in C$ may not be attained. The minimising sequence is still Cauchy (the parallelogram law holds in any inner product space), but it may converge to a point outside the space. For example, in the space of polynomials on $[0, 1]$ with the $L^2$ inner product, the best $L^2$ approximation to a non-polynomial continuous function does not exist within the space.

Without convexity: in a non-convex set, the nearest point may not be unique. Consider the unit circle $S^1$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and the origin: every point on $S^1$ is equally close. The convexity hypothesis is used in Step 3 of the existence proof to ensure midpoints stay in $C$.

Without the inner product (in a general Banach space): best approximation may exist but need not be unique. In $\ell^\infty$, the nearest point in a closed subspace to a given point can be non-unique — the parallelogram law fails, so the Cauchy argument for uniqueness breaks down. Strict convexity of the norm suffices for uniqueness, but general Banach spaces lack it.

In a Hilbert space, every closed convex set has a unique nearest point — the parallelogram law makes minimising sequences Cauchy, and completeness finishes the job.
