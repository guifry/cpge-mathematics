Norms and Equivalence in Finite Dimension

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 1

The problem

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, there are many ways to measure "distance." The Euclidean distance ($\ell^2$) comes from the Pythagorean theorem. The taxicab distance ($\ell^1$) adds coordinate-wise absolute values. The maximum coordinate ($\ell^\infty$) takes the largest absolute component. Does the choice matter?

In finite dimensions, Heine (1870s) and others showed it does not — all norms are equivalent. Convergence, continuity, compactness are all norm-independent. But in infinite dimensions, this fails spectacularly: different norms produce genuinely different topologies. Understanding when and why norms agree is the gateway to functional analysis.

1. Norms and normed vector spaces

We have been working with distances since Phase 9: the absolute value $|x - y|$ measures distance on $\mathbb{R}$, and the Euclidean distance $\sqrt{\sum (x_i - y_i)^2}$ does the same on $\mathbb{R}^n$. But a distance function is a property of pairs of points. A norm measures the "size" of a single vector, and distance then comes for free: $d(x, y) = \|x - y\|$.

Let $V$ be a real vector space. A norm on $V$ is a function $\|\cdot\| : V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying three axioms for all $x, y \in V$ and $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$:

Definition — Norm

(N1) Positive definiteness: $\|x\| \geq 0$, with $\|x\| = 0$ iff $x = 0$.

(N2) Absolute homogeneity: $\|\lambda x\| = |\lambda| \, \|x\|$.

(N3) Triangle inequality: $\|x + y\| \leq \|x\| + \|y\|$.

A pair $(V, \|\cdot\|)$ is called a normed vector space.

The three standard norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$:

$$\|x\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i|, \qquad \|x\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2}, \qquad \|x\|_\infty = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |x_i|$$

Let us verify (N1)–(N3) for $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ as an example. Positive definiteness: $\max |x_i| \geq 0$, and $\max |x_i| = 0$ iff every $x_i = 0$ iff $x = 0$. Homogeneity: $\max |\lambda x_i| = |\lambda| \max |x_i|$. Triangle inequality: for each $i$, $|x_i + y_i| \leq |x_i| + |y_i| \leq \max_j |x_j| + \max_j |y_j|$, so taking the max over $i$ gives $\|x + y\|_\infty \leq \|x\|_\infty + \|y\|_\infty$. The verification for $\|\cdot\|_1$ is similar.

For $\|\cdot\|_2$, the triangle inequality requires the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality (Phase 13, page 1): $\|x + y\|_2^2 = \|x\|_2^2 + 2\langle x, y\rangle + \|y\|_2^2 \leq \|x\|_2^2 + 2\|x\|_2\|y\|_2 + \|y\|_2^2 = (\|x\|_2 + \|y\|_2)^2$.

2. A norm induces a metric

Given a norm $\|\cdot\|$ on $V$, define $d(x, y) = \|x - y\|$. This is a metric: positivity and symmetry are immediate from (N1)–(N2), and the triangle inequality for $d$ follows from (N3). So every normed vector space is automatically a metric space, and all concepts from Phase 9 — open sets, closed sets, convergence, completeness, compactness — apply directly.

The open ball of radius $r$ centred at $a$ is $B(a, r) = \{x \in V : \|x - a\| < r\}$. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the unit ball $B(0, 1)$ is a disc for $\|\cdot\|_2$, a diamond for $\|\cdot\|_1$, and a square for $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. Different shapes — but as we shall prove, the same topology.

3. Norm equivalence

Definition — Equivalent norms

Two norms $\|\cdot\|_a$ and $\|\cdot\|_b$ on $V$ are equivalent, written $\|\cdot\|_a \sim \|\cdot\|_b$, if there exist constants $c, C > 0$ such that

$$c\,\|x\|_a \leq \|x\|_b \leq C\,\|x\|_a \qquad \text{for all } x \in V.$$

This is an equivalence relation: reflexivity ($c = C = 1$), symmetry (swap the inequalities and invert the constants), and transitivity (compose the inequalities and multiply the constants) are all immediate.

If $\|\cdot\|_a \sim \|\cdot\|_b$, then a sequence converges in one norm iff it converges in the other. To see this: suppose $x_k \to x$ in $\|\cdot\|_a$, meaning $\|x_k - x\|_a \to 0$. Then $\|x_k - x\|_b \leq C\|x_k - x\|_a \to 0$, so $x_k \to x$ in $\|\cdot\|_b$. The reverse follows from $\|x_k - x\|_a \leq (1/c)\|x_k - x\|_b$.

Likewise, open sets, continuity, and completeness are preserved. Equivalent norms generate the same topology. This is why the equivalence relation matters: it tells us when two norms give the "same" analysis.

4. Theorem: all norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent

Theorem — Norm equivalence in finite dimension

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional real vector space of dimension $n$. Then any two norms on $V$ are equivalent.

Proof

Fix a basis $(e_1, \ldots, e_n)$ and identify $V$ with $\mathbb{R}^n$. It suffices to show that every norm $\|\cdot\|$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is equivalent to $\|\cdot\|_2$ (equivalence is transitive).

Step 1. Every norm is Lipschitz with respect to $\|\cdot\|_2$. Write $x = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i e_i$. By the triangle inequality and homogeneity:

$$\|x\| = \left\|\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i e_i\right\| \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i| \, \|e_i\| \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \|e_i\|^2\right)^{1/2} \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2\right)^{1/2}$$

by Cauchy–Schwarz. Setting $C = \left(\sum \|e_i\|^2\right)^{1/2}$, we get $\|x\| \leq C \|x\|_2$.

Step 2. Similarly, $\bigl|\|x\| - \|y\|\bigr| \leq \|x - y\| \leq C\|x - y\|_2$ by the reverse triangle inequality. So $\|\cdot\|$ is continuous as a function $(\mathbb{R}^n, \|\cdot\|_2) \to \mathbb{R}$.

Step 3. The unit sphere $S = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : \|x\|_2 = 1\}$ is closed and bounded in $(\mathbb{R}^n, \|\cdot\|_2)$, hence compact by Heine–Borel (Phase 9, page 4).

Step 4. By the extreme value theorem (Phase 9, page 5), $\|\cdot\|$ attains a minimum $c > 0$ and a maximum $C'$ on $S$. We have $c > 0$ because $\|x\| = 0$ only for $x = 0$, which is not in $S$.

Step 5. For any $x \neq 0$, the unit vector $u = x / \|x\|_2$ lies in $S$, so $c \leq \|u\| \leq C'$. Multiplying by $\|x\|_2$:

$$c\,\|x\|_2 \leq \|x\| \leq C'\,\|x\|_2.$$

This holds trivially for $x = 0$ as well. Thus $\|\cdot\| \sim \|\cdot\|_2$.

∎

5. Consequences

In $\mathbb{R}^n$ (or any finite-dimensional real vector space), there is only one notion of:

Convergence. A sequence $x_k \to x$ in one norm iff it converges in every norm. The equivalence constants may differ, but zero-distance is zero-distance.

Continuity. A function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is continuous with respect to one norm iff it is continuous with respect to all norms.

Open/closed sets. The topology is unique. An open set is open for every norm.

Completeness. Since $(\mathbb{R}^n, \|\cdot\|_2)$ is complete (Phase 9, page 3), every norm on $\mathbb{R}^n$ yields a complete space.

Compactness. Heine–Borel (Phase 9, page 4) holds for all norms: a set is compact iff it is closed and bounded, and since equivalent norms agree on which sets are bounded and which are closed, compactness is norm-independent.

This is profoundly reassuring: in finite dimensions, the choice of norm is a matter of convenience, not substance. When computing, pick whichever norm makes the algebra easiest — the results will be the same. The next three pages will show that in infinite dimensions, this comfort vanishes entirely.

A concrete example of the equivalence constants: on $\mathbb{R}^n$, the three standard norms satisfy

$$\|x\|_\infty \leq \|x\|_2 \leq \|x\|_1 \leq n\,\|x\|_\infty \leq \sqrt{n}\,\|x\|_2$$

(the first two are immediate; the third uses $|x_i| \leq \max_j |x_j|$ summed $n$ times; the fourth uses Cauchy–Schwarz on $\sum |x_i| \leq \sqrt{n}\,\|x\|_2$). The constants depend on $n$ but are always finite — in infinite dimensions, such constants would blow up.

Historical note

The abstract concept of a norm was introduced by Frigyes Riesz in 1918, though specific norms had been used implicitly for decades. The equivalence of norms in finite dimensions was known to Heine and Weierstrass through their work on uniform convergence and compactness. The dramatic failure of equivalence in infinite dimensions was a driving force behind Banach's programme to axiomatise functional analysis in the 1920s.

What breaks

In infinite dimensions. Consider $C([0,1])$, the space of continuous functions on $[0, 1]$. Define two norms:

$$\|f\|_1 = \int_0^1 |f(x)|\,dx, \qquad \|f\|_\infty = \max_{x \in [0,1]} |f(x)|.$$

Take $f_n(x) = x^n$. Then $\|f_n\|_\infty = 1$ for every $n$ (the maximum is at $x = 1$). But $\|f_n\|_1 = \frac{1}{n+1} \to 0$. So $f_n \to 0$ in $\|\cdot\|_1$ but $f_n \not\to 0$ in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. The two norms are not equivalent. In infinite dimensions, the choice of norm changes which sequences converge, which sets are open, and which spaces are complete. Norm choice is no longer cosmetic — it is structural.

In finite dimensions all norms are equivalent — topology is unique; in infinite dimensions, the choice of norm changes everything.
