The Hahn-Banach Theorem

Phase 14 — Normed Vector Spaces, Page 7

The problem

You have a bounded linear functional $f$ defined on a subspace $Y$ of a normed space $X$, and you want to extend it to all of $X$ without increasing its norm. In finite dimensions, this is trivial — extend a basis for $Y$ to a basis for $X$ and define $f$ arbitrarily on the new basis vectors, then rescale.

In infinite dimensions, it is far from obvious that such an extension exists. You cannot enumerate a basis, and there is no reason a priori why the norm should be preservable. Hahn (1927) and Banach (1929) proved it can always be done — using Zorn's lemma. This is the only one of the "big three" functional analysis theorems that does not require completeness.

1. Statement (Real Case)

Hahn-Banach theorem (normed space version)

Let $Y$ be a subspace of a real normed space $X$, and let $f : Y \to \mathbb{R}$ be a bounded linear functional with $\|f\|_Y = M$. Then there exists a bounded linear functional $\tilde{f} : X \to \mathbb{R}$ such that:

(i) $\tilde{f}|_Y = f$ (it extends $f$), and

(ii) $\|\tilde{f}\|_X = M$ (it preserves the norm).

The extension agrees with $f$ on $Y$ and has the same operator norm over the larger space $X$. No information is lost, and no norm inflation occurs.

Notice what is not required: $X$ need not be complete. This is the crucial difference from Banach-Steinhaus (page 5) and the open mapping theorem (page 6), which both require Banach spaces. Hahn-Banach works for any normed space — the price is that it uses Zorn's lemma instead of Baire's category theorem.

There is also a more general "sublinear" version: if $p : X \to \mathbb{R}$ is a sublinear functional (positively homogeneous and subadditive) and $f \leq p$ on $Y$, then $\tilde{f} \leq p$ on all of $X$. The normed space version above is the special case $p(x) = M\|x\|$.

2. The One-Dimensional Extension

The analytic heart of the theorem is extending $f$ from $Y$ to $Y \oplus \mathbb{R}z$, where $z \in X \setminus Y$. Every element of $Y + \mathbb{R}z$ has the form $y + tz$ for unique $y \in Y$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$, and we must choose a value $c = \tilde{f}(z)$ such that

$$|\tilde{f}(y + tz)| = |f(y) + tc| \leq M\|y + tz\| \quad \text{for all } y \in Y,\, t \in \mathbb{R}.$$

Dividing by $|t|$ (for $t \neq 0$), this reduces to: for all $y \in Y$,

$$|f(y) + c| \leq M\|y + z\|.$$

This means $-f(y) - M\|y + z\| \leq c \leq -f(y) + M\|y + z\|$ for all $y$, i.e.

$$\sup_{y \in Y}\big(-f(y) - M\|y + z\|\big) \leq c \leq \inf_{y \in Y}\big(-f(y) + M\|y + z\|\big).$$
Proof (interval is non-empty)

Step 1. We must show the supremum on the left does not exceed the infimum on the right. For any $y_1, y_2 \in Y$,

$$f(y_1) - f(y_2) = f(y_1 - y_2) \leq M\|y_1 - y_2\|.$$

Step 2. By the triangle inequality,

$$\|y_1 - y_2\| = \|(y_1 + z) - (y_2 + z)\| \leq \|y_1 + z\| + \|y_2 + z\|.$$

Step 3. Combining: $f(y_1) - f(y_2) \leq M(\|y_1 + z\| + \|y_2 + z\|)$, which rearranges to

$$-f(y_1) - M\|y_1 + z\| \leq -f(y_2) + M\|y_2 + z\|.$$

Since this holds for all $y_1, y_2$, the supremum over $y_1$ of the left side is at most the infimum over $y_2$ of the right side. The interval of valid choices for $c$ is non-empty.

∎

Any $c$ in this interval gives a norm-preserving extension to $Y + \mathbb{R}z$. The one-dimensional case contains all the analytic content of the theorem — what remains is a maximality argument.

Note that the choice of $c$ is typically not unique — the interval can have positive length. This means the norm-preserving extension is generally not unique. In Hilbert spaces, the Riesz representation theorem (page 8) pins down the extension uniquely, but in general Banach spaces, there can be many different extensions with the same norm.

3. The Full Theorem via Zorn's Lemma

To pass from the one-dimensional extension to all of $X$, consider the set $\mathcal{P}$ of all norm-preserving extensions of $f$ to subspaces of $X$ containing $Y$. Order $\mathcal{P}$ by extension: $(g_1, Z_1) \leq (g_2, Z_2)$ iff $Z_1 \subset Z_2$ and $g_2|_{Z_1} = g_1$.

Every chain in $\mathcal{P}$ has an upper bound (take the union of domains and define the functional in the obvious way). By Zorn's lemma, $\mathcal{P}$ has a maximal element $(\tilde{f}, Z)$. If $Z \neq X$, pick $z \in X \setminus Z$ and use Section 2 to extend $\tilde{f}$ to $Z + \mathbb{R}z$, contradicting maximality. So $Z = X$.

The transfinite argument via Zorn's lemma is conceptually simple but logically subtle — it invokes the axiom of choice. We state it without further elaboration; the analytic work was done in Section 2.

4. Consequences

The Hahn-Banach theorem has far-reaching consequences for the structure of normed spaces.

Existence of norming functionals

For any $x \in X$ with $x \neq 0$, there exists $f \in X^*$ with $\|f\| = 1$ and $f(x) = \|x\|$.

Proof

Step 1. Define $g : \mathbb{R}x \to \mathbb{R}$ by $g(tx) = t\|x\|$. Then $g$ is linear and $|g(tx)| = |t|\,\|x\| = \|tx\|$, so $\|g\| = 1$.

Step 2. By Hahn-Banach, extend $g$ to $f : X \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\|f\| = 1$. Then $f(x) = g(x) = \|x\|$.

∎

This simple consequence is enormously useful. It says the dual space $X^*$ is "rich enough" to detect the norm of every element.

Further consequences

(a) Separation of points: if $f(x) = f(y)$ for all $f \in X^*$, then $x = y$. (The dual separates points.)

(b) Norm recovery: $\|x\| = \sup\{|f(x)| : f \in X^*,\, \|f\| \leq 1\}$ for all $x \in X$.

Part (b) says the norm is determined by the dual. You can compute $\|x\|$ by testing $x$ against all bounded linear functionals — and the supremum is attained (by the norming functional above).

To prove (a): if $x \neq y$, apply the norming functional to $x - y$ to get $f \in X^*$ with $f(x - y) = \|x - y\| > 0$, hence $f(x) \neq f(y)$. To prove (b): the inequality $|f(x)| \leq \|f\|\,\|x\| \leq \|x\|$ for $\|f\| \leq 1$ gives $\sup |f(x)| \leq \|x\|$. The norming functional achieves $f(x) = \|x\|$, giving equality.

These consequences mean the dual space $X^*$ completely determines the geometry of $X$. No element is "invisible" to the dual — every non-zero vector is detected by some bounded linear functional. This is far from obvious in infinite dimensions, where the dual space can be enormously large and complicated.

5. Geometric Hahn-Banach: Separation of Convex Sets

The Hahn-Banach theorem has a geometric form that is the foundation of convex optimisation and mathematical economics.

Separation theorem

Let $C$ be a closed convex subset of a normed space $X$, and let $x_0 \notin C$. Then there exists $f \in X^*$ and $\gamma \in \mathbb{R}$ such that

$$f(x_0) > \gamma \geq f(c) \quad \text{for all } c \in C.$$

That is, the hyperplane $\{x : f(x) = \gamma\}$ strictly separates $x_0$ from $C$.

The idea: the distance from $x_0$ to $C$ is positive (since $C$ is closed and $x_0 \notin C$). Construct a functional via Hahn-Banach that "witnesses" this separation. In finite dimensions, this is the supporting hyperplane theorem of convex geometry. In infinite dimensions, it requires Hahn-Banach to produce the functional.

The separation theorem is the bridge between abstract functional analysis and applied optimisation. In convex programming, the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions can be derived from separation: if a point is optimal, no descent direction exists, and this is witnessed by a separating hyperplane (the Lagrange multiplier). The duality theory of linear programming also rests on separation of convex sets.

More broadly, the geometric Hahn-Banach theorem tells us that closed convex sets in normed spaces are "determined" by the bounded linear functionals — a closed convex set is the intersection of the closed half-spaces containing it. This is the infinite-dimensional analogue of the fact that a convex polytope is the intersection of finitely many half-planes.

Historical note

Hans Hahn proved a version for specific function spaces in 1927. Banach gave the general formulation in 1929 in his book on linear operations. Unlike the other two pillars of functional analysis (Banach-Steinhaus and the open mapping theorem, pages 5-6), Hahn-Banach uses the axiom of choice (through Zorn's lemma) rather than Baire's category theorem.

This logical distinction matters: there exist models of set theory (without the axiom of choice) where Hahn-Banach fails. In contrast, Baire's theorem is provable in ZF alone. The big three of functional analysis thus rest on two different foundational pillars: Baire (for Banach-Steinhaus and open mapping) and Zorn (for Hahn-Banach).

What breaks

Without the norm condition: extensions of linear functionals always exist by pure linear algebra (extend a Hamel basis). The hard part is controlling the norm. Without the norm-preservation requirement, the theorem is trivial; with it, the theorem is deep.

Without Zorn's lemma (in non-separable spaces): for separable $X$, the subspace $Y$ has countable codimension (up to closure issues), and a sequential induction suffices — no axiom of choice is needed. For non-separable spaces, Zorn's lemma is genuinely required; the theorem is independent of ZF set theory without choice.

Without linearity: there is no general norm-preserving extension theorem for nonlinear functionals. The key inequality in Section 2 uses $f(y_1) - f(y_2) = f(y_1 - y_2)$, which is linearity. Without it, the interval of valid extensions can be empty.

Every bounded linear functional on a subspace extends to the whole space with the same norm — the dual is always rich enough.
