The Cayley–Hamilton Theorem

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 10

The problem

The characteristic polynomial of a matrix $A$ is $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$, a polynomial in the scalar $\lambda$. Here is a strange question: what happens if you plug the matrix $A$ itself into its own characteristic polynomial? Cayley (1858) tried it for $2 \times 2$ matrices and always got the zero matrix. Hamilton had encountered the same phenomenon with quaternions (1853). Is it always true?

1. The wrong proof

Before giving the real proof, we must kill a tempting but fatally flawed argument. You might think: $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$, so just set $\lambda = A$ to get $\chi_A(A) = \det(A - AI) = \det(0) = 0$. Done?

No. This is nonsense, for a precise reason. The expression $\det(A - \lambda I)$ treats $\lambda$ as a scalar. When you substitute $\lambda = A$, the expression $A - AI$ is well-defined and equals the zero matrix, so $\det(0) = 0$. But $0$ here is the number zero, not the zero matrix. You have proved $0 = 0$, which is useless. The claim $\chi_A(A) = 0$ means the $n \times n$ zero matrix, not the number zero. Substituting a matrix into a determinant is an illegal operation — the determinant is defined for a matrix argument and returns a scalar, not a matrix.

The correct proof requires a completely different approach.

2. The theorem

Theorem — Cayley–Hamilton

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix over a field $K$. Let $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = c_0 + c_1 \lambda + c_2 \lambda^2 + \cdots + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + (-1)^n \lambda^n$ be its characteristic polynomial. Then

$$\chi_A(A) = c_0 I + c_1 A + c_2 A^2 + \cdots + (-1)^n A^n = 0.$$

3. Example first

Take $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 1. Characteristic polynomial: $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda & 2 \\ 3 & 4 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1 - \lambda)(4 - \lambda) - 6 = \lambda^2 - 5\lambda - 2$.

Step 2. Compute $A^2 - 5A - 2I$:

$$A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 10 \\ 15 & 22 \end{pmatrix}.$$
$$A^2 - 5A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 10 \\ 15 & 22 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 10 \\ 15 & 20 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \checkmark$$

4. Proof

Proof

Step 1 (the adjugate). The adjugate (classical adjoint) of a matrix $M$ satisfies $M \cdot \text{adj}(M) = \det(M) \cdot I$. Apply this to $M = A - \lambda I$:

$$(A - \lambda I) \cdot \text{adj}(A - \lambda I) = \det(A - \lambda I) \cdot I = \chi_A(\lambda) \cdot I.$$

Step 2 (expand the adjugate as a polynomial in $\lambda$). The entries of $\text{adj}(A - \lambda I)$ are cofactors of $A - \lambda I$. Each cofactor is a determinant of an $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ submatrix whose entries are polynomials of degree $\leq 1$ in $\lambda$. So each cofactor is a polynomial of degree $\leq n-1$ in $\lambda$. We can write

$$\text{adj}(A - \lambda I) = B_0 + B_1 \lambda + B_2 \lambda^2 + \cdots + B_{n-1} \lambda^{n-1}$$

where $B_0, B_1, \ldots, B_{n-1}$ are constant $n \times n$ matrices (independent of $\lambda$).

Step 3 (expand both sides). Write the characteristic polynomial as $\chi_A(\lambda) = c_0 + c_1 \lambda + \cdots + c_n \lambda^n$ where $c_n = (-1)^n$. The right side of our identity is:

$$\chi_A(\lambda) \cdot I = c_0 I + c_1 I \lambda + c_2 I \lambda^2 + \cdots + c_n I \lambda^n.$$

The left side is:

$$(A - \lambda I)(B_0 + B_1 \lambda + \cdots + B_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}).$$

Expanding and collecting powers of $\lambda$:

$$AB_0 + (AB_1 - B_0)\lambda + (AB_2 - B_1)\lambda^2 + \cdots + (AB_{n-1} - B_{n-2})\lambda^{n-1} + (-B_{n-1})\lambda^n.$$

Step 4 (equate coefficients). Matching coefficients of each power of $\lambda$ gives $n + 1$ matrix equations:

$$\begin{aligned} \lambda^0: & \quad AB_0 = c_0 I \\ \lambda^1: & \quad AB_1 - B_0 = c_1 I \\ \lambda^2: & \quad AB_2 - B_1 = c_2 I \\ & \quad \vdots \\ \lambda^{n-1}: & \quad AB_{n-1} - B_{n-2} = c_{n-1} I \\ \lambda^n: & \quad -B_{n-1} = c_n I \end{aligned}$$

Step 5 (the telescoping trick). Multiply the $\lambda^0$ equation by $I$ on the left, the $\lambda^1$ equation by $A$, the $\lambda^2$ equation by $A^2$, and in general the $\lambda^k$ equation by $A^k$:

$$\begin{aligned} AB_0 &= c_0 I \\ A^2 B_1 - AB_0 &= c_1 A \\ A^3 B_2 - A^2 B_1 &= c_2 A^2 \\ &\;\vdots \\ A^n B_{n-1} - A^{n-1} B_{n-2} &= c_{n-1} A^{n-1} \\ -A^n B_{n-1} &= c_n A^n \end{aligned}$$

Step 6 (sum). Add all $n + 1$ equations. On the left, every $B_k$ term appears once with a positive sign and once with a negative sign — they all cancel (a telescoping sum). On the right we get:

$$0 = c_0 I + c_1 A + c_2 A^2 + \cdots + c_n A^n = \chi_A(A).$$
∎

5. Consequences

Powers of $A$ are controlled. Since $\chi_A(A) = 0$ and $\chi_A$ has degree $n$, we can express $A^n$ as a linear combination of $I, A, A^2, \ldots, A^{n-1}$. By induction, the same holds for $A^k$ for any $k \geq n$. The set $\{I, A, A^2, \ldots, A^{n-1}\}$ spans all powers of $A$.

The minimal polynomial. There may be a polynomial of degree less than $n$ that annihilates $A$. The monic polynomial $m_A$ of smallest degree with $m_A(A) = 0$ is called the minimal polynomial. Cayley–Hamilton tells us $m_A$ divides $\chi_A$ (since $\chi_A$ is an annihilating polynomial and $m_A$ is the smallest). The roots of $m_A$ are exactly the eigenvalues of $A$.

An explicit formula for $A^{-1}$. If $A$ is invertible, then $c_0 = \det(A) \neq 0$. From $\chi_A(A) = 0$:

$$c_0 I + c_1 A + \cdots + c_n A^n = 0.$$

Multiply both sides by $A^{-1}$:

$$c_0 A^{-1} + c_1 I + c_2 A + \cdots + c_n A^{n-1} = 0$$
$$A^{-1} = -\frac{1}{c_0}(c_1 I + c_2 A + \cdots + c_n A^{n-1}).$$

6. Example: computing $A^{-1}$ via Cayley–Hamilton

For $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ with $\chi_A(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - 5\lambda - 2$, we have $A^2 - 5A - 2I = 0$, so:

$$A^2 = 5A + 2I, \qquad A(A - 5I) = 2I, \qquad A \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}(A - 5I) = I.$$

Therefore $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}(A - 5I) = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} -4 & 2 \\ 3 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3/2 & -1/2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Quick check: $\det(A) = 4 - 6 = -2$, so $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-2}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3/2 & -1/2 \end{pmatrix}$. $\checkmark$

7. A $3 \times 3$ example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. The characteristic polynomial is:

$$\chi_A(\lambda) = \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -\lambda & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & -\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1 - \lambda)(\lambda^2 + 1) = -\lambda^3 + \lambda^2 - \lambda + 1.$$

Cayley–Hamilton predicts $-A^3 + A^2 - A + I = 0$. Let us verify.

$$A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A^3 = A \cdot A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$
$$-A^3 + A^2 - A + I = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \checkmark$$

Notice that this matrix has eigenvalues $1, i, -i$. Over $\mathbb{R}$, the minimal polynomial is the same as the characteristic polynomial (none of the factors can be dropped). But for a matrix like $I_3$, the characteristic polynomial is $(\lambda - 1)^3$ while the minimal polynomial is just $\lambda - 1$ — Cayley–Hamilton gives the weaker statement.

Historical note

Cayley (1858) verified the theorem for $2 \times 2$ and $3 \times 3$ matrices and stated it in general, but did not give a complete proof — he wrote that he had "not thought it necessary to undertake the labour of a formal proof." Hamilton (1853) had proved the analogous result for quaternions, which amounts to a $4 \times 4$ case with special structure. Frobenius (1878) gave the first rigorous proof for general matrices. The theorem bridges the two central objects of linear algebra: the characteristic polynomial (an algebraic invariant encoding eigenvalue information) and the matrix algebra (powers and polynomials in $A$).

What breaks

The naive substitution $\lambda = A$ in $\det(A - \lambda I)$. This is the most common error. It conflates scalars and matrices. The determinant function $\det : M_n(K) \to K$ maps matrices to scalars. The expression $\det(A - \lambda I)$ is a scalar polynomial in $\lambda$. Setting $\lambda = A$ inside the determinant produces $\det(0) = 0$ (a scalar), not $\chi_A(A) = 0$ (a matrix equation). The correct proof avoids the determinant entirely after extracting the adjugate identity, working instead with polynomial coefficients of matrices.

Cayley–Hamilton does not say $\chi_A$ is the minimal polynomial. For a diagonal matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is $(\lambda - 2)^2$, but the minimal polynomial is just $\lambda - 2$. Cayley–Hamilton gives an upper bound on the degree of the minimal polynomial, not an exact value.

Every matrix is a root of its own characteristic polynomial — proved not by naive substitution, but by a careful telescoping argument through the adjugate.
