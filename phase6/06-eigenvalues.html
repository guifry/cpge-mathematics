Eigenvalues and the Characteristic Polynomial

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 6

The problem

A linear map transforms space — it stretches, rotates, shears. But are there special directions that the map merely stretches without rotating? A direction $v$ such that $Av = \lambda v$ — the map just scales $v$ by $\lambda$. These eigenvectors simplify everything: if you can find a basis of eigenvectors, the map becomes diagonal — just scaling along independent axes. Matrix powers, exponentials, and differential equations all become trivial.

1. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

Definition — Eigenvalue and eigenvector

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix (or a linear map $V \to V$). A scalar $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ if there exists a nonzero vector $v$ such that $Av = \lambda v$. The vector $v$ is an eigenvector associated to $\lambda$.

The requirement $v \neq 0$ is essential. If we allowed $v = 0$, then $A \cdot 0 = \lambda \cdot 0$ for every $\lambda$ — every scalar would be an "eigenvalue," making the concept vacuous.

Geometrically, an eigenvector is a direction that the map preserves (possibly reversing it if $\lambda < 0$ or collapsing it if $\lambda = 0$). The eigenvalue $\lambda$ is the scaling factor along that direction.

2. Finding eigenvalues

The equation $Av = \lambda v$ rearranges to $(A - \lambda I)v = 0$. We need a nonzero solution $v$, which means $\ker(A - \lambda I) \neq \{0\}$. By the invertibility criterion (page 5), this happens precisely when

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0.$$

So finding eigenvalues reduces to finding roots of a polynomial.

3. The characteristic polynomial

Definition — Characteristic polynomial

The characteristic polynomial of $A \in M_n(\mathbb{K})$ is

$$\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I).$$

It is a polynomial of degree $n$ in $\lambda$, with leading term $(-\lambda)^n$ (equivalently $(-1)^n \lambda^n$). Its roots are exactly the eigenvalues of $A$.

Example. Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. Then

$$\chi_A(\lambda) = \det \begin{pmatrix} 2 - \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (2 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - 0 = \lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6 = (\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 3).$$

The eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 2$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$.

4. Eigenvalue existence

Over $\mathbb{C}$, the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra guarantees that every polynomial of degree $n \geq 1$ has at least one root. (IOU: this will be proved in Phase 10 or accepted as a deep result of complex analysis.) Therefore every $n \times n$ complex matrix has at least one eigenvalue.

Over $\mathbb{R}$, eigenvalues might not exist. A $90°$ rotation in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has no real eigenvectors — no direction is preserved or reversed. We will see this concretely in the break-box.

5. Eigenspaces and multiplicities

Definition — Eigenspace, multiplicities

The eigenspace of $\lambda$ is $E_\lambda = \ker(A - \lambda I)$. It is a subspace of $V$ (closed under addition and scalar multiplication, as any kernel is).

The algebraic multiplicity $m_a(\lambda)$ is the multiplicity of $\lambda$ as a root of $\chi_A$. The geometric multiplicity $m_g(\lambda) = \dim(E_\lambda)$.

Theorem — Geometric $\leq$ algebraic multiplicity

For every eigenvalue $\lambda$: $1 \leq m_g(\lambda) \leq m_a(\lambda)$.

Proof

Step 1. $m_g(\lambda) \geq 1$ because $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, so $E_\lambda$ contains at least one nonzero vector.

Step 2. Let $k = m_g(\lambda)$ and let $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ be a basis of $E_\lambda$. Extend to a basis $v_1, \ldots, v_k, v_{k+1}, \ldots, v_n$ of $V$. In this basis, the matrix of $A$ has the block form

$$A' = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda I_k & B \\ 0 & C \end{pmatrix}$$

because $Av_i = \lambda v_i$ for $i \leq k$ (so the first $k$ columns are $\lambda e_i$ plus something in the span of $v_{k+1}, \ldots, v_n$ — but actually $Av_i = \lambda v_i$ exactly, so the first $k$ columns have $\lambda$ on the diagonal and zeros below in the bottom-left block).

Step 3. The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(\mu) = \det(A' - \mu I) = (\lambda - \mu)^k \det(C - \mu I)$. So $(\lambda - \mu)^k$ divides $\chi_A(\mu)$, meaning the algebraic multiplicity $m_a(\lambda) \geq k = m_g(\lambda)$.

∎

6. Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues

Theorem — Independence of eigenvectors

Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent.

Proof

By induction on $k$, the number of distinct eigenvalues. For $k = 1$, a single eigenvector is nonzero, hence independent.

Inductive step. Suppose $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$ and assume

$$\alpha_1 v_1 + \alpha_2 v_2 + \cdots + \alpha_k v_k = 0. \qquad (*)$$

Apply $A$ to both sides:

$$\alpha_1 \lambda_1 v_1 + \alpha_2 \lambda_2 v_2 + \cdots + \alpha_k \lambda_k v_k = 0. \qquad (**)$$

Compute $(**) - \lambda_k \cdot (*)$:

$$\alpha_1 (\lambda_1 - \lambda_k) v_1 + \cdots + \alpha_{k-1}(\lambda_{k-1} - \lambda_k) v_{k-1} = 0.$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}$ are linearly independent. So $\alpha_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. Since all eigenvalues are distinct, $\lambda_i - \lambda_k \neq 0$, so $\alpha_i = 0$ for $i < k$. Substituting back into $(*)$: $\alpha_k v_k = 0$, and since $v_k \neq 0$, $\alpha_k = 0$.

∎

7. Diagonalisability

Theorem — Diagonalisability criterion

An $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalisable if and only if:

  1. $\chi_A$ splits into linear factors (automatic over $\mathbb{C}$), and
  2. $m_g(\lambda) = m_a(\lambda)$ for every eigenvalue $\lambda$.

Equivalently, there exists a basis of $V$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors. In matrix terms, $A = PDP^{-1}$ where $D$ is diagonal and $P$'s columns are the eigenvectors.

When $A$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues, the independence theorem above gives $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors — a basis. So matrices with distinct eigenvalues are always diagonalisable.

8. Example: diagonalising a matrix

Take $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ from above. Eigenvalues: $2$ and $3$.

Eigenvector for $\lambda = 2$: solve $(A - 2I)v = 0$: $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, so $v_2 = 0$. Take $v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

Eigenvector for $\lambda = 3$: solve $(A - 3I)v = 0$: $\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, so $v_1 = v_2$. Take $v_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Set $P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $D = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$. Then $P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Verify:

$$PDP^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = A.$$

The payoff: $A^k = PD^kP^{-1} = P \begin{pmatrix} 2^k & 0 \\ 0 & 3^k \end{pmatrix} P^{-1}$. Powers of a diagonal matrix are trivial. Diagonalisation turns exponentiation from an $O(k)$ matrix multiplication problem into a single computation.

Historical note

Cauchy (1829) introduced the characteristic equation while studying quadratic forms — he needed to find axes along which a conic section aligns with the coordinates. Sylvester coined the word "matrix" in 1850. The term "eigenvalue" comes from German Eigenwert (own value), introduced by Hilbert (1904) in the context of integral equations — the concept migrated from infinite-dimensional operator theory back to finite matrices. Cayley (1858) showed that every matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation (the Cayley–Hamilton theorem, which we will prove later in this phase).

What breaks

Over $\mathbb{R}$, eigenvalues might not exist. The rotation matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ rotates by $90°$. Its characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(\lambda) = \lambda^2 + 1$, which has no real roots. Over $\mathbb{C}$, the eigenvalues are $\pm i$ — the eigenvectors point in "complex directions."

Eigenvalues exist but diagonalisation fails. The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\chi_A(\lambda) = \lambda^2$, so $\lambda = 0$ with algebraic multiplicity $2$. But $\ker(A) = \operatorname{Span}\{e_1\}$ has dimension $1$ — geometric multiplicity is $1 < 2$. There is no basis of eigenvectors. This is the simplest nilpotent matrix: $A \neq 0$ but $A^2 = 0$. The Jordan normal form (later in this phase) handles exactly these cases.

Eigenvectors for the same eigenvalue need not be independent. The eigenspace $E_\lambda$ is a subspace, so it contains infinitely many eigenvectors (any nonzero scalar multiple of an eigenvector is again an eigenvector). The meaningful quantity is $\dim(E_\lambda)$, not the number of eigenvectors.

Eigenvalues are the roots of $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ — the directions a linear map preserves, and the key to diagonalisation.
