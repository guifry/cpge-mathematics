Matrices and Change of Basis

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 4

The problem

A linear map is an abstract object — "a function that preserves linear structure." But you need to compute with it. Cayley (1858) realised: pick a basis, and the linear map becomes a rectangular array of numbers — a matrix. Different bases give different matrices for the same map. The change-of-basis formula $P^{-1}AP$ tells you how to translate between them.

This is the bridge between abstract theory and concrete calculation. Every theorem about linear maps has a matrix translation, and every matrix computation has a geometric meaning.

1. The matrix of a linear map

Let $V$ and $W$ be finite-dimensional, with bases $\mathcal{B} = (e_1, \ldots, e_n)$ of $V$ and $\mathcal{C} = (f_1, \ldots, f_m)$ of $W$. Let $f: V \to W$ be linear.

By the unique representation theorem (page 2), each $f(e_j)$ can be written uniquely as a linear combination of the basis of $W$:

$$f(e_j) = a_{1j} f_1 + a_{2j} f_2 + \cdots + a_{mj} f_m$$
Definition — Matrix of a linear map

The matrix of $f$ with respect to $\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{C}$ is the $m \times n$ matrix $M = (a_{ij})$ whose $j$-th column is $[f(e_j)]_\mathcal{C}$, the coordinate vector of $f(e_j)$ in basis $\mathcal{C}$. We write $M = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C}}(f)$.

The key property: if $v \in V$ has coordinates $[v]_\mathcal{B} = (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)^T$, then the coordinates of $f(v)$ in basis $\mathcal{C}$ are given by matrix-vector multiplication:

$$[f(v)]_\mathcal{C} = M \, [v]_\mathcal{B}$$
Proof

Write $v = \lambda_1 e_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n e_n$. By linearity:

$$f(v) = \lambda_1 f(e_1) + \cdots + \lambda_n f(e_n) = \lambda_1 \sum_i a_{i1} f_i + \cdots + \lambda_n \sum_i a_{in} f_i = \sum_i \left(\sum_j a_{ij} \lambda_j\right) f_i$$

So the $i$-th coordinate of $f(v)$ in basis $\mathcal{C}$ is $\sum_j a_{ij} \lambda_j$, which is exactly the $i$-th entry of $M \, [v]_\mathcal{B}$.

∎

Example. The differentiation map $D: \mathbb{R}_2[x] \to \mathbb{R}_1[x]$ with the monomial bases $\mathcal{B} = (1, x, x^2)$ and $\mathcal{C} = (1, x)$. We have $D(1) = 0 = 0 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot x$, $D(x) = 1 = 1 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot x$, $D(x^2) = 2x = 0 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot x$. So the matrix is

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Check: $D(3 + 5x - x^2) = 5 - 2x$, and $M \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$, which gives $5 \cdot 1 + (-2) \cdot x = 5 - 2x$. Correct.

2. Matrix multiplication is composition

Let $f: V \to W$ and $g: W \to U$ be linear maps, with bases $\mathcal{B}$ of $V$, $\mathcal{C}$ of $W$, $\mathcal{D}$ of $U$. Let $A = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B},\mathcal{C}}(f)$ and $B = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}}(g)$.

Theorem — Composition corresponds to matrix multiplication

$\text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B},\mathcal{D}}(g \circ f) = B \cdot A$.

Proof

For any $v \in V$:

$$[(g \circ f)(v)]_\mathcal{D} = [g(f(v))]_\mathcal{D} = B\,[f(v)]_\mathcal{C} = B\,(A\,[v]_\mathcal{B}) = (BA)\,[v]_\mathcal{B}$$

Since this holds for all $v$, the matrix of $g \circ f$ is $BA$.

∎

This is why matrix multiplication is defined the way it is. The formula $(BA)_{ij} = \sum_{k} B_{ik} A_{kj}$ is not an arbitrary convention — it is forced by the requirement that matrix multiplication represent composition of linear maps. The "row times column" rule has a reason.

Consequence. Matrix multiplication is associative (because composition of functions is associative) but generally not commutative (because composition is not: rotating then projecting differs from projecting then rotating).

3. Change of basis

Now suppose $V$ is an $n$-dimensional space with two different bases $\mathcal{B} = (e_1, \ldots, e_n)$ and $\mathcal{B}' = (e_1', \ldots, e_n')$. The same vector $v$ has two different coordinate representations: $[v]_\mathcal{B}$ and $[v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$. How are they related?

Definition — Transition matrix

The transition matrix from $\mathcal{B}'$ to $\mathcal{B}$ is the $n \times n$ matrix $P$ whose $j$-th column is $[e_j']_\mathcal{B}$ — the coordinates of the new basis vectors expressed in the old basis.

Concretely, $P$ is the matrix of the identity map $\text{id}: V \to V$ when you read the input in basis $\mathcal{B}'$ and the output in basis $\mathcal{B}$: $P = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B}',\mathcal{B}}(\text{id})$. So for any $v \in V$:

$$[v]_\mathcal{B} = P \, [v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$$

The transition matrix $P$ is always invertible (since $\text{id}$ is a bijection), and $P^{-1}$ is the transition matrix from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{B}'$.

Example. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, let $\mathcal{B} = (e_1, e_2)$ be the standard basis and $\mathcal{B}' = ((1,1), (1,-1))$. Then $[e_1']_\mathcal{B} = (1,1)^T$ and $[e_2']_\mathcal{B} = (1,-1)^T$, so

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

A vector with coordinates $(3, 2)^T$ in $\mathcal{B}'$ is $3(1,1) + 2(1,-1) = (5, 1)$ in the standard basis. Check: $P \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Correct.

4. The change-of-basis formula

Now consider an endomorphism $f: V \to V$ (a linear map from $V$ to itself). In basis $\mathcal{B}$, $f$ has matrix $A = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B},\mathcal{B}}(f)$. In basis $\mathcal{B}'$, $f$ has matrix $A' = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B}',\mathcal{B}'}(f)$. How are $A$ and $A'$ related?

Theorem — Change of basis formula

$A' = P^{-1} A P$, where $P$ is the transition matrix from $\mathcal{B}'$ to $\mathcal{B}$.

Proof

Step 1. Write the identity map decomposition. For any $v \in V$:

$$[f(v)]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P^{-1} [f(v)]_\mathcal{B} = P^{-1} A\, [v]_\mathcal{B} = P^{-1} A\, P\, [v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$$

Step 2. The first equality uses $[w]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P^{-1} [w]_\mathcal{B}$ (apply $P^{-1}$ to both sides of $[w]_\mathcal{B} = P [w]_{\mathcal{B}'}$). The second uses the definition of $A$. The third uses the coordinate change for $v$.

Step 3. Since $[f(v)]_{\mathcal{B}'} = A' [v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$ for all $v$, and the above gives $[f(v)]_{\mathcal{B}'} = (P^{-1}AP)[v]_{\mathcal{B}'}$ for all $v$, we conclude $A' = P^{-1}AP$.

∎

The formula can also be understood via composition: $A' = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B}',\mathcal{B}'}(f) = \text{Mat}_{\mathcal{B}',\mathcal{B}'}(\text{id} \circ f \circ \text{id})$. Reading left to right: convert from $\mathcal{B}'$ to $\mathcal{B}$ (apply $P$), then apply $f$ in $\mathcal{B}$ (apply $A$), then convert back from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{B}'$ (apply $P^{-1}$). Three compositions give $P^{-1}AP$.

5. Similar matrices

Definition — Similar matrices

Two matrices $A, B \in \mathcal{M}_n(\mathbb{K})$ are similar if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that $B = P^{-1}AP$. We write $A \sim B$.

Similar matrices represent the same linear map in different bases. Therefore, any property of a linear map that does not depend on the choice of basis is shared by similar matrices. These invariants of similarity include:

  • Rank: $\text{rk}(A) = \text{rk}(B)$ (same image dimension)
  • Determinant: $\det(P^{-1}AP) = \det(P^{-1})\det(A)\det(P) = \det(A)$
  • Trace: $\text{tr}(P^{-1}AP) = \text{tr}(AP P^{-1}) = \text{tr}(A)$ (IOU: trace and determinant will be studied in depth on the next page)
  • Eigenvalues: (IOU: eigenvalues will be studied later in this phase)
  • Characteristic polynomial: $\det(B - \lambda I) = \det(P^{-1}(A - \lambda I)P) = \det(A - \lambda I)$

Similarity is an equivalence relation on $\mathcal{M}_n(\mathbb{K})$: reflexive ($A = I^{-1}AI$), symmetric (if $B = P^{-1}AP$ then $A = PBP^{-1} = Q^{-1}BQ$ with $Q = P^{-1}$), and transitive (if $B = P^{-1}AP$ and $C = Q^{-1}BQ$ then $C = (PQ)^{-1}A(PQ)$).

6. Example: rotation in $\mathbb{R}^2$

The rotation by $\pi/4$ (45 degrees) in $\mathbb{R}^2$ sends $e_1 = (1,0)$ to $(\cos\frac{\pi}{4}, \sin\frac{\pi}{4}) = (\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})$ and $e_2 = (0,1)$ to $(-\sin\frac{\pi}{4}, \cos\frac{\pi}{4}) = (-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})$. In the standard basis, the matrix is

$$A = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Now use the basis $\mathcal{B}' = ((1,1), (-1,1))$, whose vectors are at $45°$ and $135°$ from the $x$-axis. The transition matrix is $P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, and $P^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Computing $P^{-1}AP$:

$$P^{-1}AP = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

First, $A P = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -2 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\sqrt{2} \\ \sqrt{2} & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

Then $P^{-1}(AP) = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\sqrt{2} \\ \sqrt{2} & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} & -\sqrt{2} \\ \sqrt{2} & \sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Same matrix! This is because $\mathcal{B}'$ is itself a $45°$-rotated version of the standard basis, and the rotation is the same angle regardless of orientation — the map looks the same in any basis that is related to the standard one by rotation. In a non-rotated basis that breaks this symmetry, the matrix would differ.

Historical note

Arthur Cayley introduced matrices and matrix multiplication in 1858, in a paper titled "A Memoir on the Theory of Matrices." He wrote: "I certainly did not get the notion of a matrix in any way through quaternions: it was either directly from that of a determinant; or as a convenient mode of expression of the equations $x' = ax + by$, $y' = cx + dy$." The representation of linear maps as arrays of numbers goes back further — Gauss used coefficient tables for his elimination method, and Cramer (1750) organised coefficients systematically to state his rule for solving systems. But Cayley was the first to treat matrices as algebraic objects in their own right, with their own arithmetic.

What breaks

Forget the basis. A matrix is meaningless without knowing which basis it refers to. The same linear map produces entirely different matrices in different bases. Writing "the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$" without specifying the basis is like giving a position in miles without saying where you measured from.

Different bases for domain and codomain. For an endomorphism $f: V \to V$, the change-of-basis formula is $P^{-1}AP$, which uses the same $P$ on both sides. If you use different bases for the domain and codomain (as with a non-square map $f: V \to W$), the formula becomes $Q^{-1}AP$ with two different transition matrices $P$ and $Q$. The relation is then called equivalence of matrices, not similarity. Equivalent matrices share rank but not necessarily trace or determinant.

Non-invertible $P$. If $P$ is singular, $P^{-1}$ does not exist. The columns of $P$ must form a basis — that is, they must be linearly independent. A singular "transition matrix" would map some vectors to zero, losing information irrecoverably.

A matrix is a linear map written in coordinates; the change-of-basis formula $P^{-1}AP$ translates between coordinate systems.
