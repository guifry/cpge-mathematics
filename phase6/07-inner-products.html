Inner Products and Gram–Schmidt

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 7

The problem

So far, vector spaces have only algebraic structure — addition and scalar multiplication. There is no notion of length, angle, or distance. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, we measure lengths with Pythagoras (Phase 0, page 2) and angles with the dot product. Can we generalise this to abstract vector spaces? And once we have a notion of "perpendicular," can we systematically build orthogonal bases? Gram (1883) and Schmidt (1907) showed how.

1. The inner product

Definition — Inner product

An inner product on a real vector space $V$ is a map $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying, for all $u, v, w \in V$ and $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$:

  1. Linearity in the first argument: $\langle \alpha u + v, w \rangle = \alpha \langle u, w \rangle + \langle v, w \rangle$.
  2. Symmetry: $\langle u, v \rangle = \langle v, u \rangle$.
  3. Positive definiteness: $\langle v, v \rangle \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $v = 0$.

Symmetry plus linearity in the first argument gives linearity in the second argument too (the product is bilinear). A vector space equipped with an inner product is an inner product space.

Over $\mathbb{C}$, symmetry is replaced by conjugate symmetry: $\langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle}$, and linearity in the first argument becomes conjugate-linear (or the convention is reversed — physicists put linearity in the second argument). We work over $\mathbb{R}$ throughout this page.

2. Examples

The dot product on $\mathbb{R}^n$. For $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ and $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)$:

$$\langle u, v \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i = u_1 v_1 + \cdots + u_n v_n.$$

Bilinearity and symmetry are immediate. Positive definiteness: $\langle v, v \rangle = \sum v_i^2 \geq 0$, and $\sum v_i^2 = 0$ forces every $v_i = 0$.

The $L^2$ inner product on $C[a,b]$. For continuous functions $f, g : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x) g(x) \, dx.$$

Linearity and symmetry follow from linearity of the integral. Positive definiteness: $\int_a^b f(x)^2 \, dx \geq 0$ since the integrand is non-negative. If $f$ is continuous and $\int f^2 = 0$, then $f = 0$ everywhere (a continuous non-negative function with zero integral must vanish identically — proved from the definition of the Riemann integral in Phase 3, page 6).

3. Norm

Definition — Norm from an inner product

The norm induced by an inner product is $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$. It measures the "length" of $v$.

For the dot product on $\mathbb{R}^n$, $\|v\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}$ — the Euclidean distance from the origin, which is Pythagoras' theorem in $n$ dimensions.

4. The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality

Theorem — Cauchy–Schwarz inequality

For all $u, v$ in an inner product space:

$$|\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\| \cdot \|v\|.$$

Equality holds if and only if $u$ and $v$ are proportional (one is a scalar multiple of the other).

Proof

If $v = 0$, both sides are $0$. Assume $v \neq 0$. For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, positive definiteness gives

$$0 \leq \|u - tv\|^2 = \langle u - tv, u - tv \rangle = \|u\|^2 - 2t\langle u, v \rangle + t^2 \|v\|^2.$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is non-negative for all $t$. Its minimum occurs at $t = \langle u, v \rangle / \|v\|^2$. Substituting:

$$0 \leq \|u\|^2 - 2 \frac{\langle u, v \rangle}{\|v\|^2} \langle u, v \rangle + \frac{\langle u, v \rangle^2}{\|v\|^4} \|v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 - \frac{\langle u, v \rangle^2}{\|v\|^2}.$$

Rearranging: $\langle u, v \rangle^2 \leq \|u\|^2 \|v\|^2$. Taking square roots gives the inequality.

Equality holds iff $\|u - tv\|^2 = 0$ for this optimal $t$, i.e., $u = tv$ — the vectors are proportional.

∎

The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality lets us define angles: for nonzero $u, v$, the ratio $\langle u, v \rangle / (\|u\| \|v\|)$ lies in $[-1, 1]$, so there is a unique $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ with $\cos \theta = \langle u, v \rangle / (\|u\| \|v\|)$. This is the angle between $u$ and $v$.

5. The triangle inequality

Theorem — Triangle inequality

$\|u + v\| \leq \|u\| + \|v\|$ for all $u, v$.

Proof

Square both sides (both are non-negative, so squaring preserves the inequality):

$$\|u + v\|^2 = \|u\|^2 + 2\langle u, v \rangle + \|v\|^2 \leq \|u\|^2 + 2\|u\|\|v\| + \|v\|^2 = (\|u\| + \|v\|)^2$$

where the inequality used Cauchy–Schwarz: $\langle u, v \rangle \leq |\langle u, v \rangle| \leq \|u\|\|v\|$.

∎

6. Orthogonality

Two vectors $u$ and $v$ are orthogonal, written $u \perp v$, if $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$. A set $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ is orthonormal if $\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$ (the Kronecker delta: $1$ if $i = j$, $0$ otherwise). Each vector has norm $1$ and is perpendicular to every other.

Orthonormal sets are automatically linearly independent: if $\sum \alpha_i e_i = 0$, take the inner product with $e_j$ to get $\alpha_j = 0$.

7. The Gram–Schmidt process

Theorem — Gram–Schmidt orthogonalisation

Given linearly independent vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ in an inner product space, there exist orthonormal vectors $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ such that $\operatorname{Span}(e_1, \ldots, e_k) = \operatorname{Span}(v_1, \ldots, v_k)$ for each $k = 1, \ldots, n$.

The algorithm is constructive:

$$e_1 = \frac{v_1}{\|v_1\|}$$

For $k \geq 2$, first subtract the projections onto the previously built vectors:

$$\tilde{e}_k = v_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \langle v_k, e_j \rangle \, e_j, \qquad e_k = \frac{\tilde{e}_k}{\|\tilde{e}_k\|}.$$
Proof

Step 1. Base case: $e_1 = v_1 / \|v_1\|$ has norm $1$ and spans the same line as $v_1$. Note $\|v_1\| \neq 0$ since $v_1 \neq 0$ (linear independence).

Step 2. Inductive step: assume $e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}$ are orthonormal and span the same subspace as $v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}$. Define $\tilde{e}_k$ as above.

Step 3. $\tilde{e}_k$ is orthogonal to each $e_j$ ($j < k$): for any such $j$,

$$\langle \tilde{e}_k, e_j \rangle = \langle v_k, e_j \rangle - \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \langle v_k, e_i \rangle \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \langle v_k, e_j \rangle - \langle v_k, e_j \rangle = 0$$

using orthonormality: $\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$.

Step 4. $\tilde{e}_k \neq 0$. If $\tilde{e}_k = 0$, then $v_k = \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \langle v_k, e_j \rangle e_j \in \operatorname{Span}(e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}) = \operatorname{Span}(v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1})$, contradicting the linear independence of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$.

Step 5. $e_k = \tilde{e}_k / \|\tilde{e}_k\|$ has norm $1$ and is orthogonal to $e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}$. Since $\tilde{e}_k$ is a linear combination of $v_k$ and $e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}$ (which lie in $\operatorname{Span}(v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1})$), we have $e_k \in \operatorname{Span}(v_1, \ldots, v_k)$. Conversely, $v_k$ can be recovered from $e_k$ and the earlier $e_j$'s. So $\operatorname{Span}(e_1, \ldots, e_k) = \operatorname{Span}(v_1, \ldots, v_k)$.

∎

8. Example

Apply Gram–Schmidt to $v_1 = (1, 1, 0)$, $v_2 = (1, 0, 1)$, $v_3 = (0, 1, 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with the dot product.

$e_1$: $\|v_1\| = \sqrt{1 + 1 + 0} = \sqrt{2}$, so $e_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0)$.

$\tilde{e}_2$: $\langle v_2, e_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$.

$$\tilde{e}_2 = (1, 0, 1) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0) = (1, 0, 1) - \tfrac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1, -1, 2).$$

$\|\tilde{e}_2\| = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1 + 1 + 4} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}$, so $e_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1, -1, 2)$.

$\tilde{e}_3$: $\langle v_3, e_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0 + 1 + 0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ and $\langle v_3, e_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0 - 1 + 2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}$.

$$\tilde{e}_3 = (0, 1, 1) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1, 0) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1, -1, 2) = (0, 1, 1) - \tfrac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0) - \tfrac{1}{6}(1, -1, 2).$$
$$= \left(-\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{6},\; 1 - \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{6},\; 1 - \tfrac{1}{3}\right) = \left(-\tfrac{2}{3},\; \tfrac{2}{3},\; \tfrac{2}{3}\right) = \tfrac{2}{3}(-1, 1, 1).$$

$\|\tilde{e}_3\| = \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{3}$, so $e_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1, 1, 1)$.

Verify: $\langle e_1, e_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{12}}(1 - 1 + 0) = 0$. $\langle e_1, e_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(-1 + 1 + 0) = 0$. $\langle e_2, e_3 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{18}}(-1 - 1 + 2) = 0$. All orthogonal, all unit vectors.

9. Orthonormal bases exist

Corollary — Every finite-dimensional inner product space has an orthonormal basis

Start with any basis (which exists by the dimension theorem, page 2). Apply Gram–Schmidt. The result is an orthonormal basis.

Orthonormal bases are the "best" bases for computation. Coordinates are found by inner products: if $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ is orthonormal and $v = \sum \alpha_i e_i$, then $\alpha_i = \langle v, e_i \rangle$ (take the inner product of both sides with $e_i$ and use orthonormality). No matrix inversion needed. This is why Gram–Schmidt is so important.

Historical note

The dot product traces back to Grassmann (1844) and Gibbs (1881). Gram (1883) used inner products when studying least-squares problems — he needed to measure how "close" a function is to a linear combination of others. Schmidt (1907) formalised the orthogonalisation process for function spaces (what we now call Hilbert spaces), where the "vectors" are functions and the inner product is an integral. Schwarz (1885) proved the inequality $|\int fg| \leq \sqrt{\int f^2} \sqrt{\int g^2}$ for integrals; Cauchy (1821) had the finite-sum version $|\sum a_i b_i| \leq \sqrt{\sum a_i^2} \sqrt{\sum b_i^2}$ decades earlier. Bunyakovsky (1859) independently obtained the integral version in Russia.

What breaks

Drop positive definiteness. The Minkowski "inner product" of special relativity uses the bilinear form $\langle u, v \rangle = u_0 v_0 - u_1 v_1 - u_2 v_2 - u_3 v_3$ (signature $(+, -, -, -)$). This is symmetric and bilinear but not positive definite: the vector $(1, 1, 0, 0)$ satisfies $\langle v, v \rangle = 0$ despite $v \neq 0$. Such vectors are called lightlike — they represent the paths of photons. You lose the ability to define "length" in the usual sense. Cauchy–Schwarz fails. Gram–Schmidt fails (you cannot normalise a vector of "length" zero).

Drop symmetry. A bilinear form that is not symmetric is just a bilinear form — not an inner product. You lose the connection between $\langle u, v \rangle$ and $\langle v, u \rangle$, so "orthogonal to" is no longer a symmetric relation: $u \perp v$ need not imply $v \perp u$. The geometric interpretation collapses.

Drop linearity. The function $\langle u, v \rangle = \|u\| \cdot \|v\|$ is symmetric and positive (if we interpret it right), but not bilinear. It cannot distinguish directions — it assigns the same value regardless of the angle between $u$ and $v$.

An inner product lets you measure length and angle; Gram–Schmidt turns any basis into an orthonormal one by iteratively subtracting projections.
