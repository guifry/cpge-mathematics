The Spectral Theorem

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 9

The problem

Not every matrix can be diagonalised — page 6 showed examples with too few eigenvectors. But there is a remarkable class that always can: symmetric matrices, where $A = A^T$. Better still, the diagonalising basis can be chosen orthonormal — no stretching, just rotation.

Cauchy proved this in 1829 while classifying quadratic forms. It is one of the most important theorems in all of mathematics: it says that symmetric matrices are, in a very strong sense, completely understood.

1. The challenge of diagonalisation

Recall from page 6: an $n \times n$ matrix is diagonalisable precisely when it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. This can fail. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalue $0$ with algebraic multiplicity $2$ but geometric multiplicity $1$ — only one independent eigenvector.

For symmetric matrices, none of this goes wrong. We shall prove three facts in sequence: the eigenvalues are real, eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal, and there are always enough eigenvectors to form an orthonormal basis.

2. Real eigenvalues

Theorem — Eigenvalues of a real symmetric matrix are real

Let $A$ be a real symmetric $n \times n$ matrix. Every eigenvalue of $A$ is a real number.

Proof

Step 1. Let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue of $A$ (possibly complex), with eigenvector $v \in \mathbb{C}^n$, $v \neq 0$. So $Av = \lambda v$.

Step 2. Take the conjugate transpose of both sides. Since $A$ is real, $\bar{A} = A$, and since $A$ is symmetric, $A^T = A$. So $A^* = A$ (where $A^* = \bar{A}^T$). From $Av = \lambda v$ we get $v^* A^* = \bar{\lambda} v^*$, i.e. $v^* A = \bar{\lambda} v^*$.

Step 3. Multiply on the right by $v$: $v^* A v = \bar{\lambda} v^* v$. But also $v^* (Av) = v^* (\lambda v) = \lambda v^* v$.

Step 4. So $\lambda v^* v = \bar{\lambda} v^* v$. Now $v^* v = |v_1|^2 + \cdots + |v_n|^2 > 0$ since $v \neq 0$. Dividing, $\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$, which means $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

∎

3. Orthogonality of eigenvectors

Theorem — Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal

Let $A$ be real symmetric. If $Au = \lambda u$ and $Av = \mu v$ with $\lambda \neq \mu$, then $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.

Proof

Step 1. Compute $\langle Au, v \rangle = \langle \lambda u, v \rangle = \lambda \langle u, v \rangle$.

Step 2. Since $A$ is symmetric, $\langle Au, v \rangle = u^T A^T v = u^T A v = \langle u, Av \rangle = \langle u, \mu v \rangle = \mu \langle u, v \rangle$.

Step 3. So $\lambda \langle u, v \rangle = \mu \langle u, v \rangle$, giving $(\lambda - \mu) \langle u, v \rangle = 0$. Since $\lambda \neq \mu$, we get $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$.

∎

This is already remarkable: symmetry forces eigenvectors to be perpendicular, with no effort on our part. For a general matrix you have no such guarantee.

4. The spectral theorem

Theorem — Spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices

Let $A$ be a real symmetric $n \times n$ matrix. There exists an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$. Equivalently, there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (with $Q^T Q = I$) and a diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ such that

$$A = Q \Lambda Q^T.$$
Proof

By induction on $n$.

Base case ($n = 1$). A $1 \times 1$ matrix $(a)$ is already diagonal, and the basis $\{1\}$ is orthonormal. Done.

Inductive step. Assume the theorem holds for all real symmetric matrices of size $(n-1) \times (n-1)$. Let $A$ be $n \times n$ and symmetric.

Step 1. $A$ has at least one real eigenvalue $\lambda_1$. This follows from section 2: the characteristic polynomial has degree $n$, so it has at least one complex root, and we proved all roots are real. Let $v_1$ be a corresponding eigenvector with $\|v_1\| = 1$.

Step 2. Let $W = v_1^\perp = \{ w \in \mathbb{R}^n : \langle w, v_1 \rangle = 0 \}$. This is an $(n-1)$-dimensional subspace. We claim $A$ maps $W$ into $W$.

Step 3. Take any $w \in W$. We need $\langle Aw, v_1 \rangle = 0$. By symmetry:

$$\langle Aw, v_1 \rangle = \langle w, Av_1 \rangle = \langle w, \lambda_1 v_1 \rangle = \lambda_1 \langle w, v_1 \rangle = \lambda_1 \cdot 0 = 0.$$

So $Aw \in W$. This is the step where symmetry is essential.

Step 4. The restriction $A|_W : W \to W$ is a symmetric operator on the $(n-1)$-dimensional space $W$. (It is symmetric because for any $w_1, w_2 \in W$, $\langle A|_W w_1, w_2 \rangle = \langle Aw_1, w_2 \rangle = \langle w_1, Aw_2 \rangle = \langle w_1, A|_W w_2 \rangle$.)

Step 5. By the inductive hypothesis, $W$ has an orthonormal basis $\{v_2, \ldots, v_n\}$ of eigenvectors of $A|_W$ (and hence of $A$). Together with $v_1$, the set $\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}$ is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.

Step 6. Form the matrix $Q = (v_1 \mid v_2 \mid \cdots \mid v_n)$. Since the columns are orthonormal, $Q^T Q = I$. By construction, $AQ = Q\Lambda$ where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$, giving $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$.

∎

5. Spectral decomposition

The factorisation $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ can be unpacked column by column. Let $q_1, \ldots, q_n$ be the columns of $Q$. Then:

$$A = \lambda_1 q_1 q_1^T + \lambda_2 q_2 q_2^T + \cdots + \lambda_n q_n q_n^T.$$

Each matrix $q_i q_i^T$ is the orthogonal projection onto the line spanned by $q_i$ (as we saw on page 8). It is a rank-1 matrix. The symmetric matrix $A$ decomposes as a sum of rank-1 projections, each scaled by the corresponding eigenvalue. This is the spectral decomposition — hence the name "spectral theorem."

6. Example

Diagonalise $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 1. Characteristic polynomial: $\det(A - \lambda I) = (2 - \lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 1)$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 1$.

Step 2. Eigenvectors. For $\lambda_1 = 3$: $(A - 3I)v = 0$ gives $\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, so $v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$. For $\lambda_2 = 1$: $(A - I)v = 0$ gives $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, so $v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 3. Check orthonormality: $\langle v_1, v_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{2}(1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-1)) = 0$. Good.

Step 4. Verify:

$$Q\Lambda Q^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = A. \checkmark$$

The spectral decomposition is $A = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ — a sum of two rank-1 projections scaled by $3$ and $1$.

Historical note

Cauchy (1829) proved the spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices in the context of classifying quadratic forms — he needed to know that every quadratic form in $n$ variables can be reduced to a sum of squares by an orthogonal change of coordinates. Hermite later extended the result to complex Hermitian matrices ($A = A^*$). Hilbert (1904) generalised it to compact self-adjoint operators on infinite-dimensional spaces — that extension is the foundation of quantum mechanics and functional analysis (IOU: Phase 14).

What breaks

Drop symmetry — eigenvalues may be complex. The rotation matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $\pm i$. No real spectral decomposition exists.

Drop symmetry — diagonalisation may fail entirely. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalue $0$ with algebraic multiplicity $2$ but geometric multiplicity $1$. It is not diagonalisable at all.

Drop symmetry — eigenvectors need not be orthogonal. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvectors $(1, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$, which are not orthogonal. The factorisation $Q\Lambda Q^T$ with orthogonal $Q$ does not apply.

Symmetry is the single hypothesis that forces all three properties: real eigenvalues, enough eigenvectors, and orthogonality.

Every real symmetric matrix is orthogonally diagonalisable — a sum of independent projections onto perpendicular eigenspaces.
