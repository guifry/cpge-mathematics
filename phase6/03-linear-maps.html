Linear Maps and the Rank-Nullity Theorem

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 3

The problem

A linear map sends vectors to vectors while preserving the linear structure — addition and scalar multiplication. Rotations, projections, differentiation of polynomials: all linear maps. Some vectors get sent to zero (the kernel). Some vectors in the codomain get hit (the image). These two sets carry all the structural information about the map.

The fundamental question: how are the kernel and image related? The rank-nullity theorem answers with a conservation law — what you lose (kernel dimension) plus what you hit (image dimension) equals what you started with (domain dimension).

1. Linear maps

Definition — Linear map

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over $\mathbb{K}$. A function $f: V \to W$ is a linear map (or linear transformation, or homomorphism) if for all $u, v \in V$ and $\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$:

$$f(\lambda u + \mu v) = \lambda f(u) + \mu f(v)$$

This single condition encodes two things: $f(u + v) = f(u) + f(v)$ (additivity) and $f(\lambda v) = \lambda f(v)$ (homogeneity). Together they say: $f$ preserves the linear structure. In particular, $f(0_V) = 0_W$ always (set $\lambda = \mu = 0$).

Examples. Rotation by angle $\theta$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$: linear (it preserves addition and scaling geometrically). Projection onto a subspace: linear. The differentiation operator $D: \mathbb{R}_n[x] \to \mathbb{R}_{n-1}[x]$ sending $p \mapsto p'$: linear, because $(p + q)' = p' + q'$ and $(\lambda p)' = \lambda p'$ (proved in Phase 3, page 2). Matrix multiplication $x \mapsto Ax$: linear by the properties of matrix arithmetic.

Non-example. The map $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) = x + 1$ is not linear: $f(0) = 1 \neq 0$.

2. Kernel and image

Definition — Kernel and image

Let $f: V \to W$ be linear. The kernel (or null space) is $\ker(f) = \{v \in V : f(v) = 0_W\}$. The image (or range) is $\text{im}(f) = \{f(v) : v \in V\}$.

Theorem — Kernel and image are subspaces

If $f: V \to W$ is linear, then $\ker(f)$ is a subspace of $V$ and $\text{im}(f)$ is a subspace of $W$.

Proof

Kernel. $\ker(f) \neq \emptyset$ since $f(0_V) = 0_W$, so $0_V \in \ker(f)$. Take $u, v \in \ker(f)$ and $\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$. Then $f(\lambda u + \mu v) = \lambda f(u) + \mu f(v) = \lambda \cdot 0_W + \mu \cdot 0_W = 0_W$, so $\lambda u + \mu v \in \ker(f)$. By the subspace criterion (page 1), $\ker(f)$ is a subspace.

Image. $\text{im}(f) \neq \emptyset$ since $f(0_V) = 0_W \in \text{im}(f)$. Take $f(u), f(v) \in \text{im}(f)$ and $\lambda, \mu \in \mathbb{K}$. Then $\lambda f(u) + \mu f(v) = f(\lambda u + \mu v) \in \text{im}(f)$. Subspace criterion satisfied.

∎

3. Injectivity and the kernel

Theorem — Injectivity criterion

A linear map $f: V \to W$ is injective if and only if $\ker(f) = \{0_V\}$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $f$ is injective. We know $f(0_V) = 0_W$. If $v \in \ker(f)$, then $f(v) = 0_W = f(0_V)$, so injectivity gives $v = 0_V$. Hence $\ker(f) = \{0_V\}$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\ker(f) = \{0_V\}$. If $f(u) = f(v)$, then $f(u - v) = f(u) - f(v) = 0_W$, so $u - v \in \ker(f) = \{0_V\}$, giving $u = v$. Hence $f$ is injective.

∎

This is a powerful test. To check injectivity of a linear map, you do not need to verify that $f(u) = f(v) \Rightarrow u = v$ for all pairs — you only need to check that the equation $f(v) = 0$ has the unique solution $v = 0$.

4. Rank and nullity

Definition — Rank and nullity

Let $f: V \to W$ be linear with $V$ finite-dimensional. The rank of $f$ is $\text{rk}(f) = \dim(\text{im}(f))$. The nullity of $f$ is $\text{null}(f) = \dim(\ker(f))$.

5. The rank-nullity theorem

Theorem — Rank-nullity (dimension theorem)

Let $V$ be finite-dimensional and $f: V \to W$ linear. Then

$$\dim(\ker f) + \dim(\text{im}\, f) = \dim(V)$$
Proof

Step 1. Let $k = \dim(\ker f)$. Pick a basis $u_1, \ldots, u_k$ of $\ker(f)$. By the basis extension theorem (page 2), extend this to a basis $u_1, \ldots, u_k, v_1, \ldots, v_r$ of $V$. Then $k + r = \dim(V)$, so we need to show $\dim(\text{im}\, f) = r$.

Step 2. Claim: $f(v_1), \ldots, f(v_r)$ span $\text{im}(f)$. Take any $w \in \text{im}(f)$. Then $w = f(x)$ for some $x \in V$. Write $x = \alpha_1 u_1 + \cdots + \alpha_k u_k + \beta_1 v_1 + \cdots + \beta_r v_r$. Applying $f$:

$$w = f(x) = \alpha_1 \underbrace{f(u_1)}_{= 0} + \cdots + \alpha_k \underbrace{f(u_k)}_{= 0} + \beta_1 f(v_1) + \cdots + \beta_r f(v_r) = \sum_{j=1}^{r} \beta_j f(v_j)$$

So $w \in \text{Span}(f(v_1), \ldots, f(v_r))$.

Step 3. Claim: $f(v_1), \ldots, f(v_r)$ are linearly independent. Suppose $\gamma_1 f(v_1) + \cdots + \gamma_r f(v_r) = 0$. By linearity, $f(\gamma_1 v_1 + \cdots + \gamma_r v_r) = 0$, so $\gamma_1 v_1 + \cdots + \gamma_r v_r \in \ker(f)$. Since $u_1, \ldots, u_k$ is a basis of $\ker(f)$:

$$\gamma_1 v_1 + \cdots + \gamma_r v_r = \delta_1 u_1 + \cdots + \delta_k u_k$$

for some scalars $\delta_i$. Rearranging: $\delta_1 u_1 + \cdots + \delta_k u_k - \gamma_1 v_1 - \cdots - \gamma_r v_r = 0$. But $u_1, \ldots, u_k, v_1, \ldots, v_r$ is a basis of $V$, hence linearly independent. So all $\delta_i = 0$ and all $\gamma_j = 0$.

Conclusion. $f(v_1), \ldots, f(v_r)$ is a basis of $\text{im}(f)$, so $\dim(\text{im}\, f) = r$. Therefore $\dim(\ker f) + \dim(\text{im}\, f) = k + r = \dim(V)$.

∎

6. Consequences

Injective $\iff$ surjective for maps between spaces of equal dimension. If $\dim(V) = \dim(W) = n$ and $f: V \to W$ is linear, then rank-nullity gives $\dim(\ker f) + \dim(\text{im}\, f) = n$. If $f$ is injective, $\dim(\ker f) = 0$, so $\dim(\text{im}\, f) = n = \dim(W)$, meaning $f$ is surjective. Conversely, if $f$ is surjective, $\dim(\text{im}\, f) = n$, so $\dim(\ker f) = 0$, meaning $f$ is injective. In summary: for linear maps between equal-dimensional spaces, injective $\iff$ surjective $\iff$ bijective.

Square matrices. A matrix $A \in \mathcal{M}_n(\mathbb{R})$ is invertible if and only if $\ker(A) = \{0\}$, if and only if its columns are linearly independent, if and only if its columns span $\mathbb{R}^n$.

7. Example: differentiation

Consider the differentiation map $D: \mathbb{R}_n[x] \to \mathbb{R}_{n-1}[x]$ sending $p \mapsto p'$. We have $\dim(\mathbb{R}_n[x]) = n + 1$.

Kernel. $D(p) = 0$ means $p' = 0$, so $p$ is a constant polynomial. Hence $\ker(D) = \mathbb{R}_0[x]$ and $\dim(\ker D) = 1$.

Image. Every polynomial $q$ of degree $\leq n - 1$ is the derivative of some polynomial of degree $\leq n$ (just integrate term by term — proved in Phase 3, page 8). So $\text{im}(D) = \mathbb{R}_{n-1}[x]$ and $\dim(\text{im}\, D) = n$.

Check: $\dim(\ker D) + \dim(\text{im}\, D) = 1 + n = n + 1 = \dim(\mathbb{R}_n[x])$.

Historical note

The rank-nullity theorem is sometimes attributed to James Joseph Sylvester, who coined the term "nullity" in 1884. But the underlying ideas stretch back to Gauss's work on systems of equations in the 1810s. The modern formulation in terms of abstract vector spaces emerged in the early 20th century. It is arguably the single most fundamental structural result in linear algebra — every other dimension-counting argument in the subject is a consequence of it.

What breaks

Infinite dimensions. The right shift operator $T: \mathbb{R}^\mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}^\mathbb{N}$ defined by $T(a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots) = (0, a_1, a_2, \ldots)$ is injective (kernel is trivial) but not surjective (the sequence $(1, 0, 0, \ldots)$ is not in the image). In finite dimensions, injectivity would force surjectivity. The left shift $S(a_1, a_2, \ldots) = (a_2, a_3, \ldots)$ is surjective but not injective. The arithmetic of rank-nullity ($\infty - 1 + \infty = \infty$) becomes meaningless for infinite cardinals.

Non-linear maps. For the map $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) = x^2$, the "kernel" $\{0\}$ is one point and the "image" $[0, \infty)$ is uncountable. There is no dimension equation because $f$ is not linear.

Rank-nullity is a conservation law: what the map kills plus what it hits equals the dimension of the domain.
