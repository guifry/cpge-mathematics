Orthogonal Projection

Phase 6 — Linear Algebra, Page 8

The problem

Given a subspace $W$ and a point $v$ not in $W$, what is the closest point in $W$ to $v$? In $\mathbb{R}^3$, this is dropping a perpendicular from $v$ onto a plane. In function spaces, this is finding the best polynomial approximation to a function. Fourier (1807) implicitly solved this when computing his famous coefficients. The answer is always the same: project orthogonally.

1. Geometric intuition

Consider a vector $v$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and a plane $W$ through the origin. The closest point in $W$ to $v$ is its "shadow" on $W$ — the point you reach by dropping a perpendicular. The error vector $v - \text{proj}_W(v)$ is perpendicular to the entire plane.

The same picture works for a line through the origin: the projection of $v$ onto the line spanned by $u$ is $\frac{\langle v, u \rangle}{\|u\|^2} u$. We proved on page 7 that inner products let us measure angles. Now we use them to decompose vectors.

2. The orthogonal complement

Definition — Orthogonal complement

Let $W$ be a subspace of an inner product space $V$. The orthogonal complement of $W$ is

$$W^\perp = \{v \in V : \langle v, w \rangle = 0 \text{ for all } w \in W\}.$$
Proof that $W^\perp$ is a subspace

Step 1. $0 \in W^\perp$ since $\langle 0, w \rangle = 0$ for all $w$.

Step 2. If $v_1, v_2 \in W^\perp$, then for any $w \in W$: $\langle v_1 + v_2, w \rangle = \langle v_1, w \rangle + \langle v_2, w \rangle = 0 + 0 = 0$.

Step 3. If $v \in W^\perp$ and $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$, then $\langle \alpha v, w \rangle = \alpha \langle v, w \rangle = 0$ for all $w \in W$.

∎

3. Orthogonal decomposition

Theorem — Orthogonal decomposition

Let $W$ be a finite-dimensional subspace of an inner product space $V$. Every $v \in V$ can be written uniquely as

$$v = w + w^\perp, \qquad w \in W, \quad w^\perp \in W^\perp.$$
Proof

Step 1 (existence). Let $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ be an orthonormal basis of $W$ (exists by Gram–Schmidt, page 7). Define

$$w = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle \, e_i, \qquad w^\perp = v - w.$$

Clearly $w \in W$. We must show $w^\perp \in W^\perp$. For any $e_j$ ($j \leq k$):

$$\langle w^\perp, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \langle v, e_j \rangle - \langle v, e_j \rangle = 0.$$

Since $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ span $W$ and $\langle w^\perp, \cdot \rangle$ is linear, $w^\perp$ is orthogonal to every vector in $W$. So $w^\perp \in W^\perp$.

Step 2 (uniqueness). Suppose $v = w_1 + w_1^\perp = w_2 + w_2^\perp$ with $w_i \in W$, $w_i^\perp \in W^\perp$. Then $w_1 - w_2 = w_2^\perp - w_1^\perp$. The left side is in $W$, the right side is in $W^\perp$. So the common vector lies in $W \cap W^\perp$. But if $u \in W \cap W^\perp$, then $\langle u, u \rangle = 0$, so $u = 0$ by positive definiteness. Hence $w_1 = w_2$ and $w_1^\perp = w_2^\perp$.

∎

4. Dimension formula

Theorem — Dimension of orthogonal complement

If $V$ is finite-dimensional: $\dim(W) + \dim(W^\perp) = \dim(V)$.

Proof

Step 1. Let $k = \dim(W)$ and $n = \dim(V)$. Take an orthonormal basis $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ of $W$ and extend it (by Gram–Schmidt on any extension to a basis of $V$) to an orthonormal basis $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ of $V$.

Step 2. We claim $e_{k+1}, \ldots, e_n$ is a basis of $W^\perp$. Each $e_j$ with $j > k$ is orthogonal to $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ (by orthonormality), hence orthogonal to all of $W$, so $e_j \in W^\perp$. They are linearly independent (being part of an orthonormal set).

Step 3. Any $u \in W^\perp$ can be written as $u = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i e_i$. For $j \leq k$: $0 = \langle u, e_j \rangle = \alpha_j$. So $u = \sum_{i=k+1}^n \alpha_i e_i \in \operatorname{Span}(e_{k+1}, \ldots, e_n)$.

Therefore $\dim(W^\perp) = n - k$.

∎

5. The projection formula

Theorem — Orthogonal projection

If $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ is an orthonormal basis of $W$, the orthogonal projection of $v$ onto $W$ is

$$\operatorname{proj}_W(v) = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle v, e_i \rangle \, e_i.$$

This is just the $w$ component from the orthogonal decomposition. We already proved it lies in $W$ and that the error $v - \operatorname{proj}_W(v)$ lies in $W^\perp$.

Note that the formula does not depend on which orthonormal basis of $W$ you choose — the uniqueness of the decomposition guarantees this.

6. Best approximation

Theorem — Best approximation

$\operatorname{proj}_W(v)$ is the unique closest point in $W$ to $v$:

$$\|v - \operatorname{proj}_W(v)\| \leq \|v - w\| \quad \text{for all } w \in W,$$

with equality if and only if $w = \operatorname{proj}_W(v)$.

Proof

Let $p = \operatorname{proj}_W(v)$, so $v - p \in W^\perp$. For any $w \in W$, write

$$v - w = (v - p) + (p - w).$$

Here $v - p \in W^\perp$ and $p - w \in W$ (since $p, w \in W$). These two components are orthogonal, so by the Pythagorean theorem (which holds in any inner product space: if $a \perp b$ then $\|a + b\|^2 = \|a\|^2 + \|b\|^2$):

$$\|v - w\|^2 = \|v - p\|^2 + \|p - w\|^2 \geq \|v - p\|^2.$$

Equality holds iff $\|p - w\|^2 = 0$, i.e., $w = p$.

∎

This is a beautifully clean result. The Pythagorean theorem splits the squared distance into an unavoidable part ($\|v - p\|^2$, the distance from $v$ to $W$) and an avoidable part ($\|p - w\|^2$, the error from choosing the wrong point in $W$). Minimising means setting the avoidable part to zero.

7. Example

Project $v = (1, 2, 3)$ onto the plane $W = \operatorname{Span}\{(1, 0, 1), (0, 1, 0)\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Step 1: orthonormalise. Let $w_1 = (1, 0, 1)$, $w_2 = (0, 1, 0)$. These are already orthogonal: $\langle w_1, w_2 \rangle = 0$. Normalise: $e_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 0, 1)$, $e_2 = (0, 1, 0)$.

Step 2: compute coefficients. $\langle v, e_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1 + 0 + 3) = \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} = 2\sqrt{2}$. $\langle v, e_2 \rangle = 2$.

Step 3: project.

$$\operatorname{proj}_W(v) = 2\sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 0, 1) + 2 \cdot (0, 1, 0) = 2(1, 0, 1) + (0, 2, 0) = (2, 2, 2).$$

Step 4: verify. The error is $v - \operatorname{proj}_W(v) = (1, 2, 3) - (2, 2, 2) = (-1, 0, 1)$. Check orthogonality: $\langle (-1, 0, 1), (1, 0, 1) \rangle = -1 + 0 + 1 = 0$ and $\langle (-1, 0, 1), (0, 1, 0) \rangle = 0$. The error is indeed perpendicular to $W$.

The distance from $v$ to $W$ is $\|(-1, 0, 1)\| = \sqrt{2}$.

8. Preview: projection as a universal idea

Orthogonal projection is one of the most widely applicable ideas in mathematics:

  • Fourier series: the Fourier coefficients $a_n = \langle f, e_n \rangle$ are exactly the projection of $f$ onto the $n$-th basis function $e_n$ in the inner product space $L^2$. Fourier (1807) was projecting without knowing it.
  • Least squares regression: fitting a line to data means projecting the observation vector onto the column space of the design matrix. The normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ are the algebraic expression of "the error must be perpendicular to $W$." Gauss discovered this in 1795 (age 18) to predict the orbit of Ceres.
  • Conditional expectation: in probability (Phase 12), $E[X | Y]$ is the orthogonal projection of the random variable $X$ onto the subspace of $Y$-measurable random variables, with inner product $\langle X, Y \rangle = E[XY]$. The best $L^2$ predictor is a projection. (IOU: this will be formalised in Phase 12.)
Historical note

Fourier (1807) implicitly used projections when computing his famous coefficients — each coefficient $a_n = \int f(x) \cos(nx) \, dx$ is $\langle f, \cos(n \cdot) \rangle$ where the cosines form an orthogonal system. Hilbert (1906) recognised projection as the central idea of analysis in infinite-dimensional spaces and proved the projection theorem in full generality. The finite-dimensional version we proved here is the engine behind least-squares regression, discovered by Gauss (1795/1809) and Legendre (1805) independently — they were competing to predict planetary orbits from noisy astronomical data, and both realised that minimising squared errors gives the projection onto the column space.

What breaks

Infinite dimensions without completeness. In an infinite-dimensional inner product space that is not complete, the closest point in a closed subspace $W$ might not exist. The infimum of $\|v - w\|$ over $w \in W$ might not be achieved by any actual vector in $W$ — there is a minimising sequence that converges, but the limit might not lie in the space. You need the space to be complete (a Hilbert space) for the projection theorem to hold in infinite dimensions. (IOU: Phase 14, Functional Analysis.)

Finite dimensions are safe. Every finite-dimensional inner product space is automatically complete (IOU: Phase 9 will prove that all norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent and $\mathbb{R}^n$ is complete). So for the linear algebra in this phase, projection always works.

Without an inner product, "closest point" is ill-defined. In a vector space with no inner product, there is no notion of distance. You cannot project. You need at minimum a norm (which gives distance) and completeness (which guarantees the minimum is achieved). An inner product gives the cleanest theory because the projection has the explicit formula $\sum \langle v, e_i \rangle e_i$.

Orthogonal projection decomposes any vector into a best approximation in a subspace plus a perpendicular error — minimising distance is Pythagoras in action.
