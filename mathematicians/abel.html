Norwegian

Other notable contributions

  • Elliptic functions — inverted elliptic integrals to create elliptic functions, a breakthrough that transformed complex analysis and algebraic geometry.
  • Abelian integrals — generalised elliptic integrals to higher-genus curves, work continued by Jacobi and Riemann.
  • Abel Prize — the most prestigious award in mathematics (alongside the Fields Medal), established by Norway in 2003 in his honour.

Abel died of tuberculosis at 26, two days before a letter arrived offering him a professorship in Berlin. In his brief career, he proved the impossibility of solving the general quintic by radicals (1824), developed the theory of elliptic functions, and laid foundations for rigorous analysis — all while living in poverty in a country with almost no mathematical tradition.

His 1824 proof that the general fifth-degree polynomial has no solution by radicals was the first complete proof of this result. Ruffini had attempted the same in 1799, but his proof had gaps. Abel's approach was purely algebraic: he showed that the group of permutations involved in any radical solution must have a certain structure (solvability), and the symmetric group $S_5$ lacks it.

Abel was deeply concerned with rigour in analysis at a time when most mathematicians were not. He noticed in 1826 that Cauchy's theorem about limits of continuous functions was wrong, writing: "There are very few theorems in advanced analysis that have been demonstrated in a rigorous manner. Everywhere one finds this miserable way of reasoning from the special to the general." His own Abel's theorem (1826) on boundary behaviour of power series was a model of careful analysis.

The summation technique named "Abel summation" (summation by parts) appears in the proof of Abel's theorem and became a standard tool. Commutative groups are called "abelian" in his honour — with a lowercase 'a', one of the few instances where a mathematician's name is not capitalised. The Abel Prize, awarded annually since 2003 by the Norwegian Academy, fills the gap left by the absence of a Nobel Prize in mathematics.
