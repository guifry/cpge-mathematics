Kenneth Arrow

1921–2017

American

Economics, social choice theory, general equilibrium

Theorems in this course

  • Arrow's impossibility theorem (1951) — Phase 15, page 6: no voting system satisfies all fairness axioms simultaneously

Other notable contributions

  • Arrow–Debreu model — proved the existence of general competitive equilibrium (1954), establishing the mathematical foundations of microeconomics.
  • Arrow's learning by doing — a model of endogenous technological change where productivity increases with cumulative output (1962).
  • Arrow information paradox — the value of information cannot be known until one has the information, but then one has already acquired it.

Kenneth Joseph Arrow grew up in New York City during the Depression — an experience that shaped his lifelong interest in social welfare and collective decision-making. He studied mathematics at City College of New York, then moved to Columbia for graduate work in economics. His 1951 doctoral thesis, published as Social Choice and Individual Values, contained his impossibility theorem: no rank-order voting system for three or more candidates can simultaneously satisfy a handful of seemingly modest fairness conditions.

The result was devastating in its simplicity. Politicians and social theorists had long debated which voting system was "best"; Arrow proved that the question has no satisfying answer. The theorem launched social choice theory as a mathematical discipline and remains one of the most cited results in economics.

Arrow received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1972, at age 51 the youngest laureate in the prize's history at that time. He spent most of his career at Stanford, contributing to general equilibrium theory (with Gérard Debreu), health economics, and the economics of information. His influence extended across virtually every branch of modern economics. He remained active in research well into his nineties and died in Palo Alto in 2017.
