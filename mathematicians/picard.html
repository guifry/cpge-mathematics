French

Theorems in this course

  • Picard iteration — constructive proof of Cauchy-Lipschitz — Phase 8, page 2

Other notable contributions

  • Picard's little theorem — a non-constant entire function takes every complex value with at most one exception.
  • Picard's great theorem — in any neighbourhood of an essential singularity, a holomorphic function takes every complex value with at most one exception, infinitely often.
  • Picard-Lefschetz theory — foundational work on the topology of algebraic varieties and the monodromy of families of algebraic curves.

Charles Émile Picard was born in Paris in 1856 and became one of the dominant figures in French mathematics at the turn of the twentieth century. He entered the École Normale Supérieure at sixteen and was appointed to the Sorbonne at the remarkably young age of twenty-nine.

Picard's most celebrated results in complex analysis — his "little" and "great" theorems on the range of entire and meromorphic functions — are among the most beautiful in the subject. But his impact on this course comes through his work on differential equations. In 1890, Picard gave the elegant constructive proof of the Cauchy-Lipschitz theorem using successive approximations: define a sequence of functions by iterating the integral operator $T[\phi](t) = y_0 + \int_{t_0}^t f(s, \phi(s))\, ds$, and show that the sequence converges uniformly to the unique solution.

This Picard iteration was a landmark in the development of functional analysis. It showed that an existence theorem for differential equations could be reduced to a fixed-point argument — an idea that Banach would later abstract into the contraction mapping principle. The method is beautifully explicit: for $y' = y$, $y(0) = 1$, the Picard iterates $1, 1+t, 1+t+t^2/2, \ldots$ converge to $e^t$, reconstructing the power series term by term.

Picard served as Permanent Secretary of the Académie des Sciences for over twenty years and received almost every honour available to a French mathematician. He died in Paris in 1941.
