Aryabhata

476–550

Indian

Trigonometry, astronomy, algebra

Theorems in this course

  • Trigonometry from Scratch — Phase 0, page 4 (introduced the half-chord/sine function)

Other notable contributions

  • Place-value system — one of the earliest systematic uses of the decimal place-value system with zero as a placeholder.
  • Earth's rotation — proposed that the apparent rotation of the heavens is due to the Earth spinning on its axis, over a millennium before Copernicus.
  • Approximation of pi — gave pi as 3.1416, accurate to four decimal places, and may have recognised it as irrational.

Aryabhata, born in 476 in Kusumapura (modern Patna), was the first major mathematician-astronomer of classical India. His masterwork, the Aryabhatiya, written when he was just 23, compressed an entire mathematical and astronomical system into 121 verses of Sanskrit poetry.

His most lasting contribution to this course is the invention of the sine function. The Greeks worked with chords — the full straight line cutting across a circle. Aryabhata introduced the jya-ardha (half bowstring), later shortened to jya, which measures half the chord of double the angle. This is exactly what we now call sine. The word itself travelled through Arabic (jiba, misread as jaib meaning "fold") into Latin (sinus), giving us the modern name.

Beyond trigonometry, Aryabhata developed methods for solving linear Diophantine equations (the kuttaka method, closely related to the extended Euclidean algorithm), computed remarkably accurate astronomical constants, and worked with arithmetic and geometric series. He headed the astronomical observatory at Nalanda, one of the great intellectual centres of the ancient world. His influence spread throughout the Indian subcontinent and, via Arabic translations, into medieval European mathematics.
