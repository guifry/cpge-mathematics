Other notable contributions

  • Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions — If $\gcd(a, d) = 1$, there are infinitely many primes $\equiv a \pmod{d}$.
  • The pigeonhole principle — Formalised by Dirichlet as Schubfachprinzip, used in Diophantine approximation.
  • Dirichlet's unit theorem — Describes the structure of the group of units in a number field.

Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet was born in Duren (then part of the French Empire, now Germany) and studied in Paris, where he attended lectures by Fourier, Laplace, and Poisson. He proved a special case of Fermat's Last Theorem for $n = 5$ at age twenty, earning immediate recognition. He held positions in Breslau and Berlin before succeeding Gauss at Gottingen in 1855, where he died just four years later.

Dirichlet's 1829 paper on Fourier series convergence was a watershed moment for analysis. He gave the first rigorous conditions under which a trigonometric series converges to the function it represents, introducing the Dirichlet kernel and the modern definition of "function" (any assignment of values, not necessarily given by a formula). This single paper reshaped what "function" meant and drove the rigourisation of 19th-century analysis.

In number theory, Dirichlet introduced $L$-functions to prove his theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions — the first use of analytic methods in number theory, founding the field of analytic number theory. He also proved that absolutely convergent series can be rearranged freely, in contrast to conditionally convergent ones. His Vorlesungen uber Zahlentheorie, edited posthumously by Dedekind, became a standard reference for generations. Dirichlet was known for conceptual clarity over computational virtuosity — Minkowski said he embodied the principle that "in mathematics, the art of posing problems is more important than solving them."
