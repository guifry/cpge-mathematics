Other notable contributions

  • Cayley's formula — The number of labelled trees on $n$ vertices is $n^{n-2}$.
  • Cayley–Menger determinant — A determinant characterising when distances can be realised by points in Euclidean space.
  • Invariant theory — Systematic study of algebraic forms unchanged under linear substitutions, with Sylvester.

Arthur Cayley was born in Richmond, Surrey, and showed extraordinary mathematical talent from childhood. He entered Trinity College, Cambridge at seventeen, became Senior Wrangler in 1842, and was elected a Fellow. When his fellowship expired, he practised law for fourteen years — writing over 250 mathematical papers in his spare time — before returning to Cambridge in 1863 as the first Sadleirian Professor of Pure Mathematics.

Cayley's 1858 paper "A Memoir on the Theory of Matrices" introduced matrices as algebraic objects in their own right, not merely as notational devices for systems of equations. He defined matrix addition, multiplication, and inversion, proved the Cayley–Hamilton theorem for $2 \times 2$ and $3 \times 3$ cases, and conjectured it in general. Hamilton later verified higher-dimensional cases, and Frobenius gave the first complete proof.

In group theory, Cayley's 1854 paper gave the first abstract definition of a group (though he worked mainly with finite groups) and proved that every group can be represented as a group of permutations. His collaboration with Sylvester on invariant theory produced a vast body of work that influenced 19th-century algebra profoundly. Cayley published 966 papers over his career — one of the most prolific mathematicians in history — covering nearly every branch of pure mathematics then known.
