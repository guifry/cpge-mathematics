Pafnuty Chebyshev

1821–1894

Russian

Probability, number theory, approximation theory

Theorems in this course

  • Chebyshev's inequality — Phase 12, page 5

Other notable contributions

  • Chebyshev polynomials — the optimal polynomial approximation basis, minimising the maximum error on $[-1, 1]$.
  • Bertrand–Chebyshev theorem — proved that for any $n > 1$, there is always a prime between $n$ and $2n$.
  • Chebyshev linkage — a mechanical linkage that converts rotational motion to approximately linear motion, used in steam engines.

Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev was born in Okatovo, a village in the Kaluga region of Russia. He studied at Moscow University and spent most of his career at the University of Saint Petersburg, where he became the founding figure of the Petersburg mathematical school. His students included Markov and Lyapunov, who carried forward his probabilistic and analytic programme.

Chebyshev's inequality, proved in 1867, is one of the simplest and most powerful tools in probability: the probability of deviating more than $k$ standard deviations from the mean is at most $1/k^2$. It requires only that the variance exists — no assumptions about the shape of the distribution. Chebyshev proved it as part of his effort to establish the law of large numbers in full generality, and it remains the standard tool for quick tail estimates when nothing else is known about a distribution.

In number theory, Chebyshev made major progress toward the prime number theorem. He proved (1850) that if the ratio $\pi(n)/(n/\ln n)$ has a limit, it must be 1, and he established tight bounds on the prime-counting function. He also proved Bertrand's postulate: there is always a prime between $n$ and $2n$. The prime number theorem itself was not proved until 1896, by Hadamard and de la Vallée-Poussin.

In approximation theory, Chebyshev introduced the polynomials that bear his name, which solve the minimax approximation problem. This work founded the field of best approximation in the uniform norm and deeply influenced numerical analysis. Bienaymé independently discovered the inequality that bears Chebyshev's name, so it is sometimes called the Bienaymé–Chebyshev inequality. Chebyshev died in Saint Petersburg in 1894.
