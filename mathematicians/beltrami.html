Eugenio Beltrami

1835–1900

Italian

Differential geometry, mathematical physics

Theorems in this course

  • Singular value decomposition (co-discoverer, 1873) — Phase 13, page 4

Other notable contributions

  • Beltrami–Klein model — first concrete model of hyperbolic geometry, proving its consistency relative to Euclidean geometry.
  • Beltrami’s pseudosphere — showed that surfaces of constant negative curvature realise hyperbolic geometry locally.
  • Laplace–Beltrami operator — generalisation of the Laplacian to Riemannian manifolds, fundamental in differential geometry and physics.

Eugenio Beltrami was born in Cremona, in the Lombardy region of what was then the Austrian Empire. He began studying mathematics at the University of Pavia under Francesco Brioschi but was expelled for political agitation during the Italian unification movement. He later completed his education at Milan and held positions at Bologna, Pisa, Pavia, and Rome, eventually becoming president of the Accademia dei Lincei and a senator of the Kingdom of Italy.

Beltrami's most celebrated work concerns non-Euclidean geometry. In 1868 he published Saggio di interpretazione della geometria non-euclidea, in which he showed that hyperbolic geometry could be modelled on surfaces of constant negative curvature (the pseudosphere). This was the first proof that non-Euclidean geometry is consistent if Euclidean geometry is — a result that resolved decades of controversy about the legitimacy of Lobachevsky's and Bolyai's work.

In 1873, Beltrami independently discovered the singular value decomposition for square real matrices, motivated by problems in the theory of bilinear forms. Camille Jordan obtained the same result in 1874 by a different method. The SVD was later extended to rectangular matrices by Autonne (1915) and its optimal approximation properties were established by Eckart and Young (1936).

Beltrami also made lasting contributions to mathematical physics, including the Laplace–Beltrami operator and work on elasticity theory and electromagnetism. He died in Rome in 1900, one of the most distinguished Italian mathematicians of the 19th century.
