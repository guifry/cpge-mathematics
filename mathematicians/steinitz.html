Ernst Steinitz

1871–1928

German

Algebra, field theory, linear algebra

Theorems in this course

  • Steinitz exchange lemma — Phase 6, page 2

Other notable contributions

  • Algebraische Theorie der Körper — the 1910 paper that axiomatised field theory, defining prime fields, perfect fields, and transcendence degree.
  • Steinitz theorem on polyhedra — a graph is the edge-skeleton of a convex polyhedron if and only if it is 3-connected and planar.
  • Algebraic closure — proved that every field has an algebraic closure, unique up to isomorphism.

Ernst Steinitz was born in Laurahütte, Silesia (now Siemianowice Śląskie, Poland). He studied at the universities of Breslau, Berlin, and finally earned his doctorate at Breslau in 1894. After positions in Breslau and Berlin, he became professor at the Technische Hochschule in Breslau, where he spent most of his career.

Steinitz's most celebrated work is his 1910 paper Algebraische Theorie der Körper, which established the axiomatic foundations of field theory. This paper introduced concepts that became standard in abstract algebra: the characteristic of a field, prime fields, separable and inseparable extensions, and the notion of transcendence degree. It was one of the first works to treat algebra in the modern axiomatic style that would later be championed by Emmy Noether and her school.

For our course, the key result is his 1913 exchange lemma, which settled a question that had been assumed without proof: that every basis of a given vector space has the same number of elements. Before Steinitz, the well-definedness of dimension was taken for granted. His lemma provides the rigorous foundation, and the proof technique — systematically swapping vectors between a basis and a spanning set — remains a staple of linear algebra courses worldwide.

Steinitz died relatively young at 57 in Kiel, where he had moved in 1920. Despite a modest publication list, his influence on 20th-century algebra was profound. His axiomatisation of fields paved the way for the abstract algebraic revolution of the 1920s and 1930s.
