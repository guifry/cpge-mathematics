Other notable contributions

  • Intuitionism — founded a philosophy of mathematics rejecting the law of excluded middle and non-constructive proofs.
  • Invariance of domain — proved that a continuous injective map from an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ is an open map (1911).
  • Topological invariance of dimension — proved that $\mathbb{R}^m$ and $\mathbb{R}^n$ are not homeomorphic when $m \neq n$ (1911).

Luitzen Egbertus Jan Brouwer — known universally as "Brouwer" — was born in Overschie, a small town near Rotterdam. He completed his doctorate at the University of Amsterdam in 1907 and spent his entire career there. In his twenties he produced a series of revolutionary results in topology that transformed the subject from a collection of intuitions into a rigorous discipline.

His fixed-point theorem — every continuous function from a closed ball to itself has a fixed point — is one of the most applied results in all of mathematics. It underlies existence proofs across economics (Nash equilibrium), differential equations (Peano's theorem), and numerical analysis. Ironically, Brouwer himself came to reject the very proof techniques that established the theorem: his later philosophical programme, intuitionism, insisted that only constructive proofs are valid.

The tension between Brouwer the topologist and Brouwer the intuitionist defined much of twentieth-century philosophy of mathematics. His feud with David Hilbert over the foundations of mathematics became legendary — Hilbert eventually had Brouwer removed from the editorial board of Mathematische Annalen. Despite the controversy, Brouwer's topological work remains indispensable. He died in Blaricum in 1966 after being struck by a vehicle.
