Other notable contributions

  • Kolmogorov complexity — defined the algorithmic complexity of a string as the length of its shortest description, founding algorithmic information theory.
  • KAM theorem — with Arnold and Moser, proved the persistence of quasi-periodic orbits in nearly integrable Hamiltonian systems.
  • Kolmogorov's turbulence theory — the 1941 theory predicting the energy spectrum of turbulent flow, one of the most successful physical theories of the twentieth century.

Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov was born in Tambov in 1903 and raised by his aunts after his mother's death. He entered Moscow State University at seventeen and never left, spending his entire career there. He is widely regarded as one of the greatest mathematicians of the twentieth century, with fundamental contributions to an astonishing range of fields.

Kolmogorov's most far-reaching achievement was his 1933 monograph Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung, which axiomatised probability theory using Lebesgue's measure theory. His three axioms — non-negativity, normalisation, and countable additivity — unified 280 years of probabilistic reasoning into a rigorous mathematical framework. This course builds all of Phase 12 on Kolmogorov's foundations.

Before his work on probability, the young Kolmogorov had already constructed an integrable function whose Fourier series diverges everywhere (1926), settling a major question in harmonic analysis and showing that some regularity beyond bare integrability is essential for Fourier convergence. He later proved the strong law of large numbers in full generality (1930), one of the deepest results in probability.

His interests ranged from turbulence and dynamical systems to algorithmic complexity and the foundations of mathematical logic. He supervised over 60 doctoral students and shaped Soviet mathematics for half a century. He died in Moscow in 1987.
