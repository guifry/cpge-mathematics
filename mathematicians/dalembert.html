Other notable contributions

  • D'Alembert's wave equation — derived and solved the one-dimensional wave equation $\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$, one of the founding results of mathematical physics.
  • D'Alembert's principle — reformulated Newton's second law for constrained systems, reducing dynamics to statics and laying groundwork for Lagrangian mechanics.
  • Co-editor of the Encyclopédie — with Diderot, edited the great Enlightenment encyclopaedia, writing the mathematical and scientific articles himself.

D'Alembert was found as a baby on the steps of the church of Saint-Jean-le-Rond in Paris in 1717, abandoned by his mother. He was raised by a glazier's wife, whom he supported financially for the rest of her life. Despite this inauspicious start, he became one of the leading mathematicians and philosophers of the French Enlightenment.

His mathematical career began with a prize-winning memoir on fluid mechanics in 1741. He went on to make foundational contributions to partial differential equations, deriving and solving the wave equation in 1747. His work on vibrating strings sparked a famous debate with Euler about what constitutes a "function" — a question that would not be fully resolved until Fourier and Dirichlet in the 19th century.

D'Alembert's ratio test for convergence of series (1768) was among the first systematic convergence criteria. His attempted proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra (1746), while incomplete, was influential enough that the theorem is still called the "d'Alembert-Gauss theorem" in France.

Beyond pure mathematics, d'Alembert served as co-editor of the Encyclopédie alongside Diderot, writing the famous Discours préliminaire that outlined the Enlightenment's vision of knowledge. He was elected to the Académie des sciences at 23 and became its permanent secretary. He never married and lived modestly, turning down offers from Frederick the Great and Catherine the Great to lead their respective academies.
