Brook Taylor

1685–1731

English

Calculus, mathematical physics, perspective geometry

Theorems in this course

  • Taylor's theorem (polynomial approximation with remainder) — Phase 4, page 1

Other notable contributions

  • Integration by parts — one of the first to state and use the technique systematically.
  • Linear perspective — his Linear Perspective (1715) and New Principles of Linear Perspective (1719) gave the first rigorous mathematical treatment of artistic perspective.
  • Vibrating string problem — derived the fundamental frequency of a vibrating string, one of the earliest results in mathematical physics.

Brook Taylor published the result that bears his name in his 1715 book Methodus Incrementorum Directa et Inversa. The Taylor series expansion — expressing a function as an infinite polynomial centred at a point — was the centrepiece of the work, though Taylor stated it without a remainder term and without clear conditions for convergence. The critical addition of the remainder came from Lagrange (1797), which turned the series from a formal device into a quantitative approximation tool.

Taylor was educated at St John's College, Cambridge, and was elected to the Royal Society at 27. He was a man of broad interests: mathematics, physics, philosophy, and art. His work on perspective was groundbreaking — he introduced the concept of the vanishing point and gave the first complete mathematical theory of how three-dimensional scenes project onto a two-dimensional canvas.

His mathematical career was remarkably brief. The Methodus Incrementorum was published when he was 30, and he largely withdrew from mathematics afterwards, turning to philosophy and religion. He died at 46, having produced only a handful of mathematical works — but the one that mattered changed analysis permanently.

Taylor was not the first to notice that smooth functions could be expanded as power series — Newton and Gregory had used special cases earlier, and the Kerala school had the series for sine and cosine centuries before. But Taylor gave the first general formula for the coefficients in terms of derivatives, making the expansion systematic rather than ad hoc.
