Richard Dedekind was born in Braunschweig, the same city as Gauss, and studied at Gottingen where he was Gauss's last doctoral student (1852). He then attended Dirichlet's lectures, which profoundly shaped his mathematical outlook. After a brief spell at the ETH Zurich, he returned to Braunschweig in 1862 and remained there for the rest of his life, at the local polytechnic — a position far below his abilities.

Dedekind's greatest contribution to analysis came from a pedagogical frustration. While teaching calculus in Zurich, he realised that nobody had a rigorous definition of the real numbers. His solution, published in 1872 as Stetigkeit und irrationale Zahlen, was the Dedekind cut: partition the rationals into two halves $(L, R)$ where every element of $L$ is less than every element of $R$. Each cut is a real number. This construction fills in precisely the holes that density leaves behind.

In algebra, Dedekind's invention of ideals — motivated by the failure of unique factorisation in rings like $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ — was arguably the most important structural innovation of 19th-century algebra. He also contributed to the early development of lattice theory and gave a rigorous treatment of the integers based on set-theoretic principles. Dedekind was famously modest, meticulous, and long-lived — he outlived both his doctoral supervisor (Gauss) and his intellectual mentor (Dirichlet) by over half a century.
