Maurice Fréchet

1878–1973

French

Topology, functional analysis, probability

Theorems in this course

  • Metric spaces (definition, 1906) — Phase 9, page 1

Other notable contributions

  • Fréchet spaces — locally convex topological vector spaces metrised by a translation-invariant metric, generalising Banach spaces.
  • Fréchet derivative — the correct generalisation of the derivative to maps between normed vector spaces.
  • Fréchet distribution — one of the three extreme value distributions, fundamental in statistics and risk analysis.

Maurice René Fréchet was born in Maligny, Yonne, in Burgundy. He studied at the École Normale Supérieure and completed his doctorate in 1906 under Jacques Hadamard. His thesis, Sur quelques points du calcul fonctionnel, is one of the most consequential doctoral dissertations in the history of mathematics: it introduced metric spaces, abstract topological concepts, and the notion of compactness in general settings.

Before Fréchet, the key ideas of analysis — limits, continuity, completeness, compactness — were tied to specific contexts: real numbers, Euclidean space, or particular function spaces. Hilbert had studied $\ell^2$ spaces, Volterra had considered spaces of curves, but no one had identified the common structure. Fréchet's stroke of abstraction was to define a metric space as any set with a distance function satisfying three axioms (positivity, symmetry, triangle inequality) and then to develop analysis in this generality.

The impact was immediate and lasting. Hausdorff built his general topology on Fréchet's foundations. Banach axiomatised complete normed spaces and Fréchet himself coined the name "Banach space" in 1928 (before that they were called "spaces of type (B)"). Fréchet also introduced the derivative for maps between normed spaces (the Fréchet derivative), which is now the standard definition in infinite-dimensional calculus.

Later in his career, Fréchet worked extensively on probability and statistics, contributing to the theory of extreme values (the Fréchet distribution). He held positions at Poitiers, Strasbourg, and finally Paris. He lived to ninety-five, dying in 1973, having witnessed his abstractions become the universal language of modern analysis.
