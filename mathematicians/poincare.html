Other notable contributions

  • Poincaré conjecture — every simply connected, closed 3-manifold is homeomorphic to the 3-sphere. Proved by Perelman in 2003; one of the seven Millennium Prize Problems.
  • Poincaré recurrence theorem — in a measure-preserving dynamical system, almost every point returns arbitrarily close to its initial position.
  • Foundations of algebraic topology — Poincaré invented homology theory, the fundamental group, and the concept of a manifold, creating topology as a discipline.

Jules Henri Poincaré was born in Nancy in 1854 into a prominent family — his cousin Raymond became President of France. He studied at the École Polytechnique and the École des Mines, and was appointed to the Sorbonne at thirty-two. He is often called the last universalist: a mathematician who made fundamental contributions to nearly every branch of the subject.

Poincaré's work on differential equations and dynamical systems, carried out between 1881 and 1886, created an entirely new approach to ODEs. Rather than seeking explicit solutions (which are rarely available for nonlinear systems), Poincaré studied the qualitative behaviour of trajectories: their long-term fate, their stability, the existence of periodic orbits. The Poincaré-Bendixson theorem — stating that in the plane, a bounded trajectory that avoids equilibria must approach a periodic orbit — is the centrepiece of this theory.

His study of the three-body problem led him to discover what we now call chaos: sensitive dependence on initial conditions in deterministic systems. He also founded algebraic topology (inventing homology, the fundamental group, and Poincaré duality), made major contributions to complex analysis and number theory, and anticipated special relativity. His philosophical writings on the nature of mathematics and science remain influential.

Poincaré died suddenly in 1912 at the age of fifty-eight, leaving behind a body of work so vast that mathematicians were still absorbing its implications decades later.
