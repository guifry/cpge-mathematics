Leopold Kronecker

1823–1891

German

Algebra, number theory, mathematical logic

Theorems in this course

  • Classification of finite abelian groups — Phase 10, page 5

Other notable contributions

  • Kronecker’s theorem on diophantine approximation — if $\alpha$ is irrational, the fractional parts $\{n\alpha\}$ are dense in $[0,1]$.
  • Kronecker product — the tensor product of matrices, fundamental in multilinear algebra and quantum mechanics.
  • Constructive mathematics — championed the view that mathematics should be grounded in the natural numbers, famously declaring “God made the integers; all else is the work of man.”

Leopold Kronecker was born in Liegnitz, Silesia (now Legnica, Poland). He studied under Kummer and Dirichlet in Berlin, earning his doctorate in 1845 with a thesis on algebraic number theory. After spending several years managing a family banking business, he returned to mathematics full-time and became one of the dominant figures in Berlin mathematics.

Kronecker’s mathematical work centred on number theory and algebra. His 1870 proof of the classification of finite abelian groups — showing that every finite abelian group decomposes uniquely as a direct product of cyclic groups of prime power order — was a landmark in abstract algebra. The result was proved independently by Frobenius and Stickelberger in 1879.

The Kronecker delta $\delta_{ij}$, ubiquitous in linear algebra and analysis, bears his name and appears throughout this course: in orthonormality conditions for inner product spaces, in Fourier series coefficients, and in matrix computations.

Kronecker is equally famous for his philosophical positions. He was deeply sceptical of Cantor’s set theory and Weierstrass’s analytic methods, insisting that all mathematics should be constructible from the integers in finitely many steps. This put him at odds with most of the mathematical establishment. His programme anticipated intuitionism and constructivism by several decades, though his specific objections were often seen as obstructionist rather than productive. He died in Berlin in 1891, leaving a legacy that is as much philosophical as mathematical.
