Lennart Carleson

1928–

Swedish

Harmonic analysis, complex analysis, dynamical systems

Theorems in this course

  • Carleson's theorem — Fourier series of $L^2$ functions converge almost everywhere — Phase 11, page 2

Other notable contributions

  • Corona theorem — solved the corona problem for the algebra $H^\infty$ of bounded analytic functions on the disc (1962).
  • Hénon attractor — with Benedicks, proved the existence of strange attractors for the Hénon map, a landmark in dynamical systems (1991).
  • Carleson measure — introduced the concept now fundamental in complex and harmonic analysis.

Lennart Carleson was born in Stockholm in 1928 and studied under Arne Beurling at Uppsala. His early work was in complex analysis, but it is his 1966 proof of Lusin's conjecture that made him one of the most celebrated analysts of the twentieth century.

The conjecture, open since 1913, asked whether the Fourier series of every square-integrable function converges almost everywhere. Kolmogorov had shown in 1926 that for merely integrable functions the answer is no — he constructed an $L^1$ function whose Fourier series diverges everywhere. For $L^2$ functions the question remained open for forty years. Carleson's proof, over thirty pages of delicate estimation, introduced ideas that reshaped harmonic analysis. Richard Hunt later extended the result to all $L^p$ with $p > 1$.

Carleson's other achievements are equally striking. His solution of the corona problem (1962) was a tour de force in function algebras. In the 1990s, with Benedicks, he proved the existence of strange attractors in the Hénon family, bringing rigour to a central conjecture in dynamical systems. He served as president of the International Mathematical Union and received the Abel Prize in 2006, the Wolf Prize in 1992, and the Lomonosov Gold Medal. He remains one of the few mathematicians to have solved multiple long-standing open problems across different fields.
