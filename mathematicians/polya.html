George Pólya

1887–1985

Hungarian-American

Combinatorics, probability, mathematical pedagogy

Theorems in this course

  • Coined the name “Central Limit Theorem” — Phase 12, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • Pólya enumeration theorem — counts the number of distinct objects under group symmetries, generalising Burnside’s lemma.
  • Pólya’s random walk theorem — a simple random walk on $\mathbb{Z}^d$ is recurrent for $d \leq 2$ and transient for $d \geq 3$.
  • How to Solve It — his 1945 book on mathematical problem-solving heuristics, one of the best-selling mathematics books ever written.

George Pólya (born György Pólya) was born in Budapest into a family of Jewish origin. He studied at the University of Budapest and later at Göttingen and Paris before joining ETH Zürich in 1914, where he remained until 1940. He then moved to the United States, spending the rest of his career at Stanford University.

Pólya’s mathematical contributions span an extraordinary range. In combinatorics, his enumeration theorem (1937) provides a systematic method for counting distinct configurations under symmetry — a cornerstone of combinatorial mathematics. In probability, his random walk theorem (1921) established the beautiful dichotomy between dimensions: a drunk man will eventually return home in one or two dimensions, but not in three. In analysis, he worked with Szegő on inequalities and orthogonal polynomials, producing the influential two-volume Problems and Theorems in Analysis.

For this course, Pólya’s most relevant contribution is linguistic rather than mathematical: in 1920, he coined the term “Central Limit Theorem” (zentraler Grenzwertsatz), reflecting the theorem’s central importance in probability theory. The name stuck and is now universal.

Pólya is equally celebrated as a teacher and expositor. How to Solve It (1945) distilled decades of experience into a practical guide for mathematical problem-solving, introducing heuristics like “draw a picture,” “solve a simpler problem first,” and “look for a pattern.” The book has sold over a million copies and influenced generations of mathematicians, scientists, and engineers. He died in Palo Alto at the age of ninety-seven.
