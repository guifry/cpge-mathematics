Sunzi (literally "Master Sun") is the author of the Sunzi Suanjing (Master Sun's Mathematical Manual), a Chinese mathematical text dating from somewhere between the 3rd and 5th centuries AD. He should not be confused with the military strategist Sun Tzu who wrote The Art of War — they are different people separated by several centuries.

Almost nothing is known about Sunzi's life. His identity, dates, and position are all uncertain. What survives is his book, which is organised into three chapters: the first on units of measurement and basic arithmetic, the second on practical calculation methods, and the third on a collection of problems — one of which became one of the most important results in number theory.

The problem that earned Sunzi his place in mathematical history appears in the third chapter: "There are certain things whose number is unknown. If we count them by threes, we have two left over; by fives, we have three left over; and by sevens, two are left over. How many things are there?" This is the earliest known statement of what we now call the Chinese Remainder Theorem. Sunzi gave a solution method (the answer is 23), but a fully general algorithm and proof came much later, from the 13th-century mathematician Qin Jiushao. The theorem is now fundamental in number theory, algebra, and computer science — it underpins everything from RSA cryptography to fast integer arithmetic.
