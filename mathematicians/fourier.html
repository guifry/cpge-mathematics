Joseph Fourier

1768–1830

French

Analysis, mathematical physics, heat conduction

Other notable contributions

  • Heat equation — derived the partial differential equation governing heat conduction, the first systematic study of diffusion.
  • Fourier transform — generalised the Fourier series from periodic functions to all of $\mathbb{R}$, foundational to signal processing and quantum mechanics.
  • Greenhouse effect — first scientist to describe how the atmosphere traps heat, anticipating climate science by two centuries.

Fourier was orphaned at nine, educated by Benedictine monks, and nearly executed during the French Revolution. He survived to accompany Napoleon to Egypt as a scientific adviser, govern a province in southern France, and revolutionise mathematical physics.

In 1807, he presented a memoir to the Institut de France claiming that any periodic function — even one with sharp corners — could be decomposed into a sum of sines and cosines. The review committee of Lagrange, Laplace, and Legendre was deeply sceptical. Lagrange flatly denied the claim. But Fourier was right, mostly, and the quest to understand the exceptions drove a century of mathematical progress.

His full treatise, Théorie analytique de la chaleur (1822), gave the first systematic treatment of the heat equation and its solution by trigonometric series. The key insight was that each frequency component evolves independently: decompose the initial temperature into Fourier modes, let each decay at its own rate, and sum up. This principle — decompose, solve each piece independently, reassemble — became the template for solving linear PDEs across all of physics.

Fourier's work contained no rigorous convergence proofs; that gap was filled by Dirichlet (1829). But the questions Fourier raised — when does a trigonometric series converge? what counts as a function? — forced mathematicians to develop the modern theory of integration (Riemann, Lebesgue), set theory (Cantor), and rigorous analysis (Weierstrass). Phase 11 of this course is devoted entirely to his ideas.
