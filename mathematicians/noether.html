Other notable contributions

  • Noether's theorem (physics) — every differentiable symmetry of the action of a physical system corresponds to a conservation law. Conservation of energy comes from time symmetry; conservation of momentum from spatial symmetry.
  • Noetherian rings — rings satisfying the ascending chain condition on ideals, the foundation of commutative algebra and algebraic geometry.
  • Brauer-Noether theorem — contributions to the theory of algebras over number fields, connecting representation theory to class field theory.

Amalie Emmy Noether was born in Erlangen in 1882, the daughter of the mathematician Max Noether. She was not permitted to enrol formally at the University of Erlangen until 1904, when the rules barring women were relaxed. She completed her doctorate in 1907 under Paul Gordan and moved to Göttingen in 1915 at the invitation of Hilbert and Klein.

At Göttingen, Noether proved the theorem that physicists consider her greatest: every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity. But her deepest impact was on pure mathematics. In the 1920s, she single-handedly transformed algebra from a collection of computational techniques into a structural, axiomatic discipline. Her isomorphism theorems, her work on ideals in commutative rings, and her development of module theory created the language that all of modern algebra speaks.

The concept of a Noetherian ring — one in which every ascending chain of ideals stabilises — became the foundation of commutative algebra and algebraic geometry. Her approach to the structure theorem for finitely generated abelian groups, viewing it as a special case of modules over a PID, revealed the deep unity between number theory and polynomial algebra.

Dismissed from Göttingen in 1933 by the Nazi regime, Noether emigrated to Bryn Mawr College in the United States, where she continued to teach and inspire. She died unexpectedly in 1935, aged 53. Einstein wrote in the New York Times that she was "the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced since the higher education of women began."
