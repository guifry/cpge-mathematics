c. 624–546 BC

Greek, Miletus

Geometry

Other notable contributions

  • Thales' theorem (circle) — proved that any angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle.
  • Prediction of a solar eclipse — reportedly predicted the solar eclipse of 585 BC, though the method he used is debated.
  • Natural philosophy — proposed that water is the fundamental substance of all things, founding the tradition of seeking natural rather than supernatural explanations.

Thales of Miletus, active around 600 BC, is traditionally regarded as the first mathematician — or, more precisely, the first person known to have proved a geometric theorem rather than merely observing a pattern. He is also counted among the Seven Sages of Greece and is considered the first Western philosopher.

The story that opens this course is attributed to Thales: he measured the height of the Great Pyramid of Giza by comparing the lengths of shadows. When his shadow equalled his height, the pyramid's shadow equalled the pyramid's height. More generally, when the ratio of his height to his shadow was known, the same ratio applied to the pyramid. This is the intercept theorem (also called Thales' theorem in Continental Europe), and it is the first theorem in Phase 0.

Thales likely travelled to Egypt and Babylon, absorbing their practical mathematics and bringing it back to Greece, where he and his successors transformed it into something new: a deductive science. The Egyptians knew how to measure fields; the Babylonians could solve quadratic equations. But it was the Greeks, beginning with Thales, who demanded proof — logical demonstration that a result holds universally, not merely for the cases tested. This shift from empirical to deductive mathematics is arguably the most important intellectual transition in human history, and Thales stands at its origin.
