Other notable contributions

  • Lagrangian mechanics — reformulated Newtonian mechanics using generalised coordinates and the principle of least action in Mécanique analytique (1788).
  • Four-square theorem — proved every positive integer is a sum of at most four squares (1770).
  • Lagrange interpolation — the polynomial of degree $n$ passing through $n+1$ given points, foundational in numerical analysis.

Born Giuseppe Luigi Lagrangia in Turin, Lagrange was largely self-taught in mathematics, inspired by reading Euler. By 19 he was professor of mathematics at the Royal Artillery School in Turin, and by 30 he succeeded Euler at the Berlin Academy, where Frederick the Great wanted "the greatest mathematician in Europe" at his court.

Lagrange's 1771 Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations marks the birth of group theory. He studied which permutations of roots leave resolvent expressions invariant — precisely the group-theoretic structure that Galois would later formalise. His observation that the order of a "sub-collection" of permutations always divides the total became Lagrange's theorem, a cornerstone of finite group theory.

His Mécanique analytique (1788) reformulated all of mechanics using calculus alone, famously boasting that the book contained not a single diagram. The Lagrange multiplier method, introduced there for constrained optimisation, is now ubiquitous across mathematics, physics, economics, and machine learning.

After moving to Paris, Lagrange served on the committee that designed the metric system and chaired the review committee that assessed Fourier's scandalous 1807 memoir on heat conduction. As the most vocal critic, he declared that discontinuous functions could never be represented by sums of sines — a position that was wrong but perfectly reasonable given the absence of convergence theory. He survived the Revolution despite his foreign birth, reportedly protected by his reputation. He died in 1813, saying: "Death is not to be feared."
