George Gabriel Stokes

1819–1903

Irish-British

Mathematical physics, analysis, fluid dynamics

Theorems in this course

  • Uniform convergence (1848) — Phase 5, page 6
  • Stokes' theorem — Phase 7, page 8

Other notable contributions

  • Navier–Stokes equations — The fundamental equations of fluid dynamics, governing the motion of viscous fluids. Whether smooth solutions always exist remains a Millennium Prize Problem.
  • Stokes's law — The drag force on a sphere moving through a viscous fluid: $F = 6\pi\mu rv$.
  • Fluorescence — First described the phenomenon of fluorescence and the Stokes shift in wavelength.

George Gabriel Stokes was born in Skreen, County Sligo, Ireland, the youngest son of a Church of Ireland rector. He moved to England for school and entered Pembroke College, Cambridge, where he graduated as Senior Wrangler in 1841. He was appointed Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge in 1849 — Newton's chair — and held it for over fifty years.

Stokes was primarily a mathematical physicist, but his work had deep consequences for pure mathematics. In 1848, independently of Seidel, he identified the concept of uniform convergence and recognised that Cauchy's theorem on limits of continuous functions required this stronger condition. This insight, though published only in passing, was crucial to the rigourisation of analysis that Weierstrass later completed.

Stokes' theorem — relating the integral of a curl over a surface to a line integral around its boundary — appeared as an examination problem on the 1854 Cambridge Mathematical Tripos, set by Stokes based on a result communicated to him by Lord Kelvin in a letter. Neither Stokes nor Kelvin published a proof; it was left as an exercise for students. The theorem unifies Green's theorem, the divergence theorem, and the fundamental theorem of calculus as special cases of a single result about integrating differential forms over manifolds with boundary. Stokes was also a prolific contributor to optics, hydrodynamics, and geodesy, and served as president of the Royal Society from 1885 to 1890.
