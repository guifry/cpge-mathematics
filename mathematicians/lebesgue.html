Other notable contributions

  • Dominated convergence theorem — the most powerful tool for interchanging limits and integrals, vastly more flexible than Riemann's approach.
  • Lebesgue differentiation theorem — almost every point of an integrable function is a Lebesgue point, where the function equals the limit of its averages over shrinking balls.
  • Lebesgue's characterisation of Riemann integrability — a bounded function on $[a,b]$ is Riemann integrable iff its set of discontinuities has Lebesgue measure zero.

Henri Léon Lebesgue was born in Beauvais in 1875. His father was a typesetter and died of tuberculosis when Henri was young; his mother and teachers recognised his mathematical talent early and supported his education. He entered the École Normale Supérieure in 1894 and completed his doctoral thesis in 1902, a work that would reshape mathematics.

Lebesgue's thesis introduced what is now called the Lebesgue integral. The idea was revolutionary: instead of partitioning the domain (as Riemann did), Lebesgue partitioned the range, measuring the "size" of the set where the function takes each value. This required a new notion of "size" — Lebesgue measure — and the result was an integral that could handle far more functions than Riemann's, with vastly better convergence properties.

The impact was enormous. Lebesgue's theory made the $L^p$ spaces possible, provided the foundation for Kolmogorov's axiomatisation of probability, and gave Fourier analysis the tools it needed to handle convergence questions rigorously. The Riemann-Lebesgue lemma, the dominated convergence theorem, and the Lebesgue number lemma all bear his name. He was elected to the Académie des Sciences in 1922 and to the Collège de France in 1921. He died in Paris in 1941, having transformed the foundations of analysis.
