Robert Axelrod is a professor of political science and public policy at the University of Michigan. Though not a mathematician by training — his doctorate is in political science from Yale — his work on the evolution of cooperation has had a profound impact on game theory, evolutionary biology, and computer science.

In 1980 Axelrod ran a celebrated computer tournament: he invited game theorists to submit strategies for the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, then pitted them against one another in round-robin competition. The winner was tit-for-tat, submitted by the psychologist Anatol Rapoport — the simplest strategy in the tournament. It cooperates on the first move, then mirrors whatever the opponent did on the previous move. A second tournament in 1984, with more sophisticated entries, produced the same result.

Axelrod's 1984 book The Evolution of Cooperation drew on these tournaments to argue that cooperation can emerge among self-interested agents without central authority, provided they interact repeatedly and can recognise each other. The book influenced fields far beyond political science: evolutionary biologists used its framework to explain reciprocal altruism, and computer scientists adopted its models for distributed systems. Axelrod later extended his approach to cultural dynamics, norm formation, and the spread of cooperation in complex networks.
