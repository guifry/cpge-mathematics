Paolo Ruffini

1765–1822

Italian

Algebra, medicine, philosophy

Theorems in this course

  • Abel-Ruffini theorem: attempted proof of quintic impossibility (1799) — Phase 10, page 8

Other notable contributions

  • Ruffini's rule — an efficient method for polynomial division by a linear factor, a precursor to synthetic division still taught today.
  • Philosophy of probability — wrote on the impossibility of certain types of mathematical certainty, anticipating later developments in epistemology.
  • Medical work — as a physician, treated typhoid patients during the Napoleonic Wars, contracting the disease himself; his medical and mathematical careers ran in parallel throughout his life.

Ruffini was both a professor of mathematics and a practising physician in Modena, a combination that divided his time but perhaps sharpened his intellectual boldness. In 1799, he published Teoria generale delle equazioni, in which he attempted to prove that the general quintic equation cannot be solved by radicals — the first person to claim this impossibility and attempt a proof.

His proof, running over 500 pages, was largely correct in spirit but had gaps. The key issue was that he assumed, without proving, certain properties of permutation groups — specifically, that the only permutation groups of five elements that can arise from radical solutions have a particular structure. He was right about the conclusion, but the argument was incomplete.

Ruffini sent his proof to several leading mathematicians, including Lagrange, seeking validation. Most ignored it. Lagrange never responded. Only Cauchy acknowledged the work, writing in 1821 that he considered Ruffini's theorem "a very important truth." But by then, Abel had already given a complete proof (1824), and Ruffini's contribution was overshadowed.

The theorem is now called Abel-Ruffini, giving credit to both: Ruffini for the insight and the first serious attempt, Abel for the rigorous proof. Ruffini's key contribution was recognising that the problem of solving equations by radicals is fundamentally connected to the theory of permutations — an insight that Galois would develop into a complete theory. Ruffini died in 1822, having caught typhoid while treating patients during an epidemic, two years before Abel's proof appeared.
