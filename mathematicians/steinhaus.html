Hugo Steinhaus

1887–1972

Polish

Functional analysis, probability, game theory

Theorems in this course

  • Banach–Steinhaus theorem (uniform boundedness principle) — Phase 14, page 5

Other notable contributions

  • Steinhaus theorem — the difference set of a measurable set of positive measure contains an open interval.
  • Ham sandwich theorem — any $n$ measurable sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$ can be simultaneously bisected by a single hyperplane.
  • Fair division — pioneered mathematical methods for dividing resources fairly, including the "last diminisher" procedure.

Hugo Dyonizy Steinhaus was born in Jasło, in what was then the Austro-Hungarian Empire. He studied at the University of Lwów before earning his doctorate under David Hilbert at Göttingen in 1911, making him part of the direct lineage of German mathematical tradition.

Returning to Poland, Steinhaus became a professor at the Jan Kazimierz University in Lwów, where he co-founded what became known as the Lwów School of Mathematics. The school's legendary working style centred on the Scottish Café (Kawiarnia Szkocka), where mathematicians would gather to pose and solve problems, recording them in the famous Scottish Book. Steinhaus made a decisive contribution to this group by discovering Stefan Banach — he overheard the young man discussing the Lebesgue integral in a Kraków park and recruited him into mathematics.

Steinhaus's mathematical interests were remarkably broad. Beyond functional analysis, he made foundational contributions to probability theory, game theory, and the theory of trigonometric series. He was one of the first to apply mathematics to biology and medicine, and his 1944 paper on fair division launched an entire field. His book Mathematical Snapshots (1938) became a classic of mathematical popularisation.

He survived the Second World War in hiding under a false identity. After the war he helped rebuild Polish mathematics at the University of Wrocław, where he worked until his retirement. His partnership with Banach produced results that became cornerstones of functional analysis, most notably the uniform boundedness principle that bears both their names.
