Archimedes

c. 287–212 BC

Greek, Syracuse

Geometry, mechanics, hydrostatics, calculus precursors

  • The Squeeze Theorem — Phase 2, page 2 (method of exhaustion)
  • The Riemann Integral — Phase 3, page 6 (area under the parabola)
  • Multiple Integrals — Phase 7, page 6 (area of a disc via change of variables)

Other notable contributions

  • Archimedes' principle — a body immersed in fluid experiences an upward force equal to the weight of fluid displaced.
  • Measurement of the circle — bounded pi between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7 using inscribed and circumscribed 96-gons.
  • The Sand Reckoner — invented a positional notation to express astronomically large numbers, estimating the grains of sand needed to fill the universe.

Archimedes is the closest the ancient world came to producing a modern mathematician. Born in Syracuse around 287 BC, he studied in Alexandria before returning home, where he spent his life pushing geometry further than anyone before him.

His method of exhaustion — bounding a quantity between ever-tighter upper and lower estimates — is the direct ancestor of the squeeze theorem and, ultimately, the Riemann integral. He used it to compute the area under a parabola, the surface area and volume of a sphere, and the area of a circle, all with proofs that would satisfy a modern analyst. He also anticipated integral calculus by two millennia in his Method of Mechanical Theorems, where he "weighed" infinitesimal slices of solids against each other to guess results before proving them rigorously.

Beyond pure mathematics, Archimedes built war machines for the defence of Syracuse, discovered the law of buoyancy (allegedly in a bath), and designed the compound pulley and the Archimedean screw. He was killed during the Roman siege of Syracuse in 212 BC, reportedly while drawing geometric diagrams in the sand. His tombstone, by his own request, bore a sphere inscribed in a cylinder — his proudest theorem.
