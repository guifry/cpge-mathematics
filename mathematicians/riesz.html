Other notable contributions

  • Riesz-Markov-Kakutani representation theorem — every positive linear functional on $C(X)$ is integration against a regular Borel measure.
  • $L^p$ spaces — Riesz (independently of Fischer) developed the theory of $L^p$ spaces, introducing the $L^p$ norms and proving their completeness.
  • F. and M. Riesz theorem — with his brother Marcel, proved that a measure on the circle whose negative Fourier coefficients all vanish is absolutely continuous.

Frigyes (Frédéric) Riesz was born in Győr, Hungary, in 1880 and became one of the founders of functional analysis. His brother Marcel was also a distinguished mathematician. Riesz spent much of his career at the University of Szeged, which he and Alfréd Haar transformed into a major centre for mathematics.

Riesz's 1907 proof of what is now the Riesz-Fischer theorem — that $L^2$ is complete — was a turning point in analysis. It gave rigorous meaning to the formal manipulations of Fourier series and established that the space of square-integrable functions has the structure of a (complete) inner product space: a Hilbert space. This was one of the founding results of functional analysis.

In 1918, Riesz introduced the abstract concept of a norm on a vector space, giving analysis a geometric language that unified disparate results. He proved his celebrated lemma: in any infinite-dimensional normed space, one can construct a sequence of unit vectors that are all at least distance $1/2$ apart, which implies that the closed unit ball is never compact. This characterisation — a normed space is finite-dimensional if and only if its closed unit ball is compact — is one of the sharpest results in functional analysis.

Riesz also established that continuity and boundedness are equivalent for linear maps between normed spaces, and proved the representation theorem identifying a Hilbert space with its dual. He remained active into his seventies and died in Budapest in 1956.
