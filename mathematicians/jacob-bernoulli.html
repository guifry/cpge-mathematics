Jacob Bernoulli

1655–1705

Swiss

Probability, calculus, combinatorics

Theorems in this course

  • Compound interest limit $(1+1/n)^n \to e$ — Phase 4, page 3
  • Weak law of large numbers — Phase 12, page 6

Other notable contributions

  • Bernoulli numbers — the sequence $B_0, B_1, B_2, \ldots$ arising in the formula for sums of powers $\sum k^n$ and in the Taylor series of $x/(e^x - 1)$.
  • The Bernoulli differential equation — $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n$, reducible to a linear equation by substitution.
  • Catenary curve — proved that a hanging chain forms a hyperbolic cosine, not a parabola as Galileo had conjectured.

Jacob Bernoulli (also known as Jakob or James) was the eldest of the Bernoulli mathematical dynasty. Born in Basel, he defied his father's wish that he become a minister and instead taught himself mathematics, particularly the new calculus of Leibniz. He held the chair of mathematics at the University of Basel from 1687 until his death.

His masterwork, Ars Conjectandi (The Art of Conjecturing), was published posthumously in 1713. It contains the first rigorous proof of the law of large numbers — the theorem that sample averages converge to the true mean as the sample size grows. Bernoulli spent twenty years perfecting this proof. He was acutely aware of its importance: it provides the mathematical justification for why statistics works, why insurance is viable, and why repeated experiments reveal truth.

In analysis, Bernoulli discovered the constant $e$ through the compound interest problem. He asked: what happens to $(1 + 1/n)^n$ as $n \to \infty$? He showed the limit exists and lies between 2 and 3, though he did not compute it precisely — that would come later from Euler.

Jacob had a famously bitter rivalry with his younger brother Johann. They posed and solved problems competitively, sometimes publicly attacking each other's work. Despite the personal acrimony, the rivalry was mathematically productive: both brothers advanced the early calculus significantly. Jacob requested that a logarithmic spiral be engraved on his tombstone, with the inscription Eadem mutata resurgo — "Though changed, I arise the same." The stonemason, unfortunately, carved an Archimedean spiral instead.
