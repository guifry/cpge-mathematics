Blaise Pascal

1623–1662

French

Probability, geometry, physics, philosophy

Theorems in this course

  • Probability correspondence with Fermat (1654) — Phase 12, page 1

Other notable contributions

  • Pascal's triangle — the triangular array of binomial coefficients, which Pascal studied systematically in his Traité du triangle arithmétique (1654).
  • Pascaline (1642) — one of the first mechanical calculators, built when Pascal was 19 to help his father with tax calculations.
  • Pascal's theorem in projective geometry — if a hexagon is inscribed in a conic, the three pairs of opposite sides meet in collinear points. Proved at age 16.

Pascal was a prodigy who made significant contributions to mathematics, physics, and philosophy before dying at 39. His father Étienne, himself an amateur mathematician, educated Blaise at home in Paris, initially forbidding him from studying mathematics — which only intensified the boy's obsession. By sixteen, Pascal had proved his hexagon theorem; by nineteen, he had built a working mechanical calculator.

In 1654, Pascal and Fermat exchanged a celebrated series of letters about the "problem of points": if a game of chance is interrupted, how should the stakes be divided fairly? Pascal's approach was recursive, breaking the problem into smaller sub-games — an early instance of mathematical induction applied to probability. This correspondence is universally regarded as the founding moment of probability theory.

Pascal's study of the binomial coefficients, published in Traité du triangle arithmétique, systematised what is now called Pascal's triangle (known earlier in China, India, and Persia). He proved by induction several combinatorial identities and used the triangle to solve problems about fair division of stakes.

In physics, Pascal established the principles of hydrostatics and demonstrated atmospheric pressure by sending a barometer up the Puy de Dôme. The SI unit of pressure is named after him.

Following a religious experience in November 1654, Pascal largely abandoned mathematics for theology, producing the Pensées and the Lettres provinciales. He returned briefly to mathematics in 1658, solving problems about the cycloid under a pseudonym. He died in 1662, having compressed more mathematical achievement into his short life than most manage in a long one.
