Hans Hahn

1879–1934

Austrian

Functional analysis, topology, real analysis

Theorems in this course

  • Dual spaces and the concept of $X^*$ — Phase 14, page 4
  • Hahn–Banach theorem — Phase 14, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • Hahn decomposition theorem — every signed measure can be decomposed into a positive and negative part on disjoint measurable sets.
  • Hahn–Mazurkiewicz theorem — a non-empty Hausdorff space is a continuous image of $[0,1]$ if and only if it is compact, connected, locally connected, and metrisable.
  • Vienna Circle — a founding member of the philosophical movement that sought to ground all knowledge in logic and empirical verification.

Hans Hahn was born in Vienna and studied at the universities of Vienna, Strasbourg, Munich, and Göttingen. After serving in the First World War (where he was seriously wounded), he returned to Vienna as professor of mathematics, a position he held until his early death in 1934.

Hahn’s mathematical work centred on functional analysis and real analysis. His 1927 proof that bounded linear functionals on a subspace can be extended to the whole space without increasing the norm — the Hahn–Banach theorem — is one of the cornerstones of functional analysis. Banach independently proved the general version in 1929, and the theorem bears both names. Unlike the Baire-category results (Banach–Steinhaus, open mapping), Hahn–Banach requires Zorn’s lemma, placing it in a different logical category.

The Hahn–Banach theorem guarantees that the dual space $X^*$ of any normed space is “large enough” to separate points. This has far-reaching consequences: it implies that any non-zero vector can be detected by a bounded functional, and in its geometric form, it provides the separation of convex sets by hyperplanes — the theoretical basis of convex optimisation.

Beyond mathematics, Hahn was deeply involved in philosophy. He was one of the founders of the Vienna Circle, alongside Schlick, Carnap, and Neurath. His philosophical work advocated logical positivism: the view that meaningful statements must be either logically provable or empirically verifiable. He wrote on the foundations of mathematics, arguing for a conventionalist position.

Hahn died suddenly at fifty-five, depriving Vienna of one of its leading mathematical minds just as the political situation was deteriorating. His student Kurt Gödel would go on to transform mathematical logic, and the functional analysis programme Hahn helped build became a central pillar of 20th-century mathematics.
