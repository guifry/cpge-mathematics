Felix Hausdorff

1868–1942

German

Topology, set theory, measure theory

Theorems in this course

  • Grundzüge der Mengenlehre (1914): systematised metric and topological spaces — Phase 9, page 1
  • Axiomatisation of open sets — Phase 9, page 2

Other notable contributions

  • Hausdorff dimension — a generalisation of dimension to fractional values, fundamental in fractal geometry and dynamical systems.
  • Hausdorff measure — a measure on metric spaces that generalises Lebesgue measure and allows measuring "size" in fractional dimensions.
  • Hausdorff paradox — a precursor to the Banach–Tarski paradox, showing that the sphere minus a countable set can be partitioned into three congruent pieces.

Felix Hausdorff was born in Breslau (now Wrocław) and grew up in Leipzig. He studied mathematics and astronomy at Leipzig, where he completed his doctorate in 1891 and his habilitation in 1895. Under the pen name Paul Mongré, he also published literary and philosophical works, including the aphorism collection Sant' Ilario (1897) and the epistemological essay Das Chaos in kosmischer Auslese (1898).

Hausdorff's masterwork is Grundzüge der Mengenlehre (Foundations of Set Theory), published in 1914. This book systematised the entire field of point-set topology: it defined topological spaces via neighbourhood axioms, formalised the notions of open and closed sets, and established the vocabulary that mathematicians still use. The "Hausdorff separation axiom" (distinct points have disjoint neighbourhoods) became the standard regularity condition for topological spaces, and spaces satisfying it are still called Hausdorff spaces.

The book also contained the Hausdorff paradox, which showed that the sphere can be decomposed (using the axiom of choice) into finitely many pieces and rearranged into multiple copies. This was later refined by Banach and Tarski into the full Banach–Tarski paradox and became a key motivation for the development of measure theory and the restriction to measurable sets.

Hausdorff held positions at Bonn, Greifswald, and again at Bonn. After the Nazis came to power, he was forced to retire. As a Jew facing deportation to a concentration camp, Hausdorff, his wife Charlotte, and his sister-in-law Edith Pappenheim took their own lives in Bonn on 26 January 1942. His suicide letter to a friend contained the words: "Forgive us — we wish you and all our friends to experience better times."
