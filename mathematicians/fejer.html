Lipót Fejér

1880–1959

Hungarian

Harmonic analysis, approximation theory

Theorems in this course

  • Cesàro means of Fourier series — Phase 5, page 5
  • Fejér's theorem and the Fejér kernel — Phase 11, page 4

Other notable contributions

  • Fejér-Riesz theorem — a non-negative trigonometric polynomial can be written as the squared modulus of an analytic polynomial.
  • Conjecture on univalent functions — Fejér made influential conjectures on the coefficients of univalent functions, stimulating decades of research in complex analysis.
  • Fejér's theorem on power series — a power series converges at a boundary point if its Cesàro means converge there.

Lipót Fejér (born Leopold Weiss) was born in Pécs, Hungary, in 1880. At the age of nineteen, while still a student, he proved what is now called Fejér's theorem: the Cesàro means of the Fourier series of any continuous periodic function converge uniformly to the function. This was a sensation. The convergence of Fourier series had been a central problem in analysis for a century, and Fejér's approach — averaging the partial sums rather than taking them individually — cut through the difficulty with elegant simplicity.

The key insight was the Fejér kernel, a non-negative summability kernel that replaces the oscillatory Dirichlet kernel. Because the Fejér kernel is non-negative, the Cesàro mean computes a genuine weighted average of the function's values, which cannot overshoot. This eliminated the Gibbs phenomenon that plagues ordinary Fourier partial sums at discontinuities.

Fejér spent his career at the University of Budapest, where he became the founding figure of Hungarian mathematics. His students included John von Neumann, Pál Turán, George Pólya, and Paul Erdős — a roster that would be extraordinary for any mathematician, let alone one who proved his most famous theorem before his twentieth birthday. He was known for his warmth, his storytelling, and his gift for making difficult ideas feel natural.
