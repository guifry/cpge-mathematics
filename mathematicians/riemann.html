Other notable contributions

  • Riemann hypothesis — The conjecture that all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function have real part $1/2$, the most famous open problem in mathematics.
  • Riemannian geometry — The geometry of curved spaces, providing the mathematical framework for Einstein's general relativity.
  • Riemann surfaces — Multi-sheeted surfaces that make multi-valued complex functions single-valued, founding modern complex analysis.

Georg Friedrich Bernhard Riemann was born in Breselenz, Hanover, the son of a Lutheran pastor. Shy and prone to illness throughout his life, he studied at Gottingen under Gauss and at Berlin under Dirichlet and Jacobi. His 1851 doctoral thesis on complex function theory introduced Riemann surfaces and the Cauchy–Riemann equations in their modern form. Gauss, rarely generous with praise, called the thesis evidence of "a gloriously fertile originality."

Riemann's 1854 Habilitationsschrift contained two revolutionary works. The lecture "On the hypotheses which lie at the foundations of geometry" reimagined geometry itself, introducing the concept of an $n$-dimensional manifold with a metric tensor — the mathematics that would underpin general relativity sixty years later. The written thesis generalised Cauchy's integral to a broader class of functions, creating the Riemann integral that we use throughout this course.

In the same period, Riemann proved the rearrangement theorem: any conditionally convergent series can be rearranged to converge to any prescribed real number, or to diverge. He also contributed the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma to Fourier analysis and, in his 1859 paper on the distribution of primes, introduced the Riemann zeta function and stated the Riemann hypothesis — still unresolved. Riemann died of tuberculosis in Italy at thirty-nine, leaving behind a body of work that, despite its small size, reshaped multiple branches of mathematics.
