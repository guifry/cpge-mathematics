Other notable contributions

  • Axiomatic method — established the paradigm of building all mathematics from explicit axioms and definitions, still the standard today.
  • Euclid's Elements — thirteen books covering plane geometry, number theory, and solid geometry; the most influential textbook ever written.
  • Parallel postulate — his fifth postulate resisted proof for two millennia, ultimately leading to the discovery of non-Euclidean geometry.

Almost nothing is known about Euclid's life. He worked in Alexandria around 300 BC, probably during the reign of Ptolemy I, and he may have studied at Plato's Academy in Athens. When Ptolemy asked if there was a shorter path to geometry than the Elements, Euclid reportedly replied: "There is no royal road to geometry."

The Elements is the most successful textbook in history. Its thirteen books organise the mathematics of the Greek world — plane geometry, proportion theory, number theory, irrational magnitudes, solid geometry — into a deductive chain starting from five postulates and five common notions. Every proposition is proved from earlier ones, all the way back to the axioms. This axiomatic structure became the template for all subsequent mathematics.

For this course, Euclid's number-theoretic results in Books VII–IX are especially important. The Euclidean algorithm (Book VII) computes greatest common divisors without factoring. Euclid's lemma (Proposition VII.30) says that if a prime divides a product, it divides one of the factors — the key step in proving unique prime factorisation. And Proposition IX.20 proves there are infinitely many primes, in a proof so clean it has never been improved upon. His geometric work — similar triangles, the Pythagorean theorem — forms the foundation of Phase 0.
