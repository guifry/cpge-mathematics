Other notable contributions

  • Fischer's inequality — a determinantal inequality for positive definite matrices, bounding the determinant of a block matrix by the product of the determinants of its diagonal blocks.
  • Contributions to invariant theory — Fischer did important early work on the theory of algebraic invariants, extending results of Hilbert.

Ernst Sigismund Fischer was born in Vienna in 1875 and studied mathematics at the University of Vienna. He spent much of his career at the University of Erlangen, where he was a colleague of Emmy Noether, before being forced out by the Nazi regime in 1938.

Fischer's most famous result, proved independently of Frigyes Riesz in 1907, is the Riesz-Fischer theorem: the space $L^2$ of square-integrable functions is complete. That is, every Cauchy sequence of square-integrable functions converges (in the $L^2$ norm) to a square-integrable function. This was a foundational result for the emerging theory of Hilbert spaces and gave rigorous meaning to Parseval's identity — the statement that the sum of squared Fourier coefficients equals the integral of the squared function.

Fischer also gave the first min-max characterisation of eigenvalues (1905), later developed by Courant into the Courant-Fischer theorem. This variational approach to eigenvalues became one of the most powerful tools in spectral theory, connecting linear algebra to optimisation and PDEs.

After his dismissal from Erlangen, Fischer spent his remaining years in relative obscurity. His contributions to $L^2$ theory and spectral theory, though less widely known than those of Riesz, were equally independent and equally important. He died in 1954.
