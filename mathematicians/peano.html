Giuseppe Peano

1858–1932

Italian

Logic, analysis, foundations of mathematics

  • Peano's counterexample — mixed partials need not commute without continuity — Phase 7, page 3

Other notable contributions

  • Peano axioms — the axiomatic foundation of the natural numbers (1889), the starting point for formal arithmetic.
  • Space-filling curves — constructed a continuous surjection from $[0,1]$ onto $[0,1]^2$ (1890), shattering intuitions about dimension.
  • Modern mathematical notation — introduced $\in$, $\cup$, $\cap$, $\exists$, and many other symbols still used today.

Giuseppe Peano was born on a farm near Cuneo, in Piedmont, in 1858. He studied at the University of Turin, where he spent his entire career, becoming one of the most original mathematicians of his generation. His interests spanned analysis, logic, set theory, and linguistics.

Peano's most famous contribution to foundations is his axiomatisation of the natural numbers (1889), which reduced all of arithmetic to five axioms involving zero and a successor function. These Peano axioms remain the standard foundation for number theory. But Peano was equally productive in analysis. His existence theorem for ordinary differential equations (1890) showed that continuity of the right-hand side $f(t,y)$ suffices to guarantee at least one solution — though not uniqueness. This complemented the Cauchy-Lipschitz theorem by revealing that the Lipschitz condition is precisely what is needed for uniqueness, not existence.

Peano had a gift for finding counterexamples that revealed the limits of intuition. His counterexample to Schwarz's theorem on the commutativity of mixed partial derivatives — a function whose mixed partials $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ exist everywhere but differ at the origin — showed that the continuity hypothesis is essential, not merely convenient. His space-filling curve (1890) similarly demolished the intuition that a continuous image of a line segment cannot fill a square. Peano also introduced much of the notation that mathematicians use today, including the symbols $\in$, $\cup$, and $\cap$. He died in Turin in 1932.
