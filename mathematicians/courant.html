Other notable contributions

  • Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy condition — the CFL condition, a fundamental stability criterion for numerical solutions of PDEs (1928).
  • Finite element method — Courant's 1943 paper on variational methods laid the theoretical groundwork for the finite element method.
  • What Is Mathematics? — a classic expository book (with Robbins, 1941) that made advanced mathematics accessible to a broad audience.

Richard Courant was born in Lublinitz, Prussia (now Poland) in 1888 and became one of the most influential mathematicians and institution-builders of the twentieth century. He studied under Hilbert at Göttingen and became the director of its Mathematics Institute, transforming it into the world's leading centre for mathematics.

Courant's mathematical work centred on partial differential equations and the calculus of variations. With Fischer, he gave the variational characterisation of eigenvalues now known as the Courant-Fischer min-max theorem (1920), which characterises the $k$-th eigenvalue of a symmetric matrix as a min-max over $k$-dimensional subspaces. This result became the foundation of numerical eigenvalue computation and spectral theory for differential operators.

Forced out of Germany in 1933 by the Nazi regime, Courant emigrated to New York and built what became the Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences at NYU, now one of the world's premier centres for applied mathematics. His two-volume Methods of Mathematical Physics (with Hilbert) and his popular What Is Mathematics? (with Robbins) shaped how generations understood the subject. Courant's genius lay not only in his own theorems but in his ability to see connections between pure mathematics and its applications, and to build institutions where both could flourish.
