Pierre-Simon Laplace

1749–1827

French

Probability, celestial mechanics, mathematical physics

Other notable contributions

  • Laplace's equation and spherical harmonics — $\nabla^2 \phi = 0$, fundamental to gravitational and electrostatic potential theory.
  • Laplace transform — the integral transform that converts differential equations into algebraic ones, a cornerstone of engineering mathematics.
  • Mécanique céleste — five-volume treatise proving the stability of the solar system using perturbation theory, the crowning achievement of Newtonian gravitational theory.

Laplace was the son of a farmer from Normandy who rose to become the most powerful scientific figure of Napoleonic France. He held positions under every successive regime — the ancien régime, the Revolution, the Directorate, the Consulate, the Empire, and the Restoration — prompting accusations of political opportunism, but his mathematical brilliance was beyond dispute.

His Traité de mécanique céleste (1799–1825) systematised the gravitational theory of the solar system, proving that planetary perturbations are self-correcting and the system is stable over long timescales. When Napoleon asked why God did not appear in his system, Laplace allegedly replied: "I had no need of that hypothesis."

In probability, Laplace independently discovered Bayes' theorem and extended it far beyond Bayes's original formulation. His Théorie analytique des probabilités (1812) was the first comprehensive mathematical treatment of probability. He proved the central limit theorem for sums of general random variables, extending de Moivre's 1733 result for coin flips. The moment-generating function approach in Phase 12 follows his method, made rigorous by Lévy.

Laplace also evaluated the Gaussian integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi}$ using the polar coordinate trick (1774), served on the review committee for Fourier's 1807 heat conduction memoir alongside Lagrange and Legendre, and introduced cofactor expansion for determinants. He died in 1827, reportedly saying his last words were: "What we know is not much; what we don't know is immense."
