Rudolf Otto Sigismund Lipschitz was born in Königsberg (now Kaliningrad) in 1832 and spent most of his career at the University of Bonn. He was a broad mathematician in the nineteenth-century tradition, contributing to analysis, number theory, differential geometry, and algebra.

Lipschitz's most enduring contribution is the condition that bears his name. In 1876, he identified the precise regularity assumption on the right-hand side $f(t,y)$ of an ordinary differential equation $y' = f(t,y)$ that guarantees uniqueness of solutions: there must exist a constant $L$ such that $|f(t, y_1) - f(t, y_2)| \leq L|y_1 - y_2|$ for all $(t, y_1), (t, y_2)$ in the domain. This Lipschitz condition is stronger than continuity but weaker than differentiability, and it became the cornerstone of the Cauchy-Lipschitz (Picard-Lindelöf) existence and uniqueness theorem.

The concept proved far more versatile than its original context. Lipschitz continuity now appears throughout analysis and geometry: in fixed-point theory (contractions are Lipschitz with constant less than one), in metric space theory, in the study of Sobolev spaces, and in modern machine learning (Lipschitz constraints stabilise neural networks). Lipschitz died in Bonn in 1903, the same year that Peano's existence theorem (which drops the Lipschitz condition but loses uniqueness) became widely known.
