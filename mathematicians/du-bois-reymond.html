Theorems in this course

Other notable contributions

  • Du Bois-Reymond lemma — the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations: if a function integrates to zero against all test functions, it is zero.
  • Infinitary calculus — early work on rates of growth and the comparison of infinitely large and small quantities, anticipating Hardy's orders of infinity.
  • Contributions to potential theory — work on the Dirichlet problem and integral representations of harmonic functions.

Paul David Gustav du Bois-Reymond was born in Berlin into a distinguished intellectual family. His brother Emil was a famous physiologist. Paul studied in Zurich and Göttingen before settling at the universities of Heidelberg, Freiburg, and finally Tübingen, where he spent his last years. He was trained in the tradition of Dirichlet and Riemann, and much of his work concerned the convergence of trigonometric series.

Du Bois-Reymond's most famous result is his 1876 construction of a continuous function whose Fourier series diverges at a specific point. This was a genuine shock. Dirichlet (1829) had proved pointwise convergence for piecewise smooth functions, and the mathematical community widely believed that continuity alone should suffice. Du Bois-Reymond's counterexample showed that Fourier analysis is far subtler than anyone had suspected.

The result had a long afterlife. The question "how badly can Fourier series diverge for continuous functions?" drove much of 20th-century harmonic analysis. Banach and Steinhaus (1927) showed, using their uniform boundedness principle, that du Bois-Reymond's phenomenon is not exceptional: there is a dense $G_\delta$ set of continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at a given point. Carleson (1966) later proved that Fourier series of $L^2$ functions converge almost everywhere, resolving the question in the other direction.

Du Bois-Reymond also proved the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations, which remains an essential tool in the theory of partial differential equations and distribution theory. He died in Freiburg in 1889, aged fifty-seven.
