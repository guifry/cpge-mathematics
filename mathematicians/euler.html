Other notable contributions

  • Graph theory and the Königsberg bridges — founded graph theory by proving no walk can cross all seven bridges of Königsberg exactly once (1736).
  • Euler's polyhedron formula — $V - E + F = 2$ for convex polyhedra, a cornerstone of topology.
  • Analytical mechanics — the Euler-Lagrange equations, rigid body dynamics, and fluid mechanics equations that bear his name.

Euler is, by any reasonable measure, the most prolific mathematician in history. His collected works fill over 80 volumes, and new results kept appearing for decades after his death. He worked in every branch of mathematics that existed in his time — and invented several new ones.

Born in Basel, Switzerland, he studied under Johann Bernoulli and moved to the St Petersburg Academy at age 20. He spent productive decades in both St Petersburg and Berlin, writing on number theory, analysis, mechanics, optics, astronomy, and music theory. He lost sight in one eye in 1738 and went completely blind in 1766, but his mathematical output barely slowed — he dictated papers to assistants and computed enormous calculations in his head.

His contributions to this course are staggering. He introduced the function concept, systematised trigonometry via the unit circle, proved Fermat's little theorem and generalised it, defined the exponential function, discovered the formula $e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x$, solved the Basel problem, invented partial derivatives, and attempted an early proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. His Introductio in analysin infinitorum (1748) essentially created analysis as a discipline.

Euler's mathematical style was computational and fearless — he manipulated infinite series, products, and integrals with extraordinary intuition, often arriving at correct results by methods that would only be made rigorous a century later by Cauchy and Weierstrass. Laplace reportedly said: "Read Euler, read Euler, he is the master of us all."
