Bohemian (Czech)

Analysis, logic, philosophy of mathematics

Other notable contributions

  • Bolzano function — constructed an early example of a continuous nowhere-differentiable function, decades before Weierstrass's famous example.
  • Paradoxien des Unendlichen — posthumous work (1851) on paradoxes of the infinite, anticipating Cantor's set theory by several decades.
  • Rigorous foundations — among the first to insist on purely arithmetical proofs in analysis, rejecting geometric intuition as a substitute for rigour.

Bolzano was a Catholic priest, philosopher, and mathematician in Prague who saw more clearly than almost anyone in his generation that "obvious" geometric intuition was not a valid foundation for analysis. While his contemporaries — and even giants like Euler — freely assumed that continuous curves must cross axes and bounded sequences must cluster somewhere, Bolzano demanded proofs.

In 1817, he gave the first rigorous proof of the Intermediate Value Theorem: if a continuous function is negative at one end of an interval and positive at the other, it must be zero somewhere in between. This sounds obvious when you draw a picture, but Bolzano understood that the completeness of the real numbers was needed to make it work — on $\mathbb{Q}$, continuous functions can jump over values without hitting them.

The same 1817 paper contained the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem — every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence. This result, later rediscovered and popularised by Weierstrass, became one of the pillars of real analysis. It appears throughout this course: in the proof of the extreme value theorem (Phase 3), in the Cauchy criterion (Phase 2), in compactness (Phase 9), and in the Arzelà-Ascoli theorem (Phase 9).

Bolzano's mathematical work was largely ignored in his lifetime, partly because he published in obscure venues and partly because he was dismissed from his university position in 1819 for political reasons — his sermons criticised war, social inequality, and the Habsburg establishment. His ideas on the infinite, continuity, and rigour were rediscovered independently by Weierstrass, Cantor, and Dedekind decades later.
