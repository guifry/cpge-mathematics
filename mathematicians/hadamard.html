Jacques Hadamard

1865–1963

French

Analysis, number theory, differential equations, mathematical physics

Theorems in this course

  • Cauchy–Hadamard formula for radius of convergence (1892) — Phase 4, page 2
  • Stable manifold theorem (co-discoverer, 1901) — Phase 8, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • Prime number theorem — proved independently (with de la Vallée-Poussin) in 1896 that $\pi(n) \sim n/\ln n$.
  • Hadamard matrices — square matrices with entries $\pm 1$ and mutually orthogonal rows, used in signal processing and error-correcting codes.
  • Well-posedness of PDEs — formulated the concept of a well-posed problem (existence, uniqueness, continuous dependence on data).

Jacques Salomon Hadamard was born in Versailles and studied at the École Normale Supérieure. His career spanned over sixty years, from his doctorate in 1892 to his death in 1963 at the age of ninety-seven. He held chairs at Bordeaux, the Sorbonne, the Collège de France, and the École Polytechnique.

Hadamard's 1892 thesis on entire functions included the formula $1/R = \limsup |a_n|^{1/n}$ for the radius of convergence of a power series $\sum a_n z^n$. Cauchy had discovered the root test earlier, but Hadamard proved the definitive formula using the limit superior, which always exists. The result is now called the Cauchy–Hadamard theorem. Hadamard's insight that the radius of convergence reveals complex singularities — even for series defined on the real line — was a pivotal moment in the relationship between real and complex analysis.

In 1896, Hadamard proved the prime number theorem: the number of primes up to $n$ is asymptotic to $n/\ln n$. Charles de la Vallée-Poussin proved the same result independently. Both proofs relied on the theory of entire functions and complex analysis — it was striking that a result about integers required tools from complex function theory. This remains one of the most celebrated results in all of mathematics.

Hadamard also supervised Fréchet's groundbreaking 1906 thesis on metric spaces and was a major influence on the development of functional analysis. In PDE theory, he formulated the notion of well-posedness and constructed the famous "Hadamard example" showing that the Cauchy problem for the Laplace equation is ill-posed. He fled France during the Second World War, spending time in England, the United States, and the Soviet Union, before returning to Paris after the liberation.
