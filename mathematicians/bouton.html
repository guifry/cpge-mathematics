Charles Leonard Bouton was born in St. Louis, Missouri, and studied at Washington University before travelling to Leipzig, where he earned his doctorate in 1898 under Sophus Lie. He returned to the United States and spent his career at Harvard University, where he taught until his death.

Bouton's 1901 paper "Nim, a Game with a Complete Mathematical Theory" is today regarded as the birth of combinatorial game theory. The game of Nim — players take turns removing objects from heaps, and the player who takes the last object wins — had been played for centuries, but Bouton gave it the first complete mathematical analysis. His key insight was that a position is losing if and only if the binary XOR (exclusive or) of all heap sizes is zero. The proof is elegant and entirely elementary.

Though Bouton's other mathematical work — primarily in differential geometry and Lie theory — has been largely forgotten, his Nim paper launched a field. The Sprague–Grundy theorem, proved three decades later, showed that Bouton's XOR strategy was not a special trick but reflected a deep structural fact about all impartial games. Bouton died in Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1922, aged 52.
