Other notable contributions

  • Jordan measure (Jordan content) — an early formalisation of the notion of "area" for bounded sets, precursor to Lebesgue measure.
  • Traité des substitutions et des équations algébriques — the first textbook on group theory (1870), which made Galois's ideas accessible.
  • Jordan–Hölder theorem — the composition factors of a finite group are unique up to order and isomorphism.

Marie Ennemond Camille Jordan was born in Lyon and educated at the École Polytechnique. He spent his career split between the École Polytechnique and the Collège de France, and was elected to the Académie des Sciences in 1881. He worked simultaneously as a mathematician and as an engineer in the Corps des Mines.

Jordan's 1870 Traité des substitutions was the first systematic exposition of group theory. It made Galois's revolutionary but fragmentary ideas — published posthumously and largely ignored for decades — accessible to the mathematical community. The book introduced permutation groups, normal subgroups, and composition series, and it contains the Jordan normal form for matrices over algebraically closed fields. The Jordan normal form is the strongest structure theorem for linear maps: it gives the complete similarity invariant of a matrix, reducing every endomorphism to a canonical block-diagonal form.

In topology, Jordan stated and attempted to prove the curve theorem that bears his name: a simple closed curve in the plane divides it into exactly two connected components. His proof had gaps, and the first rigorous proof was given by Oswald Veblen in 1905. Despite the flawed proof, Jordan's identification of the result as a theorem needing proof (rather than an obvious fact) was itself a significant insight. The Jordan curve theorem is essential for the Poincaré–Bendixson theorem on planar dynamical systems.

Jordan independently discovered the singular value decomposition for square matrices in 1874, one year after Beltrami. He also developed Jordan measure (Jordan content), an early notion of area that was later superseded by Lebesgue measure but remains useful as a pedagogical stepping stone. The isomorphism theorems for groups, often attributed to Noether, had precursors in Jordan's and Dedekind's work. Jordan died in Paris in 1922, one of the most versatile mathematicians of the 19th century.
