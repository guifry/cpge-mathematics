Andrew Wiles

1953–

British

Number theory, arithmetic geometry

Theorems in this course

  • Fermat's Last Theorem (1995) — Phase 1, page 7: proved that $x^n + y^n = z^n$ has no positive integer solutions for $n \geq 3$

Other notable contributions

  • Modularity theorem (for semistable elliptic curves) — proved that every semistable elliptic curve over the rationals is modular, which implied Fermat's Last Theorem.
  • Iwasawa theory — proved the main conjecture of Iwasawa theory (with Barry Mazur), connecting $p$-adic $L$-functions to arithmetic of number fields.
  • Class number formula generalisations — contributed to the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer programme through his work on elliptic curves.

Andrew John Wiles was born in Cambridge and encountered Fermat's Last Theorem at age ten in a library book. The problem — stated by Pierre de Fermat in 1637, with the famous marginal note claiming a proof too large for the margin — had resisted all attempts for over 350 years. Wiles decided then that he would prove it.

He studied at Oxford and Cambridge, earning his doctorate under John Coates, and spent most of his career at Princeton. In 1986, Ken Ribet proved that the Taniyama–Shimura conjecture (now the modularity theorem) would imply Fermat's Last Theorem. This gave Wiles a viable strategy: prove modularity for semistable elliptic curves. He worked in near-total secrecy for seven years.

In June 1993, Wiles announced his proof in a series of lectures at Cambridge. A gap was discovered during peer review, and for over a year it seemed the proof might fail. In September 1994, working with his former student Richard Taylor, Wiles found a way to repair the argument using techniques from Iwasawa theory. The corrected proof was published in 1995 in the Annals of Mathematics, bringing to a close one of the longest-standing open problems in all of mathematics. Wiles received the Abel Prize in 2016 — the Fields Medal having an age limit he had narrowly exceeded. He is currently Royal Society Research Professor at Oxford.
