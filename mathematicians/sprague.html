Roland Percival Sprague was born in Unterliederbach, near Frankfurt. He worked primarily in recreational and combinatorial mathematics at a time when these areas received little professional attention. His most important contribution came in 1935, when he proved that every impartial combinatorial game — one where both players have the same available moves — is strategically equivalent to a single Nim heap.

This result was discovered independently by Patrick Grundy in 1939, and the theorem now bears both their names. The Sprague–Grundy theorem unified the analysis of a vast class of games: instead of analysing each game from scratch, one need only compute its Grundy value and apply the known Nim strategy. It remains the central tool in combinatorial game theory.

Sprague received his doctorate in 1950 from the Freie Universität Berlin under Alexander Dinghas, rather late in life. He held teaching positions at the Pädagogische Hochschule Berlin, becoming professor in 1955. He also gained recognition for constructing the first known perfect squared square — a square tiled by smaller squares, all of different sizes — a problem that had been open for decades. He died in Berlin in 1967.
