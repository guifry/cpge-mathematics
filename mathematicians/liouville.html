Joseph Liouville

1809–1882

French

Analysis, number theory, mathematical physics

Theorems in this course

  • Publication of Galois’s manuscripts — Phase 10, page 8

Other notable contributions

  • Liouville’s theorem (complex analysis) — every bounded entire function is constant.
  • Transcendental numbers — constructed the first provably transcendental number (Liouville’s constant, 1844), proving that not all numbers are algebraic.
  • Sturm–Liouville theory — with Sturm, developed the theory of eigenvalue problems for second-order linear ODEs, foundational for quantum mechanics and vibration analysis.

Joseph Liouville was born in Saint-Omer, in northern France. He trained at the École Polytechnique and the École des Ponts et Chaussées before devoting himself entirely to mathematics. In 1836, he founded the Journal de mathématiques pures et appliquées, universally known as Liouville’s Journal, which became one of the most important mathematical periodicals of the 19th century and continues to this day.

Liouville’s mathematical output was enormous and astonishingly varied. In analysis, he developed the theory of fractional calculus and, with Sturm, created the spectral theory of differential operators. In number theory, he proved the existence of transcendental numbers by constructing explicit examples — the Liouville numbers — whose decimal expansions have rapidly growing runs of zeros. In mechanics, he proved that Hamiltonian flows preserve phase-space volume (Liouville’s theorem in Hamiltonian mechanics).

His role in the history of algebra is equally significant. In 1843, Liouville received the manuscripts of Évariste Galois, who had died in a duel eleven years earlier at the age of twenty. Recognising their importance, Liouville carefully edited and published them in his journal in 1846, rescuing Galois theory from oblivion. Without Liouville’s editorial judgement, the connection between field extensions and group theory might have been lost for decades.

In his later years, Liouville suffered from depression and published less frequently. He died in Paris in 1882, leaving behind over 400 papers and a journal that had shaped an entire generation of French mathematics.
