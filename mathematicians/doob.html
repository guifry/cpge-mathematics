Joseph Doob

1910–2004

American

Probability theory, measure theory, potential theory

Theorems in this course

  • Martingale theory and conditional expectation — Phase 12, page 8

Other notable contributions

  • Doob's martingale convergence theorem — every $L^1$-bounded martingale converges almost surely.
  • Doob's optional stopping theorem — under integrability conditions, the expected value of a martingale at a stopping time equals its initial value.
  • Probabilistic potential theory — Doob established deep connections between classical potential theory and Brownian motion.

Joseph Leo Doob was born in Cincinnati in 1910 and spent virtually his entire career at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. He was one of the architects of modern probability theory, bringing measure-theoretic rigour to a subject that had been partly heuristic.

Doob's central achievement was the development of martingale theory in the 1940s and 1950s. Building on Kolmogorov's axiomatic foundations, Doob recognised that the notion of a "fair game" — a stochastic process whose conditional expectation of the future, given the present, equals the present value — could serve as a unifying concept across probability. His martingale convergence theorem and optional stopping theorem became indispensable tools in probability, statistics, and mathematical finance.

Doob's 1953 monograph Stochastic Processes was a landmark, setting the standard for mathematical rigour in the field. It introduced many of the concepts and techniques that are now standard: filtrations, stopping times, regular conditional probabilities, and the systematic use of measure theory in probability. Later in his career, Doob established profound connections between Brownian motion and classical potential theory, showing that harmonic functions could be characterised in terms of martingales. He received the National Medal of Science in 1979 and the Steele Prize in 1984.
