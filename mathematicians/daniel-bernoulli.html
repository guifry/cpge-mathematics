Daniel Bernoulli

1700–1782

Swiss

Probability, fluid dynamics, mathematical physics

Theorems in this course

  • St. Petersburg paradox and expected utility — Phase 12, page 3

Other notable contributions

  • Bernoulli's principle — the fundamental equation of fluid dynamics: faster flow means lower pressure. The reason aeroplanes fly.
  • Hydrodynamica (1738) — founded the kinetic theory of gases by modelling gas pressure as the result of molecular collisions.
  • Vibrating string problem — showed that the motion of a vibrating string is a superposition of its normal modes, anticipating Fourier analysis.

Daniel Bernoulli was born into the most mathematically productive family in history. His father Johann, his uncle Jacob, and several cousins and nephews were all significant mathematicians. The family dynamics were, to put it mildly, competitive. Johann Bernoulli reportedly tried to claim credit for Daniel's work on fluid dynamics and even backdated his own book to appear first.

Despite this, Daniel carved out a remarkable career. He spent years at the St. Petersburg Academy, where he produced Hydrodynamica (1738), the founding text of fluid mechanics. The book contains Bernoulli's principle — the observation that an increase in the speed of a fluid occurs simultaneously with a decrease in pressure. This is not merely an academic curiosity; it explains lift on aeroplane wings, the curve of spinning balls, and the flow of blood through arteries.

In probability, Daniel is remembered for resolving the St. Petersburg paradox. A game with infinite expected monetary value should, rationally, be worth infinite money to play. But nobody would pay more than a modest sum. Bernoulli's resolution — that people maximise expected utility, not expected money, and utility is logarithmic — was the birth of expected utility theory, which underpins modern economics and decision theory.

He also made early contributions to the theory of differential equations, the elasticity of beams, and statistical methods in medicine. He won the Grand Prize of the Paris Academy ten times. Despite training in medicine (he held a chair in anatomy and botany at Basel), mathematics was his true calling.
