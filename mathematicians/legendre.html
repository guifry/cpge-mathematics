Adrien-Marie Legendre

1752–1833

French

Number theory, analysis, elliptic integrals, geodesy

Theorems in this course

  • Method of least squares (independent discovery) — Phase 6, page 8
  • Introduced the $\partial$ notation for partial derivatives — Phase 7, page 1

Other notable contributions

  • Legendre polynomials — orthogonal polynomials arising in the solution of Laplace's equation in spherical coordinates, central to mathematical physics.
  • Quadratic reciprocity (conjecture) — conjectured the law of quadratic reciprocity, later proved by Gauss, one of the fundamental results in number theory.
  • Elliptic integrals — classified and tabulated elliptic integrals over three decades, work later subsumed by Abel and Jacobi's theory of elliptic functions.

Legendre had the misfortune of working in the same era as Gauss. Repeatedly, results he published first were superseded by Gauss's deeper versions: the method of least squares (Legendre 1805, Gauss claimed priority from 1795), quadratic reciprocity (Legendre conjectured, Gauss proved), and the prime number theorem (Legendre gave an approximation, Gauss a better one). The rivalry embittered Legendre considerably.

Despite living in Gauss's shadow, Legendre made substantial contributions in his own right. His Éléments de géométrie (1794) was the standard geometry textbook in France for over a century, and his work on elliptic integrals — spanning three decades of painstaking computation — laid the groundwork that Abel and Jacobi transformed into the theory of elliptic functions.

Legendre introduced the symbol $\partial$ for partial derivatives, now universal in mathematics. He served on the review committee alongside Lagrange and Laplace that assessed Fourier's 1807 memoir on heat conduction, and was one of the sceptical voices questioning whether discontinuous functions could truly be represented by trigonometric series.

A curious historical footnote: for nearly two centuries, the portrait used for Legendre in books and articles was actually of the politician Louis Legendre. The error was only discovered in 2005 when a caricature from an 1820 album was identified as the true likeness of the mathematician — a rotund, cheerful figure quite unlike the stern face in the wrong portrait.
