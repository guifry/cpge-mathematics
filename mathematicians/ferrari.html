Lodovico Ferrari

1522–1565

Italian

Algebra

Theorems in this course

  • Quartic formula — Phase 10, page 8

Other notable contributions

  • Public mathematical duel with Tartaglia — in 1548, Ferrari defeated Tartaglia in a public contest in Milan, vindicating Cardano's publication of the cubic formula.
  • Method of reducing the quartic — Ferrari's approach reduces the quartic to a cubic (already solved), a technique that influenced algebraic thinking for centuries.

Lodovico Ferrari had one of the most improbable mathematical careers in history. He arrived at Cardano's household in Bologna as a servant boy, possibly as young as fourteen. Cardano, recognising the boy's extraordinary intelligence, began teaching him mathematics. Within a few years, Ferrari had solved one of the great open problems of algebra: the general quartic equation.

Ferrari's method was ingenious. He reduced the quartic to a cubic by adding a carefully chosen square to both sides of the equation, then solved the resulting cubic using the formula that Cardano had obtained from Tartaglia. The quartic solution was published in Cardano's Ars Magna (1545), with full credit to Ferrari.

When Tartaglia accused Cardano of oath-breaking for publishing the cubic formula, Ferrari leapt to his master's defence. He challenged Tartaglia to a public mathematical duel — a common format in Renaissance Italy, where two mathematicians would pose problems to each other before an audience. The contest took place in Milan in August 1548. Ferrari won decisively, cementing his reputation.

Despite this triumph, Ferrari's life after the duel was brief and troubled. He obtained a position as a tax assessor in Milan and later taught mathematics in Bologna, but he died at just 43. According to his sister, he was poisoned — possibly by her, as some accounts suggest she inherited his estate and promptly remarried. The circumstances remain murky. Ferrari's mathematical legacy is secure, however: the quartic formula was the last degree for which a solution by radicals exists, as Galois would eventually prove.
