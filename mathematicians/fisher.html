Ronald Fisher

1890–1962

British

Statistics, genetics, experimental design

Theorems in this course

  • Coined the term “variance” — Phase 12, page 4

Other notable contributions

  • Maximum likelihood estimation — developed the general theory of parameter estimation by maximising the likelihood function, now the default method in statistics.
  • Analysis of variance (ANOVA) — invented the technique for comparing means across multiple groups, transforming experimental science.
  • Fisher information — defined the information a random variable carries about an unknown parameter, connected to the Cramér–Rao lower bound on estimator variance.

Ronald Aylmer Fisher was born in East Finchley, London. Despite severe short-sightedness that prevented him from serving in the First World War, he became one of the most influential scientists of the 20th century, making foundational contributions to both statistics and evolutionary biology.

Fisher’s 1918 paper “The Correlation between Relatives on the Supposition of Mendelian Inheritance” reconciled Mendelian genetics with biometry and, almost incidentally, introduced the word “variance” for the quantity $E[(X - \mu)^2]$. The term replaced a miscellany of earlier phrases and immediately became standard. His 1925 book Statistical Methods for Research Workers taught an entire generation of scientists how to analyse data, and his 1935 The Design of Experiments established randomisation, replication, and blocking as the foundations of experimental method.

Fisher’s linear discriminant analysis — finding the direction that best separates two classes of data — connects his statistical work to linear algebra. The method reduces to a generalised eigenvalue problem, and the principal axis theorem guarantees the discriminant directions are orthogonal.

Fisher held positions at the Rothamsted Experimental Station, University College London, Cambridge, and finally the CSIRO in Adelaide, where he died in 1962. His contributions to statistics are immense, though his later advocacy of eugenics and his defence of the tobacco industry have attracted justified criticism. His mathematical legacy, however, is unambiguous: modern statistics is built on Fisher’s foundations.
