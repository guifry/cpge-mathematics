Hipparchus

c. 190–120 BC

Greek, Nicaea

Trigonometry, astronomy

Other notable contributions

  • Star catalogue — compiled the first systematic catalogue of about 850 stars, classifying them by brightness into six magnitudes.
  • Precession of the equinoxes — discovered that the Earth's axis slowly wobbles, causing the equinox points to drift along the ecliptic.
  • Lunar distance — estimated the Moon's distance from Earth using parallax observations, obtaining a value remarkably close to the modern figure.

Hipparchus, born in Nicaea (modern Iznik, Turkey) around 190 BC, is often called the founder of trigonometry and the greatest observational astronomer of antiquity. He worked primarily on the island of Rhodes, where he made systematic observations over several decades.

His most important contribution to mathematics was the construction of the first known trigonometric table. He tabulated the lengths of chords — the straight line cutting across a circle at a given angle — for angles at half-degree intervals. This chord table was the ancient equivalent of a sine table (since $\text{chord}(\theta) = 2\sin(\theta/2)$), and it allowed precise computation of distances and angles in astronomy and geography.

Hipparchus used his trigonometric methods to estimate the Moon's distance from Earth. By observing a solar eclipse from two different locations and using the parallax, he obtained a value of about 60.5 Earth radii — the modern value is 60.3. He also discovered the precession of the equinoxes by comparing his star positions with earlier Babylonian records, detecting a slow drift of about 1 degree per century (the modern value is 1.4 degrees). His star catalogue, the first of its kind, remained the standard reference for over 1500 years until Tycho Brahe. Almost none of his original works survive; we know his results mainly through Ptolemy, who built on them three centuries later.
