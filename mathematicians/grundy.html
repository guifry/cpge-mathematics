Patrick Grundy

1917–1959

British

Combinatorial game theory, statistics

Theorems in this course

  • Sprague–Grundy theorem (1939) — Phase 15, page 1: every impartial game is equivalent to a Nim heap; Grundy values

Other notable contributions

  • Grundy's game — a combinatorial game where players split heaps into two unequal parts; its Grundy values remain uncomputed for large heaps.
  • Work in statistics — contributed to sampling theory and experimental design at the Rothamsted Experimental Station.

Patrick Michael Grundy was born on the Isle of Wight and studied at the University of Cambridge, where he began research in algebraic geometry. In 1941 he won a Smith's Prize for work on the theory of ideals. But it was a result from two years earlier — his 1939 paper on combinatorial games — that would make his name permanent in mathematics.

Working independently of Roland Sprague, Grundy proved that every impartial combinatorial game is equivalent to a Nim heap of some non-negative integer size. This integer, now called the Grundy value or Grundy number, completely determines the strategic content of the position. For composite games (sums of independent components), the Grundy value is simply the XOR of the component values — reducing multi-game analysis to a single binary operation.

After the Second World War, Grundy shifted to statistics, training at the Rothamsted Experimental Station and working on problems in sampling and experimental design. He died in 1959 at the age of 41, having published relatively little but leaving behind one of the most elegant and useful theorems in combinatorial mathematics. The concept that bears his name — the Grundy value — remains the fundamental tool for analysing impartial games to this day.
