Other notable contributions

  • Cauchy's integral theorem — the integral of a holomorphic function around a closed contour is zero, the foundation of complex analysis.
  • Cauchy's residue theorem — computes contour integrals via residues at poles, with applications across physics and engineering.
  • Theory of determinants — gave the first systematic treatment of determinants, including the product formula $\det(AB) = \det A \cdot \det B$.

Cauchy was, after Euler, the most prolific mathematician in history — he published nearly 800 papers. His Cours d'analyse (1821) marked the beginning of rigorous analysis, replacing the freewheeling manipulations of Euler and his contemporaries with precise definitions of limits, continuity, and convergence. The Cauchy criterion — a sequence converges if and only if its terms get arbitrarily close to each other — was the key innovation, allowing convergence to be tested without knowing the limit in advance.

He made the first serious attempt to prove that the limit of a sequence of continuous functions is continuous (1821) — and got it wrong. The error was subtle: he failed to distinguish between pointwise and uniform convergence. Abel noticed the mistake in 1826; Weierstrass later provided the correct fix. This productive failure led directly to the theory of uniform convergence, one of the central ideas in Phase 5.

In differential equations, the Cauchy-Lipschitz theorem (Phase 8) gives precise conditions for existence and uniqueness of solutions. In algebra, Cauchy's theorem (Phase 10) guarantees that a finite group of order divisible by a prime $p$ contains an element of order $p$. In spectral theory, he proved that real symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues (Phase 13). The range is extraordinary.

Cauchy was a devout Catholic and a political conservative, which cost him positions after the 1830 revolution when he refused to swear an oath to the new regime. He spent eight years in self-imposed exile in Turin and Prague before returning to Paris. His personal relationships were often difficult — he was notorious for presenting others' results as his own and for blocking younger mathematicians' careers, most infamously losing Abel's and Galois's manuscripts.
