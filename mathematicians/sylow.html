Peter Ludwig Mejdell Sylow was born in Christiania (now Oslo), Norway. He studied at the University of Christiania and, unable to secure a university position, spent forty years as a secondary school teacher at the Fredrikshald Latin School (1858–1898). Only at age sixty-six was he finally appointed to a chair at the University of Christiania, which he held until his death.

Sylow's mathematical reputation rests almost entirely on a single paper: his 1872 article "Theoremes sur les groupes de substitutions," published in Mathematische Annalen. In it, he proved three fundamental results about finite groups that now bear his name. The first Sylow theorem guarantees the existence of subgroups of prime-power order. The second proves all such subgroups are conjugate. The third constrains how many there can be. Together, these theorems provide the most powerful general tools for analysing the structure of finite groups.

The theorems give a partial converse to Lagrange's theorem: while Lagrange says the order of a subgroup divides the order of the group, it is not true that every divisor yields a subgroup ($A_4$ has order 12 but no subgroup of order 6). The Sylow theorems rescue the situation for prime-power divisors. Sylow also co-edited the complete works of Abel with Sophus Lie and delivered the first lectures on Galois theory in Norway. Despite his modest career as a schoolteacher, his theorems remain among the most frequently used results in algebra — a testament to the power of a single, perfectly placed contribution.
