Paul Lévy

1886–1971

French

Probability, functional analysis

Theorems in this course

  • Generalisation of the Central Limit Theorem — Phase 12, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • Lévy processes — stochastic processes with independent, stationary increments; generalise Brownian motion and Poisson processes.
  • Lévy’s continuity theorem — convergence in distribution is equivalent to pointwise convergence of characteristic functions.
  • Local time — introduced the concept of local time for Brownian motion, measuring how long the path spends near a given level.

Paul Pierre Lévy was born in Paris into a family with strong mathematical connections — his father Lucien was a mathematician, and his uncle was the geometer Maurice Lévy. He studied at the École Polytechnique and the École des Mines, and spent most of his career as professor at the École Polytechnique, where he taught from 1920 to 1959.

Lévy was one of the founders of modern probability theory. Working largely in isolation from the Russian school of Kolmogorov and the German school of measure theory, he developed his own highly intuitive approach to stochastic processes. His 1935 generalisation of the Central Limit Theorem weakened the conditions required for the sum of independent random variables to converge to a Gaussian, allowing non-identically distributed summands under what is now called the Lindeberg–Lévy condition.

His work on characteristic functions — the Fourier transforms of probability distributions — gave the most natural proof of the CLT. The continuity theorem, which states that convergence of characteristic functions implies convergence in distribution, became the standard tool for limit theorems. The MGF-based proof in our course follows the approach of Laplace, but it was Lévy who made it fully rigorous.

Lévy’s mathematical style was intensely geometric and intuitive. He would often state results based on deep insight before having complete proofs, leaving the rigorous details to others. His major works on Brownian motion, stable distributions, and stochastic integrals laid the foundations for much of modern stochastic analysis. He was elected to the Académie des Sciences in 1964 and died in Paris in 1971.
