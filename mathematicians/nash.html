John Nash

1928–2015

American

Game theory, differential geometry, partial differential equations

Theorems in this course

  • Nash equilibrium existence theorem (1950) — Phase 15, page 3

Other notable contributions

  • Nash embedding theorem — every Riemannian manifold can be isometrically embedded in Euclidean space (1956).
  • Nash–Moser inverse function theorem — generalises the inverse function theorem to certain Fréchet spaces, overcoming loss-of-derivative problems.
  • De Giorgi–Nash–Moser regularity — solutions of uniformly elliptic PDEs in divergence form are Hölder continuous (1958).

John Forbes Nash Jr. grew up in Bluefield, West Virginia, and arrived at Princeton's graduate programme in 1948 with a one-sentence recommendation letter: "This man is a genius." His doctoral thesis, completed at 21 and barely 27 pages long, proved that every finite game has at least one equilibrium in mixed strategies. It transformed economics, political science, and evolutionary biology.

Nash's mathematical ambitions ranged far beyond game theory. His work on the embedding problem — showing that abstract Riemannian manifolds can be realised as submanifolds of Euclidean space — stunned geometers and required entirely new analytic techniques. His results on elliptic PDEs, obtained independently of Ennio De Giorgi, resolved one of Hilbert's open problems.

In the late 1950s Nash developed paranoid schizophrenia, and spent decades in and out of psychiatric hospitals. He gradually recovered in the 1980s and 1990s, describing the process as a return to rational thinking rather than a pharmaceutical cure. He shared the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics with Harsanyi and Selten. In 2015 he received the Abel Prize for his contributions to the theory of nonlinear partial differential equations. He died in a taxi accident on the New Jersey Turnpike just days later, aged 86.
