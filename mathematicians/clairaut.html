Alexis-Claude Clairaut

1713–1765

French

Analysis, geometry, celestial mechanics

Theorems in this course

  • Clairaut–Schwarz theorem — Phase 7, page 3

Other notable contributions

  • Clairaut’s theorem on the figure of the Earth — related the shape of a rotating body to its gravitational field, founding geodesy as a mathematical discipline.
  • Clairaut’s equation — a first-order ODE of the form $y = x\,y' + f(y')$, solved by differentiating both sides.
  • Return of Halley’s Comet — computed the perturbation of the comet's orbit by Jupiter and Saturn, predicting its 1759 return to within one month.

Alexis-Claude Clairaut was a prodigy even by 18th-century standards. Born in Paris to a mathematics teacher, he read l'Hôpital's Analyse des infiniment petits at age ten, presented a paper on curves to the Académie des Sciences at thirteen, and became the youngest person ever elected to the Académie at eighteen.

His early work on space curves — Recherches sur les courbes à double courbure (1731) — was a landmark in differential geometry, extending the study of curves from the plane to three dimensions. This led naturally to his interest in surfaces and partial derivatives, where he first stated (around 1740) the result that mixed partial derivatives commute: $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \,\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \,\partial x}$. The rigorous proof came much later from Schwarz (1873), but the theorem rightly bears Clairaut's name alongside Schwarz's.

In the 1740s, Clairaut turned to celestial mechanics. His Théorie de la figure de la terre (1743) derived the shape of a rotating fluid body, proving that the Earth must be an oblate spheroid. He accompanied Maupertuis on an expedition to Lapland to measure the meridian arc, confirming Newton's prediction of polar flattening. Later, he computed the gravitational perturbations on Halley's Comet and correctly predicted its perihelion passage in 1759 — a triumph of Newtonian mechanics.

Clairaut died suddenly in Paris at 52, possibly from overwork. His name endures in differential equations, multivariable calculus, and geodesy.
