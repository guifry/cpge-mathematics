Reinhard Selten

1930–2016

German

Game theory, experimental economics, bounded rationality

Theorems in this course

  • Nash equilibrium refinements — Phase 15, page 3: subgame-perfect equilibrium and selection among multiple equilibria

Other notable contributions

  • Subgame-perfect equilibrium — refined Nash equilibrium by requiring rational play in every subgame, eliminating non-credible threats (1965).
  • Trembling hand perfection — a further refinement requiring equilibria to be robust to small mistakes in strategy execution (1975).
  • Experimental economics — pioneered laboratory experiments testing game-theoretic predictions with human subjects.

Reinhard Selten was born in Breslau (now Wroclaw, Poland) to a half-Jewish family. His childhood was scarred by the Nazi era — his father's shop was destroyed on Kristallnacht, and the family endured years of persecution. After the war, Selten studied mathematics at Frankfurt, where he encountered game theory for the first time and recognised it as a framework that could bring mathematical rigour to strategic reasoning.

His most influential contribution was the concept of subgame-perfect equilibrium (1965): a Nash equilibrium that remains an equilibrium in every subgame. This seemingly technical refinement has enormous bite. It rules out strategies that rely on threats a player would never actually carry out, forcing the analysis to respect the sequential structure of the game. The concept is now so fundamental that it is taught in every introductory game theory course.

Selten later introduced the trembling hand perfect equilibrium (1975), which requires strategies to remain optimal even when opponents make small mistakes. He also became a leading figure in experimental economics, testing whether real human beings behave as game theory predicts (often they do not, which Selten found more interesting than confirming the theory). He shared the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics with Nash and Harsanyi. Selten died in Poznan in 2016.
