Hermann Grassmann

1809–1877

German

Linear algebra, multilinear algebra, linguistics

Theorems in this course

  • Abstract vector spaces (1844) — Phase 6, page 1
  • Inner product / dot product — Phase 6, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • Exterior algebra — The algebra of wedge products, capturing the notion of oriented area and volume in any dimension.
  • Grassmannians — The space of all $k$-dimensional subspaces of a vector space, fundamental in algebraic geometry.
  • Grassmann's law in linguistics — A phonological rule in Indo-European languages governing aspirated consonants.

Hermann Gunther Grassmann was born in Stettin (now Szczecin, Poland) and spent most of his life as a schoolteacher there, never holding a university position. He was a true polymath: mathematician, physicist, linguist, and publisher. His father was also a mathematics teacher, and Hermann largely taught himself advanced mathematics.

Grassmann's masterwork, the Ausdehnungslehre (Theory of Extension, 1844), was decades ahead of its time. In it, he defined abstract vector spaces with full generality — vectors of any dimension, the eight axioms, linear independence, basis, and dimension — long before these concepts entered mainstream mathematics. The book was almost completely ignored. Mathematicians found it impenetrable, partly because Grassmann wrote in a philosophical style alien to the mathematical community, and partly because the ideas were simply too abstract for the 1840s.

Grassmann published a revised edition in 1862, rewritten in conventional mathematical style, but it fared little better. Discouraged, he turned to Sanskrit philology and produced a highly regarded translation of the Rigveda and a Sanskrit dictionary. Only near the end of his life did mathematicians — notably Peano, Gibbs, and Clifford — begin to recognise the depth of his algebraic work. Today, Grassmann algebras and Grassmannians are central objects in differential geometry, physics (particularly in the theory of fermions), and algebraic geometry.
