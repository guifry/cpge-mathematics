Other notable contributions

  • Fermat's Last Theorem — the claim that $x^n + y^n = z^n$ has no positive integer solutions for $n \geq 3$. Stated in 1637, proved by Andrew Wiles in 1995.
  • Fermat's principle of least time — light travels between two points along the path that takes the least time. The foundation of geometric optics.
  • Fermat numbers — $F_n = 2^{2^n} + 1$. Fermat conjectured all are prime; Euler showed $F_5$ is not.

Fermat was a lawyer by profession — a councillor at the Parliament of Toulouse — and a mathematician by passion. He published almost nothing during his lifetime, preferring to communicate results in letters to other mathematicians, often without proof. This habit produced some of the most productive and frustrating correspondence in mathematical history.

In number theory, Fermat's little theorem ($a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ for $p$ prime and $\gcd(a,p) = 1$) is the foundation of modern primality testing and public-key cryptography. He stated it in a 1640 letter to Frénicle de Bessy, characteristically without proof. His "Last Theorem" — the marginal note claiming $x^n + y^n = z^n$ has no positive integer solutions for $n \geq 3$ — took 358 years to prove.

In calculus, Fermat developed the method of adequality around 1636 to find maxima and minima of curves. The idea was essentially the derivative: set $f(x)$ "approximately equal" to $f(x + e)$, divide by $e$, then set $e = 0$. This predates Newton and Leibniz by decades and gives Fermat a strong claim to being the inventor of differential calculus — at least the part concerned with optimisation.

In 1654, Fermat and Pascal exchanged letters about the "problem of points" — how to divide stakes fairly in an interrupted game of chance. This correspondence is the founding document of probability theory. Fermat's approach was combinatorial: enumerate all possible continuations and count favourable outcomes. Together, they transformed gambling intuitions into mathematics.
