Abraham de Moivre

1667–1754

French-born, worked in England

Probability, trigonometry, complex numbers

Theorems in this course

  • Concept of variance (deviation from the mean) — Phase 12, page 4
  • De Moivre–Laplace theorem (first CLT) — Phase 12, page 7

Other notable contributions

  • De Moivre's formula — $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$. The key identity connecting complex numbers and trigonometry.
  • The Doctrine of Chances (1718) — a landmark probability textbook that systematised the theory of games of chance.
  • Stirling's approximation — de Moivre found the approximation $n! \approx C\sqrt{n}(n/e)^n$ before Stirling; Stirling later determined the constant $C = \sqrt{2\pi}$.

Abraham de Moivre was born a Huguenot in Vitry-le-François, France. After the revocation of the Edict of Nantes in 1685 made life impossible for Protestants, he emigrated to England, where he spent the rest of his life. He never secured a university position — despite being elected to the Royal Society and being a close friend of Newton and Halley — and earned his living as a tutor and consultant to gamblers and insurance underwriters.

His Doctrine of Chances (1718, with expanded editions in 1738 and 1756) was the most important probability textbook of the 18th century. In it, de Moivre introduced the concept of deviation from the mean and the notion that outcomes cluster around a central value. He was the first to compute the bell curve: in 1733 he proved that the binomial distribution with $n$ trials and probability $p$ is approximately normal for large $n$. This is the de Moivre–Laplace theorem — the first instance of the central limit theorem, arguably the most important result in probability.

De Moivre also discovered the formula $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$, which connects complex exponentiation to trigonometry and prefigures Euler's formula. His work on recurrence relations and generating functions influenced the development of combinatorics.

A famous legend holds that near the end of his life, de Moivre noticed he was sleeping fifteen minutes longer each day, computed when the progression would reach 24 hours, predicted he would die on that date — and did. The story is likely apocryphal, but it suits a man who spent his life computing probabilities.
