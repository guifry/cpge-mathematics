William Vickrey

1914–1996

Canadian-American

Economics, mechanism design, public finance

Theorems in this course

  • Vickrey (second-price) auction (1961) — Phase 15, page 6: truthful bidding as dominant strategy
  • Second-price auction example — Phase 15, page 4: dominant strategy in Vickrey auctions

Other notable contributions

  • Congestion pricing — proposed time-varying tolls on roads to reduce traffic congestion, decades before the idea gained policy traction.
  • Vickrey–Clarke–Groves mechanism — a generalisation of the second-price auction to multi-item settings where truthful reporting is dominant.
  • Cumulative averaging income tax — proposed that income tax be computed on cumulative lifetime earnings to smooth incentive distortions.

William Spencer Vickrey was born in Victoria, British Columbia, and moved to the United States as a young man. He earned his doctorate at Columbia University under Carl Shoup, studying tax policy, and spent his entire career at Columbia. His interests were decidedly practical: how to design tax systems, auction mechanisms, and urban transport infrastructure to maximise social welfare.

His 1961 paper on auctions introduced the second-price sealed-bid format — now universally called the Vickrey auction. The key insight was elegant: if the highest bidder pays not their own bid but the second-highest, then bidding your true valuation is a dominant strategy. No game-theoretic sophistication is needed; honesty is simply optimal. This result became a cornerstone of mechanism design theory.

Vickrey was also a pioneer of congestion pricing, proposing in the 1950s and 1960s that road tolls should vary by time of day to reflect the true social cost of driving during rush hour — an idea that took half a century to reach widespread implementation. He was awarded the 1996 Nobel Prize in Economics jointly with James Mirrlees, but died of a heart attack just three days after the announcement, never having the chance to deliver a Nobel lecture. His colleague C. Lowell Harriss accepted the prize on his behalf.
