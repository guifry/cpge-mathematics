Pythagoras

c. 570–495 BC

Greek, Samos

Geometry, number theory, music theory

Theorems in this course

  • The Pythagorean Theorem — Phase 0, page 2

Other notable contributions

  • Musical intervals — discovered that harmonious musical intervals correspond to simple ratios of string lengths (octave = 2:1, fifth = 3:2, fourth = 4:3).
  • Pythagorean school — founded a philosophical and mathematical community in Croton whose motto "all is number" shaped the development of mathematics for centuries.
  • Numerology and philosophy — developed a metaphysical system identifying numbers with abstract qualities, influencing Plato and subsequent Western philosophy.

Pythagoras of Samos, born around 570 BC, is one of the most famous names in mathematics — and one of the most elusive. He wrote nothing that survives, and almost everything we know about him comes from authors writing centuries later. The theorem bearing his name was known to the Babylonians over a thousand years before his birth.

What Pythagoras and his followers likely contributed was not the theorem itself but the idea of proving it. The Babylonians knew that $3^2 + 4^2 = 5^2$ and compiled extensive lists of Pythagorean triples, but there is no evidence they proved the relationship holds for all right triangles. The Greek innovation — and the Pythagorean school's lasting contribution — was the insistence that mathematical truths must be demonstrated by logical argument, not merely verified by example.

Pythagoras founded a semi-religious community in Croton (southern Italy) around 530 BC, where mathematics, music, and philosophy were studied as interconnected paths to understanding the cosmos. The Pythagoreans discovered that harmonious musical intervals arise from simple integer ratios, reinforcing their belief that "all is number." This worldview was shattered when one of their own — traditionally Hippasus — proved that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational, showing that not all geometric magnitudes can be expressed as ratios of integers. The resulting crisis drove the development of Eudoxus' theory of proportion and, ultimately, the real number system.
