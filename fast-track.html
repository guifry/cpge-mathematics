Fast Track

12-Month Curriculum — concours fluency + stochastic calc readiness

A focused path through the full curriculum for part-time study (~1.5h/day). Same pages, different order, phases triaged by relevance. 70% of your time should go to problem-solving, not reading proofs.

See FAST_TRACK_CURRICULUM.md for the full rationale.

How to study each page

  1. Read the problem box only. Stop. Spend 5–10 minutes trying to see why it’s hard.
  2. Read the theorem statement. Stop. Try to prove it yourself for 10–15 minutes.
  3. Read the proof. Compare against your failed attempt.
  4. Close the page. Reproduce the proof from memory on paper.
  5. Do 3–5 exercises that use this theorem. Same session or next day.
  6. One week later: 2–3 harder exercises without rereading. Spaced retrieval.

Month 0 — Refresher (before you start)

Pen-and-paper fluency assumed by the course. Read the theory (~45 min), do all exercises (~2-3 hours). Gate: you should be able to factor any quadratic on sight and manipulate fractions/roots/exponents without hesitation before moving on. Factoring is the prerequisite for everything.

  • Arithmetic Rules
    Start here. Exponent rules, logarithm rules, fraction arithmetic, square root algebra.
  • Factoring
    Expanding, common factors, quadratic factoring, patterns, grouping. + 10 exercises.
  • 1. Equation Solving Toolkit
    Isolating $x$ by position. Inequalities. Systems.
  • 2. Polynomials and Factoring
    Quadratic formula, factoring patterns, polynomial division, rational root theorem.
  • 3. Concepts Refresher
    Complex numbers, combinatorics ($n!$, $\binom{n}{k}$), elementary statistics.
  • 4. Equation Solving Exercises
    10 exercises with hidden solutions. Linear to trigonometric.
  • 5. Polynomial Exercises
    10 exercises with hidden solutions. Factoring, division, Vieta, partial fractions.
  • 6. Concept Exercises
    10 exercises with hidden solutions. Complex numbers, counting, statistics.
  • 7. Trigonometry
    Unit circle, key values, identities, solving trig equations. + 10 exercises.

Month 1 — Foundations

Skim the geometry, then build the real analysis foundation. Epsilon-delta fluency is non-negotiable.

Phase 0 — Geometry (skim)

  • 1. Similar Triangles and Measurement
    Thales measures the Great Pyramid. Same angles → same ratios.
  • 2. The Pythagorean Theorem
    $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Two proofs. Why it's about areas, not algebra.
  • 3. $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational
    The rationals have holes. Why $\mathbb{R}$ was invented.
  • 4. Trigonometry from Scratch
    Sine and cosine as angle-to-ratio converters.
  • 5. The Unit Circle
    $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$. Proves $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$.
  • 6. Coordinate Geometry
    Descartes turns points into numbers and curves into equations.
  • 7. Trigonometric Identities
    Addition formulae, double angle, sum-to-product. The trig manipulation toolkit.

Phase 2 — Limits and Convergence (thorough)

  • 0. Inequalities
    Triangle inequality, AM-GM, Bernoulli, Cauchy-Schwarz. The engine of every convergence proof.
  • 1. Completeness of $\mathbb{R}$
    The supremum axiom fills the holes in $\mathbb{Q}$.
  • 2. The Squeeze Theorem
    Archimedes traps $\pi$ between polygons.
  • 3. Monotone Convergence Theorem
    Bounded + monotone ⇒ converges.
  • 4. Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem
    Every bounded sequence hides a convergent subsequence.
  • 5. The Cauchy Criterion
    Convergence without knowing the limit.
  • 6. Intermediate Value Theorem
    Continuous + sign change ⇒ zero crossing.
  • 7. Dense Subsets of $\mathbb{R}$
    $\mathbb{Q}$ is everywhere dense yet full of holes.

Practice: prove 10 limits from scratch using epsilon-delta. Squeeze theorem exercises. Prove convergence of 5 recursive sequences.

Months 2–3 — Calculus

Derivatives, then integration. Every rule proved from the limit definition. Month 2 = Part A, Month 3 = Part B.

Part A — Derivatives (month 2)

  • 1. The Derivative
    The slope of a curve at a point, as a limit of secant slopes.
  • 2. Differentiation Rules
    Sum, product, quotient, chain rule — each from the definition.
  • 3. Derivatives of Sine and Cosine
    The geometric squeeze: $\sin(h)/h \to 1$.
  • 4. The Mean Value Theorem
    Local information (derivative) → global information (total change).
  • 5. L'Hôpital's Rule
    When $0/0$ blocks you, compare rates of vanishing.

Practice: differentiation drills (50+ functions). MVT application problems. L'Hôpital limit exercises.

Part B — Integration (month 3)

  • 6. The Riemann Integral
    Partitions, upper and lower sums, the definition from scratch.
  • 7. Integrability and Properties
    Continuous on a closed interval ⇒ integrable.
  • 8. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
    Differentiation and integration are inverse operations.
  • 9. Integration Techniques
    Substitution = chain rule backwards. Parts = product rule backwards.

Practice: 50+ integrals. Substitution, parts, partial fractions. This is where muscle memory forms.

Month 4 — Power Series and Taylor

Write complicated functions as infinite polynomials. Taylor expansions are concours bread and butter.

  • 1. Taylor's Theorem
    Matching derivatives to find coefficients. The Lagrange remainder.
  • 2. Power Series and Radius of Convergence
    Ratio test, Cauchy-Hadamard. Why $1/(1+x^2)$ has radius $1$.
  • 3. The Exponential Function
    $e^x = \sum x^n/n!$ — defined by the series, all properties proved.
  • 4. Sine and Cosine from Scratch
    Trig functions from pure arithmetic. $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ proved algebraically.
  • 5. Term-by-Term Operations
    Differentiating and integrating power series term by term.
  • 6. Abel's Theorem
    Boundary behaviour. $\ln(2) = 1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - \cdots$

Practice: Taylor-expand 20 functions. Remainder estimation problems. Find radius of convergence for 15 power series.

Month 5 — Convergence Toolkit

Systematic tools for taming series. Every exam has at least one "determine convergence" problem.

  • 1. The Comparison Test
    Compare with a series you already know.
  • 2. The Ratio and Root Tests
    d'Alembert and Cauchy automate comparison.
  • 3. The Alternating Series Test
    Alternating signs rescue the harmonic series.
  • 4. Absolute and Conditional Convergence
    Riemann rearrangement theorem. Absolute convergence is the safe notion.
  • 5. Cesàro Means
    When averages converge even if the sequence doesn't.
  • 6. Uniform Convergence
    Cauchy's error and Weierstrass's fix.
  • 7. The Weierstrass M-Test
    The practical shortcut for uniform convergence.
  • 8. The Weierstrass Approximation Theorem
    Polynomials approximate any continuous function.

Practice: classify 30+ series (converge/diverge, absolute/conditional). Uniform convergence problems.

Months 6–7 — Linear Algebra

~40% of CPGE exams. Eigenvalues, diagonalisation, projections. This needs two months.

  • 0. Systems of Linear Equations
    Gaussian elimination, row echelon form, Cramer's rule. The motivating problem.
  • 1. Vector Spaces
    Grassmann's abstraction — the 8 axioms.
  • 2. Basis and Dimension
    All bases have the same size. Dimension is well-defined.
  • 3. Linear Maps and Rank-Nullity
    Kernel + image = what you started with.
  • 4. Matrices and Change of Basis
    Linear maps as arrays. $P^{-1}AP$ translates between bases.
  • 5. Determinants
    Detects invertibility and measures volume scaling.
  • 6. Eigenvalues and the Characteristic Polynomial
    Special directions a map merely stretches. Diagonalisability.
  • 7. Inner Products and Gram-Schmidt
    Length and angle in abstract spaces. Cauchy-Schwarz. Orthonormalisation.
  • 8. Orthogonal Projection
    The closest point in a subspace.
  • 9. The Spectral Theorem
    Symmetric matrices always diagonalise with orthonormal eigenbasis.
  • 10. The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem
    Every matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial.
  • 11. Jordan Normal Form
    When diagonalisation fails, Jordan provides the next best thing.

Practice: diagonalise 20 matrices. Eigenvalue computations. Gram-Schmidt by hand. Projection problems. Jordan form exercises.

Month 8 — Differential Equations

Central to concours and stochastic calculus. Solve equations relating a function to its derivatives.

  • 1. First-Order ODEs
    Separable and linear equations. The integrating factor.
  • 2. The Cauchy-Lipschitz Theorem
    Existence and uniqueness. Picard iteration.
  • 3. Gronwall's Inequality
    The universal growth bound for solutions.
  • 4. Linear ODE Solution Structure
    Solutions form a vector space. Wronskian, characteristic polynomial.
  • 5. Variation of Parameters
    Lagrange's method for non-homogeneous equations.
  • 6. Systems and the Matrix Exponential
    $\mathbf{y}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{y}(0)$. Where linear algebra meets ODEs.
  • 7. Stability and Lyapunov's Method
    Prove stability without solving the ODE.
  • 8. The Poincaré-Bendixson Theorem
    No chaos below three dimensions.

Practice: solve 30+ ODEs. Phase portrait sketching. Stability analysis. Matrix exponential computations.

Month 9 — Multivariable Calculus (focused)

Partial derivatives, Jacobians, Lagrange multipliers. Skip vector calculus proofs — focus on computation.

  • 1. Partial Derivatives and the Gradient
    Differentiate in each direction separately. The gradient points uphill.
  • 2. The Multivariable Chain Rule
    The chain rule is matrix multiplication — the Jacobian.
  • 3. Schwarz's Theorem
    Mixed partials commute when continuous.
  • 4. The Implicit and Inverse Function Theorems
    Solve $F(x,y) = 0$ for $y$ when the Jacobian is invertible.
  • 5. Lagrange Multipliers and the Second Derivative Test
    Optimise under constraints. The Hessian classifies critical points.
  • 6. Multiple Integrals and Fubini's Theorem
    Double integrals as iterated single integrals. Change of variables.

Pages 7–8 (Green, Stokes, divergence) — read if time permits, not priority.

Practice: gradient/Jacobian computations. Lagrange multiplier optimisation problems. Double integral exercises.

Months 10–11 — Probability

Non-negotiable for quant finance. CLT, conditional expectation, moment generating functions.

  • 0. Counting
    Factorials, permutations, combinations, binomial coefficients, stars and bars.
  • 1. Probability Spaces
    Kolmogorov's axioms, counting, law of total probability.
  • 2. Conditional Probability and Bayes' Theorem
    Updating beliefs with evidence.
  • 3. Random Variables and Expectation
    Linearity of expectation — the single most useful fact in probability.
  • 4. Variance and Covariance
    Measuring spread. Independence and correlation.
  • 5. Markov and Chebyshev Inequalities
    Universal tail bounds from moments alone.
  • 6. The Law of Large Numbers
    Sample means converge to true means.
  • 7. The Central Limit Theorem
    Sums of independent variables → Gaussian.
  • 8. Conditional Expectation as Projection
    The best predictor minimises mean-squared error.

Practice: conditional probability problems. Expectation/variance computations. CLT applications. Past concours probability papers.

Month 12 — Fourier Basics + Spectral Theory

Light month. Focus on computation and key results, not deep proofs.

Phase 11 — Fourier (basics)

  • 1. Fourier Series
    Decompose periodic functions into pure frequencies.
  • 2. Convergence of Fourier Series
    Dirichlet conditions. When does the series converge?
  • 3. Bessel's Inequality and Parseval's Theorem
    Energy conservation. $\sum 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ as a corollary.

Phase 13 — Spectral Theory (highlights)

  • 3. The Spectral Theorem
    $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ for symmetric matrices.
  • 4. Singular Value Decomposition
    Any matrix: $A = U\Sigma V^T$. Rotate-stretch-rotate.
  • 5. The Rayleigh Quotient
    Eigenvalues as extrema of $x^TAx/x^Tx$.

Practice: compute Fourier coefficients. SVD by hand for small matrices. Parseval identity applications.

Exercise sources

The course pages are exposition-only. You need external problems:

  • Past CPGE concours papers — "annales concours X MP/PC maths", or e3a/CCINP for easier. Do them timed.
  • Oral exam problems — "oraux X ENS maths". Shorter, more targeted.
  • Gourdon, Les maths en tête (Analyse + Algèbre) — gold standard for CPGE problems with solutions.
  • Francinou-Gianella-Nicolas, Oraux X-ENS series.

After the 12 months

For stochastic calculus, you'll still need: measure theory (Lebesgue integral, sigma-algebras), martingales, Brownian motion, Itô calculus. But the essential core gives you the prerequisites. The jump is ~3–4 months additional.
