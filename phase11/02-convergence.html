Convergence of Fourier Series

Phase 11 — Fourier Analysis, Page 2

The problem

On page 1, we computed Fourier coefficients and wrote the square wave as $f(x) = \frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\sin((2k+1)x)}{2k+1}$. But does this infinite sum actually equal $f(x)$?

At points where $f$ is continuous, yes — Dirichlet proved this in 1829. At the discontinuity $x = 0$, the partial sums converge to $0$, the average of the left and right limits ($-1$ and $+1$). But this averaging behaviour is not at all obvious from the formula. And for wilder functions, things can go badly wrong: there exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at certain points.

The central question of this page: when exactly does a Fourier series converge, and to what value?

1. Partial sums and the Dirichlet kernel

Write $S_N(f)$ for the $N$-th partial sum of the Fourier series of $f$:

$$S_N(f)(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl(a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)\bigr)$$

This is a trigonometric polynomial of degree $N$: a finite sum of harmonics up to frequency $N$. As $N$ increases, we include higher and higher frequencies, and the approximation should improve.

The convergence question: does $S_N(f)(x) \to f(x)$ as $N \to \infty$? And if so, in what sense — at each individual point (pointwise)? with the error shrinking uniformly across all $x$ (uniform convergence)? in some averaged sense ($L^2$ convergence)? These are genuinely different questions with different answers.

To attack all of them, we need a more workable expression for $S_N$ — one that exposes its dependence on $f$ and $N$ simultaneously.

We want to rewrite $S_N(f)(x)$ as an integral involving $f$ and some kernel function. Substitute the integral formulas for each coefficient from page 1. For $n \geq 1$:

$$a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\bigl[\cos(nt)\cos(nx) + \sin(nt)\sin(nx)\bigr]\,dt$$

By the cosine subtraction formula (see Phase 4, page 4), $\cos(nt)\cos(nx) + \sin(nt)\sin(nx) = \cos(n(x - t))$. So:

$$a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\cos\bigl(n(x-t)\bigr)\,dt$$

Adding the $a_0/2$ term (which contributes $\frac{1}{2\pi}\int f(t)\,dt = \frac{1}{\pi}\int f(t) \cdot \frac{1}{2}\,dt$) and summing over $n = 1, \ldots, N$:

$$S_N(f)(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\left[\frac{1}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{N}\cos\bigl(n(x-t)\bigr)\right]dt$$

The expression in brackets depends only on $u = x - t$. We give it a name:

Dirichlet kernel

The Dirichlet kernel of order $N$ is:

$$D_N(u) = \frac{1}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{N}\cos(nu)$$

With this definition, the partial sum becomes a convolution:

$$S_N(f)(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t)\,D_N(x - t)\,dt$$

This is a powerful rewriting. Instead of studying the partial sum directly (which involves all the coefficients $a_n, b_n$), we study the single kernel $D_N$. The convergence of $S_N(f)(x) \to f(x)$ depends entirely on the properties of $D_N$ as $N \to \infty$.

2. Closed form of the Dirichlet kernel

Dirichlet kernel — closed form

For $u$ not a multiple of $2\pi$ (i.e. $\sin(u/2) \neq 0$):

$$D_N(u) = \frac{\sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr)}{2\sin(u/2)}$$

At $u = 0$ (and more generally $u = 2k\pi$), $D_N(0) = N + \frac{1}{2}$ by continuity.

Proof

The trick is to multiply by $2\sin(u/2)$ and watch a telescoping cancellation. This is a standard technique for summing cosine series.

Step 1. Compute $2\sin(u/2) \cdot D_N(u)$:

$$2\sin(u/2)\,D_N(u) = 2\sin(u/2) \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 2\sin(u/2)\sum_{n=1}^{N}\cos(nu) = \sin(u/2) + \sum_{n=1}^{N} 2\sin(u/2)\cos(nu)$$

Step 2. Apply the product-to-sum identity $2\sin A\cos B = \sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B)$ with $A = u/2$ and $B = nu$:

$$2\sin(u/2)\cos(nu) = \sin\bigl(u/2 + nu\bigr) + \sin\bigl(u/2 - nu\bigr)$$

Since $\sin(-\alpha) = -\sin(\alpha)$, we can write $\sin(u/2 - nu) = -\sin(nu - u/2) = -\sin((n - 1/2)u)$. So:

$$2\sin(u/2)\cos(nu) = \sin\bigl((n + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr) - \sin\bigl((n - \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr)$$

Step 3. Sum from $n = 1$ to $N$. Write out the terms:

$$n = 1: \quad \sin(\tfrac{3}{2}u) - \sin(\tfrac{1}{2}u)$$
$$n = 2: \quad \sin(\tfrac{5}{2}u) - \sin(\tfrac{3}{2}u)$$
$$n = 3: \quad \sin(\tfrac{7}{2}u) - \sin(\tfrac{5}{2}u)$$
$$\vdots$$
$$n = N: \quad \sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr) - \sin\bigl((N - \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr)$$

Each $\sin((n+\frac{1}{2})u)$ in one line cancels with $-\sin((n+\frac{1}{2})u)$ from the next line (where it appears as $\sin(((n+1) - \frac{1}{2})u)$). Everything cancels except the very first subtracted term and the very last added term:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl[\sin\bigl((n + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr) - \sin\bigl((n - \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr)\bigr] = \sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr) - \sin(u/2)$$

Step 4. Combine with the initial $\sin(u/2)$ term:

$$2\sin(u/2)\,D_N(u) = \sin(u/2) + \sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr) - \sin(u/2) = \sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})u\bigr)$$

Divide both sides by $2\sin(u/2)$ (valid when $\sin(u/2) \neq 0$, i.e. $u$ is not a multiple of $2\pi$).

At $u = 0$, both sides have the form $0/0$. By L'Hôpital or by direct substitution into the series definition: $D_N(0) = \frac{1}{2} + N = N + \frac{1}{2}$, which agrees with $\lim_{u \to 0}\frac{\sin((N+1/2)u)}{2\sin(u/2)} = \frac{N + 1/2}{1} = N + \frac{1}{2}$.

∎

Two key properties of the Dirichlet kernel:

Property 1: unit mass. Integrating the series definition term by term:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} D_N(u)\,du = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{1}{2}\,du + \sum_{n=1}^{N}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos(nu)\,du = \pi + 0 + 0 + \cdots = \pi$$

So $\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} D_N(u)\,du = 1$. If $D_N$ were non-negative and concentrated at $u = 0$, the convolution $\frac{1}{\pi}\int f(t)D_N(x-t)\,dt$ would simply pick out $f(x)$. But $D_N$ oscillates and takes negative values, which is what makes convergence non-trivial.

Property 2: concentration. As $N$ grows, $D_N$ develops a tall, narrow peak at $u = 0$. The height is $D_N(0) = N + \frac{1}{2}$ (set $u = 0$ in the series: $\frac{1}{2} + N$ terms each equal to $1$). The main peak has width approximately $2\pi/(2N+1)$, shrinking as $N$ increases.

Outside the main peak, $D_N$ oscillates rapidly and the positive and negative lobes nearly cancel. Intuitively, $D_N$ concentrates more and more of its total mass near $u = 0$, so the convolution $\frac{1}{\pi}\int f(t)D_N(x-t)\,dt$ increasingly "samples" $f$ near $t = x$.

If $D_N$ were non-negative (like a Gaussian bell curve), convergence would be automatic: the convolution would be an average of $f$ near $x$, and as the averaging window shrank (with increasing $N$), the result would tend to $f(x)$. But $D_N$ takes large negative values — its side lobes do not vanish and do not shrink relative to the main peak. This is the fundamental difficulty of Fourier convergence.

In fact, $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |D_N(u)|\,du \sim C\log N$: the total variation of $D_N$ grows without bound. This is why convergence of Fourier series is a hard theorem rather than a trivial observation, and why it can fail for some continuous functions.

3. The Riemann-Lebesgue lemma

Before tackling Dirichlet's theorem, we need a fundamental fact about oscillatory integrals. This lemma appears everywhere in analysis — it is the engine behind most Fourier convergence results.

Riemann-Lebesgue lemma

If $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$, then

$$\lim_{N \to \infty}\int_a^b f(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx = 0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{N \to \infty}\int_a^b f(x)\cos(Nx)\,dx = 0$$

The intuition is physical. Imagine $f$ as a slowly varying "envelope" and $\sin(Nx)$ as a rapidly oscillating carrier wave. Over each half-period of the oscillation (length $\pi/N$), $f$ is nearly constant, so the integral of $f(x)\sin(Nx)$ over that half-period is approximately $f(x_0) \int \sin(Nx)\,dx \approx 0$, since sine integrates to zero over a half-period. As $N \to \infty$, the cancellation becomes perfect.

Proof

We prove the sine case; the cosine case is identical.

Step 1. First suppose $f = \mathbf{1}_{[c,d]}$ is the characteristic function of a subinterval $[c, d] \subseteq [a, b]$. Then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx = \int_c^d \sin(Nx)\,dx = \left[-\frac{\cos(Nx)}{N}\right]_c^d = \frac{\cos(Nc) - \cos(Nd)}{N}$$

Since $|\cos(\alpha)| \leq 1$ for all $\alpha$, $|\cos(Nc) - \cos(Nd)| \leq 2$, so the whole expression is bounded by $\frac{2}{N}$, which tends to $0$ as $N \to \infty$.

Step 2. A step function is a finite linear combination $\phi = \sum_{k=1}^{m} \alpha_k\,\mathbf{1}_{[c_k, d_k]}$. By linearity of the integral:

$$\int_a^b \phi(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx = \sum_{k=1}^{m}\alpha_k \cdot \frac{\cos(Nc_k) - \cos(Nd_k)}{N}$$

Each term is bounded by $2|\alpha_k|/N$, and a finite sum of terms each $O(1/N)$ is itself $O(1/N) \to 0$. The number of terms $m$ is fixed (independent of $N$), so we do not need uniform bounds.

Step 3. Now let $f$ be a general integrable function. Since $f$ is Riemann integrable (see Phase 3, page 6), for any $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a step function $\phi$ such that $\int_a^b |f(x) - \phi(x)|\,dx < \varepsilon$. Then:

$$\left|\int_a^b f(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx\right| \leq \left|\int_a^b \phi(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx\right| + \int_a^b |f(x) - \phi(x)| \cdot |\sin(Nx)|\,dx$$

The first term tends to $0$ by Step 2 (choose $N$ large enough that it is less than $\varepsilon$). The second term is at most $\int_a^b |f(x) - \phi(x)| \cdot |\sin(Nx)|\,dx \leq \int_a^b |f - \phi|\,dx < \varepsilon$ (using $|\sin(Nx)| \leq 1$). So for all sufficiently large $N$:

$$\left|\int_a^b f(x)\sin(Nx)\,dx\right| < \varepsilon + \varepsilon = 2\varepsilon$$

Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, the integral tends to $0$.

∎

In the language of Fourier coefficients: the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma says $a_n \to 0$ and $b_n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. The Fourier coefficients of any integrable function decay to zero.

This has an immediate practical consequence: if a trigonometric series $\sum (a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx)$ has coefficients that do not tend to zero, it cannot be the Fourier series of any integrable function. For instance, the series $\sum \cos(nx)$ (all coefficients equal to $1$) is not the Fourier series of any integrable function.

The lemma gives a quick "sanity check" on proposed Fourier representations. It is a necessary condition for being a Fourier series, though far from sufficient — the harmonic series $\sum 1/n$ has terms tending to zero but diverges.

The lemma does not tell us how fast the coefficients decay. That depends on the smoothness of $f$: a discontinuous function like the square wave has $b_n = O(1/n)$, while a smooth function can have coefficients decaying like $1/n^k$ or even exponentially. We will see this interplay between smoothness and decay throughout this page.

4. Dirichlet's convergence theorem

Dirichlet's theorem

Let $f$ be a $2\pi$-periodic function that is piecewise smooth on $[-\pi, \pi]$ — meaning $f$ and $f'$ are piecewise continuous with at most finitely many jump discontinuities. Then for every $x$:

$$\lim_{N \to \infty} S_N(f)(x) = \frac{f(x^+) + f(x^-)}{2}$$

where $f(x^+) = \lim_{t \to x^+} f(t)$ and $f(x^-) = \lim_{t \to x^-} f(t)$.

Let us unpack the hypotheses and conclusion carefully.

Piecewise smooth means: the interval $[-\pi, \pi]$ can be split into finitely many subintervals on each of which $f$ is continuously differentiable. At the endpoints of these subintervals, $f$ may have jump discontinuities, but the one-sided limits $f(x^+)$, $f(x^-)$, $f'(x^+)$, $f'(x^-)$ must all exist and be finite. This covers essentially every function you encounter in practice: polynomials, trig functions, the square wave, the sawtooth, piecewise linear functions, and any "reasonable" engineering signal.

The key hypothesis is the existence of one-sided derivatives. At a jump discontinuity, $f$ must approach the jump from each side with a well-defined slope — it cannot oscillate infinitely as it approaches the discontinuity. The function $f(x) = \sin(1/x)$ near $x = 0$, for instance, oscillates infinitely often and does not satisfy the piecewise smooth condition.

The conclusion says two things:

At a point of continuity: $f(x^+) = f(x^-) = f(x)$, so the Fourier series converges to $f(x)$. This is the "normal" case — the series reconstructs the function.

At a jump discontinuity: if $f$ jumps from $L = f(x^-)$ on the left to $R = f(x^+)$ on the right, the series converges to $\frac{L + R}{2}$ — the midpoint of the jump. For the square wave at $x = 0$: $f(0^-) = -1$, $f(0^+) = +1$, so $S_N(f)(0) \to \frac{-1 + 1}{2} = 0$. This is why we defined $f(0) = 0$ on page 1 — the Fourier series "wants" the function to take the average value at discontinuities.

Proof

Fix a point $x_0 \in [-\pi, \pi]$. We show $S_N(f)(x_0) \to \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2}$. The strategy: rewrite $S_N(f)(x_0) - \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2}$ as an oscillatory integral, then kill it with Riemann-Lebesgue.

Step 1. Using $S_N(f)(x_0) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x_0 - t)\,D_N(t)\,dt$ and the fact that $\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} D_N(t)\,dt = 1$, write:

$$S_N(f)(x_0) - \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\left[f(x_0 - t) - \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2}\right]D_N(t)\,dt$$

Step 2. Split the integral at $t = 0$. Since $D_N$ is even ($D_N(-t) = D_N(t)$, visible from the cosine series definition), we have $\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi} D_N(t)\,dt = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{0} D_N(t)\,dt = \frac{1}{2}$. So:

$$S_N(f)(x_0) - \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\bigl[f(x_0 - t) - f(x_0^-)\bigr]D_N(t)\,dt}_{I_1} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{0}\bigl[f(x_0 - t) - f(x_0^+)\bigr]D_N(t)\,dt}_{I_2}$$

This splitting is natural: when $t > 0$ (and small), $x_0 - t$ is slightly less than $x_0$, approaching from the left, so $f(x_0 - t) \to f(x_0^-)$. We subtract $f(x_0^-)$ so that the integrand vanishes as $t \to 0^+$. Similarly, when $t < 0$ (and small in absolute value), $x_0 - t > x_0$, approaching from the right, so we subtract $f(x_0^+)$.

Step 3. We show $I_1 \to 0$. Substitute $D_N(t) = \frac{\sin((N+1/2)t)}{2\sin(t/2)}$:

$$I_1 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\frac{f(x_0 - t) - f(x_0^-)}{2\sin(t/2)}\,\sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})t\bigr)\,dt$$

Step 4. Define $g(t) = \frac{f(x_0 - t) - f(x_0^-)}{2\sin(t/2)}$ for $t \in (0, \pi]$. The potential difficulty is at $t = 0$, where both numerator and denominator tend to zero. Factor:

$$g(t) = \frac{f(x_0 - t) - f(x_0^-)}{t} \cdot \frac{t}{2\sin(t/2)}$$

As $t \to 0^+$: the first factor tends to $-f'(x_0^-)$ (the left derivative, which exists because $f$ is piecewise smooth). The second factor tends to $1$ (since $\sin(t/2) \sim t/2$). So $g$ has a removable singularity at $t = 0$, and after extending it continuously, $g$ is integrable on $[0, \pi]$.

Step 5. Since $g$ is integrable on $[0, \pi]$, we may apply the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma (with the role of $N$ played by $N + \frac{1}{2}$, which tends to $\infty$):

$$I_1 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi} g(t)\,\sin\bigl((N + \tfrac{1}{2})t\bigr)\,dt \to 0 \quad \text{as } N \to \infty$$

Step 6. For the second integral $I_2$, substitute $s = -t$ (so the integral runs from $0$ to $\pi$). When $t < 0$, $x_0 - t > x_0$, so $f(x_0 - t) \to f(x_0^+)$ as $t \to 0^-$, i.e. $s \to 0^+$. Define $h(s) = \frac{f(x_0 + s) - f(x_0^+)}{2\sin(s/2)}$ for $s \in (0, \pi]$. By the same argument as for $g$, $h$ has a removable singularity at $s = 0$ (the limit is $f'(x_0^+)$), so $h$ is integrable. By Riemann-Lebesgue, $I_2 \to 0$.

Step 7. Since both $I_1 \to 0$ and $I_2 \to 0$, we conclude:

$$S_N(f)(x_0) \to \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2}$$

At a point of continuity, $f(x_0^+) = f(x_0^-) = f(x_0)$, so $S_N(f)(x_0) \to f(x_0)$. At a jump, the series converges to the midpoint of the jump.

∎

5. Uniform convergence

Pointwise convergence tells you the series converges at each individual point. But for many applications — passing limits through integrals, exchanging sum and derivative — you need something stronger: uniform convergence, meaning $\sup_x |S_N(f)(x) - f(x)| \to 0$.

Uniform convergence cannot hold near discontinuities. Each partial sum $S_N(f)$ is a finite sum of continuous functions, hence continuous. If $f$ has a jump of size $J$, then $\sup_x |S_N(f)(x) - f(x)| \geq J/2$ for all $N$, since the continuous $S_N$ cannot match the discontinuous $f$ uniformly.

So uniform convergence requires $f$ to be continuous. But even continuity is not quite enough — we also need the periodic extension to be continuous, i.e. $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$. The precise criterion:

Uniform convergence criterion

If $f$ is continuous on $[-\pi, \pi]$, piecewise $C^1$, and satisfies $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$, then the Fourier series converges uniformly to $f$.

The condition $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$ ensures that the $2\pi$-periodic extension of $f$ is continuous at $x = \pm\pi$ (where the copies of $[-\pi, \pi]$ are "glued together"). If $f(-\pi) \neq f(\pi)$, the periodic extension has a jump discontinuity at every odd multiple of $\pi$, and uniform convergence fails there. The function $f(x) = x$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$ is a prime example: $f(-\pi) = -\pi \neq \pi = f(\pi)$, which is why the sawtooth wave's Fourier series converges only pointwise, not uniformly.

Proof

Step 1. We show the Fourier coefficients decay like $O(1/n)$ by integrating by parts. For $b_n$:

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(nx)\,dx$$

With $u = f(x)$ and $dv = \sin(nx)\,dx$:

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\left[-\frac{f(x)\cos(nx)}{n}\right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} + \frac{1}{\pi n}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f'(x)\cos(nx)\,dx$$

Step 2. Evaluate the boundary term:

$$\left[-\frac{f(x)\cos(nx)}{n}\right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} = -\frac{f(\pi)\cos(n\pi)}{n} + \frac{f(-\pi)\cos(-n\pi)}{n} = \frac{\cos(n\pi)}{n}\bigl[f(-\pi) - f(\pi)\bigr]$$

Since $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$ by hypothesis, this is zero. So:

$$b_n = \frac{1}{n}\left(\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f'(x)\cos(nx)\,dx\right) = \frac{\alpha_n}{n}$$

where $\alpha_n$ is the $n$-th Fourier cosine coefficient of $f'$. The same argument gives $a_n = \beta_n / n$ where $\beta_n$ is a Fourier sine coefficient of $f'$.

Step 3. The derivative $f'$ is piecewise continuous and hence square-integrable: $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |f'(x)|^2\,dx < \infty$. By Bessel's inequality (IOU: proved on page 3), the sum of squares of any set of Fourier coefficients is bounded by $\frac{1}{\pi}\int |f'|^2$. In particular, $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \alpha_n^2 < \infty$ and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \beta_n^2 < \infty$.

Step 4. Apply Cauchy-Schwarz (see Phase 6, page 7) to bound the sum of absolute values:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |b_n| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{|\alpha_n|}{n} \leq \left(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \alpha_n^2\right)^{1/2}\left(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}\right)^{1/2} < \infty$$

The series $\sum 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ converges (we established this as the Basel problem in Phase 4, page 5). So the right-hand side is finite, and $\sum |b_n| < \infty$. The identical argument (integrating by parts with $\cos(nx)$ instead of $\sin(nx)$) gives $\sum |a_n| < \infty$.

Step 5. For each $n$ and every $x$:

$$|a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)| \leq |a_n| + |b_n| =: M_n$$

We have shown $\sum M_n < \infty$. By the Weierstrass M-test (see Phase 5, page 7), the Fourier series converges uniformly on $[-\pi, \pi]$.

∎

Notice the key mechanism: integration by parts transfers one power of $n$ from the coefficient to the derivative, turning $a_n$ into $\beta_n / n$. This is a general principle:

If $f$ is $C^k$ (k-times continuously differentiable) and all derivatives up to order $k-1$ match at $\pm\pi$, then $a_n, b_n = O(1/n^{k+1})$. Each additional derivative you can integrate by parts buys one more power of decay. For a $C^\infty$ function with matching boundary conditions, the coefficients decay faster than any power of $1/n$ — they vanish "superpolynomially."

Conversely, discontinuities slow down the decay. The square wave (a jump discontinuity in $f$) has coefficients $b_n = O(1/n)$. The sawtooth $f(x) = x$ on $(-\pi, \pi)$ also has $b_n = O(1/n)$, because although $f$ itself is continuous on the open interval, its periodic extension has a jump at $\pm\pi$. If $f$ is genuinely continuous with $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$ and is piecewise $C^1$, then $a_n, b_n = O(1/n^2)$. For $C^2$ functions: $O(1/n^3)$, and so on.

This smoothness-decay correspondence is one of the deepest principles in harmonic analysis. It explains why smooth signals are "low-frequency" (their Fourier coefficients are concentrated at small $n$) while rough signals spread their energy across all frequencies. In signal processing, this is the basis of low-pass filtering: removing high-frequency components smooths a signal, and adding them back sharpens it.

The correspondence also has a converse flavour: if you know the Fourier coefficients decay like $O(1/n^k)$, you can deduce (under appropriate conditions) that $f$ has some degree of differentiability. The Fourier coefficients encode the regularity of the function — they are a "fingerprint" of smoothness.

At the extreme end: for analytic functions (those with convergent Taylor series on a strip around the real axis in the complex plane), the Fourier coefficients decay exponentially: $|c_n| \leq Ce^{-\alpha|n|}$ for some $\alpha > 0$. The width of the analyticity strip determines $\alpha$. This is why analytic functions are so well-behaved from the Fourier perspective — even 5 or 10 terms of the series give an excellent approximation.

At the other extreme: a function with a jump discontinuity has coefficients that decay like $1/n$ — the slowest possible rate for a bounded function. This is precisely why Fourier series struggle with discontinuities and why phenomena like Gibbs overshoot appear.

For the sawtooth $f(x) = x$ on $(-\pi, \pi)$: $f$ is smooth on the open interval, but the periodic extension has a jump at $\pm\pi$, so $b_n = O(1/n)$. If instead we took $f(x) = x(\pi - x)(\pi + x)$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$ — a smooth function with $f(-\pi) = f(\pi) = 0$ — integration by parts gives $a_n, b_n = O(1/n^4)$, and the Fourier series converges very rapidly.

The lesson is practical: if you want fast convergence, ensure the function and its periodic extension are as smooth as possible. Every discontinuity — whether in $f$ itself, its derivative, or its higher derivatives — shows up as a slower decay rate in the Fourier coefficients.

We will make the smoothness-decay correspondence fully precise using Parseval's theorem on page 3, which links the energy of $f$ in physical space to the energy of its Fourier coefficients in frequency space. That identity completes the circle: orthogonality gave us the coefficients, Dirichlet tells us the series converges, and Parseval tells us it converges with no energy lost.

Historical note

Dirichlet published his convergence theorem in 1829 in Crelle's Journal (the first major German mathematics journal), under the title Sur la convergence des séries trigonométriques. It was written in French, the international language of mathematics at the time. This was the first rigorous proof that a Fourier series converges to the function it represents, under explicit conditions. Before Dirichlet, Fourier's claims were unproved conjectures, and Lagrange had actively denied their validity.

Dirichlet's work was a watershed for analysis. It forced mathematicians to think carefully about what "function" meant — Dirichlet himself gave his famous definition of "arbitrary function" (any assignment of values, not necessarily given by a formula) in this very paper. The question "for which functions does the Fourier series converge?" drove much of 19th-century analysis. Riemann extended the theory to broader function classes. Du Bois-Reymond (1876) shocked the community by constructing a continuous function whose Fourier series diverges at a point.

The ultimate positive result came only in 1966, when Lennart Carleson proved that the Fourier series of any square-integrable function converges almost everywhere. This is one of the deepest results in 20th-century analysis — the proof is over 30 pages of intricate estimation. Carleson received the Abel Prize in 2006, largely for this work.

Lebesgue's theory of integration (1901) was itself motivated in part by Fourier convergence problems. The question "what is the largest class of functions for which the Fourier series converges?" demanded a broader notion of integration, and Lebesgue provided it.

What breaks

Without piecewise smoothness: there exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at a point. Paul du Bois-Reymond constructed the first such example in 1876, and it shocked the mathematical community. It showed that continuity alone does not guarantee pointwise convergence.

How bad can it get? Carleson (1966) proved the deepest positive result: for any $L^2$ function, the Fourier series converges almost everywhere (everywhere except on a set of measure zero). This includes all continuous functions. So for continuous functions, divergence can only happen on a "negligible" set.

For merely $L^1$ functions (integrable but not square-integrable), the situation is worse: Kolmogorov (1926) constructed an integrable function whose Fourier series diverges everywhere. So some regularity beyond bare integrability is essential.

Without periodicity matching ($f(-\pi) \neq f(\pi)$): Dirichlet's theorem still applies, so the series converges pointwise everywhere. But it does not converge uniformly. Near the artificial jump at $\pm\pi$ (where the periodic extension glues together), the partial sums develop persistent overshoot of about $9\%$ of the jump height. This is the Gibbs phenomenon, which we study on page 4. The overshoot does not shrink as $N \to \infty$ — it just narrows.

Pointwise convergence does not imply uniform convergence: the square wave's Fourier series converges pointwise at every point (by Dirichlet's theorem), but not uniformly. The convergence is slowest near the discontinuities, where the partial sums always overshoot. For the square wave, $\sup_x |S_N(f)(x) - f(x)|$ does not tend to $0$ — it stays above $0.08$ no matter how large $N$ is. Uniform convergence requires the periodic extension to be continuous.

The decay rate matters for applications. The Riemann-Lebesgue lemma tells us $a_n, b_n \to 0$, but that alone does not guarantee convergence of the series $\sum(a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx)$. Compare with the harmonic series: $1/n \to 0$ but $\sum 1/n = \infty$. You need the coefficients to decay fast enough that $\sum(|a_n| + |b_n|) < \infty$. This absolute convergence is guaranteed when $a_n, b_n = O(1/n^{1+\varepsilon})$ for any $\varepsilon > 0$, and in particular when $a_n, b_n = O(1/n^2)$ — which is precisely the decay rate for $C^1$ functions with $f(-\pi) = f(\pi)$. This is the uniform convergence criterion proved above.

Dirichlet's theorem: the Fourier series of a piecewise smooth function converges at every point — to the function value at continuity points, and to the average of left and right limits at jumps.
