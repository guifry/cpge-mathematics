Uniform Convergence

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit, Page 6

The problem

Cauchy (1821) stated what seemed obvious: "the limit of a sequence of continuous functions is continuous." It felt like a theorem that hardly needed proof. But Fourier's work on heat conduction produced sequences of continuous functions whose pointwise limit was discontinuous. Abel (1826) noticed the error. Weierstrass (1841, published later) found the fix: Cauchy's claim is wrong for pointwise convergence, but true for a stronger notion — uniform convergence.

1. Pointwise convergence

Definition — Pointwise convergence

A sequence of functions $(f_n)$ defined on a set $D$ converges pointwise to $f$ if for every $x \in D$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x) = f(x)$$

Equivalently: for every $x \in D$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(x, \varepsilon)$ such that $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$.

The critical feature: $N$ depends on $x$. At some points convergence may be fast; at others, agonisingly slow. Different points march at different speeds, and this is what causes trouble.

To see the dependence on $x$ concretely, consider $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0, 1]$ (which we analyse fully below). For $x = 0.5$, we have $f_n(0.5) = (0.5)^n$, and $f_{10}(0.5) \approx 0.001$ — close to $0$ already at $n = 10$. But for $x = 0.99$, $f_{10}(0.99) = 0.99^{10} \approx 0.904$, and we need $n \approx 460$ to get $f_n(0.99) < 0.01$. The closer $x$ is to $1$, the larger $N$ must be. No single $N$ works for all $x$ simultaneously.

2. The canonical counterexample

Define $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0, 1]$. Each $f_n$ is continuous (it is a polynomial). What is the pointwise limit?

  • If $0 \leq x < 1$: $x^n \to 0$ (since $|x| < 1$).
  • If $x = 1$: $1^n = 1$ for all $n$.

So the pointwise limit is:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } 0 \leq x < 1 \\ 1 & \text{if } x = 1 \end{cases}$$

This function is discontinuous at $x = 1$. A sequence of continuous functions has converged to a discontinuous function. Cauchy's "theorem" is false.

The problem is visible: near $x = 1$, convergence is extremely slow. For $x = 1 - 1/n$, we have $f_n(x) = (1 - 1/n)^n \to 1/e \approx 0.37$, far from the limit $f(x) = 0$. The closer $x$ is to $1$, the larger $N(x, \varepsilon)$ must be. No single $N$ works for all $x$.

3. Uniform convergence

Definition — Uniform convergence

A sequence $(f_n)$ converges uniformly to $f$ on $D$ if:

$$\sup_{x \in D} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Equivalently: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(\varepsilon)$ (independent of $x$) such that $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$ and all $x \in D$.

The crucial difference: $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on $x$. The entire function $f_n$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $f$ simultaneously at every point, once $n$ is large enough. Think of a horizontal $\varepsilon$-tube around the graph of $f$ — uniform convergence means $f_n$ eventually lives entirely inside that tube.

4. The uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous

Theorem — Uniform limit preserves continuity

Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of functions continuous on $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. If $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $D$, then $f$ is continuous on $D$.

Proof

Step 1. Fix $x_0 \in D$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$. We must show $|f(x) - f(x_0)| < 3\varepsilon$ for $x$ close to $x_0$.

Step 2. Since $f_n \to f$ uniformly, choose $N$ such that $|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$ for all $x \in D$ and all $n \geq N$. Fix this $N$.

Step 3. Since $f_N$ is continuous at $x_0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| < \varepsilon$ whenever $|x - x_0| < \delta$ and $x \in D$.

Step 4. For $|x - x_0| < \delta$, apply the triangle inequality:

$$|f(x) - f(x_0)| \leq |f(x) - f_N(x)| + |f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| + |f_N(x_0) - f(x_0)|$$

The first term is $< \varepsilon$ (uniform convergence at $x$). The second is $< \varepsilon$ (continuity of $f_N$). The third is $< \varepsilon$ (uniform convergence at $x_0$). So:

$$|f(x) - f(x_0)| < 3\varepsilon$$

Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous at $x_0$. Since $x_0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is continuous on $D$.

∎

This is the classic $3\varepsilon$ argument. The triangle inequality splits the distance into three hops: from $f$ to $f_n$, across $f_n$ (using its continuity), and back from $f_n$ to $f$. Uniform convergence ensures the first and third hops are small simultaneously for all $x$.

5. A positive example: uniform convergence on $[0, 1/2]$

The same sequence $f_n(x) = x^n$ does converge uniformly to $f(x) = 0$ on $[0, c]$ for any fixed $c < 1$. Let us verify this. On $[0, c]$:

$$\sup_{x \in [0, c]} |x^n - 0| = c^n$$

Since $0 \leq c < 1$, we have $c^n \to 0$. So $\sup |f_n - f| \to 0$, confirming uniform convergence. The limit $f(x) = 0$ is continuous, as the theorem guarantees.

The problem on $[0, 1]$ is entirely due to the endpoint $x = 1$. On any strict sub-interval $[0, c]$ with $c < 1$, everything works perfectly. This pattern — uniform convergence on compact subsets of the interior, but failure at boundary points — is typical of power series and recurs throughout analysis.

6. Revisiting the counterexample

We now confirm that $f_n(x) = x^n$ does not converge uniformly on $[0, 1]$. The pointwise limit is $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in [0,1)$ and $f(1) = 1$. Compute:

$$\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \geq |f_n(1) - f(1)| = |1 - 1| = 0$$

That bound is useless. Instead, look at the sup over $[0, 1)$. For any $n$, the function $x^n$ is increasing on $[0,1]$, so:

$$\sup_{x \in [0,1)} |x^n - 0| = \lim_{x \to 1^-} x^n = 1$$

So $\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| = 1$ for every $n$ (taking the jump at $x = 1$ into account as well). This does not tend to $0$. The convergence is not uniform, which is why the limit function is discontinuous — perfectly consistent with our theorem.

7. Uniform convergence preserves integrability

Theorem — Swapping limits and integrals

Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of integrable functions on $[a, b]$. If $f_n \to f$ uniformly on $[a, b]$, then $f$ is integrable on $[a, b]$ and:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n(x)\, dx = \int_a^b f(x)\, dx$$
Proof

Step 1. Since each $f_n$ is integrable (hence bounded) and $f_n \to f$ uniformly, $f$ is bounded on $[a,b]$. Moreover, $f$ is the uniform limit of continuous functions if each $f_n$ is continuous, hence continuous and integrable. More generally, uniform convergence preserves integrability (the upper and lower sums of $f$ can be controlled by those of $f_n$ plus the uniform error), but the key estimate below works regardless.

Step 2. For the limit interchange, let $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $N$ so that $\sup_{x \in [a,b]} |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$ for $n \geq N$. Then for $n \geq N$:

$$\left|\int_a^b f_n(x)\, dx - \int_a^b f(x)\, dx\right| = \left|\int_a^b (f_n(x) - f(x))\, dx\right|$$
$$\leq \int_a^b |f_n(x) - f(x)|\, dx \leq \int_a^b \varepsilon\, dx = (b - a)\varepsilon$$

Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $\int_a^b f_n \to \int_a^b f$.

∎

This is the payoff: uniform convergence lets you swap the limit and the integral. Without uniformity, this can fail. In Phase 4 we used term-by-term integration of power series (Phase 4, page 5) — that relied on uniform convergence on compact subsets of the disc of convergence, which is precisely what the Weierstrass M-test provides.

8. The logical structure

Let us summarise the relationships we have established. Write "p.w." for pointwise and "u." for uniform.

  • Uniform convergence $\Rightarrow$ pointwise convergence (immediate from the definitions: if one $N$ works for all $x$, it certainly works for each individual $x$).
  • Pointwise convergence $\not\Rightarrow$ uniform convergence ($x^n$ on $[0,1]$).
  • u. convergence + each $f_n$ continuous $\Rightarrow$ limit $f$ is continuous.
  • u. convergence + each $f_n$ integrable $\Rightarrow$ $\int f_n \to \int f$.
  • u. convergence + each $f_n$ differentiable $\not\Rightarrow$ $f$ differentiable.

The third point is a common pitfall. To get $f' = \lim f_n'$, the standard theorem requires that the derivatives $f_n'$ converge uniformly (not $f_n$ itself) and that $f_n(x_0)$ converges at a single point $x_0$. This stronger condition was used implicitly in Phase 4, page 5 for term-by-term differentiation of power series.

Historical note

Cauchy's erroneous theorem (1821) that the limit of continuous functions is continuous was one of the most productive mistakes in the history of mathematics. Abel pointed out counterexamples in 1826. Seidel (1847) and Stokes (1848) independently identified the issue, each introducing their own version of what we now call uniform convergence. Weierstrass, in unpublished lectures from 1841, gave the definitive formulation. It was the clarification of this single error that drove the entire rigourisation of analysis in the 19th century.

What breaks

Remove uniform convergence (use pointwise instead). Continuity fails: $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0,1]$ gives a discontinuous limit. Integrability can also fail in more exotic cases.

Uniform convergence does NOT preserve differentiability. Even if each $f_n$ is differentiable and $f_n \to f$ uniformly, $f$ need not be differentiable. Example: $f_n(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + 1/n}$ converges uniformly on $\mathbb{R}$ to $f(x) = |x|$, which is not differentiable at $0$. To preserve derivatives, you need uniform convergence of the derivatives $f_n'$, plus pointwise convergence of $f_n$ at one point.

Convergence on an unbounded domain. On $[0, \infty)$, $f_n(x) = x/n$ converges pointwise to $0$ but not uniformly ($\sup x/n = \infty$). Even on bounded domains, the distinction between pointwise and uniform matters.

Uniform convergence is the price you pay for passing limits through continuous operations — it forces the entire function to converge at the same rate.
