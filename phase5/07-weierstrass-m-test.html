The Weierstrass M-Test

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit, Page 7

The problem

You know from page 6 that uniform convergence is the right notion — it preserves continuity and integrability. But how do you actually prove that a function series $\sum f_n(x)$ converges uniformly? Checking the definition directly means computing $\sup_x |R_N(x)|$ where $R_N$ is the tail of the series, which is often hard. Weierstrass found a shortcut: if you can bound each $|f_n(x)|$ by a constant $M_n$ independent of $x$, and $\sum M_n$ converges, the function series converges uniformly.

1. The idea

The strategy is domination. If each function $f_n$ is bounded by a constant $M_n$ (no dependence on $x$), then the function series $\sum f_n(x)$ is controlled by the number series $\sum M_n$. If the number series converges, the function series inherits both absolute and uniform convergence.

This reduces the problem of uniform convergence — which involves functions — to ordinary convergence of a series of numbers, which we already know how to handle (comparison test, ratio test, etc., from pages 1–2).

2. The theorem

Theorem — Weierstrass M-test

Let $(f_n)$ be a sequence of functions defined on a set $D$, and let $(M_n)$ be a sequence of non-negative real numbers such that:

$$|f_n(x)| \leq M_n \quad \text{for all } x \in D \text{ and all } n$$

If $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} M_n$ converges, then $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)$ converges uniformly and absolutely on $D$.

Proof

Step 1. Absolute convergence. For each fixed $x \in D$:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |f_n(x)| \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} M_n < \infty$$

By the comparison test (page 1), $\sum f_n(x)$ converges absolutely for each $x \in D$. Let $f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)$.

Step 2. Uniform convergence. Let $S_N(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} f_n(x)$ be the $N$-th partial sum. The tail (remainder) is:

$$|f(x) - S_N(x)| = \left|\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} f_n(x)\right| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |f_n(x)| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} M_n$$

Step 3. The right-hand side is the tail of the convergent series $\sum M_n$. It does not depend on $x$. Since $\sum M_n$ converges, its tail tends to $0$:

$$\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} M_n \to 0 \quad \text{as } N \to \infty$$

Step 4. Therefore:

$$\sup_{x \in D} |f(x) - S_N(x)| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} M_n \to 0$$

This is exactly the definition of uniform convergence.

∎

The proof is short because the hard work is hidden in the comparison test. The M-test is really just "comparison test, but uniformly in $x$."

3. Example: $\sum \sin(nx)/n^2$

Consider $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}$ on $D = \mathbb{R}$. For all $x$ and all $n$:

$$\left|\frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}\right| \leq \frac{1}{n^2} = M_n$$

We know $\sum 1/n^2$ converges (the $p$-series with $p = 2 > 1$; this was established in page 1). By the M-test, $\sum \sin(nx)/n^2$ converges uniformly and absolutely on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

Since each $\sin(nx)/n^2$ is continuous, the uniform limit is continuous by the theorem on page 6. So $f(x) = \sum \sin(nx)/n^2$ defines a continuous function on $\mathbb{R}$.

4. Example: the exponential series on bounded intervals

Consider $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n / n!$ on $D = [-R, R]$ for a fixed $R > 0$. For all $x \in [-R, R]$:

$$\left|\frac{x^n}{n!}\right| \leq \frac{R^n}{n!} = M_n$$

The series $\sum R^n / n!$ converges (it equals $e^R$, as we proved in Phase 4, page 3). By the M-test, $\sum x^n/n!$ converges uniformly on $[-R, R]$.

This works for any fixed $R$, so the exponential series converges uniformly on every bounded interval. However, the convergence is not uniform on all of $\mathbb{R}$ (since no single $M_n = R^n/n!$ works for all $x$ simultaneously).

5. A non-example: why the M-test fails for $\sum x^n$ on $[0, 1)$

Consider $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n$ on $D = [0, 1)$. We know this converges pointwise to $1/(1-x)$ for each $x \in [0,1)$. Can we apply the M-test?

The "obvious" bound is $|x^n| \leq 1$ for $x \in [0,1)$, giving $M_n = 1$. But $\sum M_n = \sum 1 = \infty$. The M-test is inconclusive.

Could a cleverer bound work? No. The supremum $\sup_{x \in [0,1)} |x^n| = 1$ for every $n$ (since $x^n \to 1$ as $x \to 1^-$). So any valid bound satisfies $M_n \geq 1$, and $\sum M_n$ must diverge. The M-test cannot prove uniform convergence here — and rightly so, because the convergence is not uniform on $[0, 1)$. The partial sums $S_N(x) = (1 - x^{N+1})/(1-x)$, and $\sup_{x \in [0,1)} |1/(1-x) - S_N(x)| = \sup_{x \in [0,1)} x^{N+1}/(1-x) = \infty$.

On $[0, r]$ with $r < 1$, though, $M_n = r^n$ works and $\sum r^n = 1/(1-r)$ converges. The M-test gives uniform convergence on every compact sub-interval — just not on the full half-open interval $[0, 1)$.

6. Power series converge uniformly on compact subsets

Corollary — Uniform convergence of power series

Let $\sum a_n x^n$ be a power series with radius of convergence $R > 0$. For any $0 < r < R$, the series converges uniformly on $[-r, r]$.

Proof

Step 1. Since $r < R$, the series $\sum |a_n| r^n$ converges (we are inside the disc of convergence; absolute convergence holds by Phase 4, page 2).

Step 2. For $x \in [-r, r]$: $|a_n x^n| \leq |a_n| r^n = M_n$.

Step 3. Apply the M-test with $M_n = |a_n| r^n$. The series $\sum M_n$ converges, so $\sum a_n x^n$ converges uniformly on $[-r, r]$.

∎

This is the result that justifies the term-by-term differentiation and integration of power series from Phase 4, page 5. Those operations required uniform convergence, and the M-test is how we get it. The chain is: M-test $\Rightarrow$ uniform convergence on compact subsets $\Rightarrow$ legitimate term-by-term operations.

7. Normal convergence

A series of functions $\sum f_n$ is normally convergent on $D$ if:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sup_{x \in D} |f_n(x)| < \infty$$

Setting $M_n = \sup_{x \in D} |f_n(x)|$, we see that normal convergence is exactly the condition of the M-test with the tightest possible bounds. So normal convergence implies uniform (and absolute) convergence.

The converse is false: a series can converge uniformly without being normally convergent. But in practice, the M-test (which amounts to verifying normal convergence with possibly looser bounds) is how you prove uniform convergence in almost every concrete case.

8. The M-test and continuity combined

The M-test is most powerful when combined with the results from page 6. Suppose each $f_n$ is continuous on $D$, $|f_n(x)| \leq M_n$ for all $x \in D$, and $\sum M_n < \infty$. Then:

  1. $\sum f_n$ converges uniformly on $D$ (by the M-test).
  2. Each partial sum $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} f_n$ is continuous (finite sum of continuous functions).
  3. The limit $f = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n$ is continuous (uniform limit of continuous functions preserves continuity).

This chain of reasoning is the standard way to prove that a function series defines a continuous function. We used it in section 3 for $\sum \sin(nx)/n^2$.

Similarly, if each $f_n$ is integrable on $[a,b]$ and $\sum M_n$ converges, then term-by-term integration is valid:

$$\int_a^b \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)\, dx = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \int_a^b f_n(x)\, dx$$

This is because uniform convergence lets you swap the integral and the limit (page 6, section 7), and the limit of the partial sums is the series.

Historical note

Weierstrass developed the M-test in his Berlin lectures during the 1860s–1880s, where he single-handedly rebuilt the foundations of analysis. His students — including Cantor, Schwarz, and Mittag-Leffler — disseminated his methods. The M-test became the standard tool for establishing uniform convergence of power series, Fourier series, and series solutions to differential equations. Before Weierstrass, mathematicians manipulated infinite series of functions with little concern for convergence; after him, such manipulations required justification.

What breaks

If $\sum M_n$ diverges: the test is inconclusive. The function series might still converge uniformly — the M-test is sufficient, not necessary. For instance, $\sum (-1)^n x^n / n$ converges uniformly on $[0, 1]$ (by the alternating series estimation), but the "obvious" bound $M_n = 1/n$ gives $\sum 1/n = \infty$.

If the bounds depend on $x$: you do not get uniform convergence, only pointwise. The entire point of the M-test is that $M_n$ is a constant, independent of $x$.

At the boundary of the disc of convergence: if $r = R$, the series $\sum |a_n| R^n$ might diverge, so the M-test does not apply at the boundary. This is why we proved uniform convergence only on $[-r, r]$ with $r < R$. Boundary behaviour requires separate tools like Abel's theorem.

Bound each function by a constant, converge the constants, and uniform convergence follows — the M-test reduces function series to number series.
