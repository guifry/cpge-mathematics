Cesàro Means

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit, Page 5

The problem

Consider the sequence $0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, \ldots$ It does not converge — it bounces between $0$ and $1$ forever. But look at its arithmetic means:

$$\sigma_1 = 0, \quad \sigma_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad \sigma_3 = \tfrac{1}{3}, \quad \sigma_4 = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad \sigma_5 = \tfrac{2}{5}, \quad \sigma_6 = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad \ldots$$

These averages seem to settle near $1/2$. Cesàro (1890) asked two questions. First: when a sequence does converge, do its arithmetic means converge to the same limit? Second: can averaging assign a meaningful "limit" to sequences that don't converge at all?

1. Arithmetic means of a sequence

Given a sequence $(a_n)_{n \geq 1}$, define its Cesàro means (or arithmetic means) by:

$$\sigma_n = \frac{a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k$$

The Cesàro mean $\sigma_n$ is the average of the first $n$ terms. It smooths out oscillations: wild swings in $a_n$ get diluted as more terms enter the average.

For $a_n = 0, 1, 0, 1, \ldots$ (i.e. $a_n = (1 - (-1)^n)/2$), the sum of the first $n$ terms is $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor$. So $\sigma_n = \lfloor n/2 \rfloor / n \to 1/2$, confirming what we saw above.

2. The Cesàro mean theorem

Theorem — Cesàro Mean

If $a_n \to L$ as $n \to \infty$, then $\sigma_n \to L$.

In words: the arithmetic means of a convergent sequence converge to the same limit.

Proof

Step 1. Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, there exists $N$ such that $|a_k - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $k > N$.

Step 2. Split the Cesàro mean:

$$\sigma_n - L = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}(a_k - L) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{N}(a_k - L) + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=N+1}^{n}(a_k - L)$$

Step 3. Bound each piece. Let $M = \sum_{k=1}^{N} |a_k - L|$. This is a fixed finite number (it depends on $N$ and the sequence, but not on $n$). The first piece satisfies:

$$\left|\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{N}(a_k - L)\right| \leq \frac{M}{n}$$

The second piece: each $|a_k - L| < \varepsilon$ for $k > N$, and there are at most $n - N$ such terms, so:

$$\left|\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=N+1}^{n}(a_k - L)\right| \leq \frac{(n - N)\varepsilon}{n} < \varepsilon$$

Step 4. Combine. For $n > N$:

$$|\sigma_n - L| \leq \frac{M}{n} + \varepsilon$$

Since $M/n \to 0$, choose $n_0 > N$ with $M/n_0 < \varepsilon$. Then for all $n \geq n_0$:

$$|\sigma_n - L| < 2\varepsilon$$

Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $\sigma_n \to L$.

∎

The key idea: the "early terms" (the first $N$, which might be far from $L$) form a fixed finite sum that gets diluted by $1/n$. The "late terms" (after $N$) are all close to $L$, so their average is close to $L$. The early terms become negligible; the late terms dominate.

3. Examples

A convergent sequence. Take $a_n = 1 + (-1)^n / n$, which converges to $1$. The Cesàro means are:

$$\sigma_n = 1 + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{(-1)^k}{k}$$

The partial sums of $\sum (-1)^k / k$ are bounded (they converge to $-\ln 2$ by Phase 4, page 6), so dividing by $n$ sends them to $0$. Hence $\sigma_n \to 1$, confirming the theorem.

A divergent sequence with convergent means. Take $a_n = (-1)^n$. This diverges — it oscillates between $-1$ and $1$. But:

$$\sigma_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}(-1)^k = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is even} \\ -1/n & \text{if } n \text{ is odd}\end{cases}$$

Either way, $\sigma_n \to 0$. The Cesàro mean "sees" the midpoint of the oscillation.

4. A concrete worked example

Let us trace through the proof with a specific sequence to see the mechanism in action. Take $a_n = (-1)^n / \sqrt{n}$, which converges to $0$ (the terms alternate in sign and shrink to $0$). We verify that $\sigma_n \to 0$.

Let $\varepsilon = 0.1$. We need $|a_k| < 0.1$ for $k > N$, i.e. $1/\sqrt{k} < 0.1$, so $k > 100$. Take $N = 100$.

The "early" piece is $M = \sum_{k=1}^{100} |a_k - 0| = \sum_{k=1}^{100} 1/\sqrt{k}$. This is a finite number — roughly $\int_1^{100} x^{-1/2}\, dx = 2(\sqrt{100} - 1) = 18$, so $M \approx 18$.

For $n \geq n_0 = \lceil M / \varepsilon \rceil = 180$, we get $M/n < 0.1$. The "late" piece is already bounded by $\varepsilon = 0.1$. So for $n \geq 180$:

$$|\sigma_n| < 0.1 + 0.1 = 0.2$$

With $\varepsilon = 0.01$, we would need $N = 10000$ and $n_0$ around $200 \cdot 10000 / 0.01$ — much larger. The proof works for any $\varepsilon$, but the required $n_0$ grows as $\varepsilon$ shrinks. This is typical of epsilon-delta arguments: they guarantee eventual proximity, without optimising how quickly you get there.

5. Cesàro means of bounded sequences

The Cesàro mean theorem assumes $a_n \to L$. But what if $a_n$ is merely bounded? The means $\sigma_n$ always satisfy $\liminf a_n \leq \liminf \sigma_n \leq \limsup \sigma_n \leq \limsup a_n$. This is sometimes called the Cesàro squeeze: the means cannot escape the range of the original sequence's limit points.

In particular, if $a_n$ is bounded and $\liminf a_n = \limsup a_n = L$, then $a_n \to L$ and $\sigma_n \to L$ by the theorem. If $\liminf a_n < \limsup a_n$, the means land somewhere in between — as we saw with $(-1)^n$, where $\liminf = -1$, $\limsup = 1$, and the Cesàro mean tends to $0$, the midpoint.

6. Cesàro summability

Given a series $\sum a_n$ with partial sums $S_n = a_1 + \cdots + a_n$, the series is Cesàro summable to $L$ if the Cesàro means of $(S_n)$ converge to $L$:

$$\frac{S_1 + S_2 + \cdots + S_n}{n} \to L$$

By the Cesàro mean theorem, every convergent series is Cesàro summable to the same sum. But Cesàro summability is strictly weaker — it can assign a "sum" to some divergent series.

Grandi's series. Consider $1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \cdots$, i.e. $a_n = (-1)^{n+1}$. The partial sums are:

$$S_1 = 1, \quad S_2 = 0, \quad S_3 = 1, \quad S_4 = 0, \quad \ldots$$

The partial sums oscillate between $0$ and $1$, so the series diverges in the ordinary sense. But the Cesàro means of $(S_n)$ are:

$$\frac{S_1 + \cdots + S_n}{n} = \frac{\lceil n/2 \rceil}{n} \to \frac{1}{2}$$

So Grandi's series is Cesàro summable to $1/2$. This agrees with the Abel sum we computed in Phase 4, page 6: $\sum (-1)^n x^n = 1/(1+x)$, and $\lim_{x \to 1^-} 1/(1+x) = 1/2$.

This is no coincidence. Abel summability and Cesàro summability are both regular summation methods: they agree with ordinary convergence when it holds. Moreover, it can be shown that every Cesàro-summable series is also Abel-summable to the same value. The converse is false — Abel summability is strictly more powerful — but for the series we encounter in practice, the two methods typically agree.

Another example. Consider the series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n (n+1)$, i.e. $1 - 2 + 3 - 4 + \cdots$ The partial sums are $S_1 = 1$, $S_2 = -1$, $S_3 = 2$, $S_4 = -2$, $\ldots$ The Cesàro means of $(S_n)$ oscillate with growing amplitude and do not converge, so this series is not Cesàro summable. However, it is Abel summable: $\sum (-1)^n (n+1) x^n = 1/(1+x)^2$ and $\lim_{x \to 1^-} 1/(1+x)^2 = 1/4$. This illustrates that Abel summation reaches further than Cesàro.

7. The converse is false

The Cesàro mean theorem says: convergence implies Cesàro convergence. The converse fails. We already saw this: $a_n = (-1)^n$ has $\sigma_n \to 0$, but $a_n$ does not converge.

A more dramatic example: let $a_n = n$ when $n$ is a perfect square, and $a_n = 0$ otherwise. Then $a_n$ does not converge (it is unbounded). But the non-zero terms are sparse enough that:

$$\sigma_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{\substack{k=1 \\ k \text{ is a perfect square}}}^{n} k = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{\lfloor\sqrt{n}\rfloor} j^2$$

Since $\sum_{j=1}^{m} j^2 = m(m+1)(2m+1)/6 \sim m^3/3$ and $m = \lfloor\sqrt{n}\rfloor \sim \sqrt{n}$, we get $\sigma_n \sim n^{3/2}/(3n) = \sqrt{n}/3 \to \infty$. So in this case, even the Cesàro means diverge.

For a cleaner example where the means converge but the sequence does not: $a_n = (-1)^n$ suffices. The point stands: Cesàro convergence is strictly weaker than ordinary convergence.

Historical note

Ernesto Cesàro introduced this notion of summability in 1890. The idea of "regularising" divergent series by averaging had precursors — Euler freely assigned values to divergent series by analytic continuation — but Cesàro gave the first rigorous framework.

The real payoff came in 1904 when Fejér proved that the Cesàro means of the Fourier series of any continuous function converge uniformly to that function. This was a triumph: Fourier series suffer from convergence pathologies (the Gibbs phenomenon, du Bois-Reymond's example of a continuous function whose Fourier series diverges at a point), but their Cesàro means always behave well. Cesàro summation rescued Fourier analysis from its convergence troubles.

What breaks

The converse fails. The Cesàro means of $(-1)^n$ converge to $0$, but the sequence itself does not converge. Cesàro convergence is strictly weaker than ordinary convergence. This is a feature, not a bug — it extends the notion of "limit" to a wider class of sequences.

Cesàro summability is not ordinary summability. When we say Grandi's series is Cesàro summable to $1/2$, we are not saying it converges to $1/2$ in the ordinary sense. The partial sums still oscillate. Using Cesàro sums as if they were ordinary sums without flagging the distinction is a recipe for error.

Not all divergent sequences are Cesàro summable. The sequence $a_n = n$ has $\sigma_n = (n+1)/2 \to \infty$. Averaging does not help when the terms grow without bound.

The arithmetic means of a convergent sequence converge to the same limit — and sometimes the means converge even when the sequence itself does not.
