The Comparison Test

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit, Page 1

The problem

Euler (1735) computed $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$, one of the most celebrated results in mathematics. But before you can ask what the sum equals, you need to know that it converges at all. You cannot add infinitely many terms and hope for the best — the harmonic series $\sum 1/n$ diverges, as we established in Phase 2, page 3. So why should $\sum 1/n^2$ be any different?

The idea is simple: if every term of your series is smaller than the corresponding term of a series you already know converges, then your series converges too. You don't need to compute the sum — you just need a ceiling.

1. A concrete example first

We want to show $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1/n^2$ converges. Notice that for $n \geq 2$:

$$\frac{1}{n^2} \leq \frac{1}{n(n-1)} = \frac{1}{n-1} - \frac{1}{n}$$

The right-hand side is a telescoping series. Its partial sums are:

$$\sum_{n=2}^{N} \left(\frac{1}{n-1} - \frac{1}{n}\right) = 1 - \frac{1}{N} \to 1$$

So the partial sums of $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1/n^2$ satisfy:

$$S_N = 1 + \sum_{n=2}^{N} \frac{1}{n^2} \leq 1 + \sum_{n=2}^{N} \frac{1}{n(n-1)} \leq 1 + 1 = 2$$

The partial sums are increasing (every term is positive) and bounded above by $2$. By the monotone convergence theorem (Phase 2, page 3), the series converges. We haven't computed the sum — just proved it exists. (Euler later showed it equals $\pi^2/6$, but that requires Fourier analysis from Phase 11.)

2. The Direct Comparison Test

Theorem — Direct Comparison Test

Let $(a_n)$ and $(b_n)$ be sequences with $0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$ for all $n \geq N_0$ (some fixed $N_0$). Then:

  1. If $\sum b_n$ converges, then $\sum a_n$ converges.
  2. If $\sum a_n$ diverges, then $\sum b_n$ diverges.

Part (ii) is the contrapositive of part (i), so we only need to prove (i).

Proof

Step 1. Since finitely many terms do not affect convergence, we may assume $0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$ for all $n \geq 1$ without loss of generality.

Step 2. Let $A_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n$ and $B_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} b_n$ be the partial sums. Since $a_n \geq 0$, the sequence $(A_N)$ is increasing. Since $a_n \leq b_n$, we have $A_N \leq B_N$ for all $N$.

Step 3. By hypothesis, $\sum b_n$ converges, so $(B_N)$ converges to some limit $B$. Since partial sums of a non-negative series are increasing, $B_N \leq B$ for all $N$. Therefore $A_N \leq B$ for all $N$.

Step 4. The sequence $(A_N)$ is increasing and bounded above by $B$. By the monotone convergence theorem, it converges.

∎

3. More examples

Example 1: $\sum 1/n!$ converges. For $n \geq 1$, we have $n! \geq 2^{n-1}$ (since $n! = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdots n \geq 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdots 2 = 2^{n-1}$). Therefore:

$$\frac{1}{n!} \leq \frac{1}{2^{n-1}} = \frac{2}{2^n}$$

The geometric series $\sum 2/2^n = 2$ converges, so $\sum 1/n!$ converges by comparison. (We computed this sum as $e$ in Phase 4, page 3.)

Example 2: $\sum 1/2^n$ converges (geometric series). We can use this as a benchmark. Any series whose terms are eventually smaller than $C/2^n$ (for some constant $C$) converges.

Example 3: divergence direction. Since $1/\sqrt{n} \geq 1/n$ for $n \geq 1$ and $\sum 1/n$ diverges, the comparison test (part ii) gives $\sum 1/\sqrt{n}$ divergent.

Example 4: $\sum 1/(n^2 + 1)$ converges. For all $n \geq 1$, $n^2 + 1 > n^2$, so $1/(n^2 + 1) < 1/n^2$. Since $\sum 1/n^2$ converges (section 1), comparison gives $\sum 1/(n^2 + 1)$ convergent.

The method in summary: to prove convergence of $\sum a_n$ (with $a_n \geq 0$), find a convergent series $\sum b_n$ with $a_n \leq b_n$. To prove divergence, find a divergent series $\sum b_n$ with $a_n \geq b_n$. The art is in choosing $b_n$ — typically a geometric series, a $p$-series ($\sum 1/n^p$), or a telescoping series.

4. The Limit Comparison Test

The direct comparison test requires $a_n \leq b_n$ exactly. But often two series have terms that are roughly proportional for large $n$ — say $a_n \approx 3 b_n$ — without one dominating the other term by term. The limit comparison test handles this.

Theorem — Limit Comparison Test

Let $a_n > 0$ and $b_n > 0$ for all $n$. Suppose:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = L$$

where $0 < L < \infty$. Then $\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ either both converge or both diverge.

Proof

Step 1. Since $a_n/b_n \to L$ with $L > 0$, choose $\varepsilon = L/2 > 0$. There exists $N$ such that for all $n \geq N$:

$$L - \frac{L}{2} < \frac{a_n}{b_n} < L + \frac{L}{2}$$

That is, $\frac{L}{2}\, b_n < a_n < \frac{3L}{2}\, b_n$ for all $n \geq N$.

Step 2. Suppose $\sum b_n$ converges. Then $\sum \frac{3L}{2}\, b_n$ converges (scalar multiple). Since $a_n < \frac{3L}{2}\, b_n$ for $n \geq N$, the direct comparison test gives $\sum a_n$ convergent.

Step 3. Suppose $\sum b_n$ diverges. Then $\sum \frac{L}{2}\, b_n$ diverges. Since $a_n > \frac{L}{2}\, b_n$ for $n \geq N$, the divergence direction of the direct comparison test gives $\sum a_n$ divergent.

∎

5. Using the Limit Comparison Test

Example 1. Does $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2 + n}$ converge?

Compare with $b_n = 1/n^2$. We have:

$$\frac{a_n}{b_n} = \frac{n^2}{n^2 + n} = \frac{1}{1 + 1/n} \to 1$$

Since $L = 1 \in (0, \infty)$ and $\sum 1/n^2$ converges (section 1), so does $\sum 1/(n^2 + n)$.

Example 2. Does $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n + 3}{n^3 - n + 1}$ converge?

For large $n$, the dominant terms are $n/n^3 = 1/n^2$. Compare with $b_n = 1/n^2$:

$$\frac{a_n}{b_n} = \frac{(n+3)\, n^2}{n^3 - n + 1} = \frac{n^3 + 3n^2}{n^3 - n + 1} \to 1$$

Converges by limit comparison with $\sum 1/n^2$.

Example 3. Does $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n \ln n}$ converge?

Compare with $b_n = 1/n$. Then $a_n/b_n = 1/\ln n \to 0$. Here $L = 0$, which is outside the range of the theorem. The limit comparison test does not apply directly. (In fact, this series diverges — provable by the integral test, which we'll meet in later pages.)

6. The boundary cases $L = 0$ and $L = \infty$

When $L = 0$: $a_n/b_n \to 0$ means $a_n$ is much smaller than $b_n$. If $\sum b_n$ converges, so does $\sum a_n$ (from Step 2 of the proof, the upper bound still works). But if $\sum b_n$ diverges, we cannot conclude anything about $\sum a_n$ — it might converge (smaller terms) or diverge.

When $L = \infty$: $a_n$ is much larger than $b_n$. If $\sum b_n$ diverges, so does $\sum a_n$. But if $\sum b_n$ converges, we cannot conclude anything about $\sum a_n$.

In summary: the boundary cases give one-directional information only. The full two-directional equivalence requires $0 < L < \infty$.

Historical note

Augustin-Louis Cauchy systematised convergence tests in his Cours d'analyse (1821), the first rigorous textbook on analysis. Before Cauchy, mathematicians — Euler chief among them — manipulated infinite series freely, sometimes obtaining correct results by inspired formal computation, sometimes producing nonsense. Cauchy demanded that every convergence claim come with a proof. The comparison test is the simplest and oldest of his tools: reduce an unknown series to a known one.

The limit comparison test is a refinement that appeared throughout the 19th century as analysts developed the theory of asymptotic equivalence. Its power lies in reducing convergence questions to the art of finding the dominant term of a sequence — the term that controls its behaviour for large $n$.

What breaks

Wrong direction: if $a_n \geq b_n \geq 0$ and $\sum b_n$ converges, you know nothing about $\sum a_n$. Example: $a_n = 1/n \geq 1/n^2 = b_n$, and $\sum 1/n^2$ converges, but $\sum 1/n$ diverges. Having a convergent lower bound is useless.

$L = 0$ in limit comparison: $a_n = 1/n^2$, $b_n = 1/n$. Here $a_n/b_n = 1/n \to 0$. The series $\sum b_n$ diverges, but $\sum a_n$ converges. The limit $L = 0$ says "$a_n$ is negligible compared to $b_n$" — this only helps when $\sum b_n$ converges (then $\sum a_n$ converges too), not when $\sum b_n$ diverges.

Negative terms: the direct comparison test as stated requires $a_n \geq 0$. For series with mixed signs, comparison of absolute values tests absolute convergence. For conditional convergence, you need different tools (the alternating series test on page 3).

To prove a non-negative series converges, trap it beneath a series you already know converges.
