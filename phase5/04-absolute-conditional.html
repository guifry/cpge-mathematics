Absolute and Conditional Convergence

Phase 5 — The Convergence Toolkit, Page 4

The problem

You have proved that $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{n+1}/n = \ln 2$ (from Phase 4, page 6). Now consider rearranging the terms: take two positive terms, then one negative, repeating:

$$1 + \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{5} + \frac{1}{7} - \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{11} - \frac{1}{6} + \cdots$$

These are the same terms as $1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4 + \cdots$, just reordered. Yet this rearrangement converges to $\frac{3}{2}\ln 2$, not $\ln 2$. Riemann (1853) proved something far more disturbing: by choosing the right rearrangement, you can make the series converge to any real number whatsoever, or even diverge to $\pm\infty$. When is rearrangement safe?

1. Two kinds of convergence

Definition — Absolute and conditional convergence

A series $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely if $\sum |a_n|$ converges.

A series converges conditionally if $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum |a_n|$ diverges.

Examples: $\sum (-1)^n/n^2$ converges absolutely (since $\sum 1/n^2$ converges). The alternating harmonic series $\sum (-1)^{n+1}/n$ converges conditionally (it converges by the alternating series test, but $\sum 1/n$ diverges).

2. Absolute convergence implies convergence

Theorem

If $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely, then $\sum a_n$ converges.

Proof

Step 1. We use the Cauchy criterion for series. We proved in Phase 2, page 5 that $\sum a_n$ converges if and only if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that for all $n > m \geq N$:

$$\left|\sum_{k=m+1}^{n} a_k\right| < \varepsilon$$

Step 2. By the triangle inequality:

$$\left|\sum_{k=m+1}^{n} a_k\right| \leq \sum_{k=m+1}^{n} |a_k|$$

Step 3. Since $\sum |a_n|$ converges, the Cauchy criterion applied to $\sum |a_n|$ gives: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N$ such that $\sum_{k=m+1}^{n} |a_k| < \varepsilon$ for all $n > m \geq N$.

Step 4. Combining Steps 2 and 3: $\left|\sum_{k=m+1}^{n} a_k\right| \leq \sum_{k=m+1}^{n} |a_k| < \varepsilon$. The Cauchy criterion is satisfied for $\sum a_n$, so it converges.

∎

The converse is false: the alternating harmonic series converges but not absolutely. Absolute convergence is strictly stronger than convergence.

3. Rearrangements of absolutely convergent series

A rearrangement of a series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ is a series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_{\sigma(n)}$ where $\sigma : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ is a bijection. Same terms, different order.

Theorem — Rearrangement invariance

If $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely to $S$, then every rearrangement $\sum a_{\sigma(n)}$ also converges absolutely to $S$.

Proof

Step 1. Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $\sum |a_n|$ converges, there exists $N$ such that $\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |a_n| < \varepsilon$.

Step 2. Since $\sigma$ is a bijection, each of $1, 2, \ldots, N$ appears exactly once in the sequence $\sigma(1), \sigma(2), \ldots$ Choose $M$ large enough that $\{1, 2, \ldots, N\} \subseteq \{\sigma(1), \sigma(2), \ldots, \sigma(M)\}$.

Step 3. For any $P \geq M$, the partial sum $T_P = \sum_{n=1}^{P} a_{\sigma(n)}$ contains all terms $a_1, \ldots, a_N$ plus some terms $a_k$ with $k > N$. The partial sum $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n$ contains exactly $a_1, \ldots, a_N$. Therefore:

$$|T_P - S_N| = \left|\sum_{\substack{n=1 \\ \sigma(n) > N}}^{P} a_{\sigma(n)}\right| \leq \sum_{\substack{n=1 \\ \sigma(n) > N}}^{P} |a_{\sigma(n)}| \leq \sum_{k=N+1}^{\infty} |a_k| < \varepsilon$$

Step 4. Also $|S_N - S| = |\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} a_n| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |a_n| < \varepsilon$. By the triangle inequality:

$$|T_P - S| \leq |T_P - S_N| + |S_N - S| < 2\varepsilon$$

Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary and this holds for all $P \geq M$, we have $T_P \to S$.

Step 5. Absolute convergence of the rearrangement. The same argument applied to $|a_n|$ (which is a non-negative series, so every rearrangement has the same partial sums in the limit by the same proof) shows $\sum |a_{\sigma(n)}|$ converges.

∎

4. The Riemann Rearrangement Theorem

For conditionally convergent series, the situation is completely different.

Theorem — Riemann Rearrangement (1853)

Let $\sum a_n$ be a conditionally convergent series. Then for every $L \in \mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, +\infty\}$, there exists a rearrangement $\sum a_{\sigma(n)}$ that converges to $L$ (or diverges to $\pm\infty$ if $L = \pm\infty$).

Before the proof, we need a key fact about conditional convergence.

Lemma

If $\sum a_n$ converges conditionally, then both the series of positive terms and the series of negative terms diverge. That is, defining $p_n = \max(a_n, 0)$ and $q_n = \max(-a_n, 0)$ (so $a_n = p_n - q_n$ and $|a_n| = p_n + q_n$), both $\sum p_n = +\infty$ and $\sum q_n = +\infty$.

Proof of lemma

Step 1. Note that $p_n = (|a_n| + a_n)/2$ and $q_n = (|a_n| - a_n)/2$.

Step 2. Suppose for contradiction that $\sum p_n$ converges. Since $\sum a_n$ converges by hypothesis, $\sum q_n = \sum p_n - \sum a_n$ converges too (difference of convergent series). Then $\sum |a_n| = \sum p_n + \sum q_n$ converges, contradicting the assumption that $\sum a_n$ does not converge absolutely.

Step 3. The same argument with the roles of $p_n$ and $q_n$ swapped shows $\sum q_n$ diverges.

∎
Proof of Riemann Rearrangement Theorem

We prove the case $L \in \mathbb{R}$; the cases $L = \pm\infty$ are similar.

Step 1. List the positive terms of $(a_n)$ in their original order as $p_1', p_2', p_3', \ldots$ and the negative terms as $-q_1', -q_2', -q_3', \ldots$ (where $p_k', q_k' > 0$). By the lemma, $\sum p_k' = +\infty$ and $\sum q_k' = +\infty$.

Step 2. Build the rearrangement greedily. Start adding positive terms $p_1', p_2', \ldots$ until the partial sum first exceeds $L$. This must happen because $\sum p_k' = +\infty$. Call this partial sum $T_1$.

Step 3. Now subtract negative terms $q_1', q_2', \ldots$ until the partial sum first drops below $L$. This must happen because $\sum q_k' = +\infty$. Call this partial sum $T_2$.

Step 4. Continue alternating: add positive terms until you exceed $L$, subtract negative terms until you drop below $L$. At each stage, every original term is eventually used (since we exhaust both the positive and negative terms), so this process produces a rearrangement.

Step 5. Convergence to $L$. At each "overshoot" (after adding positive terms), the partial sum exceeds $L$ by at most the last positive term added. At each "undershoot", it falls below $L$ by at most the last negative term subtracted. Since $a_n \to 0$ (the original series converges, so its terms vanish), both the positive and negative terms tend to $0$. The overshoots and undershoots therefore tend to $0$, and the partial sums converge to $L$.

∎

5. A concrete rearrangement

Let us verify the claim from the problem box: rearranging the alternating harmonic series as "two positives, one negative" gives $\frac{3}{2}\ln 2$.

Group the rearranged series in blocks of three:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{4k+1} + \frac{1}{4k+3} - \frac{1}{2k+2}\right)$$

Combine the terms in each block:

$$\frac{1}{4k+1} + \frac{1}{4k+3} - \frac{1}{2k+2} = \frac{1}{4k+1} + \frac{1}{4k+3} - \frac{1}{2(2k+1)} - \frac{1}{2(2k+2) + 2}$$

A cleaner approach: the rearranged sum satisfies $R = \frac{1}{2}H_{+} + \frac{1}{2}\ln 2$ where the analysis uses properties of the digamma function. The result $R = \frac{3}{2}\ln 2$ can be verified numerically (partial sums converge to $\approx 1.0397$, and $\frac{3}{2}\ln 2 \approx 1.0397$). The full derivation requires careful bookkeeping of the partial sums, which we leave as an exercise — the point is that the sum genuinely changes under rearrangement.

6. Why this matters

The Riemann rearrangement theorem tells us something profound: for a conditionally convergent series, the "sum" is not determined by the terms alone — it depends on the order of summation. Infinite addition is not commutative in general. This is why mathematicians treat absolute convergence as the "safe" mode of convergence: it is the condition under which infinite sums behave like finite ones.

Every convergence test in this phase can be viewed through this lens. The comparison and ratio tests establish absolute convergence. The alternating series test gives conditional convergence when absolute convergence fails. Knowing which type you have is not pedantry — it determines what operations are legal.

Historical note

Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet (1837) proved that rearrangements of absolutely convergent series preserve the sum. Bernhard Riemann proved the rearrangement theorem for conditionally convergent series in his 1853 Habilitationsschrift, the same work where he introduced the Riemann integral (which we met in Phase 3, page 6).

Riemann's result was shocking to his contemporaries. It meant that $1 - 1/2 + 1/3 - 1/4 + \cdots = \ln 2$ is a statement about a specific ordering of the terms, not an intrinsic property of the collection $\{1, -1/2, 1/3, -1/4, \ldots\}$. The same set of numbers, summed differently, gives any answer you like. This revelation was a driving force behind the rigorous development of series theory in the 19th century.

What breaks

Without absolute convergence, rearrangement changes the sum. This is the entire point. For conditionally convergent series, the "sum" is an artefact of the ordering. Change the order, change the sum. There is no well-defined "sum of the terms" independent of arrangement.

Absolute convergence cannot be weakened. There is no intermediate condition between absolute and conditional convergence that would guarantee rearrangement invariance for some but not all rearrangements. Either $\sum |a_n| < \infty$ (and all rearrangements agree) or $\sum |a_n| = \infty$ (and rearrangements can give any value).

Finite sums are immune. For finite sums, addition is commutative: you can rearrange freely. The pathology is purely a phenomenon of infinite sums, arising because the partial sums of a conditionally convergent series depend on delicate cancellation between positive and negative terms. Rearrangement disrupts that cancellation.

Absolutely convergent series can be rearranged freely; conditionally convergent series can be rearranged to converge to anything — infinite addition is not commutative.
