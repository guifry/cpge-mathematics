√2 is Irrational

The First Crisis — Phase 0, Page 3

The crisis

The problem

The Pythagoreans (~500 BC) built their entire worldview on a single axiom: all numbers are ratios of whole numbers. "All is number" — and by number they meant ratio. Every length, every harmony, every cosmic proportion could be expressed as some fraction $p/q$.

Then someone drew a unit square and asked a fatal question. By the Pythagorean theorem (which we proved on the previous page), the diagonal has length

$$d = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$$

So: what fraction equals $\sqrt{2}$?

Try to find it

Your calculator says $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41421356\ldots$ — the decimal goes on without repeating. Let's try some fractions:

Each fraction gets closer. But no fraction lands exactly on $\sqrt{2}$. Maybe we just haven't tried hard enough? Maybe the right fraction has a very large denominator and we simply haven't stumbled on it?

The Pythagoreans must have felt this. The diagonal exists — you can draw it with a straightedge — but no ratio captures it. The proof that it never will is one of the oldest and most beautiful arguments in all of mathematics.

Historical note

Legend says the Pythagoreans were so disturbed by this discovery that they drowned Hippasus at sea for revealing it. Whether true or apocryphal, the story captures something real: this result destroyed the foundation of Pythagorean mathematics. A length that physically exists — you can construct it with compass and straightedge — but that cannot be expressed as a ratio of whole numbers. Their entire metaphysics, built on the primacy of ratio, was broken.

The discovery of incommensurable magnitudes (as the Greeks called them) triggered a centuries-long reformulation of Greek geometry. Eudoxus (~370 BC) eventually developed a theory of proportion that could handle irrational ratios — a theory so careful that it anticipated Dedekind's construction of the real numbers in 1872, over two millennia later.

What "irrational" means, precisely

A rational number is any number that can be written as $p/q$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$. Examples: $3/4$, $-7/1$, $0/1$. Every terminating or repeating decimal is rational (e.g. $0.333\ldots = 1/3$).

An irrational number is a real number that is not rational. It cannot be written as $p/q$ for any integers $p, q$. Not "we haven't found the right fraction" — there is provably no such fraction. The decimal expansion goes on forever without ever settling into a repeating pattern.

The claim: $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

The proof method: contradiction

Before we prove anything, let's understand the technique we'll use. Proof by contradiction works like this:

  1. You want to prove that statement $X$ is true.
  2. You assume $X$ is false.
  3. You reason logically from that assumption, following every step carefully.
  4. You arrive at a contradiction — something impossible, like $1 = 0$ or "this fraction is both in lowest terms and not in lowest terms".
  5. Since the logic was valid, the only thing that could be wrong is the assumption. Therefore $X$ is true.

Think of it like a detective. "If the suspect were innocent, he'd have been at home at 9pm. But we have camera footage showing he wasn't home at 9pm. Contradiction. Therefore, he's not innocent." The logic is airtight — the only escape is to reject the hypothesis.

Our hypothesis to destroy: "$\sqrt{2}$ is rational."

Theorem

$\sqrt{2}$ is irrational. That is, there exist no integers $p, q$ with $q \neq 0$ such that $\sqrt{2} = \dfrac{p}{q}$.

Proof

Step 1. Assume the opposite. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. Then we can write

$$\sqrt{2} = \frac{p}{q}$$

where $p$ and $q$ are positive integers with no common factor — that is, the fraction is in lowest terms. (Why can we assume this? If $p$ and $q$ shared a common factor, we'd just divide it out. For instance, $6/4$ becomes $3/2$. Every fraction can be reduced to lowest terms. So we lose nothing by starting there.)

Step 2. Square both sides.

$$2 = \frac{p^2}{q^2}$$

Multiply both sides by $q^2$:

$$p^2 = 2q^2$$

This is the equation we'll squeeze a contradiction from.

Step 3. $p$ must be even. The equation says $p^2 = 2q^2$. The right side is $2 \times (\text{something})$, so $p^2$ is even (divisible by 2).

Now here is the key step: if $p^2$ is even, then $p$ itself must be even. Why? Consider the only alternative: suppose $p$ were odd. Every odd number has the form $2m + 1$ for some integer $m$. Square it:

$$(2m+1)^2 = 4m^2 + 4m + 1 = 2(2m^2 + 2m) + 1$$

That's odd. So: odd squared is always odd. Equivalently, if $p^2$ is even, then $p$ cannot be odd. Therefore $p$ is even.

Step 4. Substitute. Since $p$ is even, write $p = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Plug into $p^2 = 2q^2$:

$$(2k)^2 = 2q^2$$
$$4k^2 = 2q^2$$

Divide both sides by 2:

$$q^2 = 2k^2$$

Step 5. $q$ must be even. By exactly the same argument as Step 3 — $q^2 = 2k^2$ means $q^2$ is even, which forces $q$ to be even.

Step 6. Contradiction. We now have: $p$ is even and $q$ is even. Both are divisible by 2. But we assumed in Step 1 that $p/q$ was in lowest terms — that $p$ and $q$ share no common factor. Having a common factor of 2 directly contradicts this assumption.

Since our logic was valid at every step, the only thing that can be wrong is the assumption we started with. Therefore $\sqrt{2}$ is not rational. $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

∎

Unpacking the proof

Let's look at why this works, not just that it works.

The engine of the proof is parity — the even/odd distinction. The number 2 has a special property: when you square a fraction in lowest terms, the 2 in the numerator ($p^2 = 2q^2$) forces $p$ to absorb a factor of 2. But then $p = 2k$, so $p^2 = 4k^2$, and the equation $4k^2 = 2q^2$ forces $q$ to absorb a factor of 2 as well.

It's a chain reaction. The factor of 2 is contagious: it infects $p$, then through the equation it infects $q$. But a fraction in lowest terms is supposed to be immune to shared factors. That's the contradiction.

Notice the proof would work just as well for $\sqrt{3}$, $\sqrt{5}$, or $\sqrt{p}$ for any prime $p$. The argument generalises: $\sqrt{n}$ is irrational whenever $n$ is not a perfect square. The mechanism is always the same — a prime factor that contaminates both numerator and denominator.

Why this matters

This isn't a curiosity. It's a structural revelation about numbers.

The rationals have holes. The number $\sqrt{2}$ is a definite point on the number line — you can construct it geometrically, pin it down with arbitrary precision. But it doesn't exist in the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. If your number system is $\mathbb{Q}$, this point is a gap, a void.

Limits can escape. Consider the sequence $1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, 1.4142, \ldots$ — each term is rational, and the sequence clearly "converges" to something. But the thing it converges to ($\sqrt{2}$) isn't in $\mathbb{Q}$. The limit doesn't exist in your number system. This is catastrophic for doing analysis (calculus, differential equations, anything involving limits) over $\mathbb{Q}$ alone.

This is why $\mathbb{R}$ was invented. The real numbers $\mathbb{R}$ are, at their core, the rationals with all the holes filled in. The formal constructions — Dedekind cuts (1872) or Cauchy sequences — are specifically designed to close exactly these gaps. Every "convergent" sequence of rationals gets a limit in $\mathbb{R}$, even when that limit is irrational.

Looking ahead. In Phase 1, we'll study the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ — the precise property that guarantees $\mathbb{R}$ has no holes. That property is the direct fix for the crisis we've just uncovered. The entire edifice of analysis (continuity, differentiation, integration, convergence) rests on completeness, which rests on the fact that $\mathbb{Q}$ is broken in exactly the way this proof demonstrates.

What breaks

If $\sqrt{2}$ were rational, $\mathbb{Q}$ would have no holes (at least not this one), and the real numbers might never have been invented. But $\sqrt{2}$ isn't rational, and $\mathbb{Q}$ is riddled with gaps. The entire apparatus of analysis — limits, continuity, the derivative, the integral, every theorem in calculus — is built on $\mathbb{R}$, which exists precisely because $\mathbb{Q}$ is incomplete. This one proof is the crack in the foundation that forced mathematics to build a new one.

$\sqrt{2}$ is a length you can draw but a number you can't write as a fraction — and that gap in the rationals is why the real numbers had to be invented.
