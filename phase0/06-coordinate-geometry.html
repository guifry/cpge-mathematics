Coordinate Geometry

Algebra Meets Geometry — Phase 0, Page 6

The limitation

The problem

Euclid proved roughly 465 propositions in the Elements. Every one required a separate diagram and a separate argument. "The circle centred at point $A$ with radius 3 passes through point $B$" — how do you verify this without drawing? "A line intersects a circle in two points" — how do you find them without a compass construction?

Geometry is powerful, but it is awkward to compute with. You can prove that two shapes intersect, but finding exactly where requires ingenuity specific to each problem. There is no general method — no algorithm — that turns geometric questions into mechanical computation.

There must be a way to turn geometric problems into algebraic equations — solvable by computation, not construction.

Historical note

René Descartes published La Géométrie in 1637, as an appendix to his philosophical masterpiece Discourse on the Method. Pierre de Fermat arrived at essentially the same idea independently, around the same time, though his work circulated only in manuscript.

Descartes' insight: assign numbers to points. A point in the plane becomes a pair of numbers $(x, y)$. A curve becomes an equation in $x$ and $y$. Geometry becomes algebra.

This was arguably the single most important mathematical innovation between ancient Greece and Newton. Without it, calculus could not have been invented.

The coordinate system

Choose a point and call it the origin $O$. Draw two perpendicular lines through it — the $x$-axis (horizontal) and the $y$-axis (vertical). Choose a unit of measurement.

Now every point in the plane has an address: the pair $(x, y)$, where $x$ is the horizontal distance from the origin (positive to the right, negative to the left) and $y$ is the vertical distance (positive upward, negative downward). The point $(3, 2)$ means "3 units right, 2 units up." The point $(-1, -4)$ means "1 unit left, 4 units down."

This is so familiar today — every graph you have ever seen uses it — that it is easy to forget this was revolutionary. Before Descartes, a "point" was a location you described in words or indicated on a diagram. After Descartes, a point is a pair of numbers. Numbers you can manipulate, substitute into equations, feed into algorithms. The entire plane becomes a number system.

Lines as equations

Consider a straight line through the points $(0, 1)$ and $(2, 5)$. What is the pattern? As $x$ increases by 1, $y$ increases by $\ldots$ let us check. From $x = 0$ to $x = 2$, $y$ goes from 1 to 5. That is a rise of 4 over a run of 2 — the slope is:

$$m = \frac{5 - 1}{2 - 0} = 2$$

For every unit you move right, you move 2 units up. And the line crosses the $y$-axis at $y = 1$ (the $y$-intercept). So for any point $(x, y)$ on this line:

$$y = 2x + 1$$

That is the line. Not a drawing of a line — the line is this equation. Every point satisfying $y = 2x + 1$ lies on the line; every point on the line satisfies $y = 2x + 1$. The geometric object and the algebraic equation are the same thing, viewed from different angles.

Every geometric question about the line becomes an algebraic question about the equation. "Does the point $(3, 7)$ lie on the line?" becomes "does $7 = 2(3) + 1$?" Yes, $7 = 7$, so yes. No diagram needed.

The distance formula

Theorem — Distance Formula

The distance between points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

This is the Pythagorean theorem (page 02) wearing a coordinate disguise. Draw the horizontal leg from $(x_1, y_1)$ to $(x_2, y_1)$ — its length is $|x_2 - x_1|$. Draw the vertical leg from $(x_2, y_1)$ to $(x_2, y_2)$ — its length is $|y_2 - y_1|$. The straight-line distance $d$ is the hypotenuse. By Pythagoras:

$$d^2 = (x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2$$

Take the square root and you are done. Everything connects: the Pythagorean theorem from page 02, proved via similar triangles from page 01, now rephrased in the language of coordinates. One theorem, three forms, each more powerful than the last.

Circles as equations

A circle is the set of all points at a fixed distance (the radius $r$) from a fixed point (the centre). Take a circle centred at the origin with radius $r$. A point $(x, y)$ lies on the circle exactly when its distance from $(0, 0)$ equals $r$. By the distance formula:

$$\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r$$

Square both sides:

$$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$$

That is the equation of a circle. The unit circle from page 05 — the circle of radius 1 centred at the origin, the foundation of the modern definition of sine and cosine — is simply:

$$x^2 + y^2 = 1$$

And a point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ on it satisfies $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. That identity, which we proved geometrically on page 05, is just the equation of the unit circle written in trigonometric coordinates. Descartes' framework turns a geometric identity into an algebraic tautology.

For a circle centred at $(a, b)$ rather than the origin, the distance from $(x, y)$ to $(a, b)$ must equal $r$:

$$(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$$

Where lines meet circles

Here is the payoff. Where does the line $y = x + 1$ intersect the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$? Before Descartes, you would need a geometric construction — draw the line, draw the circle, find their meeting points by eye or by compass. After Descartes, you substitute one equation into the other:

$$x^2 + (x + 1)^2 = 1$$

Expand:

$$x^2 + x^2 + 2x + 1 = 1$$
$$2x^2 + 2x = 0$$

Factor:

$$2x(x + 1) = 0$$

So $x = 0$ or $x = -1$. Substituting back into $y = x + 1$: when $x = 0$, $y = 1$; when $x = -1$, $y = 0$. The two intersection points are $(0, 1)$ and $(-1, 0)$.

A geometric problem — "where does this line cross this circle?" — solved by pure computation. No diagram, no compass, no special ingenuity. Just substitute, expand, factor, solve. This is the power of Descartes' idea: it mechanises geometry.

Parabolas and beyond

Lines and circles are just the beginning. The equation $y = x^2$ defines a parabola — a U-shaped curve where every point $(x, x^2)$ satisfies the equation. The shape is not assumed or drawn freehand; it emerges from the equation.

More complex equations produce more complex curves:

  • $y = x^3$ — a cubic curve, symmetric about the origin
  • $x^3 + y^3 = 3xy$ — the folium of Descartes, a looped curve Descartes himself studied
  • $y^2 = x^3 - x$ — an elliptic curve, central to modern cryptography (this is what secures your browser's HTTPS connections)

All described by equations. All amenable to algebraic analysis — finding intersections, tangent lines, areas, symmetries — without ever picking up a compass.

Why this matters for everything that follows

Coordinate geometry is not just "another topic." It is the language in which everything after this point will be written.

  • Functions become curves. A function $f(x)$ is a rule; its graph $y = f(x)$ is a curve in the coordinate plane. When we study functions in Phase 2, we will be studying curves.
  • Derivatives become slopes. The derivative (Phase 3) is the slope of the tangent line to a curve at a point — a geometric concept, expressed algebraically, computed by a limit. All three ideas (geometry, algebra, limits) meeting at one point on Descartes' plane.
  • The unit circle definition of sine depends on coordinates. On page 05, we defined $\cos\theta$ and $\sin\theta$ as the $x$- and $y$-coordinates of a point on the unit circle. Without Descartes, "the $x$-coordinate of a point" is meaningless. No coordinates, no unit circle definition, no extension of trig beyond acute angles.
  • Integrals become areas under curves. The integral (Phase 3) computes the area between a curve and the $x$-axis. "Area under $y = f(x)$ from $x = a$ to $x = b$" only makes sense when curves live in a coordinate plane.

In short: calculus lives in Descartes' coordinate plane. Every limit, every derivative, every integral is a statement about functions, and functions are curves in coordinates.

What breaks

Without coordinates, you cannot define functions as curves, cannot compute slopes (derivatives), cannot compute areas under curves (integrals). Calculus as we know it would not exist.

Newton and Leibniz invented calculus in the 1660s–1680s. Descartes published La Géométrie in 1637. The 30-year gap is not coincidence. Newton needed coordinate geometry before he could invent calculus — he needed curves to be equations before he could ask "what is the slope of this equation at this point?" Descartes made Newton possible.

Phase 0 complete

Here is everything you now have, and why each piece was needed:

  • Similar triangles (page 01): same angles guarantee same ratios. The foundation of measurement at a distance.
  • Pythagorean theorem (page 02): $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. Connects lengths to squares, gives us the distance formula.
  • $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational (page 03): $\mathbb{Q}$ has holes, motivating $\mathbb{R}$ — the number system calculus needs.
  • Trigonometry (page 04): angles become ratios, via similar triangles. Measurement of the unmeasurable.
  • The unit circle (page 05): trig extended to all angles, $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ proved, $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$ established.
  • Coordinate geometry (this page): geometry = algebra. Shapes become equations, solvable by computation.

These six pages are the toolkit. They let us measure, compute, and describe. But they cannot handle infinity — infinite sums, infinite precision, approaching but never reaching. The sequence $1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, \ldots$ converges to $\sqrt{2}$, but what does "converges" actually mean? We used the word on page 03 without defining it. For that, we need a new idea.

Next: Phase 1 — Number Theory. Before chasing infinity, the ancients counted, factored, and discovered primes. The arithmetic of whole numbers — then on to Phase 2, limits and convergence.

Turn points into numbers and shapes into equations — then geometry becomes computation, and computation is something you can always do.
