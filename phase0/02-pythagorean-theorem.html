02. The Pythagorean Theorem

Why right triangles obey a law about squares, and what it unlocks

The Problem

You're building a house. You need the corner walls to meet at exactly 90 degrees. No protractor, no laser level — you have a rope and some stakes.

Egyptian builders figured this out thousands of years ago: tie 12 equally spaced knots in a rope, stretch it into a triangle with sides 3, 4, and 5 knots long. The corner opposite the longest side is always a perfect right angle.

It works every time. But why? What's special about 3, 4, 5? Does it work for other numbers? And given two sides of a right triangle, can you always find the third?

Historical Note

The Babylonians knew this relationship by at least 1800 BC. Clay tablet Plimpton 322, sitting in the Columbia University library, lists fifteen Pythagorean triples — sets of integers satisfying the theorem — over a thousand years before Pythagoras was born.

The theorem is named after Pythagoras of Samos (~570–495 BC), who allegedly gave the first proof, though no written record of his proof survives. Chinese mathematicians proved it independently around 200 BC in the Zhoubi Suanjing, using a beautiful visual argument we'll see below.

This theorem has more known proofs than any other in mathematics — over 370. Contributors include Euclid, the Indian mathematician Bhaskara II (who wrote just "Behold!" next to his diagram), US President James Garfield (1876), and in 2023, two high-school students from New Orleans who produced a trigonometric proof that mathematicians had long thought impossible.

The theorem

Theorem — Pythagorean Theorem

In a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse $c$ (the side opposite the right angle):

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

That's the algebraic statement. But algebra came two thousand years after Pythagoras. The original meaning is geometric, and it's worth seeing that first.

What $a^2$ actually means — the geometric view

Build a square on each side of the triangle. The square on side $a$ has area $a^2$. The square on side $b$ has area $b^2$. The square on the hypotenuse $c$ has area $c^2$.

The theorem says: the area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the areas of the squares on the two legs.

This is the original meaning. It's a statement about land, about physical area. The Greeks didn't think of $a^2$ as "$a$ times $a$" — they thought of it as "the square whose side is $a$." When you read "the square on the hypotenuse," they meant a literal square, drawn on papyrus or scratched in sand.

Proof 1 — The Chinese visual proof

This is perhaps the most elegant proof in all of mathematics. It requires nothing beyond the area of a triangle ($\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$) and expanding a binomial. It appeared in the Zhoubi Suanjing around 200 BC.

Proof 1 — Area rearrangement

Start with a right triangle having legs $a$, $b$ and hypotenuse $c$. Make four identical copies.

Step 1. Arrange the four triangles inside a large square of side length $(a + b)$, with their hypotenuses facing inward. They fit perfectly, leaving a tilted square in the centre whose side is $c$.

Step 2. Compute the area of the large square two ways.

Way 1 — directly:

$$(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$$

Way 2 — as four triangles plus the inner square:

$$4 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}ab + c^2 = 2ab + c^2$$

Step 3. These are the same area, so set them equal:

$$a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = 2ab + c^2$$

Step 4. Subtract $2ab$ from both sides:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

∎

The entire proof rests on one fact: the four triangles plus the inner square tile the big square with no gaps or overlaps. Once you see that, the algebra is three lines. This is why Bhaskara could get away with writing just "Behold!" — the diagram does the work.

Proof 2 — Euclid's proof (I.47)

Proof 2 — Via similar triangles (Euclid)

This is the proof in Euclid's Elements, Proposition I.47, from around 300 BC. It connects directly to what we saw on the previous page about similar triangles.

Setup. Take right triangle $ABC$ with the right angle at $C$. Drop the altitude from $C$ perpendicular to the hypotenuse $AB$, hitting it at point $D$.

Key observation. This altitude splits the original triangle into two smaller triangles: $ACD$ and $CBD$. Both are similar to the original triangle $ABC$. (They share an angle with the original, and both have a right angle — so by AA similarity, they're similar.)

From similarity of $\triangle ACD \sim \triangle ABC$:

$$\frac{AC}{AB} = \frac{AD}{AC} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad AC^2 = AB \cdot AD$$

That is: $b^2 = c \cdot AD$.

From similarity of $\triangle CBD \sim \triangle ABC$:

$$\frac{BC}{AB} = \frac{BD}{BC} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad BC^2 = AB \cdot BD$$

That is: $a^2 = c \cdot BD$.

Add them. Since $D$ lies on $AB$, we have $AD + BD = AB = c$:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c \cdot BD + c \cdot AD = c(AD + BD) = c \cdot c = c^2$$

∎

Notice how different this feels from Proof 1. No area rearrangement, no binomial expansion. Just similar triangles and the fact that the two segments of the hypotenuse add back to the whole. Euclid's proof makes the Pythagorean theorem a consequence of similarity — which is arguably the deeper insight.

Verify it

Before trusting the theorem, let's check it against known right triangles.

The 3-4-5 triangle (the Egyptian rope trick):

$$3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5^2 \quad\checkmark$$

The 5-12-13 triangle:

$$5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13^2 \quad\checkmark$$

The 8-15-17 triangle:

$$8^2 + 15^2 = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17^2 \quad\checkmark$$

These integer triples $(a, b, c)$ satisfying $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ are called Pythagorean triples. There are infinitely many of them. Euclid gave a formula: for any positive integers $m > n$, the triple $(m^2 - n^2,\; 2mn,\; m^2 + n^2)$ works. Try $m=2, n=1$: $(3, 4, 5)$. Try $m=3, n=2$: $(5, 12, 13)$.

The converse

The theorem goes the other direction too. Not just "right triangle implies $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$," but also:

Theorem — Converse of Pythagoras

If a triangle has sides $a$, $b$, $c$ with $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, then the angle opposite side $c$ is a right angle.

This is precisely what the Egyptian builders used. They didn't need to prove a triangle was right-angled by measuring the angle. They measured the sides instead. Mark 3, 4, 5 on a rope, pull it taut into a triangle, and the corner opposite the longest side is guaranteed to be 90 degrees. The converse is what makes the theorem practical.

A consequence that changes everything

Draw a square with side length 1. What is its diagonal?

The diagonal splits the square into two right triangles, each with legs $1$ and $1$. By Pythagoras:

$$d^2 = 1^2 + 1^2 = 2$$

So $d = \sqrt{2}$. This is a real, constructible length — you can draw it with a compass and straightedge. It sits right there on the page.

But here's the problem. The Pythagoreans believed all lengths are ratios of whole numbers. Every distance, they claimed, is some fraction $p/q$. If that's true, then $\sqrt{2}$ must be a fraction. Is it?

The next page proves it isn't. $\sqrt{2}$ cannot be written as any fraction. This discovery — a direct consequence of the Pythagorean theorem applied to the humblest possible triangle — allegedly caused a crisis in the Pythagorean school. Legend has it that the discoverer, Hippasus, was drowned at sea for revealing the secret.

The theorem that bears their name led straight to the destruction of their worldview.

What Breaks Without This

Distance in coordinate geometry is the Pythagorean theorem. The distance between points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

This is just Pythagoras applied to the horizontal and vertical legs of a right triangle. Without it, Descartes' entire coordinate system (page 06) has no way to measure distance. No distance formula means no circles, no conic sections, no calculus of curves, no physics.

Trigonometry collapses. The identity $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ is the Pythagorean theorem applied to the unit circle. Without it, there's no coherent relationship between sine and cosine.

Higher dimensions vanish. Distance in 3D — $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$ — is iterated Pythagoras. In $n$ dimensions, same idea. Without the 2D case, there's no generalisation.

The areas of the squares on the legs add up to the area of the square on the hypotenuse — that's why $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
