Trigonometric Identities

Phase 0 — Geometry and Measurement, Page 7

The problem

You can compute $\sin(30°)$ and $\sin(45°)$ from the triangles you already know. But what about $\sin(75°)$? You don't have a right triangle with a 75° angle memorised. However, $75° = 45° + 30°$. If you had a formula for $\sin(\alpha + \beta)$ in terms of $\sin\alpha$, $\cos\alpha$, $\sin\beta$, $\cos\beta$, you could compute any angle from a few known ones.

This is why the addition formulae were invented — they turn a small table of known values into an engine for computing any angle.

1. The addition formulae

Theorem — Addition formulae

For all $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$
$$\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$
Proof (geometric, using the unit circle)

Consider three points on the unit circle: $A$ at angle $0$, $B$ at angle $\alpha + \beta$, $C$ at angle $\alpha$, and $D$ at angle $-\beta$.

The chord from $A$ to $B$ has the same length as the chord from $D$ to $C$, because both subtend an arc of $\alpha + \beta$.

Chord $AB$: from $(1, 0)$ to $(\cos(\alpha+\beta), \sin(\alpha+\beta))$. Squared length:

$$|AB|^2 = (\cos(\alpha+\beta) - 1)^2 + \sin^2(\alpha+\beta)$$
$$= \cos^2(\alpha+\beta) - 2\cos(\alpha+\beta) + 1 + \sin^2(\alpha+\beta) = 2 - 2\cos(\alpha+\beta)$$

Chord $DC$: from $(\cos\beta, -\sin\beta)$ to $(\cos\alpha, \sin\alpha)$. Squared length:

$$|DC|^2 = (\cos\alpha - \cos\beta)^2 + (\sin\alpha + \sin\beta)^2$$
$$= \cos^2\alpha - 2\cos\alpha\cos\beta + \cos^2\beta + \sin^2\alpha + 2\sin\alpha\sin\beta + \sin^2\beta$$
$$= 2 - 2(\cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta)$$

Setting $|AB|^2 = |DC|^2$ gives $\cos(\alpha+\beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta$.

For sine: use $\sin(\alpha+\beta) = \cos(\pi/2 - \alpha - \beta) = \cos((\pi/2 - \alpha) - \beta)$. Apply the cosine formula just proved, then use $\cos(\pi/2 - \alpha) = \sin\alpha$ and $\sin(\pi/2 - \alpha) = \cos\alpha$ (from the unit circle, Phase 0 page 5).

∎

2. Everything else follows

Every identity below is a direct consequence of the addition formulae. No new ideas — just substitution.

Subtraction formulae

Replace $\beta$ with $-\beta$ and use $\sin(-\beta) = -\sin\beta$, $\cos(-\beta) = \cos\beta$:

$$\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta - \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$
$$\cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$
Double angle formulae

Set $\beta = \alpha$:

$$\sin(2\alpha) = 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha$$
$$\cos(2\alpha) = \cos^2\alpha - \sin^2\alpha = 2\cos^2\alpha - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2\alpha$$

The three forms of $\cos(2\alpha)$ come from $\sin^2\alpha + \cos^2\alpha = 1$.

Half-angle formulae

Rearrange the double angle formulae with $\alpha = \theta/2$:

$$\cos^2\frac{\theta}{2} = \frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2} \qquad\qquad \sin^2\frac{\theta}{2} = \frac{1 - \cos\theta}{2}$$
Product-to-sum

Add/subtract the addition formulae:

$$\cos\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta) + \cos(\alpha+\beta)]$$
$$\sin\alpha\sin\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta) - \cos(\alpha+\beta)]$$
$$\sin\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\sin(\alpha+\beta) + \sin(\alpha-\beta)]$$
Sum-to-product

Let $p = \alpha + \beta$ and $q = \alpha - \beta$, so $\alpha = (p+q)/2$ and $\beta = (p-q)/2$:

$$\sin p + \sin q = 2\sin\frac{p+q}{2}\cos\frac{p-q}{2}$$
$$\cos p + \cos q = 2\cos\frac{p+q}{2}\cos\frac{p-q}{2}$$

3. Tangent

Tangent addition

From $\tan\theta = \sin\theta/\cos\theta$ and the addition formulae:

$$\tan(\alpha + \beta) = \frac{\tan\alpha + \tan\beta}{1 - \tan\alpha\tan\beta}$$

Double angle: $\tan(2\alpha) = \dfrac{2\tan\alpha}{1 - \tan^2\alpha}$.

Historical note

Ptolemy (c. 150 AD) derived the addition formulae geometrically in the Almagest to build his chord tables — the ancient equivalent of a sine table. His method used cyclic quadrilaterals (Ptolemy's theorem). The unit-circle proof above is essentially Euler's modernisation (1748). The product-to-sum identities were crucial for astronomers before calculators: they converted expensive multiplications into cheap additions (the technique called prosthaphaeresis).

What breaks

If you forget the sign in the cosine formula — writing $\cos(\alpha+\beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta$ — then $\cos(\pi) = \cos^2(\pi/2) + \sin^2(\pi/2) = 0 + 1 = 1$. But $\cos(\pi) = -1$. The minus sign is load-bearing.

If you confuse product-to-sum with sum-to-product — they go in opposite directions. Product-to-sum is for integrating $\sin(mx)\cos(nx)$ (Phase 3, page 9). Sum-to-product is for solving equations like $\sin(3x) + \sin(x) = 0$.

The addition formulae are the only trig identity — everything else is a substitution into $\sin(\alpha \pm \beta)$ and $\cos(\alpha \pm \beta)$.
