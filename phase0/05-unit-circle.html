The Unit Circle

Phase 0 — Extending Trigonometry to All Angles
The Problem

On the previous page we defined sine, cosine, and tangent as ratios in a right triangle. That definition is clean, natural, and it works — for angles between $0°$ and $90°$.

But real problems do not stay in that range. A wheel rotates through $270°$. A pendulum swings to negative angles. A ship's bearing can be $225°$. An electrical signal oscillates through thousands of degrees worth of angle.

What is $\sin(150°)$? You cannot build a right triangle with a $150°$ angle — a right triangle already uses up $90°$, and the remaining two angles must be positive and sum to $90°$. The triangle definition literally cannot represent this. What about $\sin(240°)$? $\sin(-30°)$? $\sin(720°)$?

The triangle definition is silent. We need a new definition — one that works for every angle, positive, negative, or beyond $360°$ — and that agrees with the triangle definition in the range where both apply.

1. The idea: use a circle, not a triangle

Here is the conceptual leap. Instead of placing the angle inside a triangle, place it at the centre of a circle. Specifically, use a circle of radius 1 centred at the origin of the coordinate plane. This is called the unit circle.

Start at the point $(1, 0)$ — the rightmost point of the circle, on the positive $x$-axis. Now rotate counter-clockwise by an angle $\theta$. You land on some point on the circle. That point has coordinates $(x, y)$.

The definition is: those coordinates are the cosine and sine.

2. The definition

Definition — Sine and Cosine via the Unit Circle

Let $P$ be the point on the unit circle ($x^2 + y^2 = 1$) reached by rotating angle $\theta$ counter-clockwise from the positive $x$-axis. Then:

$$P = (\cos\theta,\; \sin\theta)$$

$\cos\theta$ is the $x$-coordinate of $P$. $\sin\theta$ is the $y$-coordinate of $P$.

Negative angles mean clockwise rotation. Angles beyond $360°$ mean you have gone around more than once. The definition handles both without any extra rules.

That is the entire definition. Everything else follows from it.

3. Why this agrees with the triangle definition

For $0° < \theta < 90°$, the point $P = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ sits in the first quadrant: both coordinates are positive. Drop a vertical line from $P$ to the $x$-axis. You get a right triangle with:

  • Hypotenuse = the radius = $1$ (it is the unit circle)
  • Adjacent side (along the $x$-axis) = $x$-coordinate = $\cos\theta$
  • Opposite side (vertical drop) = $y$-coordinate = $\sin\theta$

Now apply the triangle definitions from page 04:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{\sin\theta}{1} = \sin\theta \quad \checkmark$$
$$\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{\cos\theta}{1} = \cos\theta \quad \checkmark$$

The triangle ratios and the unit circle coordinates give the same numbers, because the hypotenuse is $1$ and dividing by $1$ changes nothing. The old definition is a special case of the new one. The unit circle does not replace the triangle — it generalises it.

4. Verify with known values

Before using the new definition for unfamiliar angles, let us check it reproduces values we already know.

$\theta = 0°$. No rotation at all. The point stays at $(1, 0)$. So $\cos(0°) = 1$ and $\sin(0°) = 0$. Correct: in a "triangle" with no angle, there is no opposite side.

$\theta = 90°$. Quarter turn counter-clockwise. The point moves to $(0, 1)$ — the top of the circle. So $\cos(90°) = 0$ and $\sin(90°) = 1$. This matches what you would expect: at $90°$ the "adjacent" side has collapsed to zero and the "opposite" side equals the hypotenuse.

$\theta = 30°$. From page 04, the triangle definition gives $\sin(30°) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\cos(30°) = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$. The unit circle point is $\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\; \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$. Check: $\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 = \tfrac{3}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} = 1$. It lies on the unit circle. The values match.

$\theta = 45°$. From page 04: $\sin(45°) = \cos(45°) = \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$. The unit circle point is $\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\; \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)$. Check: $\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} = 1$. Correct.

5. Now the new cases work

The whole point of the unit circle definition is that it does not stop at $90°$. Let us compute the values the triangle definition could not touch.

$\sin(150°)$

$150° = 180° - 30°$. The point is in the second quadrant — past $90°$ but before $180°$. It has the same height above the $x$-axis as the $30°$ point, but its $x$-coordinate is negative (it is on the left side of the $y$-axis).

So the point is $\left(-\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\; \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$. Therefore $\sin(150°) = \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\cos(150°) = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

The sine is positive (the point is above the $x$-axis) and the cosine is negative (the point is to the left of the $y$-axis). This is consistent and natural. The triangle definition would have given you nothing.

$\sin(240°)$

$240° = 180° + 60°$. The point is in the third quadrant — both coordinates negative. It mirrors the $60°$ point across the origin: $\left(-\tfrac{1}{2},\; -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)$.

So $\sin(240°) = -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ and $\cos(240°) = -\tfrac{1}{2}$. Both negative, as expected for the third quadrant.

$\sin(-30°)$

A negative angle means clockwise rotation. Going $30°$ clockwise from $(1, 0)$ lands at $\left(\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\right)$ — same $x$-coordinate as $30°$, but the $y$-coordinate flips sign.

So $\sin(-30°) = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\cos(-30°) = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$. This immediately tells you that sine is an odd function: $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin(\theta)$. And cosine is an even function: $\cos(-\theta) = \cos(\theta)$. These symmetries are obvious from the circle — reflecting across the $x$-axis preserves the $x$-coordinate and negates the $y$-coordinate.

$\sin(720°)$

$720° = 2 \times 360°$. Two full rotations. You end up back at $(1, 0)$. So $\sin(720°) = 0$ and $\cos(720°) = 1$. Same as $\sin(0°)$ and $\cos(0°)$. Sine and cosine are periodic with period $360°$: adding $360°$ to the angle changes nothing. Again, obvious from the geometry.

6. The Pythagorean identity

The unit circle is defined by the equation $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. The point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on this circle by definition. Substituting:

$$\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$$

This is the most important identity in trigonometry. On the previous page, we derived it from the Pythagorean theorem applied to a right triangle — but that proof only works for $0° < \theta < 90°$. The unit circle makes it true for all $\theta$, because the point is always on the circle, no matter what angle you use.

The identity $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ is literally the equation of the unit circle, rewritten using the names we gave the coordinates.

7. A bound we will need later

Theorem — Sine and Cosine Are Bounded

For all $\theta$:

$$-1 \leq \sin\theta \leq 1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad -1 \leq \cos\theta \leq 1$$
Proof

From the Pythagorean identity: $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$.

Since $\cos^2\theta \geq 0$ (it is a square), we have $\sin^2\theta \leq 1$. Taking square roots: $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$, which is the same as $-1 \leq \sin\theta \leq 1$.

The argument for cosine is identical: $\sin^2\theta \geq 0$ forces $\cos^2\theta \leq 1$, so $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$.

▮

This matters because in Phase 1, when we prove limits like $\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$ using the squeeze theorem, we will need the bound $|\sin\theta| \leq 1$ as a step. It is not a fact we assume — it is proved, from the Pythagorean theorem via the unit circle.

8. The four quadrants

The sign pattern of sine and cosine is determined by which quadrant the point falls in:

Quadrant Angle range $\cos\theta$ ($x$) $\sin\theta$ ($y$)
I$0° < \theta < 90°$$+$$+$
II$90° < \theta < 180°$$-$$+$
III$180° < \theta < 270°$$-$$-$
IV$270° < \theta < 360°$$+$$-$

This is just reading off the signs of the $x$- and $y$-coordinates in each quadrant of the coordinate plane. Nothing to memorise if you can picture the circle.

9. Radians — a brief note

Degrees divide a full circle into $360$ parts. That number is convenient (divisible by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, ...) but mathematically arbitrary. Mathematicians prefer radians, which measure the angle by the arc length it subtends on the unit circle.

A full circle has circumference $2\pi r = 2\pi \cdot 1 = 2\pi$. So a full turn is $2\pi$ radians. Conversions:

$$180° = \pi \text{ rad} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad 1° = \frac{\pi}{180} \text{ rad}$$

Common values: $90° = \frac{\pi}{2}$, $60° = \frac{\pi}{3}$, $45° = \frac{\pi}{4}$, $30° = \frac{\pi}{6}$.

Why bother? Because the formulas of calculus are cleaner in radians. In particular, the derivative $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos(x)$ is only true when $x$ is in radians. If $x$ is in degrees, a conversion factor of $\frac{\pi}{180}$ creeps in everywhere. The power series for sine (Phase 3) will use radians. For this page, degrees are fine.

Historical note

The triangle definition of trigonometry served mathematics well for over two thousand years, from Hipparchus (c. 150 BC) through the medieval Islamic mathematicians who developed it into a mature computational tool. But it was always tethered to geometry — you needed a triangle to talk about sine and cosine.

Leonhard Euler changed this in 1748 in his Introductio in analysin infinitorum. He redefined the trigonometric functions as coordinates on the unit circle, treating them as functions of a real number (the arc length or angle), not as properties of triangles. This conceptual shift — from geometry to analysis — was what allowed calculus to work with trigonometric functions. You need sine defined on all of $\mathbb{R}$ before you can differentiate it. The triangle definition gives you a function on $(0°, 90°)$. Euler's definition gives you a function on $(-\infty, \infty)$.

It is one of the cleanest examples of mathematical progress: the old definition is not wrong, it is incomplete. The new definition absorbs it as a special case and extends it to the full domain that analysis requires.

10. Tangent on the unit circle

Tangent is still defined as the ratio $\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$. On the unit circle, this is $\frac{y\text{-coordinate}}{x\text{-coordinate}}$. It is undefined when $\cos\theta = 0$ (i.e. at $\theta = 90°, 270°$, or in radians $\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}$, ...) because you would be dividing by zero.

Geometrically, $\tan\theta$ is the slope of the line from the origin to the point $P$. At $90°$ and $270°$, that line is vertical, and vertical lines have undefined slope. Consistent.

11. What breaks without the unit circle

What breaks

Without the unit circle extension, trigonometry is trapped in the interval $(0°, 90°)$. Specifically:

  • No rotation. You cannot describe a wheel turning $270°$, a robot arm sweeping through $180°$, or an orbit completing multiple revolutions. The triangle definition gives you at most a quarter turn.
  • No negative angles. A pendulum swings forward (positive) and backward (negative). An AC current alternates polarity. The triangle definition has no concept of direction.
  • No sine as a function on $\mathbb{R}$. Calculus needs $\sin : \mathbb{R} \to [-1, 1]$, a function defined on every real number. The triangle definition gives $\sin : (0°, 90°) \to (0, 1)$. You cannot differentiate a function that only exists on a quarter of a turn.
  • No waves. Sound, light, electricity, radio, quantum mechanics — all modelled by sinusoidal functions that oscillate through $(-\infty, +\infty)$. Without the extension, no wave equation, no Fourier analysis, no signal processing.

The triangle definition is a starting point. The unit circle is where trigonometry actually lives.

Sine and cosine are the $y$- and $x$-coordinates of a point walking around the unit circle — and because it is a circle, $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$.
